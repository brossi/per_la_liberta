MI spinsi sino a Zurigo, ove trovai un nucleo di compatrioti sottrattisi, coll'esilio, alla bufera reazionaria succeduta ai moti del Febbraio. Fra essi era Radonitz che mi accolse con molta cortesia. Egli non fece la benché minima allusione all'incidente di Locamo. Mi recai a far visita anche al Caronti. Le sue parole non potevano essere più lusinghiere; ma al primo vedermi, la sua faccia aveva assunto una espressione di meraviglia, non scevra da una tinta di inquietudine. Forse, pensai, è una mia immaginazione. Giuseppe Mazzini mi aveva tanto mal prevenuto!...

Quel negoziante di chincaglierie, fallito a Como, viveva a Zurigo con grande lusso, alle spalle di una ricca signora, fabbricante di cioccolata. Si era però ingegnato di metter su una modesta fabbrica di cappelli di feltro e parecchi Italiani vi trovavano di che guadagnarsi il sostentamento. Divenni del loro numero e mi parve un raggio di fortuna. Ma fiutando l'aria, non tardai a scoprire qualcosa di eterogeneo. Eran strette di mano che si risolvevano in un semplice contatto della pelle; risposte evasive; monosillabi, lindi per sé stessi, ma che acquistavano un sapore subdolo per la smorfia del viso che li accompagnava. Nel complesso, una freddezza, una freddezza!... persino a merenda, quando le labbra si scambiavano frasi più spontanee, i fili misteriosi che connettono l'anime, difettavano.

Di che mi si faceva appunto? Forse di essere venuto lì d'attorno al loro pane e di sottrarne una porzioncella?

Le cose improvvisamente mutarono e così radicalmente, da farmi sospettare una intesa. Ma quale, ma perchè?... Sparite le angolosità, mi davano del tu; mi battevano confidenzialmente sulla spalla, mi offrivano del tabacco... pareva si fossero finalmente decisi a considerarmi, in tutto e per tutto, come uno dei loro. Tanto che un Sabato, mi proposero una notturna gita di piacere sul lago. Quattro colpi di remo, quattro boccali, magari otto, un coro patriottico sotto il cielo stellato. Sarebbe come un rivivere oltre l'Alpi, nel bel paese ove languivano le nostre innamorate.

Per non sembrare scortese, accettai. Volle il caso che, per rimediare a non so quale dimenticanza, quella sera stessa tornassi alla fabbrica. Con grande sorpresa la vidi illuminata. Nell'avvicinarmi udii distintamente la voce concitata dell'esule Orioli che diceva: "Per Dio, non mi va! Non avrei più il coraggio di pensare alla mia povera vecchia!" Entrai e parve che una mano di ferro mettesse il bavaglio ad ogni bocca (1).

Da quel giorno nessuno più mi parlò né del lago, né dei boccali, né del cielo stellato. Il perché non seppi, né mi curai di indagarlo. Tuttavia n'ebbi la certezza che la subitanea cordialità dei miei compagni era simulata; che io mi trovavo, come un intruso, solo ed odiato in mezzo a loro. Finii per sentirmene disgustato e decisi di partire per Parigi.

Insalutato ospite? Non totalmente. Il Caronti, onesto o disonesto che fosse, mi aveva procurato lavoro; credetti mio dovere il fargli una visita di congedo. Mi procurai così una nuova sorpresa. Al mio arrivo a Zurigo, egli mi aveva accolto con parole ilari e cortesi, ma con un viso che rivelava la sua disillusione; ora, conosciuto appena il motivo della mia visita, si profuse in un diluvio di frasi esprimenti rammarico; ma la sua intera sensazione non si tenne compressa e improntò il viso con segni che ritenni indiscutibili. La mia partenza gli cagionava un piacere intenso.

Giunsi a Parigi nel Dicembre (1853) con due franchi in tasca ed una lettera di raccomandazione per certo Zappa, cappellaio torinese, la quale mi era stata rilasciata dal mio capo fabbrica, l'unica persona, a Zurigo, che mi avesse dimostrato qualche simpatia. Coi due franchi non feci molto cammino; ma la lettera mi giovò. Il Zappa, uomo dalle opinioni liberali e dal cuore largo, mi offerse subito l'ospitalità. Bisognava adattarsi a dormire su un materasso, in cucina; ma al verde e di quella stagione, l'offerta riesciva tutt'altro che disprezzabile. Tanto più che trovai modo di sdebitarmi, dando lezioni d'italiano alla padrona di casa.

La signora Zappa era francese. Aveva idee più che liberali, rivoluzionarie. Esse bastarono a stabilire fra noi rapporti cordialissimi. Non era giovane, non era bella; ma conservava una grazia al cui fascino non riesciva facile il sottrarsi. Quando parlava del 2 Dicembre, al quale aveva preso parte attiva, e delle fiere repressioni a cui, per buona ventura, seppe sottrarsi, diventava eloquente e, nel calore delle rievocazioni, dimenticava persino i corsetti che fabbricava ed ai quali, a dir vero, più che all'eroismo, doveva la propria popolarità.

Per non vivere alle spalle di quella buona gente, mi diedi d'attorno e finii per trovare un'occupazione presso il ponte della Bastiglia. Essa mi fruttava due franchi al giorno. Quale occupazione? Per quattordici ore filate giravo, a forza di braccia, la ruota di una giostra.

L'avere le mani alla ruota, non mi impediva di avere gli occhi alla politica. E a quell'epoca, anche per chi non fosse in Francia, occuparsi di politica era un occuparsi di Luigi Napoleone.

Il colpo di Stato, considerato con esecrazione dalle coscienze nette, benedetto dal Papa, venne accolto dagli altri Sovrani come una grande prodezza, combinata ad invidiabile perspicacia. Il sangue versato non era di quello ritenuto azzurro e lo stupore, da cui venne presa la Francia liberale, era ai loro occhi, la pace ristabilita. E poiché Luigi Napoleone liberava i Governi reazionari dallo spettro della Repubblica, troppo spesso confuso, e ad arte, con quello del terrore, riesciva naturalissimo che i Governi reazionari si affrettassero a convergere benevolmente il loro sguardo verso Luigi Napoleone, a riconoscerne l'avvento, ad aspettarsene di grandi cose.

Dopo il colpo di Stato, la Dittatura! Napoleone era troppo scaltro per abusarne, senza contrapporre alle persecuzioni odiose, una sequela di misure atte ad adescare la moltitudine. La Francia rigurgitava di braccia disposte ad inalzare le barricate. Non potendo incatenarle, provvide, con sagace precipitazione, ad accaparrarsele. Per impulso partito da Parigi, ogni centro della Francia, grande e piccolo, venne preso dalla febbre dei prestiti e delle pubbliche migliorie. E l'onda di benessere che si diffuse nelle classi lavoratrici, ebbe sensibile incremento dalle Leggi sanitarie intese a migliorarne le abitazioni; dal rimaneggio delle imposte che, levate dagli oggetti di prima necessità, gravarono su quelli di lusso. Aggiungasi la larghezza dei prestiti agricoli.

In tutta la Francia, ai colpi di cannone, erano così successi quelli del martello e l'effetto morale, che naturalmente scaturiva dalle migliorate condizioni economiche, veniva rafforzato dalle splendide promesse che mantenevano verde la speranza, che facevano supporre duraturo quell'inaspettato periodo di straordinaria prosperità. Luigi Napoleone, a dargli retta, abboniva la guerra. Egli si proponeva molte conquiste, ma eran le conquiste della Pace... terreni da bonificare, vasti tratti da aprire alla coltivazione, porti da approfondire, canali da scavare, fiumi da rendere navigabili. In queste imprese utili e gloriose, la spada avrebbe ceduto il primato agli attrezzi del lavoro ed ogni contadino, ogni operaio, animato da buoni propositi, sarebbe diventato suo fido guerriero.

Concetti degni d'encomio! Ma per potervi prestar fede, era necessario radere dalla mente ogni triste ricordo; perdere di vista il fatto che quell'uomo fatale consumava mezza giornata a dissimulare, con frasi lusinghiere, le male azioni che si riserbava di compiere nell'altra mezza.

Disgraziatamente, dalle moltitudini, costrette quasi in perpetuo, a consacrare ogni attività per soddisfare, a mala pena, i bisogni dello stomaco, non è ragionevole l'aspettarsi un culto dell'ideale antiveggente e pertinace. L'anima popolare non procede alle sante rivendicazioni se non sotto l'impulso di circostanze eccezionali e quindi a sbalzi, necessariamente altrettanto tumultuosi quanto fugaci. Ed è molto quando non tenti di rifarsi del tempo perduto, deturpando la sua ira magnanima con manifestazioni feroci. Poi il bisogno ineluttabile risospinge la mandria a brucar l'erba... l'anima rimanda a tempi migliori i suoi conati verso il meglio e si riaddormenta.

Il Popolo francese non comprese, o non volle comprendere, che ogni principe nuovo, sorto assaporando il delitto e propenso a perpetuarlo, non vi si attenta finché, praticando astutamente la virtù, non abbia dato agio al mal seme di assodare le proprie radici. La laboriosa digestione intorpidì il pensiero, le rosee tinte del presente impedirono di intuire i fantasmi minacciosi che si sarebbero addensati all'orizzonte. E quando il Senato servile ristabilì, nella persona di Luigi Napoleone e nei suoi eredi maschi, la dignità imperiale, la moltitudine si affrettò a ratificare. Quasi otto milioni di Sì... un'esigua minoranza di duecentocinquantatremila No.

Il 1 Dicembre 1852, un anno solo dopo essersi imbrattate le mani e l'animo nel sangue dei propri concittadini, Luigi Napoleone cingeva, a Saint Cloud, la corona di Francia e assumendolo, incideva nella Storia il nome di Napoleone III.

Ricostituito l'Impero, ritoccata la Costituzione, i vari rami del Governo assunsero, in apparenza, la loro regolare funzione; in realtà, la Francia continuò ad essere retta a Dittatura... larvata sinché si vuole, ma non meno assoluta.

Il principe consorte, venuto da Londra nella primavera del 1854, per cementare l'amicizia che si delineava tra l'Inghilterra e il nuovo Impero, ebbe ad esprimere, in proposito, la propria alta meraviglia. Napoleone stesso candidamente gli confidava che egli non permetteva mai ai suoi Ministri di radunarsi per discutere la pubblica cosa. Ogni singolo Ministro trattava direttamente con lui qualsiasi affare d'importanza e raramente veniva confidato all'uno, quello che egli aveva deliberato coll'altro.

Questo governo tutto personale, riputato pernicioso persino dai vecchi sovrani, induceva noi liberali, a formulare, sull'avvenire della Francia, i più foschi pronostici. L'idea fissa, sulla quale doveva imperniarsi la politica di Napoleone III, divenne subito manifesta... emulare il grande intelletto che aveva tratto dal nulla la propria casa; che aveva impresso orme profonde in tanta e sì eletta parte del mondo. Egli si accingeva all'ardua impresa con una sfrenata ambizione, con una smania smisurata d'intrigo, un'assoluta mancanza di fede alla data parola ed ai trattati, una corruzione atta a minare ben altre costituzioni; l'assenza di ogni principio rigeneratore; un ingegno mediocre appena appena; la sua presunzione; il suo gran nome.

Come mai l'immensa sproporzione fra i mezzi e lo scopo avrebbe potuto non allarmarci? Il pensiero, la dinamica di un grande Stato, concentrati in quella natura fosca ed ambigua erano, ai nostri sguardi, la potenza consacrata a compiere il male, a sospingere, con passi precipitosi, una nobile Nazione allo scorno ed alla rovina. A me dev'essere pur lecito, dopo mezzo secolo, il valermi della Storia per poter accertare, coll'indiscutibile senno del poi, quanto le nostre previsioni fossero fondate.

Napoleone III ebbe un lampo di intelligenza pratica alleandosi coll'Inghilterra. Ma, cingendo il proprio capo della corona, non aveva soffiato via dall'animo la sua naturale duplicità. Egli non seppe mostrarsi alleato sincero e i suoi grossolani raggiri irritarono siffattamente la fine diplomazia britannica, da trovarsela ostile nei momenti di supremo bisogno. L'Inghilterra gli avrebbe anzi mosso guerra dichiarata e aperta, se le vittorie germaniche non l'avessero prevenuta.

Napoleone III non seppe tramutare la guerra colla Russia in una gloriosa sollevazione di Popoli anelanti a reprimere la tirannia dei barbari. Essa si ridusse ad un lungo, sanguinoso assedio e quando Sebastopoli cadde, fu perchè il generale Pelissier si rifiutò virilmente, e non senza frasi pungenti, di assoggettarsi agli ordini venuti da Parigi.

La guerra d'Italia! Si insiste nel dichiararla caldeggiata da lui solo, dimenticando lo slancio col quale il Popolo francese sottoscrisse il prestito indetto per coprirne le spese. Due sono le battaglie campali di quella guerra: Magenta e Solferino. La prima fu vinta quasi a dispetto dell'Imperatore, per la disobbedienza del generale Mac Mahon. Nella seconda la fibra napoleonica ebbe come un postumo bagliore e fu l'unica vittoria del secondo Impero dovuta ai meriti personali di Napoleone III. Ma subito dopo, la sua volgarità ripiglia il sopravvento. Egli stracciava, senza preavviso, i patti dell'alleanza; insultava il Re che gli aveva eroicamente combattuto al fianco. E lo scopo sostanziale della guerra, quello di sostituire nella Penisola, all'austriaca, la propria influenza, doveva, grazie al virile patriottismo del partito d'azione italiano, fallire miseramente. Napoleone III, anche vincitore, uscì dalla lotta cruenta, irremissibilmente sconfitto.

La spedizione nel Messico, originata da speculazioni finanziarie non confessabili, è avventura degna di un filibustiere. Incominciò con un tradimento analogo a quello che spianava al suo Oudinot la via di Roma, finì come la guerra d'Italia, colla vergognosa rotta dei patti solennemente assunti. Il fatto che Massimiliano, e non l'Imperatore, cadde vittima del piombo messicano, sta a dimostrare quanto poco la Giustizia umana, o divina, governi le cose del mondo.

Nei negoziati segreti, che precedettero e seguirono la disfatta della Danimarca e le strepitose vittorie della Prussia sull'Austria, Napoleone, giocando a partita doppia, offrendo contemporaneamente la propria alleanza all'una e all'altra parte contendente, mirava ad ottenere una mancia dal vincitore; a rivendicare alla Francia la linea del Reno. Ma venne deluso e schernito e coperto di ridicolo dalla sagacia di Bismarck.

Che dire della guerra franco-prussiana da lui voluta, da lui intrapresa senza la più elementare conoscenza delle forze nemiche e delle proprie?

Io non giudico la grandezza degli uomini dal loro successo, perché conosco sconfitte infinitamente più gloriose di qualsivoglia trionfo. Ma perchè ciò sia, le rovine devono apparirci come irradiate e rese sante dalla grandezza del pensiero, dalla nobiltà del sacrificio, dalla sublimità dello scopo. La vita del terzo Napoleone mi sembra, sotto questo punto di vista, arida quanto un deserto.

Si capisce! si capisce! Nessuno poteva precorrere i tempi e scorgere nell'incerto futuro, la catastrofe positiva. Ma già nella primavera del 1854, abbondavano le ragioni tangibili che legittimavano le diffidenze, anzi, l'odio profondo dei liberali. Luigi Napoleone, calpestando i principii che aveva giurato di difendere pena la morte, aveva, coi suoi delitti, fiaccata la Francia e le toglieva ogni possibilità di quelle iniziative democratiche che sono la massima tra le sue glorie. Egli si era mostrato più codardamente feroce appunto contro le fazioni che avevano maggiormente contribuito a trarlo dalla quasi oscurità in cui, per tanto tempo, aveva vissuto. La gratitudine pesa all'anime volgari. Egli doveva pur rendere di tutto ciò un conto più stretto di quelli che si bilanciano su una pagina di Storia... la Storia troppo spesso venale e menzognera, come la coscienza degli uomini che la scrivono.

Ai liberali italiani riesciva, in modo speciale, tormentosa la potenza di quell'uomo che aveva corrisposto ai loro benefici ed alle loro aspettative propugnando gli interessi del secolare nemico della Patria; che aveva sacrificato il fiore della gioventù italica per vituperare Roma risorta; per risospingerla nelle braccia del papato; per prostituirla a viva forza, a quanto di più mostruoso era sopravvissuto alla barbarie dei secoli tramontati.

Nella primavera del 1854, già si riteneva più che probabile, imminente, l'alleanza di Napoleone III coi Savoia. E poiché era noto che l'Impero non voleva l'unità d'Italia e Vittorio Emanuele II e i suoi Ministri non la ritenevano possibile, se ne arguiva che, da simile connubio, sarebbe tutt'al più, derivato un ingrandimento territoriale del Piemonte. Qualche principe francese sostituito ai tirannelli che infestavano l'Italia; ma il Papa a Roma, sostenuto dall'armi imperiali e ligio, per forza d'istinti e di circostanze, più agli interessi dell'Impero che alla causa dell'indipendenza italiana. La storia avrebbe rinnovato le sue antiche fasi; noi avremmo avuto sul collo, coi vecchi, i nuovi signori.

Quest'ordine d'idee traeva forza dal matrimonio di Napoleone III con Eugenia Montijo, contessa di Teba, spagnola di bellezza impareggiabile, irrequieta, intelligente, astuta, pertinace, cattolica non per calcolo, ma per radicate convinzioni. Grazie alla sua influenza, sul trono di Francia, virtualmente, con Napoleone III, siedeva il prete.

La causa della Libertà non avrebbe potuto riprendere il suo fatale andare, né in Francia, né in Italia, sinché tra essa e l'avvenire si frapponesse Napoleone III. Dunque bisognava pensare a sopprimerlo... affrettare il ritorno delle cose al loro ordine naturale; risparmiare, con lieve sacrificio, una sequela di tumulti e d'odi e fiumi di sangue...

Meditai a lungo, presi i miei appunti e scrissi... E a chi potevo scrivere se non alla coscienza più serena che conoscessi, all'uomo che aveva saputo sorprendere i palpiti della Patria e proclamarla viva e preconizzarla grande, quando tutti la credevano inesorabilmente distesa nel sepolcro?

Egli trovavasi allora a Londra. Gli comunicai minutamente un mio piano (di cui avrò occasione di parlare più oltre) chiedendogli, per effettuarlo, il suo aiuto; disposto ad andare sino al fondo, o a recedere, secondo il suo consiglio. Felice Orsini, che a quell'epoca, era tuttora amicissimo del Mazzini, vide la mia lettera ed è ad essa che devo i miei futuri rapporti col patriota di Meldola.

Ma, né a quella mia prima comunicazione, né a due altre successive, Giuseppe Mazzini rispose. Mi rammentai del Pianciani e, in una quarta lettera, a lui diretta, sfogai tutta la mia amarezza per quel silenzio, indubbiamente voluto, che mi sapeva di immeritato disprezzo.

La risposta non si fece aspettare e fu breve e terribile. "A Londra mi s'accusava d'essere stato causa volontaria dell'arresto di Fortunato Calvi e del fallimento dei tentativi del Calore. L'accusa veniva da Zurigo."

Così l'Austria, impotente a strozzarmi, si era vendicata della mia fuga!... isolarmi, annientarmi colla calunnia. E fu come se un velo mi cadesse improvvisamente dagli occhi. Adesso comprendevo la condotta de' miei compagni di Zurigo; né occorse grande penetrazione per interpretare il movente segreto delle stranezze del Caronti. Il veleno austriaco era stato sparso dalle sue labbra.

Al Pianciani replicai senza por tempo di mezzo e, nel mio risentimento, non misurai neppure le frasi che alludevano al Mazzini; così facile — osservai — a prestare orecchio alle diffamazioni di un uomo, sulla cui onoratezza egli stesso, pochi mesi prima, mi aveva espresso i più gravi sospetti. Conclusi chiedendo i mezzi per recarmi ad affrontare il mio accusatore.

Il Pianciani mi inviò centocinquanta franchi; ma accompagnati dalla quasi ingiunzione di starmi quieto e d'aspettare dal tempo la mia rivendicazione. Intascati i quattrini, abbracciai, con grande effusione, i coniugi Zappa e voltai le spalle a Parigi... Era uno splendido mattino del Maggio 1854 quando improvvisamente, dalle alture, vidi stendersi ai miei piedi il lago azzurro e scintillare, ai primi raggi del sole, i tetti umidi della vecchia Zurigo.

Mi feci annunciare al Caronti, senza rivelare il mio nome, come un esule che desiderava comunicargli un messaggio importante. Lunga fu l'anticamera; ma breve il nostro colloquio. Egli era ancora in veste da camera e mi ricevette stando ritto, i due pollici conficcati nella cintola. Non appena mi conobbe, impallidì e mormorò tra i denti: "Che cosa è venuto a far qui?" — Ve lo dirò fra un secondo! — Serrai l'uscio e fattomi innanzi così che, quasi, i nostri fiati si confondevano, appagai di tutto punto la sua curiosità, rafforzando il mio dire colla richiesta di una soddisfazione. "E badate bene! L'esigo e l'avrò prima di uscire da questa camera!"

Spiò, con sguardo furtivo, oltre la finestra spalancata e si provò a guadagnar tempo, protestando d'essere innocente. Anzi, egli aveva sempre avuto per me una stima incondizionata, un affetto quasi fraterno. Sparlare di chicchessia!? Avrebbe preferito gli cascasse a brandelli la lingua!

— E sia! Ma le parole se le porta il vento e gli scritti rimangono. Attestate con un po' di nero sul bianco, la vostra stima incondizionata; rilasciatemi una formale ritrattazione. Questo e non altro mi appagherà!

Mentre io dicevo, le sue labbra tremavano. Credo comprendesse, dalla mia fisionomia, che non era tempo di tergiversazioni. Si fece alla scrivania, si asciugò l'onesto sudore dalla fronte e scrisse. Poi, senza voltarsi, allungò il braccio e mi porse il foglio.

Lessi e mi accontentai di dire: Sta bene!

Allora, come se fossimo vecchi amici intenti a gareggiare di gentilezze, egli si sbracciò ad offrirmi i suoi servigi... in qualunque parte del mondo, in qualsiasi tempo. Non avevo che a manifestare il desiderio. Se poi ero diretto verso l'Italia e stimavo opportuna una piccola sosta a Lugano... mi avrebbe consegnato una commendatizia pel Conte Grillanzoni che vi si era stabilito.

Evidentemente voleva scoprire terreno per prepararmi qualche sua tristizia. Gli troncai le frasi in bocca. Quel che avrei fatto ignoravo; ad ogni modo ciò riguardava me solo. Rigirata la chiave, uscii.

Nel corridoio mi sentii mettere una mano sulla spalla. E una voce mi sussurrò nell'orecchio: "Come va Moretto?"

Era Angelo Fumagalli, pallido e sorridente. Si pose un dito attraverso le labbra e soggiunse: "Vieni in scuderia. Parleremo a nostro agio!" Mi prese per la destra e, con mite violenza, mi trascinò, come se fossi un fanciullo. "So tutto, ho inteso tutto! Ti ho visto attraversare il giardino e ti ho subito riconosciuto. Mi son detto: Qui vi sono delle novità. Quali novità? Ho avuto dei brutti sospetti e... perchè nascondertelo? ho commesso un'azionacela, ho posto l'orecchio all'uscio. Ma meglio così! Non vi saranno più equivoci. Sai che quella carogna ti ha persino accusato d'aver rubato un orologio al suo cocchiere? Questa poi non l'ho mai creduta. Quanto al resto... tu devi perdonarci. Che cosa vuoi? Siamo cascati nella trappola come un branco d'imbecilli. E l'hai scampata bella, sai? Ti rammenti quel progetto di gita notturna sul lago? Si era complottato di stordirti con un colpo di randello sul capo e di gettarti, mani e piedi legati, in pasto ai pesci! Il Caronti vi avrebbe denunciati e, d'un sol colpo, liberava l'Austria di un intero vespaio. Il Satanasso! Per fortuna il Grioli ebbe giudizio per tutti e ti ha salvato. Benedetto lui! Noi si credeva di vendicare la Patria!"

Mi abbracciò e singhiozzava. Poi precisamente come aveva fatto l'altro, ma con diversa mira: "Ed ora che intendi di fare?"

— Mio caro, ho tentato tutto quanto dipendeva da me per compiere il mio dovere e mi son visto sospettato da coloro appunto che avrebbero dovuto difendermi. Mi resta una sola via: persistere, rispondere alle accuse col silenzio e coll'azione.

Partii da Zurigo coll'intenzione di riparare nel Piemonte e feci la mia sosta a Lugano e la mia visita al Conte Grillanzoni. Mi accolse freddamente... compresi e gli porsi la ritrattazione del Caronti. Allora egli mi mostrò a sua volta, una lettera ricevuta pochi giorni prima. In essa lo stesso messere lo esortava a fare di tutto perchè io non ritornassi. Il mio carattere irascibile, diceva, era causa di continui corrucci e di scandali che, pel buon nome della nostra emigrazione e nell'interesse della causa "che ci sta tanto a cuore" era imperioso l'evitare.

I PUSHED on as far as Zurich, where I found a nucleus of compatriots who had escaped, by way of exile, the reactionary storm that had followed the disturbances of February. Among them was Radonitz, who received me with great courtesy. He made not the slightest allusion to the incident at Locarno. I also paid a visit to Caronti. His words could not have been more flattering; but upon first seeing me, his face had assumed an expression of wonder not unmixed with a tinge of unease. Perhaps, I thought, it is my imagination. Giuseppe Mazzini had so thoroughly prejudiced me against him!

That dealer in trinkets, who had gone bankrupt at Como, was living in Zurich in great luxury, at the expense of a wealthy lady who manufactured chocolate. He had, however, contrived to set up a modest felt hat factory, and several Italians found there the means to earn their sustenance. I became one of their number, and it seemed to me a ray of fortune. But sniffing the air, I was not long in discovering something heterogeneous. There were handshakes that resolved themselves into a mere contact of skin; evasive replies; monosyllables, innocent enough in themselves, but which acquired a subtly treacherous flavour from the grimace of the face that accompanied them. In all, a coldness, a coldness! — even at the afternoon meal, when lips exchanged more spontaneous phrases, the mysterious threads that connect souls were wanting.

Of what were they finding fault with me? Perhaps of having come there about their bread and filching from it a small portion?

Things suddenly changed, and so radically as to make me suspect some understanding among them. But what understanding, and why? — The sharp edges were gone; they used the familiar *tu* with me; they clapped me confidentially on the shoulder, offered me tobacco — it seemed they had at last made up their minds to regard me, in every respect, as one of themselves. So much so that one Saturday, they proposed a nocturnal pleasure excursion on the lake. Four strokes of the oar, four tankards, eight perhaps, a patriotic chorus under the starlit sky. It would be like a reliving, beyond the Alps, of that fair land where our sweethearts languished.

So as not to seem discourteous, I accepted. It so chanced that, to remedy some forgotten errand or other, that very evening I returned to the factory. To my great surprise I found it lit up. As I drew nearer I heard distinctly the excited voice of the exile Orioli, saying: "By God, it does not suit me! I should never have the courage to think of my poor old mother again!" I entered, and it seemed as though an iron hand had gagged every mouth. *(1)*

From that day forward, no one spoke to me again either of the lake, or of the tankards, or of the starlit sky. The reason I never learned, nor did I trouble to investigate it. Nevertheless I had the certainty that the sudden cordiality of my companions was feigned; that I found myself, like an intruder, alone and hated in their midst. I ended by feeling thoroughly disgusted, and resolved to depart for Paris.

Without bidding farewell to mine hosts? Not entirely. Caronti, honest or dishonest as he might be, had procured work for me; I considered it my duty to pay him a farewell visit. I thus obtained a new surprise. Upon my arrival in Zurich, he had received me with cheerful and courteous words, but with a face that betrayed his disillusionment; now, having barely learned the reason for my visit, he poured forth a flood of phrases expressing regret; but his entire feeling could not keep itself compressed, and stamped his face with signs which I judged unmistakable. My departure was causing him intense pleasure.

I reached Paris in December of 1853 with two francs in my pocket and a letter of recommendation for a certain Zappa, a Turinese hatter, which had been given me by my foreman at the factory — the one person in Zurich who had shown me any sympathy. With the two francs I did not make much headway; but the letter served me well. Zappa, a man of liberal opinions and generous heart, immediately offered me his hospitality. I had to make do with sleeping on a mattress in the kitchen; but penniless and in that season, the offer was anything but contemptible. The more so as I found a way to repay the debt, by giving Italian lessons to the mistress of the house.

Signora Zappa was French. She had ideas that were more than liberal — they were revolutionary. These were sufficient to establish between us the most cordial relations. She was not young, she was not beautiful; but she preserved a grace whose charm it was not easy to escape. When she spoke of the 2nd of December, in which she had taken an active part, and of the fierce repressions from which, by good fortune, she knew how to extricate herself, she became eloquent, and in the fervour of her evocations forgot even the corsets she manufactured and to which, truth to tell, she owed her popularity rather more than to heroism.

In order not to live at the expense of those good people, I set myself to looking about, and ended by finding employment near the Pont de la Bastille. It earned me two francs a day. What employment? For fourteen unbroken hours I turned, by sheer force of arms, the wheel of a merry-go-round.

Having my hands to the wheel did not prevent me from keeping my eyes on politics. And in that epoch, even for one who was not in France, to occupy oneself with politics was to occupy oneself with Louis Napoleon.

The coup d'état, regarded with execration by uncorrupted consciences, blessed by the Pope, was greeted by the other Sovereigns as a great feat of prowess combined with enviable sagacity. The blood that was spilt was not of the kind deemed blue, and the stupefaction into which liberal France was thrown was, in their eyes, peace restored. And since Louis Napoleon was freeing the reactionary Governments from the spectre of the Republic — too often confused, and artfully so, with that of the Terror — it was the most natural thing in the world that the reactionary Governments should hasten to bend their gaze benevolently towards Louis Napoleon, to recognise his advent, and to expect great things of him.

After the coup d'état, the Dictatorship! Napoleon was too cunning to abuse it without counterposing to odious persecutions a series of measures calculated to seduce the multitude. France was overflowing with hands disposed to raise barricades. Unable to chain them, he provided, with shrewd haste, to win them over. By impulse sent out from Paris, every centre of France, great and small, was seized by the fever of loans and public improvements. And the wave of well-being that spread through the working classes received a sensible increase from the sanitary laws designed to improve their dwellings; from the reordering of taxes, which, lifted from articles of prime necessity, were laid upon luxury goods. To this one must add the generosity of the agricultural loans.

Throughout France, the cannon-shots had thus been succeeded by the blows of the hammer, and the moral effect which naturally sprang from the improved economic conditions was reinforced by the splendid promises that kept hope green and made that unexpected period of extraordinary prosperity seem likely to endure. Louis Napoleon, to hear him tell it, abhorred war. He proposed many conquests — but they were the conquests of Peace: lands to be reclaimed, vast tracts to be opened to cultivation, harbours to be deepened, canals to be dug, rivers to be made navigable. In these useful and glorious undertakings, the sword would yield primacy to the implements of labour, and every peasant, every workman animated by good intentions would become his faithful warrior.

Sentiments worthy of commendation! But in order to place faith in them, it was necessary to wipe from the mind every bitter memory; to lose sight of the fact that that fateful man spent half his day dissembling, with flattering phrases, the evil deeds he reserved for himself to commit in the other half.

Unfortunately, from the multitudes — constrained almost perpetually to consecrate every faculty to the scant satisfaction of the body's needs — it is not reasonable to expect a worship of the ideal that is far-seeing and tenacious. The popular soul does not press forward to its holy vindications save under the impulse of exceptional circumstances, and therefore by fits and starts, necessarily as tumultuous as they are fleeting. And it is a great deal if it does not then attempt to make up for lost time by defiling its magnanimous wrath with ferocious manifestations. Then the ineluctable need drives the herd back to graze — the soul defers its strivings toward the better to happier times, and falls asleep again.

The French people did not understand, or would not understand, that every new prince, risen to power having tasted crime and inclined to perpetuate it, does not venture upon it so long as, craftily practising virtue, he has not allowed the evil seed time to consolidate its roots. The laborious digestion stupefied thought; the rosy hues of the present prevented men from divining the threatening phantoms that were massing on the horizon. And when the servile Senate restored, in the person of Louis Napoleon and his male heirs, the imperial dignity, the multitude hastened to ratify it. Almost eight million Yeas — a meagre minority of two hundred and fifty-three thousand Nays.

On the 1st of December 1852, only a year after having stained his hands and soul in the blood of his fellow-citizens, Louis Napoleon donned, at Saint Cloud, the crown of France; and in assuming it, inscribed in History the name of Napoleon III.

The Empire reconstituted, the Constitution retouched, the various branches of Government assumed, in appearance, their regular function; in reality, France continued to be governed by Dictatorship — veiled as much as one might wish, but no less absolute.

The Prince Consort, who came from London in the spring of 1854 to cement the friendship that was taking shape between England and the new Empire, had occasion to express his own great wonder in this regard. Napoleon himself candidly confided to him that he never permitted his Ministers to assemble together to discuss public affairs. Each individual Minister dealt directly with him on any matter of importance, and what he had deliberated with one was rarely confided to another.

This wholly personal government, deemed pernicious even by the old sovereigns, led us liberals to frame the most sombre prognostications regarding France's future. The fixed idea upon which the politics of Napoleon III were to pivot became manifest at once — to emulate the great intellect that had drawn its house from nothing; that had impressed deep traces upon so great and so elect a portion of the world. He was embarking upon the arduous enterprise with unbridled ambition, a measureless mania for intrigue, an absolute want of faith to the given word and to treaties, a corruption fit to undermine quite other constitutions; the absence of every regenerating principle; a mediocre intelligence, barely so; his own presumptuousness; his great name.

How could the immense disproportion between the means and the object have failed to alarm us? The thought, the driving force of a great State, concentrated in that dark and ambiguous nature, were, in our eyes, the power consecrated to the accomplishment of evil, to propelling, with precipitate steps, a noble Nation toward disgrace and ruin. I must surely be permitted, after half a century, to avail myself of History in order to verify, with the indisputable wisdom of hindsight, how well founded our forebodings were.

Napoleon III had one flash of practical intelligence in allying himself with England. But in crowning his head with the imperial diadem, he had not blown from his soul his natural duplicity. He knew not how to show himself a sincere ally, and his gross machinations so thoroughly irritated the fine diplomacy of Britain as to find it hostile to him in his moments of supreme need. England would indeed have moved declared and open war against him, had the Germanic victories not forestalled her.

Napoleon III knew not how to transform the war with Russia into a glorious uprising of peoples yearning to crush the tyranny of the barbarians. It was reduced to a long and bloody siege, and when Sebastopol fell, it was because General Pelissier manfully refused — and not without pungent phrases — to submit to the orders that came from Paris.

The war for Italy! It is insisted that it was championed by him alone, forgetting the impulse with which the French people subscribed the loan raised to cover its expenses. There are two pitched battles in that war: Magenta and Solferino. The first was won almost in spite of the Emperor, through the disobedience of General Mac Mahon. In the second, the Napoleonic fibre had something of a posthumous gleam, and it was the only victory of the Second Empire owing to the personal merits of Napoleon III. But immediately afterward, his vulgarity regains the upper hand. He tore up, without warning, the articles of the alliance; insulted the King who had heroically fought at his side. And the essential object of the war — that of substituting his own influence in the Peninsula for the Austrian — was destined, thanks to the virile patriotism of the Italian party of action, to fail most miserably. Napoleon III, even as a conqueror, emerged from the bloody struggle irremediably defeated.

The expedition to Mexico, originating in financial speculations that could not be avowed, is an adventure worthy of a filibuster. It began with a betrayal analogous to that which smoothed the way to Rome for his Oudinot, and ended, as had the war for Italy, with the shameful tearing up of solemnly undertaken obligations. The fact that it was Maximilian, and not the Emperor, who fell a victim to Mexican lead, serves to demonstrate how little human or divine Justice governs the affairs of the world.

In the secret negotiations that preceded and followed the defeat of Denmark and the resounding victories of Prussia over Austria, Napoleon, playing a double game and simultaneously offering his alliance to both contending parties, aimed at obtaining a gratuity from the victor and at reclaiming for France the line of the Rhine. But he was disappointed and mocked and covered with ridicule by the sagacity of Bismarck.

What is to be said of the Franco-Prussian war, which he willed, which he undertook without the most elementary knowledge of the enemy's forces or his own?

I do not judge the greatness of men by their success, for I know defeats infinitely more glorious than any triumph. But for that to be so, the ruins must appear to us as irradiated and rendered sacred by the grandeur of the thought, the nobility of the sacrifice, the sublimity of the purpose. The life of the third Napoleon seems to me, from this point of view, arid as a desert.

It is understandable, perfectly understandable! No one could have outrun the times and discerned in the uncertain future the positive catastrophe. But already in the spring of 1854 there abounded tangible reasons that legitimised the mistrust — nay, the profound hatred — of liberals. Louis Napoleon, trampling underfoot the principles he had sworn on pain of death to defend, had, by his crimes, broken France and deprived her of every possibility of those democratic initiatives which are the greatest of her glories. He had shown himself most cowardly and ferocious precisely against the factions that had contributed most to drawing him out of the near-obscurity in which, for so long a time, he had lived. Gratitude weighs heavy upon vulgar souls. He was yet to render a stricter account of all this than those that are balanced upon a page of History — History, too often venal and mendacious, like the consciences of the men who write it.

For Italian liberals it was, in particular, a torment to contemplate the power of that man who had repaid their benefits and their hopes by championing the interests of the ancient enemy of the Fatherland; who had sacrificed the flower of Italian youth to dishonour Rome reborn; to thrust her back into the arms of the papacy; to prostitute her by main force to the most monstrous things that had survived from the barbarism of ages past.

In the spring of 1854, it was already considered more than probable — imminent — that Napoleon III would ally himself with the House of Savoy. And since it was known that the Empire did not desire the unity of Italy, and that Vittorio Emanuele II and his Ministers did not deem it possible, it was inferred that from such a union there would derive, at most, a territorial enlargement of Piedmont. Some French prince substituted for the petty tyrants who infested Italy; but the Pope at Rome, sustained by the imperial arms and subservient, by force of instinct and circumstance, more to the interests of the Empire than to the cause of Italian independence. History would renew its ancient phases; we should have upon our necks, along with the old masters, the new ones.

This train of ideas derived further strength from the marriage of Napoleon III to Eugenia Montijo, Countess of Teba — a Spanish lady of matchless beauty, restless, intelligent, astute, tenacious, and Catholic not by calculation but by deeply rooted convictions. Thanks to her influence, upon the throne of France there sat virtually, alongside Napoleon III, the priest.

The cause of Liberty could not have resumed its fatal course, either in France or in Italy, so long as Napoleon III stood interposed between it and the future. Therefore it was necessary to think of suppressing him — to hasten the return of things to their natural order; to spare, at slight sacrifice, a sequence of tumults and hatreds and rivers of blood.

I meditated long, took my notes, and wrote. And to whom could I write, if not to the most serene conscience I knew — to the man who had been able to surprise the heartbeats of the Fatherland and proclaim her living, and to foretell her greatness, when all believed her inexorably laid out in the tomb?

He was at that time in London. I communicated to him in minute detail a plan of mine — of which I shall have occasion to speak further on — requesting his assistance in carrying it out; disposed to go to the uttermost limit, or to draw back, as his counsel might dictate. Felice Orsini, who at that period was still on the closest terms of friendship with Mazzini, saw my letter, and to it I owe my future relations with the patriot of Meldola.

But neither to that first communication of mine, nor to two further ones that followed, did Giuseppe Mazzini reply. I recalled Pianciani, and in a fourth letter, addressed to him, I poured out all my bitterness at that silence — undoubtedly deliberate — which tasted to me of undeserved contempt.

The reply was not long in coming, and it was brief and terrible. "In London I was accused of having been the voluntary cause of the arrest of Fortunato Calvi and of the failure of the attempts on the Calore. The accusation came from Zurich."

Now I understood the conduct of my companions in Zurich; nor did it require any great penetration to interpret the secret motive behind the eccentricities of Caronti. The Austrian poison had been diffused by his lips.

I concluded by requesting the means to go and confront my accuser.

Pianciani sent me one hundred and fifty francs, but accompanied by what was almost an injunction to keep quiet and to await my vindication from time. Having pocketed the money, I embraced the Zappa couple with great warmth, and turned my back on Paris. It was a splendid morning in May of 1854 when, suddenly, from the heights, I saw spreading at my feet the blue lake, and glittering in the first rays of the sun, the damp rooftops of old Zurich.

I had myself announced to Caronti, without revealing my name, as an exile who wished to communicate an important message to him. The anteroom was long, but our interview brief. He was still in his dressing-gown and received me standing upright, his two thumbs thrust into his waistband. As soon as he recognised me, he went pale and muttered between his teeth: "What has he come here to do?" — I shall tell you in a moment! — I shut the door, and stepping forward so that our very breath was almost mingled, I satisfied his curiosity to the full, reinforcing my words with a demand for satisfaction. "And mark well: I require it, and I shall have it before I leave this room!"

He peered furtively out of the wide-open window and attempted to gain time, protesting his innocence. On the contrary, he had always entertained for me an unconditional esteem, an almost brotherly affection. Speak ill of anyone? He would have preferred his tongue to fall from his mouth in shreds!

— Very well! But words are carried off by the wind, and writing remains. Set down in black and white your unconditional esteem; furnish me with a formal retraction. That and nothing else will satisfy me!

As I spoke, his lips trembled. I believe he understood, from my expression, that this was no time for evasion. He went to the writing desk, wiped the honest sweat from his brow, and wrote. Then, without turning round, he extended his arm and held out the sheet to me.

I read it, and contented myself with saying: Very well!

Thereupon, as though we were old friends vying with one another in civilities, he fell over himself to offer me his services — in whatever part of the world, at whatever time. I had only to express the wish. If moreover I was making for Italy, and thought a brief halt at Lugano advisable, he would furnish me with a letter of introduction for Count Grillanzoni, who had settled there.

He was evidently seeking to reconnoitre the ground in order to prepare some fresh villainy for me. I cut off his phrases at the mouth. What I would do I did not know; in any case it concerned me alone. Turning the key, I went out.

In the corridor I felt a hand laid upon my shoulder. And a voice whispered in my ear: "How goes it, Moretto?"

It was Angelo Fumagalli, pale and smiling. He put a finger across his lips and added: "Come to the stables. We can talk at our ease!" He took my right hand and, with gentle violence, dragged me along as though I were a child. "I know everything, I heard everything! I saw you cross the garden and recognised you at once. I said to myself: There is something fresh afoot here. What, though? I had some ugly suspicions, and — why hide it from you? — I did a rather shabby thing: I put my ear to the door. But better so! There will be no more misunderstandings. Do you know that that scoundrel even accused you of having stolen a watch from his coachman? That one I never believed. As for the rest — you must forgive us. What would you have? We fell into the trap like a pack of imbeciles. And you had a narrow escape, let me tell you! Do you remember that plan for a nocturnal excursion on the lake? A plot had been hatched to stun you with a blow of a cudgel on the head and to throw you, hands and feet bound, as food for the fishes! Caronti would then have denounced you all, and at one stroke freed Austria of an entire hornets' nest. The very devil! Fortunately Grioli had sense enough for everyone and saved you. God bless him! We thought we were avenging the Fatherland!"

He embraced me and was sobbing. Then, precisely as the other had done, but with different intent: "And now what do you mean to do?"

— My dear fellow, I have tried everything within my power to do my duty, and I have found myself suspected by the very men who should have defended me. One road alone remains to me: to persist, to answer the accusations with silence and with action.

I left Zurich with the intention of taking refuge in Piedmont, and made my halt at Lugano and my visit to Count Grillanzoni. He received me coolly — I understood, and held out to him the retraction signed by Caronti. He thereupon showed me in turn a letter received a few days before. In it that same worthy was urging him to do everything in his power to prevent my return. My irascible character, he said, was the cause of continual vexations and scandals which, for the good name of our emigration and in the interest of the cause "so dear to us all," it was imperative to avoid.

I renounced Piedmont and returned immediately to Zurich. In the meantime Angelo Fumagalli had, with indignant manner, taken his leave of Caronti. From him I learned that Felice Orsini had been making enquiries to discover my address.

A few days later I was engaged in the attempt at Maloggia.

The *coup d'état*, regarded with execration by clear consciences, blessed by the Pope, was received by the other Sovereigns as a great feat, combined with enviable perspicacity. The blood shed was not of that deemed blue, and the stupor which seized liberal France was, in their eyes, peace restored. And since Louis Napoleon freed the reactionary Governments from the spectre of the Republic, too often confused, and artfully so, with that of terror, it was perfectly natural that the reactionary Governments should hasten to converge their gaze benevolently upon Louis Napoleon, to recognise his advent, to expect great things of him.

After the *coup d'état*, the Dictatorship! Napoleon was too shrewd to abuse it without counterposing to the odious persecutions a series of measures apt to entice the multitude. France was overflowing with arms ready to raise the barricades. Unable to chain them, he proceeded, with sagacious haste, to win them over. By an impulse starting from Parigi, every centre in France, large and small, was seized by a fever of loans and public improvements. And the wave of well-being that spread through the working classes was given a perceptible increase by the sanitary Laws intended to improve their housing; by the reshuffling of taxes which, lifted from items of first necessity, were laid upon those of luxury. To this may be added the generosity of agricultural loans.

Throughout France, the cannon shots had thus been succeeded by those of the hammer, and the moral effect, which naturally sprang from the improved economic conditions, was reinforced by the splendid promises that kept hope green, that led one to suppose that this unexpected period of extraordinary prosperity would be lasting. Louis Napoleon, to hear him tell it, abhorred war. He proposed many conquests, but they were the conquests of Peace... lands to be reclaimed, vast tracts to be opened to cultivation, ports to be deepened, canals to be dug, rivers to be made navigable. In these useful and glorious enterprises, the sword would yield primacy to the tools of labour, and every peasant, every worker, animated by good intentions, would become his faithful warrior.

Concepts worthy of praise! But to be able to believe them, it was necessary to erase from the mind every sad memory; to lose sight of the fact that that fatal man consumed half the day dissimulating, with flattering phrases, the evil actions he reserved for himself to carry out in the other half.

Unfortunately, from the multitudes, compelled almost in perpetuity to consecrate every activity to satisfying, with great difficulty, the needs of the stomach, it is not reasonable to expect a cult of the prescient and pertinacious ideal. The popular soul proceeds to holy vindications only under the impulse of exceptional circumstances, and therefore in bursts, necessarily as tumultuous as they are fleeting. And it is a great thing when it does not attempt to make up for lost time by disfiguring its magnanimous wrath with ferocious manifestations. Then ineluctable need drives the herd back to graze... the soul postpones its strivings toward the better to better times, and falls asleep again.

The French People did not understand, or did not wish to understand, that every new prince, having risen by tasting crime and inclined to perpetuate it, does not attempt it until, by astutely practising virtue, he has given the evil seed time to secure its own roots. Laborious digestion dulled the thought, the rosy hues of the present prevented the intuition of the menacing phantoms that would gather on the horizon. And when the servile Senate re-established, in the person of Louis Napoleon and his male heirs, the imperial dignity, the multitude hastened to ratify it. Almost eight million Yeses... a slender minority of two hundred and fifty-three thousand Noes.

On the 1st of December 1852, a single year after having stained his hands and soul in the blood of his own fellow citizens, Louis Napoleon donned, at Saint Cloud, the crown of France and in assuming it, engraved upon History the name of Napoleon III.

The Empire reconstituted, the Constitution retouched, the various branches of Government assumed, in appearance, their regular function; in reality, France continued to be ruled as a Dictatorship... veiled as you will, but no less absolute.

The Prince Consort, who came from Londra in the spring of 1854 to cement the friendship that was taking shape between England and the new Empire, had occasion to express, on this subject, his high wonder. Napoleon himself candidly confided to him that he never permitted his Ministers to gather to discuss public affairs. Each individual Minister dealt directly with him on any matter of importance, and what he had decided with one was rarely confided to another.

This entirely personal government, reputed pernicious even by the old sovereigns, induced us liberals to formulate, upon the future of France, the darkest prognoses. The fixed idea, upon which the policy of Napoleon III was to be hinged, immediately became manifest... to emulate the great intellect who had drawn his own house from nothing; who had impressed deep footprints upon so great and so select a part of the world. He was girding himself for the arduous enterprise with an unbridled ambition, with an immeasurable craving for intrigue, an absolute lack of faith in his given word and in treaties, a corruption apt to undermine far greater constitutions; the absence of every regenerating principle; a barely mediocre intellect; his presumption; his great name.

How could the immense disproportion between the means and the end have failed to alarm us? The thought, the dynamics of a great State, concentrated in that dark and ambiguous nature were, to our eyes, the power consecrated to accomplishing evil, to driving, with precipitous steps, a noble Nation to scorn and ruin. It must be permissible for me, after half a century, to avail myself of History to be able to ascertain, with the indisputable wisdom of hindsight, how well-founded our predictions were.

Napoleon III had a flash of practical intelligence in allying himself with England. But, in placing the crown upon his own head, he had not blown from his soul his natural duplicity. He did not know how to show himself a sincere ally, and his clumsy deceptions so irritated the fine British diplomacy that he found it hostile in moments of supreme need. England would indeed have waged declared and open war upon him, had the Germanic victories not forestalled her.

Napoleon III did not know how to transform the war with Russia into a glorious uprising of Peoples longing to repress the tyranny of the barbarians. It was reduced to a long, bloody siege, and when Sebastopoli fell, it was because General Pelissier manfully refused, and not without pungent phrases, to submit to the orders come from Parigi.

The war of Italy! It is insisted that it was championed by him alone, forgetting the enthusiasm with which the French People subscribed to the loan issued to cover its expenses. There are two pitched battles in that war: Magenta and Solferino. The first was won almost in spite of the Emperor, by the disobedience of General Mac Mahon. In the second, the Napoleonic fibre had, as it were, a posthumous glimmer, and it was the only victory of the second Empire due to the personal merits of Napoleon III. But immediately after, his vulgarity regained the upper hand. He tore up, without warning, the pacts of the alliance; he insulted the King who had heroically fought at his side. And the substantial aim of the war, that of substituting in the Peninsula his own influence for the Austrian, was, thanks to the virile patriotism of the Italian party of action, to fail miserably. Napoleon III, even as a victor, emerged from the bloody struggle, irretrievably defeated.

The expedition in Messico, originating from unconfessable financial speculations, is an adventure worthy of a filibuster. It began with a betrayal analogous to that which cleared the way for his Oudinot to Roma, and ended like the war of Italy, with the shameful breach of solemnly assumed pacts. The fact that Massimiliano, and not the Emperor, fell victim to Mexican lead, demonstrates how little human, or divine, Justice governs the things of the world.

In the secret negotiations that preceded and followed the defeat of Denmark and the resounding victories of Prussia over Austria, Napoleon, playing a double game, simultaneously offering his alliance to one and the other contending party, aimed to obtain a gratuity from the victor; to claim for France the line of the Rhine. But he was deluded and mocked and covered with ridicule by the sagacity of Bismarck.

What is to be said of the Franco-Prussian war, which he wanted, which he undertook without the most elementary knowledge of the enemy's forces and of his own?

I do not judge the greatness of men by their success, for I know defeats infinitely more glorious than any triumph. But for this to be so, the ruins must appear to us as if irradiated and made holy by the greatness of the thought, by the nobility of the sacrifice, by the sublimity of the purpose. The life of the third Napoleon seems to me, from this point of view, as arid as a desert.

Of course! Of course! No one could run ahead of the times and perceive in the uncertain future the positive catastrophe. But already in the spring of 1854, tangible reasons abounded which legitimised the diffidence, nay, the profound hatred of the liberals. Louis Napoleon, by trampling on the principles he had sworn to defend on pain of death, had, with his crimes, broken France and deprived her of any possibility of those democratic initiatives which are the greatest of her glories. He had shown himself most cowardly and ferocious precisely against the factions that had most contributed to drawing him from the near obscurity in which, for so long, he had lived. Gratitude weighs upon vulgar souls. He would surely have to render a stricter account of all this than those which are balanced on a page of History... History, too often venal and mendacious, like the conscience of the men who write it.

To Italian liberals, the power of that man was especially tormenting, for he had responded to their benefactions and their expectations by championing the interests of the age-old enemy of the Fatherland; he had sacrificed the flower of Italic youth to vituperate resurrected Roma; to push her back into the arms of the papacy; to prostitute her by main force to all that was most monstrous to have survived the barbarism of bygone centuries.

In the spring of 1854, the alliance of Napoleon III with the House of Savoy was already considered more than probable, imminent. And since it was known that the Empire did not want the unity of Italy and that Vittorio Emanuele II and his Ministers did not believe it possible, it was inferred that, from such a union, a territorial enlargement of Piemonte would, at most, derive. Some French prince substituted for the petty tyrants who infested Italy; but the Pope in Roma, supported by imperial arms and bound, by force of instinct and circumstance, more to the interests of the Empire than to the cause of Italian independence. History would have renewed its ancient phases; we would have on our necks, with the old masters, the new.

This order of ideas drew strength from the marriage of Napoleon III with Eugenia Montijo, Countess of Teba, a Spaniard of incomparable beauty, restless, intelligent, astute, pertinacious, Catholic not by calculation, but by deep-rooted conviction. Thanks to her influence, on the throne of France, virtually, with Napoleon III, sat the priest.

The cause of Liberty could not have resumed its fatal course, neither in France nor in Italy, so long as Napoleon III interposed himself between it and the future. Therefore it was necessary to think of suppressing him... to hasten the return of things to their natural order; to spare, with a slight sacrifice, a succession of tumults and hatreds and rivers of blood...

I meditated at length, took my notes, and wrote... And to whom could I write if not to the most serene conscience I knew, to the man who had known how to capture the heartbeats of the Fatherland and proclaim it alive and prophesy it great, when all believed it inexorably laid in its tomb?

He was then in Londra. I communicated to him in detail a plan of mine (of which I shall have occasion to speak further on), asking him for his help to carry it out; prepared to go to the very end, or to desist, according to his counsel. Felice Orsini, who at that time was still a very close friend of Mazzini, saw my letter, and it is to it that I owe my future relations with the patriot of Meldola.

But to neither that first communication of mine, nor to two successive others, did Giuseppe Mazzini reply. I remembered Pianciani and, in a fourth letter, addressed to him, I vented all my bitterness at that silence, undoubtedly deliberate, which felt to me like undeserved contempt.

The reply was not long in coming, and it was brief and terrible. "In Londra I was accused of having been the voluntary cause of the arrest of Fortunato Calvi and of the failure of the Calore attempts. The accusation came from Zurigo."

Thus Austria, powerless to strangle me, had taken revenge for my flight!... to isolate me, to annihilate me with calumny. And it was as if a veil suddenly fell from my eyes. Now I understood the conduct of my companions in Zurigo; nor did it take great penetration to interpret the secret motive for Caronti's oddities. The Austrian poison had been spread by his lips.

I replied to Pianciani without a moment's delay and, in my resentment, did not even measure the phrases that alluded to Mazzini; so ready—I observed—to lend an ear to the defamations of a man about whose honour he himself, a few months before, had expressed to me the gravest suspicions. I concluded by asking for the means to go and confront my accuser.

Pianciani sent me one hundred and fifty francs; but accompanied by the near-injunction to keep quiet and to await my vindication from time. Having pocketed the money, I embraced, with great effusion, the Zappas and turned my back on Parigi... It was a splendid morning in May 1854 when suddenly, from the heights, I saw the blue lake spread out at my feet and the damp roofs of old Zurigo glitter in the first rays of the sun.

I had myself announced to Caronti, without revealing my name, as an exile who wished to communicate an important message to him. The wait in the antechamber was long; but our colloquy was brief. He was still in his dressing gown and received me standing, his two thumbs stuck in his waistband. As soon as he knew me, he grew pale and muttered between his teeth: "What have you come here to do?" — I shall tell you in a second! — I locked the door and, stepping forward so that our breaths almost mingled, I satisfied his curiosity to the full, reinforcing my words with the demand for satisfaction. "And mark my words! I demand it and I will have it before I leave this room!"

He spied, with a furtive glance, beyond the wide-open window and tried to gain time, protesting his innocence. On the contrary, he had always had for me an unconditional esteem, an almost fraternal affection. To speak ill of anyone!? He would have preferred his tongue to fall out in shreds!

— So be it! But words are carried away by the wind, and writings remain. Attest in a bit of black on white to your unconditional esteem; give me a formal retraction. This and nothing else will satisfy me!

While I was speaking, his lips trembled. I believe he understood, from my physiognomy, that it was not a time for tergiversations. He went to the writing desk, wiped the honest sweat from his brow, and wrote. Then, without turning, he stretched out his arm and handed me the paper.

I read it and was content to say: That is fine!

Then, as if we were old friends intent on competing in kindnesses, he bent over backwards to offer me his services... in whatever part of the world, at any time. I had only to express the desire. If, then, I were headed towards Italy and deemed a short stop in Lugano opportune... he would give me a letter of introduction for Count Grillanzoni who had settled there.

Evidently he wanted to discover the ground to prepare some new mischief for me. I cut his sentences short. What I would do, I did not know; in any case, that concerned me alone. Turning the key, I went out.

In the corridor, I felt a hand placed on my shoulder. And a voice whispered in my ear: "How goes it, Moretto?"

It was Angelo Fumagalli, pale and smiling. He placed a finger across his lips and added: "Come to the stable. We will speak at our ease!" He took me by the right hand and, with gentle violence, dragged me along as if I were a child. "I know everything, I heard everything! I saw you cross the garden and recognised you at once. I said to myself: There is news here. What news? I had some ugly suspicions and... why hide it from you? I committed a nasty little act, I put my ear to the door. But it is better this way! There will be no more misunderstandings. Do you know that that carrion even accused you of having stolen a watch from his coachman? That one I never believed. As for the rest... you must forgive us. What would you have? We fell into the trap like a pack of imbeciles. And you had a narrow escape, you know? Do you remember that plan for a nocturnal trip on the lake? It had been plotted to stun you with a blow of a club on the head and to throw you, hands and feet tied, as food for the fishes! Caronti would have denounced you all and, at a single stroke, rid Austria of a whole hornets' nest. The Satan! Fortunately, Grioli had sense for all of us and saved you. Bless him! We thought we were avenging the Fatherland!"

He embraced me and sobbed. Then, precisely as the other had done, but with a different aim: "And now what do you intend to do?"

— My dear fellow, I have attempted everything that depended on me to do my duty, and I have seen myself suspected by the very ones who ought to have defended me. Only one path remains for me: to persist, to answer the accusations with silence and with action.

I left Zurigo with the intention of repairing to Piemonte and made my stop in Lugano and my visit to Count Grillanzoni. He received me coldly... I understood and handed him Caronti's retraction. Then he, in turn, showed me a letter received a few days before. In it, the same gentleman exhorted him to do everything to prevent my return. My irascible character, he said, was the cause of continuous vexations and scandals which, for the good name of our emigration and in the interest of the cause "that is so dear to our hearts," it was imperative to avoid.

I gave up on Piemonte and returned immediately to Zurigo. In the meantime, Angelo Fumagalli had, in a resentful manner, taken his leave of Caronti. From him I learned that Felice Orsini had made inquiries to know my address.

A few days later I was engaged in the Maloggia attempt.

## Chapter Twenty-Two

HISTORIANS affirm that the spy Felicita Buanvecchiato, having caught some fragment of a conversation held by Pietro Fortunato Calvi, hastened to reveal our attempt to the Authorities, who thus had full wind of it even before we had taken the first steps to carry it into effect.

To what extent that denunciation went, I know not; but I do know that in bringing about our ruin it was well-nigh superfluous.

How I came from London to Belluno without molestation or mishap, I have already narrated. Others, having set out from different points but with the identical objective, did not have equal fortune.

Foremost among the merits of a conspirator is that of knowing how to judge, at a glance, which papers he must keep and which destroy — a simple art, and yet so difficult. Ignorance of it has caused the collapse of infinite enterprises that would have altered the course of History; it has furnished the executioner with innumerable victims.

Francesco Chinaglia and a certain Cerruti, both from Lonato, both of our number, cross the Alps in all safety and arrive at a small village on the border of the Tyrol and Cadore. Here they commit the fatal indiscretion of raising their elbows too much and picking a quarrel with the innkeeper. They are arrested, taken to the gendarmerie, and searched. What is this? An itinerary marked with initials and annotations that arouse some suspicion. The Police take notice and detain them, and arrest, at brief intervals, Luigi Moratti of Castiglione delle Stiviere, one Roberto Morin, and a Fontana. Either they carry in their pockets the same compromising itinerary, or they cannot give a satisfactory account of themselves. The official report states that these men were arrested at the same time as Calvi, who passed through those parts on the 17th of September. When the latter was interrogated, he gave himself out as a certain Meyer, a subject of the Duchy of Baden, and produced a corresponding passport; but it availed him nothing. He was taken to the Police and searched, like the others — and from the lining of his overcoat was drawn forth, signed by Mazzini, Kossuth, and Ledru Rollin, his commission as general-in-chief of the Italian revolution. It was Calvi himself who, out of an excessively modest conception of his own influence, had insisted upon obtaining that document. A formality as vain as it was fatal. Who would have contested the first place, or denied obedience to the modern Leonidas, in that Cadore which was his Thermopylae?

He was conveyed to Mantova. The grim walls of that fortress had already swallowed up don Bastiano Barozzi, my father, and my sister Luisa. Poor Luisa! — her indomitable instinct for self-expression confined within a veritable sepulchre; her delicate constitution tortured by the iron, inhuman discipline; her sensitive heart rent by the thought of her father’s sufferings and by the unheard-of miseries that surrounded her; the modesty of her girlhood violated at every hour by the coarse searches of the turnkeys, insolent and brutal — not a word of comfort, not love, not light!

Did she receive compensation for it? Margherita di Savoia, having become Queen of Italy, was pleased to pay a visit to the women who had, for political reasons, savoured the delights of Mantova. She haughtily forgot Luisa of the Counts of Rudio. From her point of view, she was not wrong. Our family had fought and suffered, in the dark times, so that Italy might exist; but it had not fought and suffered to round out the dominions of the House of Savoia.

The Castello di San Giorgio at Mantova had become, by then, what the Spielberg had been in the days of Ferdinando. Other fortresses compressed, in their dungeons, the pulsings of hearts that yearned for the redemption of the Fatherland; but in none, as at Mantova, did the ferocity of the oppressor manifest itself in tragedies so vast, so sinister, so unrelenting. And yet, fatally, from that torment of bodies the Italian soul blazed forth. In their fearful caution, the Culoz, the Kraus, the Benedeck did their utmost to conceal from the civilised world the identity of the imprisoned and the terrible barbarity of their methods of procedure. But there are virtues that cannot be hidden, that spread their light beyond the walls of any Bastille; revealing, by their very excellence, the full abjection of the ephemeral supremacy that has sought to crush them.

Italy saw, trembling, passing through the narrow compass of those secret cells without pomp, in rags almost, yet no less bright than of old, the iron temper of her Attilio Regolo, of her Trasea Peto; no less serene than in the ancient world, the soul of more than one Socrate — and it was no small spur towards the finding of herself again. A period of history sweating martyrdom, it found keen researchers who co-ordinated its pages, who documented, with meticulous care, its every phase — but not yet its own Tacitus to immortalise it in prose of granite, to draw from it an austere monument worthy of such greatness. Let him arise! — on that day the thousand and thousand statues that Italy lavishes upon her crossroads will seem the toys of pygmies.

When Calvi was thrust into the Castello di San Giorgio, don Giovanni Grioli, Pietro Frattini, Angelo Scarsellini, Count Carlo Montanari, Carlo Poma, Giovanni Zambelli, don Enrico Tazzoli, Tito Speri, the hero of the ten days of Brescia — giants of duty, a chosen contingent from every class of society — had already submitted, beneath the shadow of the noose, to the extreme insult of despotism.

A designated victim, there was instead immured, some months later — precisely on the 28th of March 1855 — Felice Orsini, who was afterwards, by sawing through the double bars of a window and lowering himself on his bedsheets and crippling a foot, to regain his liberty and exercise so great an influence upon my destiny.

Felice Orsini was, for a certain period, confined in Cell No. 9, where my father was held together with several other patriots. He told me, in 1857 in London, that the good old man, breaking prison discipline through the kindly condescension of the warders, used to help his fellow captives to bear the bitterness of their long imprisonment by gathering them about him and humming the romances then most in vogue.

Felice Orsini also had the opportunity to correspond, by tapping on the wall, with Pietro Fortunato Calvi. They encouraged one another, expressing the hope that they might soon go forth, free among the free. But the heroic defender of Cadore harboured no illusion whatsoever. He knew too well the systems of government dear to Austria and, from the same wretched fate of the ten martyrs who had preceded him within those walls, he inferred his own inexorable condemnation.

Throughout the long, tedious trial, he displayed the imperturbable calm of a man accustomed, upon the field of battle, to look death in the face, and only kindled with noble indignation when his judges, having brought him face to face with an abject informer, did not blush to prefer the purchased word of that creature to his own — the word of a loyal enemy, a man of honour.

His participation in the movements aimed at overthrowing, by force, a tyranny that had imposed itself by force, he admitted without reticence; the companions who had been arrested with him he defended to the last, affirming that they had joined him to take part in an alpine excursion, knowing nothing of his subversive designs; the secret of the other conspirators’ complicity remained jealously guarded within his adamantine breast, and my father and Luisa and don Bastiano Barozzi, held long on simple suspicion, eventually came out into the open by virtue of a decree of amnesty.

And the sad foreboding of Pietro Fortunato Calvi came true. By sentence of the 17th of January 1855, confirmed at Vienna the following June, he was condemned to die by the noose. At thirty-eight years of age! For an enterprise that was scarcely conceived, much less begun. So anxious did Austria show herself to suppress the men who, to the power of thought, would have known how to couple action, and become agitators and leaders of other men!

In his last days, he desired a meeting with his companions in misfortune, and, rather than seeking comfort from them, took pains to comfort them. He wrote most affectionate letters to his brother Luigi and to his father who, a faithful servant of Austria, condemned as madness and worse that headlong patriotism of his, that irrepressible need for action. And this thought cost him the only tear that History records of him.

And he wrote a letter to Teresa Duodo of Padova, the young woman whose kisses he had promised himself as the ineffable reward, once the Fatherland was redeemed. That same young woman, broken by the tragic parting, lived on in voluntary, perpetual widowhood.

Austria had devised, in those times, a new species of mercy — to grant some clemency, provided the condemned man requested it in humiliating terms and as a repentant sinner. If the clemency were not sought, she could say: “The charge of cruelty flung at me from every corner of the world is unjust. I was prepared to pardon, but it was not wished.” Whereas if the petition for clemency reached her, the very terms in which it was couched stripped all prestige from the imprudent patriot — to live physiologically, but at the price of one’s own moral suicide.

It was affirmed, in my time, that Calvi was urged to solicit that singular clemency. He is said to have answered with scorn: “My hatred for Austria is more intense than my love of life. I shall ask nothing and I want nothing from the oppressors of my Country!” If this be legend, how well it harmonises with that strong and upright temper!

But what is certainly not legend is the dignified bearing with which he faced death. He moved towards the gallows as towards a goal foreseen from the very outset of his heroic struggle with the oppressor, and it seemed that, having meditated long years upon its horrors, his spirit had grown impervious to them.

Having crossed the Ponte di San Giorgio, he thrust his head from the funeral carriage and gazed, with sorrowful eye, upon the city and the countryside. I am firmly persuaded that he, a lover of “the truer and the greater,” mourned at that moment his inability to have realised the yearning of his life. Over those lands he saw no sacred colours of redemption raised aloft; he saw no free men scattered across the fields, cheerfully at their labours.

He drew back and returned to the profound meditations upon what lies beyond the grave — which he, despite the residue left in his soul by sinisterly reactionary schools, had always pictured as Hellenically serene.

Having arrived at the foot of the gallows, he took the cigar from his mouth and gave it, still lit, to one of his guards. He thrust aside with scornful roughness the arm of one who sought to help him mount the plank set beneath the column from which he was to hang. From that height he looked down, smiling, upon the Austrian officers, several of whom had removed their hats in a sign of respect. And having kissed monsignor Martini repeatedly — a priest held in disfavour, for his honesty, in those places where prebends are dispensed — he said in a firm voice: “I am ready!” Thereupon the executioner set about his work.

To crown the misfortune, the gallows did not correspond to the great stature of Calvi, whose feet tickled the earth. His agony was long and exceedingly painful. Edoardo Winkler, commanding the military escort, was seized with fierce indignation and could not contain himself. He cursed the executioner and, once the execution was complete, departed almost fleeing, his hands thrust into his hair.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi was the last of the great men murdered for the Italian cause. He remained hanging for several hours; lowered at evening, he was cast into a pit dug at the foot of the gallows.

But his greatness moved even a hyena — the notorious warder Francesco Casati — who composed his remains in a coffin and preserved his relics for the veneration of resurgent Italians.

And the virtue that the Mantuan Bastille had been unable to conceal seemed to blaze forth from that humble grave, dug at the foot of a scaffold. His moral influence was so great, so intense the treasury of latent energies that it stirred to life, as to disquiet the oppressors. The Marshal Radetzky, already decrepit, already on the point of abandoning for ever the hateful contests of the world, felt no shame in pressing the Special Tribunal that had condemned Calvi to draw up a defamation, in order to paralyse the “deleterious” influence of that shade.

He was to find in the President Visentin the base soul disposed to second him. How many official lucubrations, which nonetheless serve as a guide for exalting or vilifying the individuals belonging to history, repeat an analogous origin and have no greater worth than that of Visentin’s!

The sycophancy that blossomed after the hour of triumph, busily engaged in the jealous care of attributing all merit for the redemption of Italy to the great and the small who knew how to profit by it, glides with the lightness of an aeroplane over the beneficent influence exercised, beyond the grave, by the martyrs who sacrificed their existence to rouse the indolent. It is an influence that may be intuited, but that eludes all calculation — all the easier, therefore, to adulterate the truth!

Yet Calvi was fortunate in this: the spite of the partisan cliques, which spared no adversary, however great and unblemished, seemed to blunt itself before this figure so disinterested, so calm in its audacity. And it was said that Italy “would have had in the hero of Cadore a second Garibaldi.” But the opportunity of becoming one was criminally wrested from him.
