THUS on the fateful 6th of February I was neither at the assault on the Gran Guardia in Milano, nor aboard the *Kadetzky* on Lago Maggiore.

Instead, early in the morning, I was climbing a steep path that, branching off from the Ceresio, a few steps from Lugano, led to a villa gleaming white as the snow that surrounded it. Its green grilles were the only conspicuous note against that vast whiteness. Having reached the level ground that faced the villa, I stopped to contemplate it, and the words of Fortunato Calvi passed through my mind: "Let the arms do their duty; the mind keeps watch and will do its own!"

But if arriving up there had been an easy undertaking, being introduced to the man was not to prove so easy for me. The surroundings, seemingly solitary and tranquil, concealed fluttering hearts and were as though saturated with a bustling silence. I felt, from beyond the walls, the suspicious eye that was spying on me, and in the robust domestic who approached me, stooped and subdued, I glimpsed the conspirator ready to straighten up and, if need be, even attempt a bold stroke. Instead of answering his questions, I pronounced the password and extended my hand to him as to a brother. I added that I was the bearer of a letter of introduction to the Master, from Colonel Calvi.

But the Master, being extremely busy, had given positive orders not to be disturbed. And as I insisted and pleaded the urgency of the matter, the feigned domestic snatched the letter from my right hand, assuring me that he would deliver it personally. It was a stroke of luck that he invited me to enter.

I am in a spacious and cold ground-floor room... on the mantelpiece were four logs, beautifully arranged, but not lit. A writing desk, a few bamboo chairs... the walls were bare. Here too the predominant note is whiteness. In the recess of the wide window, between two white curtains, hung a cage of brass wire, wrapped in a pink gauze... inside, a canary was hopping about. I planted myself before it; but my thoughts immediately turned elsewhere... to the little great man, towards whom converged, from every part of Europe, such an impetus of hatred, such an impetus of love. I expected to see the sturdy "domestic" reappear at any moment to announce that I was expected, that I would be welcome. I heard the door creak on its hinges and I turned. The man who stood before me was slender, dressed in black, with a black cravat at his neck. He had a high and clear forehead; his cheeks were pale, almost olivine. I felt my soul as if enveloped by the warm and serene gaze of Giuseppe Mazzini.

He was clasping between his fingers the letter that had informed him of our failure and had hurried to get a fuller account of the particulars. He listened to my narration with great composure, and when, by way of conclusion, I drew out the money I had wrested from avaricious egoism, he took it and put it away, without counting it, in the drawer of the writing desk. Not a word, not the blink of an eye!... whether because he deemed honesty disdainful of praise, or because his grave thoughts prevented him from dwelling on minor things.

He sat down and motioned for me to do likewise. But I remained standing and apart, and while he meditated, I observed, marveling, that slender, spare body, the pallor of his face, the intellectual, most delicate profile. His grizzled head seemed to me too heavy for the neck that supported it—an impression perhaps accentuated by his posture, for the little great man was supporting his broad forehead with his gaunt fingers.

I felt perplexed. How could such a man have been able to endure so many hardships, so many intellectual struggles, so much fury of battles? Whence came the strength that had roused so many of the inert, formed so many consciences, stirred up so many fires, revived the Fatherland?

As soon as he began to speak, I was persuaded that a great heart has great impulses, even in a slender breast. One is accustomed to admonish: "Write as you speak." Giuseppe Mazzini, in grave moments, spoke instead as he wrote.

I summarize and weave together the various parts of his speech:

"Nothing has been attempted... therefore nothing is lost! We must set to work again... but not with such people. He who meditates an undertaking and then hesitates and quibbles, works for his own enemy and unconsciously digs the abyss that will swallow himself and the hopes of the Fatherland.

"Today's sun is destined to illuminate new audacities. A people, that was never miserly with its own blood, will hurl itself, with the courage of despair, unarmed against the powerful oppressor. The streets of its city, full of gaiety, will be transformed into an inferno of groans and slaughter. If heroism be of no avail, if the arrogance of lead and numbers triumphs once more over right, the youth of Milano will tread alone the path of exile, will climb alone the steps of the gallows. But when the hatred of the present oppression, the virile impulses, the disregard for torments, have the virtue to ripen fate, we too shall reap the fruit of victory, we brothers of Italy. The Milanese rise up and sacrifice themselves for a cause that is ours. And should we limit ourselves to the part of idle spectators? Shall we demonstrate, with our egoism, to the enemy and to the world, that our chains suit us well; that the dignity of free men is unbecoming to our souls?

"Let Milano have the certainty of having aroused, beyond its walls, sympathies of intent and of fervor... and not sterile sympathy! In arduous trials, fates hang by a thread; nor is aid limited to arms and munitions. Let our solidarity sustain the arm of the courageous: let our ardor strengthen the sacred flame that drives them to action. It is imperative, it is imperative!"

He remained a moment in thought and his gaunt fingers tormented his clear forehead, as if to draw from it, by force, an inspiration. "How to provide," he murmured, "in so short a time?" Suddenly, with great emphasis:

"Varese is the point! Access for aid is easy there; the garrison is small; the citizens are patriotic and impetuous, the "Giovine Italia" is well-rooted, the pledges I received from there are serious and almost tangible. It is a matter of launching a spark.

"*Parva favilla gran fiamma seconda*."

"A thorny undertaking, not a desperate one. Whoever leaves Lugano today is on the spot tomorrow; he confers with the leaders, distributes manifestos, spreads the news of the Milanese insurrection, revives their spirits, calls them to arms, picks off the enemies one by one, organizes the barricades, sounds the alarm bells, raises the countryside.

"To do this, a legion is not needed, one man is enough, but a man who has the dexterity and audacity of a feline, the sacred enthusiasm of heroes; a man who would prefer, if need be, to die with weapon in hand, than to live a slave. Where to find him?"

Such words, spoken by such a man and at such a moment, could not simply slide off my soul. My youthful exuberance overflowed and, grown bold, I dared to presume upon myself. I said:

"Master, I began to serve my Country when the first down had barely sprouted on these lips. I have imperiled my life in Venezia, in Roma. Having emigrated to Parigi, I did my best to oppose the parricidal arms of Napoleon. And Fortunato Calvi can attest that, even recently, in Lombardo-Veneto, I faced more than once, for the good cause, the peril of dying by the halter. Certainly, I have not accomplished great things, but..."

— I understand, I understand! The spirit of sacrifice cannot be inexhaustible.

— But no, but no, Master! Have you not taught us that a right comes from a duty fulfilled? I have fulfilled mine, as best I knew how. Now I claim the right to be deemed worthy of offering once more my arm and my blood, for the good of my country.

He raised his head and I felt myself, once again, as if enveloped by the Master's warm and serene gaze. He said simply: "The Varese enterprise is yours!"

I departed at dusk, escorted by some smugglers... the smugglers who gave Austria so much trouble and whom resurrected Italy, though so prodigal with marble and bronze for the heroes... of the sixth day, seems to have totally forgotten. We walked over crags and through ravines, in silence; I marveling at their agility, they, probably, at mine. At daybreak we were at the border.

There stood in those days, near the point where we were to cross it, a hovel. At the top of its door was fixed a pine branch. This meant, in the figurative language of those parts: "Here one drinks;" just as the myrtle meant in ancient times "Here one makes love!" The leader of the party directed his steps thither, and all of us, one at a discreet distance from another, followed in his tracks.

That innkeeper, situated astride two states, sold wine and grappa to give himself... a social position. His greatest income came from the secret understandings established with the bold men who defrauded the customs. The smugglers descending into Lombardia and those who, from the Kingdom, were penetrating into the Republic, would make a stop at his cottage... to spy out the land, to receive orders, to deposit there merchandise and subversive literature.

Those who had escorted me gathered around the innkeeper. They began to confabulate in a low voice, in an agitated manner; I, prudently apart, observed some pictures pasted on the wall. Then we drank to each other's health and we drank again. Growing impatient, I went outdoors and cast a longing eye far, far over the plain below, in the direction of Varese.

But just as I was architecting days of activity, and a tiny bit of heroism too, a courier arrived at a full gallop, who was to push me back into the grey life, among the resigned, among the unknown. I was so absorbed that I did not notice him until I heard myself called by name. "Ah, it is you! I had already despaired of reaching you. I bring you a most urgent letter!" He fumbled in his greatcoat and handed it to me. As I broke the seal, my hand trembled. I read:

"Return immediately, your sacrifice would be useless. In Milano all is lost."
"Mazzini."

Brief lines, immense misfortune! To my eyes they seemed to be written with a pen dipped in the blood of martyrs. I hastily retraced my steps and, before the setting of the sun, I saw Giuseppe Mazzini again.
