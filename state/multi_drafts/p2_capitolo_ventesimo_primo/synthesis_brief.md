LIBERO... fra un anno!" Non lo avevo detto solo per egoismo, né solo per pietà e tanto meno per millanteria. Il mio primo tentativo di fuga era stato meditato, appunto di quei giorni alla Roquette. Vi avevo fatto la conoscenza di un Inglese che scontava sei mesi di carcere per bancarotta fraudolenta. Era uomo dotato di una certa coltura, di poche parole; possedeva, da quanto potevo arguire, mezzi più che bastevoli. Come la grande maggioranza dei suoi connazionali, si interessava di politica e odiava Napoleone. Egli stesso venne, per così dire, incontro all'animo mio e si offerse d'aiutarmi con tutti i suoi mezzi. "In chiesa coi santi, in taverna coi ghiottoni!" E, d'altra parte, se la società ipocrita trova comodo il tener calcolo unicamente delle colpe di un uomo per deprimerlo, malgrado le sue virtù; io trovo più logico, più fraterno il por mente alle sue virtù e l'inalarlo, malgrado le sue colpe. Aveva realmente il mio inglese acquistato in modo riprovevole le sue ricchezze? Lo spendere per ridare il marito alla vedova consorte, il padre al figlio, ritornava a buon uso il mal tolto. Accettai.

Avevamo calcolato che egli ritornasse tra i liberi prima che io fossi a Tolone. Sapevamo che i condannati, prima d'essere trasportati alle terre tropicali, fanno una tappa, più o meno lunga, a quel bagno. Non avevamo una pianta della località e il concertare un piano ben definito riesciva affare sgarrato; ma non si ignorava che se è possibile l'indurre, per danaro, un'umana creatura a commettere atti turpi, è del pari possibile l'indurla, per danaro, a tentare atti di generoso ardimento.

Uscito dalla Roquette, il mio Inglese si sarebbe recato a Tolone. Facile l'accertare, dopo il mio arrivo, la località ove fossi rinchiuso e le persone alla cui custodia ero affidato. Contemporaneamente, egli avrebbe organizzato un mezzo di trasporto rapido, per mare o per terra. Il danaro, profuso a larga mano, avrebbe spezzato le mie catene, offrendomi così l'opportunità di approfittare di quel mezzo... l'Italia e la Spagna non sono, dalla costa francese, l'ultima Tuie. Più studiavo il carattere del mio inglese e più mi convincevo che egli possedeva doti che lo rendevano capace di condurre a buon termine l'impresa.

Dirò che il giorno della sua liberazione arrivò ben presto ed io, abbracciandolo, gli consegnai, clandestinamente, una lettera per l'Elisa... la sola, come poi seppi, che ella ricevette da parte mia.

Una settimana dopo, ero trasportato per ferrovia, in uno scompartimento cellulare, sino a Marsiglia e, da Marsiglia, per vettura a cavalli, sino a Tolone. Era il mese d'Aprile (1858) e la natura rifioriva in modo portentoso quand'io fui chiuso in una casamatta, sotto il livello del mare e incatenato, con altri disgraziati, ad una spranga fissa nell'umido muraglione.

Si vuol sapere quale occupazione avesse il Conte Carlo di Rudio per il periodo di circa sette mesi? Vestivo una casacca rossa e un paio di calzoni gialli; portavo un berretto frigio di color verde, distintivo, questo, dei condannati a perpetuità (i condannati a tempo lo portavano rosso); ero senza mustacchi ed avevo i capelli tagliati a strisce. Dormivo, quando potevo dormire, su una larga trave. Le ore che vegliavo, passavo dando una caccia rabbiosa agli insetti che mi attanagliavano e sfregando l'anello che mi avvinceva. Questa mia ultima fatica fu tanto persistente e tenace che, per ben quattro volte, riescii a farmi condurre dal fabbro ferraio e a percorrere così, reggendo tra le mani la mia catena, un quarto di miglia all'aria libera... per ben quattro volte rivedendo così il Sole!

Come alla Roquette, la curiosità morbosa mi espose alla visita umiliante di questo e di quel personaggio. A quell'epoca, la Czarina tentava ristorare l'egra salute respirando l'aria balsamica della Riviera. Gli ufficiali di una squadra russa, in rotta per Nizza, ove avrebbe pigliato a bordo la pallida sovrana, approdarono a Tolone e vollero vedere colui che aveva tentato d'uccidere Napoleone... l'uomo che inflisse alla loro Patria l'umiliazione di Sebastopoli. Si proffersero di elargirci una somma di danaro per provvedere ai miei bisogni più stringenti. Ma i Regolamenti non mi concedevano altro che pane ed acqua e i carcerieri vi si opposero. Così che io non ne ricavai se non che la sterile soddisfazione di poter dire che persino le steppe dell'Europa orientale contribuirono il loro contingente allo sciame degli importuni.

Ma tra i miei visitatori ve ne fu uno, di tutt'altro genere, che merita un accenno tutto speciale.

Suor Teresa non si era accontentata della sua prima lettera e della mia prima risposta. Essa me ne aveva inviate una dozzina almeno. Mi esortavano tutte a confidare in Dio, a pentirmi dei miei trascorsi; a rivolgermi, con trasporto, alle larghe braccia della misericordia divina che certo non mi avrebbero respinto. Uggiato a morte, le avevo scritto, pochi giorni prima di lasciare la Roquette, che io ero educato a prestar fede ai fatti, solo ai fatti. Le parole scivolavano inesorabilmente sull'animo mio e le sue non erano altro se non che parole! Credevo d'aver troncata la commedia... ma non per nulla si è degni dell'apparizione del Cristo. Inaspettatamente rividi, anche a Tolone, i caratteri fini ed eleganti della mia sorella in Dio.

Mi annunciavano, niente meno! la visita di un vescovo in viaggio, come missionario, per la China. Aprissi a lui ogni mio pensiero. Era uomo santo, ricolmo di grazia celeste e non avrebbe mancato di "toccarmi il cuore".

Un giorno, ad ora affatto insolita, il cancello della casamatta si apre ed entra un vecchio sulla sessantina, avvolto in una stoffa color viola. Il carceriere mi addita e parte. Mi trovavo di fronte l'annunciato vescovo missionario... il quale si proponeva, evidentemente, di iniziare i suoi trionfi apostolici rendendo, colla mia conversione, un servigio all'Impero.

Diede la stura al colloquio, affermando che si trovava in quel luogo per incarico della sorella Teresa e soggiunse tosto che, assecondando le esortazioni di quella pia, egli aveva provveduto, con sufficiente larghezza, ai bisogni terrestri di mia moglie e del bimbo mio.

A questo punto, lo scrutai intensamente in viso ed altro non vi scorsi se non che un'espressione di candida bonomia. Eppure le sante labbra vescovili avevano mentito come quelle di un paltoniere.

Comunque sia, supponendo d'aver ben disposto il mio animo col preteso beneficio, quel vaso purissimo della grazia celeste, si provò a spalmare il balsamo che aveva deposto sul mio petto e, con voce pacata, mi annunciò come suor Teresa avesse pensato anche a me. Egli era portatore di un suo regalo. Senz'altro aggiungere, trasse di sotto la veste viola, un rosario di coralli legati in argento, appeso al quale vidi un crocefisso ed una medaglia.

Osservai senza stendere la mano e, con voce non meno pacata: "Bellissimo regalo... se potessi impegnarlo e permutarlo in tabacco. Ma molte cose vengono negate qui dentro che sono concesse ai semplici mortali. Ringrazi suor Teresa da parte mia e le scriva che ho rifiutato".

Gli occhi del vescovo ebbero un lampo da Inquisitore. "Figlio mio — disse — non rifiuterete almeno quest'altro pegno di fraterno amore. E' benedetto dal Papa e vi porterà fortuna!" E qui mi fa dondolare d'innanzi agli occhi un paio di abitini di raso, colle sembianze del Cristo ricamate in oro.

— Oh padre! In questo triste luogo, con tanto sudiciume... A che potrebbero servire se non di comodo nido ai miei pidocchi?

Egli non si diede per vinto e, senza profferir verbo, trasse e mi stese un elegante libro da messa. Ma io gli osservai che, da tempo, più non pregavo e che, in quell'accozzaglia di parole, la mia mente non avrebbe trovato pascolo alcuno.

Il colloquio ebbe bruscamente fine. Il vescovo ripose i suoi doni e, voltandomi le spalle, se ne andò, senza salutarmi, con passo che voleva sembrare dignitoso e solenne. Credo pensasse: "Se i cinesi sono tutti come questo cane, sto fresco!"

Allora, alzando le spalle, guardai i miei compagni di catena. Taluno mi osservava con aria esterrefatta, come se fossi il demonio; altri, con occhi sfavillanti, come se i miei capelli tagliati a strisce non giungessero a contender loro i tratti dell'eroe. Ma non ero né un eroe, né un demonio. Ero semplicemente un uomo ragionevole che, ispirandosi ai propri profondi convincimenti, sfuggiva all'arti subdole dell'impostura.

Credo opportuno il narrare qui, per analogia, un incidente occorso a mia moglie, mentre io già scontavo la mia condanna a Cayenna.

Nottingham, ove l'Elisa si era ritirata a passare, colla madre, i suoi vedovi giorni, era, ed è certo tuttavia, sede vescovile. I cattolici considerano quel vescovado come una rocca avanzata della verità contro l'eresia e, fantasticando, la circondano dell'aria serena che ispira i casti pensieri... una specie di oasi ove, il giorno, svolazzano le colombe; a notte scura, gli angeli luminosi.

Inaspettatamente, l'Elisa ricevette invito di recarsi al vescovado per affari di somma importanza. Memore dei miei consigli, ella non si attentò di andarvi da sola. Ma qui penso che la parola si addica meglio alle labbra della mia Elisa. Premette il bottone di un campanello e la signora subito accorse. Egli la pregò di ridire, semplicemente, il colloquio avuto col vescovo di Nottingham.

"Non c'è gran che da raccontare. Mi recai al vescovado colla mamma. Ella fu pregata di attendere nell'anticamera; io fui immediatamente condotta nello studio di Monsignore... un uomo alto, magro, pelle ed ossa. Mi accolse con una faccia compunta e, spiegando una lettera, che si guardò bene dal consegnarmi, mi disse d'essere oltremodo dolente di avere tristi nuove da comunicarmi. Un suo collega, di servizio al penitenziario di Cayenna, aveva scritto per notificargli la morte di mio marito; la quale era del resto già confermata dalla stampa e dalle Autorità francesi. Benché la sciagura fosse irreparabile ed egli simpatizzasse col mio inevitabile dolore, non era senza una profonda e serena soddisfazione del suo animo di sacerdote che egli poteva assicurarmi che ti eri sinceramente pentito dei tuoi trascorsi, che eri spirato riconoscendo la santità della religione dei tuoi padri. A questo proposito avevi anzi manifestato l'ardente desiderio che io, per la mia eterna salvezza, abbracciassi la religione cattolica e mi facessi, possibilmente, monaca. Mi ingiungevi poi, come espressione della tua ultima, assoluta volontà, di affidare il nostro figlio alle cure di qualche Istituzione cattolica, apostolica, romana, perché egli venisse allevato nei principi che ti avevano sorretto nella tua disgrazia e confortato al tuo letto di morte."

"E che hai risposto all'ottimo ecclesiastico?" "Per un momento non trovai né lagrime, né parole. Poi un lampo attraversò la mia mente e, guardandolo fisso, gli dissi: "Il Conte Carlo di Rudio non può essere morto così. E poiché questa è menzogna, è parimenti menzogna che egli non sia più. Io lo sento, mio marito vive!""

— Il prete sgualcì la lettera, la buttò sullo scrittoio e, con faccia orribile (che rivelava il desiderio di bruciarmi viva) allentando e stringendo le dita quasi fossero artigli, disse a denti stretti: "Ah se fossimo in Francia!" Fui presa da grande spavento e, gettando un grido, fuggii. Ero tanto sconvolta, che nulla potei dire alla mamma finché fummo sulla via, lontano, ben lontano!"

Così — proseguì il Conte di Rudio — ebbe fine il tentativo del clero cattolico di far la spia per conto del secondo Impero.

Mentre io, tra gli orrori della casamatta, mi affaticavo a rodere l'anello della catena per rivedere alla sfuggita il Sole, il mio Inglese si adoperava, dal canto suo, a condurre ad effetto la nostra intesa, con astuzia, con pertinacia tutta anglosassone. Aveva assunto tutte le informazioni possibili sulla località ove mi trovavo rinchiuso e sulle abitudini del carcere; si era procurato abiti di travestimento; aveva preparato luoghi di temporaneo rifugio; mezzi di trasporto sino ai Pirenei. Coll'aiuto di non so qual donna, era giunto a mettersi in comunicazione diretta col mio carceriere immediato. Si era persino stabilito che il giorno della mia fuga coinciderebbe con quello della mia prossima gita dal fabbro ferraio. Impensatamente, fui tratto al cospetto del Comandante dell'Arsenale.

Mi sentii annunciare che la domane verrei imbarcato sulla fregata La Durance. "Voi andate a Cayenna — soggiunse il Comandante in tono secco, ma non privo di un sottostrato di affabilità. Clima pessimo! Gli Europei, costretti a vivere nelle vostre condizioni, non vi possono campare più di due o tre anni. Io sono autorizzato a dirvi che, se svelate i vostri complici, sarete immediatamente rimesso in libertà e fornito dei mezzi necessari ad uscire dai confini della Francia. Dipende da voi il morire all'ergastolo, o il vivere in seno alla vostra famiglia. Scegliete!"

Risposi che nulla sapevo. Ma avessi pur avuto delle rivelazioni a fare, troppo cattivo era il concetto in cui tenevo i servitori dell'Impero, per poter prestare la minima fede alle loro promesse. Sceglievo l'ergastolo!

— Come vi piace! ribatté ruvidamente il Comandante e diede ordine che mi riconducessero alla casamatta.

La domane ero in alto mare. Il mio primo tentativo di fuga fallito!

Capitolo Ventesimo Secondo

A DURANCE filava portando nel suo ampio ventre, diviso in tre ponti, circa trecento condannati. Io e il Gomez eravamo rinchiusi nel più profondo. Ma il Comandante, che era un orleanista e non doveva nutrire soverchie antipatie per noi, non appena seppe chi eravamo, ci fece trasportare sul ponte superiore. Anzi egli spinse la sua benevole parzialità sino a permettermi di salire, di quando in quando, sopra coperta per fumare liberamente... i Regolamenti prescrivevano la bagarella di cinquanta colpi di corda a chi l'avesse fatto nei compartimenti.

Poggiammo all'isola di Santa Cruz (Canarie) per approvvigionarci d'acqua e vi rimanemmo due giorni, a finestre chiuse, per toglierci ogni possibilità di fuga; poi si procedette direttamente all'Isola reale del Saluto, ove vennero sbarcati circa duecento forzati.

Io, il Gomez ed un altro centinaio di derelitti passammo a bordo di un vaporino e l'11 Dicembre del 1858, dopo un giorno e mezzo di navigazione addizionale, eravamo ancorati di fronte alla Montagne d'Argent, che sorge sul confluente del San Giorgio e dell'Ojapok, a distanza relativamente breve dal confine del Brasile. Fioriva un tempo, in quella località, una colonia libera e libere braccia vi coltivavano il caffè ed ogni frutto tropicale che possa tentare il palato umano. Il Governo francese ne aveva fatto acquisto. I suoi ufficiali occuparono la casa del piantatore; numerose tettoie di giunchi, sorrette da pali, vi vennero erette a riparo dei condannati; la terra venne forata qua e là, per sepellirvi, a lenta agonia, i refrattari alla disciplina; sorse anche qualche edificio sostanziale pei magazzini, per la difesa e per l'offesa... l'avanguardia della civiltà si era trasformata in un ergastolo. Ma le bellezze naturali dissimula­vano meravigliosamente l'inferno che gli uomini vi avevano accumulato.

Il comandante, un Maggiore d'infanteria marina, che la Repubblica aveva messo a riposo e l'Imperatore reintegrato e fregiato d'insegne onorifiche, si affrettò a riceverci "in consegna" e a fare il nostro appello. Quando pronunciò il mio nome, l'ufficiale che ci aveva accompagnato dall'isola del Saluto, gli porse una lettera. Evidentemente mi riguardava. Egli la lesse e, squadrandomi dal capo ai piedi: "Ah siete voi quel " (giù una parolaccia da porcile!) "Sono contento di vedervi qui e vi giuro che qui lascerete la pelle!"...

Quella villania era tanto fuor di proposito, che persino le guardie fecero una smorfia di disgusto. Ma io non mi scomposi e, quasi lusingato da un complimento: "Grazie dell'augurio e lo ricambio di cuore!" Egli strinse le mascelle; ma aveva la ferocia gallica, non lo spirito francese e rimase a bocca chiusa.

Quel piccolo incidente mi persuase che alla Montagne d'Argent tirava, per me, aria cattiva e, sin d'allora, mi proposi di reprimere ogni mio impeto. La libertà relativa che veniva concessa ai disciplinari — pensai — mi può offrire il destro di guadagnarmene una maggiore... la libertà degli uomini inciviliti. Ma era destino che i miei saggi proponimenti svanissero non più tardi del giorno successivo.

Nel capannone che mi venne assegnato era una cinquantina di galeotti... formavano la mia squadra. Passai la prima notte dormendo, nella mia amaca, un sonno tormentato da uno sciame d'insetti. Al sorgere del sole ci avviammo, le ossa rotte, verso la foresta. Portavo un gran cappello di paglia, un paio di zoccoloni di legno, un paio di calzoni di tela, una blusa bruna, sul cui dosso era segnato, a grandi caratteri, il mio numero... il battesimo dell'ergastolo!

Nel traversare una palude, ebbi la mala sorte di perdere uno dei miei zoccoloni e i mozziconi dei giunchi, recisi a pelo d'acqua per facilitare il guado, mi tagliuzzarono la pianta del piede. Dovetti industriarmi a percorrere tre miglia zoppicando e segnando, a sangue, ogni mia orma; finché giunsi, ultimo della mia squadra, nel folto della foresta... splendida foresta che, per poco, non mi faceva dimenticare l'essere galeotto e lo spasimo.

Una catasta di tronchi. Il compito della giornata era di trasportarli, a spalla, sino al penitenziario, compiendo cinque viaggi consecutivi. Adocchiai un tronco che, alla vista, sembrava leggero; ma era "bois fer" e quando l'ebbi all'altezza de' miei ginocchi, ricadde rumorosamente sulla catasta. Allora, invece di ritentare la prova, sedetti, risoluto a riposare quel tanto che bastesse a ripigliar lena. Il galeotto guardiano stette ad osservarmi e quando fu certo del mio pensiero: "Alzati sporcaccione! Se non ti sbrighi, ti fo correre a calci nel sedere. Maledetto te e tutto il seme italiano!"

Se si fosse accontentato di aizzarmi colla semplice minaccia avrei, probabilmente, presa la cosa con tutta filosofia; ma quello sprezzo alla mia nazionalità, suscitò nel mio petto l'ira accumulata contro i prepotenti che ne facevano strazio. Mulinai la mia 'sabre d'abattis' e diedi un salto del quale, considerate le mie condizioni, non mi sarei creduto capace. "Bada! gli Italiani non soffrono insulti! Il primo... passi! Al secondo, la tua zucca non vede più il sole dall'alto delle tue spalle!"

I miei compagni, partiti ad uno ad uno, eran già lontani. Soli, perfettamente soli in località deserta! Inghiottì, impallidendo, la pillola. Tuttavia, a ostentazione di bravura, mi chiese il nome. "Accontentati del numero!" e gli voltai le spalle.

Si incominciava male! Ma il caso volle che il mio guardiano, pur compitando più o meno correttamente, non sapesse scrivere. Per fare il suo rapporto, dovette ricorrere allo scritturale dell'Ingegnere soprintendente; il quale, colla scorta del numero, verificò, sui registri, il mio nome e chi mi fossi. Quali sentimenti lo animassero a mio riguardo, ignoro; ma seppi che persuase il mio guardiano a non incrudelire. E nelle sue esortazioni insinuò destramente la paura: "Quell'uomo — disse — non teme né Leggi, né castighi, né Dio, né il diavolo. Se giura vendetta, o tosto o tardi, sei spacciato!"

Al suo ritorno nella foresta, il guardiano mi chiamò in disparte. "Noi abbiamo al penitenziario certe stoffe!... Ma so fare le mie distinzioni". E per dimostrarmelo, domandava scusa delle sue male parole e mi accordava licenza di non lavorare, né allora né poi. Mi celassi, giornalmente, nel folto... sarei ricomparso all'ultima partenza della mia squadra. Ed allungò la destra, come per suggellare un patto.

Così, per un buon mese, passai le ore destinate al lavoro, disteso frammezzo ad una vegetazione portentosa, fumando la sigaretta, ascoltando il complicato sussurrìo della foresta. Nei miei occhi era, in quell'ore, un intreccio fantastico di ricami non più veduti... foglie enormi tagliate nel velluto, fusti alti e diritti come minareti; fiori che parevano orgogliosi dei propri colori, asservivano, ai mal celati amori, insetti strani; uccelli dai riflessi metallici, sbucati dall'ombre tutte un fruscio, mi passavano ad ali spiegate, e come folgore, sul capo. Erano antri di un verde cupo, pieni di mistero; eran volte maestose, gocciolanti rugiada ed aromi, dalle quali il Sole traguardava placidamente, signorilmente, quasi vegliando che ogni fil d'erba avesse il fatto suo... Il Sole che adorato, coscientemente o incoscientemente, in tutte le età, da tutti i Popoli, aveva, con secolare energia, inalato a sé stesso quel tempio, degno di un Dio. Oh quanto è bella la Terra quando non è deturpata dalla cupidigia dell'uomo!... Tempio degno anche d'Elisa!... La sua imagine, intensamente rievocata, mi dominava come se ella fosse presente!

Fu in quella foresta ed in quell'ore che io meditai il mio secondo tentativo di fuga. Il piano a cui mi appigliai in modo definitivo, mi venne dall'arte primitiva degli Indiani scorrenti nelle regioni meridionali della Guiana: abbattere un grosso albero, scavarlo in modo da foggiare una piroga capace. Con essa avrei risalito, di notte tempo, l'Ojapok per un tratto di cinquanta miglia ed attraversato poi il San Giorgio, per approdare sulle terre del Brasile. Sfuggito alla caccia delle Autorità francesi, avrei dovuto affrontare, probabilmente, la ferocia dei selvaggi e, indubbiamente gli ostacoli, quasi insuperabili, delle regioni inesplorate e del clima. Ma il premio valeva il rischio!

Tutto questo non si poteva neppure iniziare senza l'aiuto di parecchi complici. Scandagliai l'animo di Antonio Gomez. Ma l'ardente cospiratore politico, soddisfatto della sua posizione di assistente cantiniere e paventando, probabilmente, il peggio, finse di non comprendermi. Bisognava ricorrere a materiale meno nobile. Con quali criteri sceglierlo? Come acquistare su esso l'ascendente necessario a legarlo ai miei intenti?

Anche in questo caso, la mia buona stella, più che la mia saggezza, mi venne in aiuto.

Il penitenziario riceveva le sue provvigioni di carne fresca dal Brasile. A periodi, più o meno fissi, arrivava un veliero con un carico di bestie bovine. La spiaggia, che moriva gradatamente nell'Oceano, non gli concedeva profondità sufficiente ad ormeggiare presso la terra ferma, così che l'ancora veniva gettata al largo, ad una distanza di circa tre miglia. Il guardiano sceglieva allora un galeotto ogni decuria e ne formava una squadra speciale che recavasi penosamente, coll'acqua oltre la cintola, sin presso il veliero. I buoi selvatici, inferociti, venivano gettati a mare e gli uomini li sospingevano, con grandi urli e con grandi sferzate, sin entro uno stecconato, nel penitenziario.

Incominciato quel lavoro, si procedeva, senz'interruzione, sino a scarico compiuto, saltando, all'uopo, anche qualche pasto. Alla Montagne d'Argent, si mangiava per decurie. Il capo riceveva in consegna le vettovaglie, assottigliate dalla sordida speculazione degli ufficiali, e le compariva, su una specie di tavola, nelle dieci gamelle. Ogni galeotto correva alla sua e si divorava, seduti su qualche gradino, o su un tronco d'albero, o sulla nuda terra. Nei giorni di scarico, al capo della decuria correva l'obbligo di custodire la razione del galeotto assente e di consegnargliela al suo ritorno.

Scelto a prestar mano a quel lavoro indiavolato, un giorno feci il mio ritorno definitivo alle otto della sera, quando la mia decuria già russava nelle amache. La fame, inasprita dalla incessante ginnastica, dal lungo bagno, dall'aspra brezza del mare, era diventata feroce. Scuoto il capo della decuria, sollevo un mezzo subbuglio, ed egli mostrandomi, invece della gamella, il pugno: "Tais toi, Italien de m..." (La parola di Cambronne).

Mastico a vuoto e sto zitto. La sera del giorno successivo, mi metto nel rango colla decuria e attendo, d'attorno al tavolo. Quando il capo arriva, sorreggendo la pentola traboccante di minestra, mi lancio e le applico un calcio poderoso. Il capo resta inebetito, a braccia tese, a mani vuote. "Ci vogliono — dico — nove Francesi per far digiunare un Italiano, ma un Italiano solo basta per far digiunare nove Francesi!"

Adesso sono gli altri che sollevano il subbuglio. Il guardiano, certo Cosson, accorre e informatosi della faccenda, mi domanda bruscamente: "Perché, invece di vendicarti, non hai inoltrato il tuo rapporto?"

— Perché gli Italiani non fanno la spia!

Cosson, rubando il mestiere a Salomone, condannò a cinquanta colpi di corda tanto il capo della decuria, quanto il Conte Carlo di Rudio. Ma la mia risolutezza e, sopratutto, la mia fiera risposta, mi avevano improvvisamente inalato nel concetto di quei disgraziati. Essi attorniarono il guardiano e tanto dissero, che egli si prestò a ringoiare la sua sentenza. Da quel giorno, i galeotti accondiscesero, bontà loro! a trattarmi fraternamente come degno della loro congrega; da quel giorno mi riescì impresa relativamente facile lo studiare i vari umori, lo scegliere otto individui tanto amanti della libertà da affrontare allegramente, nel tentativo di riacquistarla, qualsiasi pericolo.

Lasciavamo il nostro capannone di notte, strisciando, come belve che escano dalla tana, per deludere gli occhi eventualmente ancor desti. Si procedeva per diverse vie, sino ad una località prestabilita, sull'orlo della foresta; poi, uniti, ci inoltravamo ne' suoi labirinti. Sin dalla prima notte, avevamo tracciato il nostro cammino con segni pressoché invisibili agli altri mortali, scongiurando, per tal modo, il pericolo di smarrirci.

Un albero di gigantesche proporzioni fu tagliato al piede, con fatica relativamente lieve; ma, cadendo, esso diede sulla fitta e vi rimase appoggiato. Bisognava arrampicarcisi e scavarlo stando aggrappati... complicazione che richiese un tempo prezioso. Finimmo per deciderci a ricorrere al fuoco. La luce non poteva essere scorta dal penitenziario e il fumo era reso invisibile dalla notte; ma fra tanta vegetazione resinosa, fra tanti arbusti morti, fra tante lische, fra tanto cascame che si accumulava da secoli, l'operazione richiedeva grande oculatezza, o si sarebbe alimentato un incendio così rapido e così vasto che difficilmente gli stessi diluvi dei tropici lo avrebbero domato.

Quella nostra notturna intrapresa costituisce, per me, uno di quegli incidenti la cui impressione solo la morte può cancellare. Non si era in quella terra quali pionieri di civiltà e taluno potrebbe anche, ironicamente, osservare che non eravamo farina da missionari. Ma l'ambiente oh la grandezza dell'ambiente, reso ineffabile dalla tacita notte, come penetrava, colla sua, nella nostra essenza e quanto ci trasformava! Attorno a noi, non era cosa che non fosse nata e cresciuta nella più sconfinata libertà e la libertà diventava quasi tangibile in ogni atomo d'aria da noi respirato. A me pareva che i muscoli, affaticandosi, esalassero da ogni loro fibra, qualcosa di luminoso, come se ritornassero alle cose la vita incontaminata che le cose ci prestavano. V'eran momenti in cui avrei giurato d'essere vigoroso ed eterno come la vergine foresta. E diventammo siffattamente parte omogenea dell'ambiente che, dopo le prime notti, branchi innumerevoli di scimmie, attratti dal chiarore della resina, venivano a contemplare e quasi ad incoraggiare, rallegrandola, l'opera nostra.

Dopo un paio di mesi, o poco più, riescimmo a trascinare in una sinuosità del fiume, e a nascondere tra le liane, un canotto largo sette piedi, lungo venticinque. Galleggiava a meraviglia. "Ed ora — esclamai, quando ci separammo pieni di entusiasmo — ai remi ed alla vela!"

Inaspettatamente, un avvenimento, a cui sul primo suo manifestarsi, nessuno prestò seria attenzione, sconvolse l'intera Colonia penale e con essa ogni mia speranza.

Di pieno giorno, con un cielo terso che è tutta una lirica, con un sole capace d'indurire, ai suoi raggi diretti, uova di struzzo... una sentinella vien presa da forti tremiti. Sostituita al più presto, fu vista recarsi a passi lenti, tremando, nella baracca antidiluviana che portava il nome pomposo di "Gabinetto di decenza". Ne uscì terrea, sconvolta. Affermò che, per quanto si sforzasse, non era riescita a scander una sola goccia d'acqua. I galeotti risero sonoramente... la cosa sembrava loro tanto buffa! A sera, il poverino aveva sbocchi di sangue coagulato, nero come il carbone... due giorni dopo era cadavere!

Ma prima ancora che egli avesse dato gli ultimi tratti, cinque, sette, dieci forzati, pur dimostrando al termometro, un'alta temperatura, tremavano come foglie. Il penitenziario era invaso dalla febbre gialla.

La catastrofe fu rapida oltre ogni dire. A quei tempi, si riteneva che la sede del male fosse negli intestini; i rimedi, scarsissimi e affatto inadeguati alla vastità della moria, venivano, per maggiore disdetta, applicati con criteri erronei. Il Comandante diede l'ordine che si aprissero i magazzini e la cantina e si lasciassero a piena disposizione dei sani. Da quella cantina, spalancata e nereggiante, mi pareva uscisse la sensazione della fatalità. Se la febbre gialla non era accovacciata là dentro, essa vi aveva qualche misterioso alleato. I galeotti vi erano inesorabilmente sospinti dal vizio e dalla paura. Vi si imbestialivano e passavano dall'ubriachezza al delirio; dal delirio al nulla!... con morte tanto più fulminea, quanto più erano robusti.

Le disposizioni per le vittime, semplicissime; gettarli, senza cerimonie, nella calce viva.

Ogni sera, il capo guardiano si recava a rapporto nella casa del Comandante.

Una notte morirono al Comandante, quasi contemporaneamente, la moglie e due figli ed egli cessò d'interessarsi della mia salute. Non molto dopo, è il capo guardiano che cessa di recarsi al solito rapporto. Aveva troppo facilmente ceduto alle tentazioni della cantina e, quando fu trovato disteso sulla terra battuta, si sospettò, a tutta prima, che fosse semplicemente ubriaco. Ma aveva le gengive coperte da bava sanguigna... morì senz'aver riacquistato né la parola, né un barlume di coscienza e fu uno dei tanti che dovetti afferrare pei piedi. "Uno, due, tre..." Prima di lanciarlo, avrei voluto dirgli un "Requiescat!"... m'aveva risparmiato cinquanta colpi di corda. Ma laggiù in quel baratro... sarebbe parsa ironia!

Prestai mano a sepellire, ad uno ad uno, in quel modo, i miei otto complici nel tentativo di fuga ed altri, ed altri... Contai sin oltre il centinaio; poi mi confusi e non contai più. Qualche disgraziato, che già pareva avesse passato il confine al di là del quale più non si soffre, gettato appena in quell'orribile sepoltura comune, fu visto rinvenire e contorcersi per lo spasimo!

Quando la febbre, non trovando più carne da rodere, scomparve, i Deputati francesi sollevarono, a Parigi, il solito chiasso umanitario. Ma il Ministro rispose loro, in aria di trionfo, che già aveva dato gli ordini opportuni per trasportare l'intera Colonia all'isola del Saluto. E in realtà una sera, vediamo disegnarsi sulla luce crepuscolare, la nera striscia di fumo di una nave che procedeva, a tutto vapore, verso la nostra spiaggia. Era l'"Abeille" che ci portava gli ordini di Sua Eccellenza. Il giorno seguente l'intera Colonia era a bordo... Di oltre seicento, tra condannati, militari, impiegati civili ed inservienti, eravamo ridotti a sessantatre! La maggior parte dei superstiti aveva già sofferto di febbre gialla in altri tempi e su altri lidi; il che li aveva, pare, resi refrattari, se non assolutamente invulnerabili; parecchi, ombre di sé stessi, mostravano d'esserne stati allora, allora tocchi, se non recisi... tra questi, Antonio Gomez... Oh quanto era trasformata la sua faccia da prete bonaccione! Tre soli non ne avevano riportato, né allora, né prima, il sangue infetto. Fra i tre il forzato di Rudio! Dall'alto dell'"Abeille", a cui già spumeggiava rotto d'attorno, l'Oceano, volgo un ultimo sguardo alla Montagne d'Argent e penso, con profondo rammarico, al mio canotto che galleggiava, solitario ed immobile, sotto le folte liane. Il paesaggio rimpicciolisce rapidamente. L'inferno che gli uomini vi avevano accumulato non era più; restava una delle sue più funeste conseguenze, il vasto carnale a cui eravamo miracolosamente sfuggiti. Natura, indifferente, stendeva su tanto orrore, tinte incantevoli e nell'aria scintillante vibravano i suoi misteriosi amoreggiamenti!

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-FIRST

"FREE... within a year!" I had not said it out of egotism alone, nor out of pity alone, and still less out of boastfulness. My first attempt at escape had been conceived during precisely those days at the Roquette. There I had made the acquaintance of an Englishman who was serving six months' imprisonment for fraudulent bankruptcy. He was a man possessed of a certain culture, of few words; he had, so far as I could infer, means more than sufficient. Like the great majority of his countrymen, he took an interest in politics and hated Napoleon. He came, so to speak, of his own accord to meet the inclinations of my heart and offered to assist me with all the resources at his command. "In church with the saints, in the tavern with the gluttons!" And, moreover, if hypocritical society finds it convenient to take account only of a man's faults in order to depress him, despite his virtues, I find it more logical, more fraternal, to fix one's mind upon his virtues and to exalt him, despite his faults. Had my Englishman truly acquired his riches by reprehensible means? To spend them in order to restore the husband to his widowed consort, the father to his son, was to put ill-gotten gains to good use. I accepted.

We had calculated that he would return among the free before I was conveyed to Toulon. We knew that the condemned, before being transported to the tropical territories, make a stay of greater or lesser duration at that penal establishment. We had no plan of the locality, and to concert a well-defined scheme proved a vague and uncertain business; but it was not unknown that if it is possible to induce a human creature, by means of money, to commit base acts, it is equally possible to induce one, by the same means, to attempt acts of generous daring.

Once out of the Roquette, my Englishman was to make his way to Toulon. It would be easy, after my arrival, to ascertain the place where I was confined and the persons into whose custody I was entrusted. At the same time, he would have organised a means of rapid transport, by sea or by land. Money, lavished with a free hand, would have broken my chains, thus offering me the opportunity to avail myself of that means... Italy and Spain are not, from the French coast, the ultima Thule. The more I studied the character of my Englishman, the more I became convinced that he possessed qualities which rendered him capable of bringing the enterprise to a successful conclusion.

I shall say that the day of his liberation came very soon, and I, embracing him, slipped into his hands, clandestinely, a letter for Elisa... the only one, as I later learned, that she ever received from me.

A week later, I was transported by railway, in a cellular compartment, as far as Marseilles, and from Marseilles, by horse-drawn coach, as far as Toulon. It was the month of April (1858), and nature was reflowering in a manner most wondrous, when I was shut away in a casemate below the level of the sea and chained, together with other wretches, to an iron bar fixed into the damp wall.

One wishes to know what occupation fell to Count Carlo di Rudio during a period of approximately seven months? I wore a red jacket and a pair of yellow trousers; I wore a Phrygian cap of green colour, this being the distinguishing mark of those condemned in perpetuity (those with a term sentence wore it red); I was without moustaches and had my hair cut in strips. I slept, when I could sleep, upon a broad plank. The hours in which I lay wakeful I passed in waging a furious war upon the insects that tormented me, and in chafing the ring that bound me. This last labour of mine was so persistent and so tenacious that, on no fewer than four occasions, I succeeded in having myself conducted to the blacksmith's forge, and thus traversing, holding my chain between my hands, a quarter of a mile in the open air... on no fewer than four occasions thus beholding the Sun again!

As at the Roquette, morbid curiosity exposed me to the humiliating inspection of one personage and another. At that period, the Czarina was endeavouring to restore her ailing health by breathing the balmy air of the Riviera. The officers of a Russian squadron, en route for Nice, where they were to take the pallid sovereign on board, put in at Toulon and wished to see the man who had attempted to kill Napoleon... the man who had inflicted upon their Fatherland the humiliation of Sebastopol. They offered to bestow upon us a sum of money to provide for my most pressing needs. But the Regulations permitted me nothing beyond bread and water, and the jailers opposed it. So that I derived from it nothing but the barren satisfaction of being able to say that even the steppes of eastern Europe had contributed their contingent to the swarm of importunate visitors.

But among my visitors there was one, of an entirely different sort, who merits a particular mention all his own.

Sister Teresa had not rested content with her first letter and my first reply. She had sent me at least a dozen more. They all exhorted me to trust in God, to repent of my past transgressions; to turn, with fervour, towards the outstretched arms of divine mercy, which would assuredly not repel me. Wearied to death, I had written to her, a few days before leaving the Roquette, that I had been educated to place faith in facts, and in facts alone. Words slid inexorably off my soul, and hers were nothing but words! I believed I had put an end to the comedy... but it is not for nothing that one is deemed worthy of an apparition of Christ. Unexpectedly, I saw again, even at Toulon, the fine and elegant handwriting of my sister in God.

It announced to me, no less! the visit of a bishop travelling, as a missionary, to China. I was to open to him my every thought. He was a holy man, replete with celestial grace, and he would not fail to "touch my heart."

One day, at a most unusual hour, the gate of the casemate opened and there entered an old man of about sixty, enveloped in a cloth of violet colour. The jailer pointed me out and departed. I found myself face to face with the announced missionary bishop... who evidently proposed to inaugurate his apostolic triumphs by rendering, through my conversion, a service to the Empire.

He opened the conversation by affirming that he was in that place at the request of Sister Teresa, and added at once that, heeding the exhortations of that pious lady, he had provided, with sufficient liberality, for the earthly needs of my wife and my child.

At this point I scrutinised his face intently and discerned therein nothing but an expression of candid good nature. Yet those saintly episcopal lips had lied like those of a vagabond.

Be that as it may, supposing that he had thus well disposed my spirit with the pretended beneficence, that most pure vessel of celestial grace proceeded to apply the balm he had laid upon my breast, and, in a calm voice, announced to me how Sister Teresa had thought of me as well. He was the bearer of a gift from her. Without further preamble, he drew from beneath his violet robe a coral rosary mounted in silver, from which I saw suspended a crucifix and a medal.

I looked without stretching out my hand, and with a voice no less calm: "A most beautiful gift... if I could pawn it and exchange it for tobacco. But many things are denied in here that are permitted to simple mortals. Thank Sister Teresa on my behalf and write to tell her that I have refused."

The bishop's eyes flashed with the gleam of an Inquisitor. "My son," he said, "you will not refuse at least this other token of brotherly love. It is blessed by the Pope and will bring you good fortune!" And with that he set swinging before my eyes a pair of small scapulars of satin, with the image of Christ embroidered in gold.

— Oh, Father! In this wretched place, with so much filth... what could they serve for, save as a comfortable nest for my lice?

He did not admit defeat, and, without uttering a word, drew out and spread before me an elegant missal. But I observed to him that I had long since ceased to pray, and that in that jumble of words my mind would find no sustenance whatsoever.

The conversation came to an abrupt end. The bishop gathered up his gifts and, turning his back to me, departed without so much as bidding me farewell, with a step that was intended to appear dignified and solemn. I believe he was thinking: "If the Chinese are all like this wretch, I am in for a poor time of it!"

Then, shrugging my shoulders, I looked at my fellow prisoners in the chain. Some regarded me with an air of stupefaction, as though I were the devil; others with sparkling eyes, as if my hair cropped in strips did not suffice to conceal from them the features of a hero. But I was neither a hero nor a devil. I was simply a rational man who, guided by his own deep convictions, was evading the underhand arts of imposture.

I consider it fitting to recount here, by way of analogy, an incident that befell my wife while I was already serving my sentence in Cayenne.

Nottingham, where Elisa had retired to spend, together with her mother, her days of widowhood, was, and certainly still is, an episcopal seat. Catholics regard that bishopric as an advanced stronghold of truth against heresy and, in their fancy, surround it with the serene air that inspires chaste thoughts... a sort of oasis where, by day, doves flutter about; and in the dark of night, luminous angels.

Unexpectedly, Elisa received an invitation to present herself at the episcopal residence on a matter of the greatest importance. Mindful of my counsel, she did not venture to go alone. But here I think the words come better from the lips of my Elisa herself. He pressed a bell, and the lady came at once. He asked her to relate, in simple terms, the conversation she had had with the Bishop of Nottingham.

"There is not a great deal to tell. I went to the episcopal residence with my mother. She was asked to wait in the anteroom; I was immediately conducted into Monsignor's study... a tall, thin man, all skin and bone. He received me with a solemn face and, unfolding a letter which he was careful not to hand to me, told me he was exceedingly grieved to have sorrowful news to impart. A colleague of his, serving at the penitentiary of Cayenne, had written to inform him of my husband's death; which had moreover been confirmed by the French press and the French Authorities. Although the calamity was irreparable and he sympathised with my inevitable grief, it was not without a profound and serene satisfaction of his priestly soul that he could assure me you had sincerely repented of your past transgressions, that you had departed this life acknowledging the sanctity of the religion of your fathers. On this subject you had indeed expressed the ardent desire that I, for the sake of my eternal salvation, should embrace the Catholic faith and enter a convent if at all possible. You further enjoined me, as the expression of your last and absolute will, to entrust our son to the care of some Catholic, apostolic, Roman institution, so that he might be reared in the principles which had sustained you in your misfortune and comforted you upon your deathbed."

"And what did you reply to this excellent ecclesiastic?" "For a moment I found neither tears nor words. Then a flash crossed my mind and, fixing him with my gaze, I said: 'Count Carlo di Rudio cannot have died in such a manner. And since this is a lie, it is equally a lie that he is no more. I feel it; my husband lives!'"

— The priest crumpled the letter, flung it onto the writing-desk, and with a terrible expression — which betrayed the desire to burn me alive — opening and closing his fingers as though they were talons, he said through clenched teeth: "Ah, if we were in France!" I was seized with great terror and, letting out a cry, I fled. I was so shaken that I could say nothing to my mother until we were in the street, far away, very far away!"

Thus — continued the Count di Rudio — ended the attempt of the Catholic clergy to act as informers on behalf of the Second Empire.

While I, amid the horrors of the casemate, toiled at gnawing through the ring of my chain in order to catch a fleeting glimpse of the Sun, my Englishman was working, for his part, to bring our agreement to fruition, with all the astuteness and tenacity so thoroughly Anglo-Saxon. He had gathered every possible piece of intelligence concerning the locality where I was confined and the habits of the prison; he had procured disguises; he had prepared places of temporary refuge; means of transport as far as the Pyrenees. With the aid of some woman or other, he had managed to enter into direct communication with my immediate jailer. It had even been settled that the day of my escape should coincide with my next visit to the blacksmith's forge. Unexpectedly, I was brought before the Commander of the Arsenal.

I was informed that on the following day I would be embarked on the frigate *La Durance*. "You are going to Cayenne," added the Commander in a dry tone, yet not devoid of an underlying affability. "A wretched climate! Europeans, compelled to live in your conditions, cannot survive there more than two or three years. I am authorised to tell you that, if you reveal your accomplices, you will be immediately released and furnished with the means necessary to leave the borders of France. It is within your power to die in the penal establishment, or to live in the bosom of your family. Choose!"

I replied that I knew nothing. But even had I disclosures to make, the opinion in which I held the servants of the Empire was far too low for me to place the slightest faith in their promises. I chose the penal establishment!

— As you please! retorted the Commander roughly, and gave orders that I be conducted back to the casemate.

On the following day I was on the high seas. My first attempt at escape had failed!

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-SECOND

*La Durance* sailed on, bearing in her broad belly, divided into three decks, some three hundred condemned men. Gomez and I were confined in the deepest. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and could not have harboured excessive antipathy toward us, no sooner learned who we were than he had us transferred to the upper deck. He even carried his benevolent partiality so far as to permit me to go up on deck from time to time and smoke freely... the Regulations prescribed the trifling penalty of fifty lashes of the rope for anyone who had done so in the compartments.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz (Canaries) to take on water and remained there two days, with portholes shut, to deprive us of every possibility of escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale du Salut, where some two hundred convicts were disembarked.

Gomez, myself, and about a hundred more wretches passed on board a small steamer, and on the 11th of December, 1858, after a day and a half of additional navigation, we were anchored off the Montagne d'Argent, which rises at the confluence of the Saint-Georges and the Oyapock, at a relatively short distance from the border of Brazil. There had once flourished in that place a free colony, and free hands had there cultivated coffee and every tropical fruit capable of tempting the human palate. The French Government had purchased it. Its officers occupied the planter's house; numerous shelters of rushes, supported by posts, were erected there as cover for the condemned; the ground was bored here and there to bury, in a slow agony, those refractory to discipline; some substantial buildings were also raised for the storehouses, for defence and for offence... the vanguard of civilisation had been transformed into a penal establishment. Yet the beauties of nature marvellously dissembled the hell that men had accumulated there.

The commandant, a Major of the marine infantry, whom the Republic had placed on half-pay and the Emperor reinstated and decorated with honourable insignia, hastened to receive us "in charge" and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it and, looking me up and down: "Ah, so you are that—" (down came a term fit for a sty!) "I am glad to see you here, and I swear to you that here you will leave your skin!"...

That act of vileness was so entirely uncalled for that even the guards made a grimace of disgust. But I did not lose my composure, and, as though flattered by a compliment: "I thank you for the good wish and return it with all my heart!" He clenched his jaw; but he had Gallic ferocity, not the French spirit, and he remained silent.

That small incident convinced me that at the Montagne d'Argent the air blew ill for me and, from that very moment, I resolved to repress every impulse. The relative liberty granted to those who kept good discipline — I reflected — may offer me the opportunity to earn a greater one... the liberty of civilised men. But it was my fate that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the hut assigned to me were about fifty galley-slaves... they formed my squad. I passed the first night sleeping, in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise we set off, our bones aching, towards the forest. I wore a great straw hat, a pair of wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, a brown blouse, upon the back of which was marked, in large characters, my number... the baptism of the penal establishment!

In crossing a swamp, I had the misfortune to lose one of my clogs, and the stumps of the rushes, cut level with the water to facilitate the ford, cut into the sole of my foot. I was obliged to manage three miles of limping, marking every footstep with blood; until I arrived, the last of my squad, in the heart of the forest... a splendid forest that, for a moment, almost made me forget that I was a convict and the pain.

A pile of trunks. The task of the day was to carry them on our shoulders to the penitentiary, making five consecutive journeys. I picked out a trunk that appeared light to the eye; but it was *bois fer* — ironwood — and when I had raised it to the height of my knees, it fell back with a crash upon the pile. Then, instead of making another attempt, I sat down, resolved to rest long enough to recover my strength. The convict guard stood watching me, and when he was certain of my intention: "Get up, you filthy wretch! If you don't stir yourself, I'll send you along with a kick in the seat of your trousers. A curse on you and all the Italian breed!"

Had he contented himself with goading me with a simple threat, I should, in all probability, have taken the matter with full philosophy; but that contempt for my nationality aroused in my breast the fury accumulated against the tyrants who made sport of it. I swung my *sabre d'abattis* and made a leap of which, given my condition, I should not have thought myself capable. "Take care! Italians do not suffer insults! The first one... let it pass! At the second, your skull will see the sun no more from the top of your shoulders!"

My companions, having set off one by one, were already far away. Alone, perfectly alone in a deserted spot! He swallowed, turning pale, the bitter pill. Nevertheless, by a show of bravado, he demanded my name. "Be content with the number!" and I turned my back on him.

A poor beginning! But it so happened that my guard, though he could spell out words after a fashion, could not write. To make his report, he had to have recourse to the clerk of the supervising Engineer; who, with the aid of my number, verified my name and identity in the registers. What feelings animated him toward me I cannot say; but I learned that he persuaded my guard not to be harsh with me. And in his exhortations he deftly insinuated the element of fear: "That man," he said, "fears neither Laws, nor punishments, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later you are done for!"

On his return to the forest, the guard called me aside. "We have at the penitentiary certain rough sorts!... But I know how to make my distinctions." And to prove it, he begged my pardon for his foul words and granted me leave not to work, neither then nor henceforth. I was to conceal myself, each day, in the depths of the forest... I was to reappear only when the last departure of my squad was imminent. And he extended his right hand, as if to seal a compact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours appointed for labour stretched amid a wondrous vegetation, smoking a cigarette, listening to the complex murmuring of the forest. In my eyes during those hours there was a fantastic interweaving of embroideries never before seen... enormous leaves cut from velvet, trunks tall and straight as minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their colours, pressing into the service of their ill-concealed loves strange insects; birds with metallic glints, bursting from shadows all a-rustle, flew past me with wings outspread and swift as lightning above my head. There were recesses of deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults dripping with dew and fragrance, through which the Sun gazed down placidly, majestically, as if watching over every blade of grass to ensure it had its portion... the Sun which, worshipped, consciously or unconsciously, in all ages, by all Peoples, had with its age-old energy drawn upward into itself that temple, worthy of a God. Oh how beautiful is the Earth when it is not defaced by the cupidity of man!... A temple worthy also of Elisa!... Her image, intensely evoked, held dominion over me as though she were present!

It was in that forest and in those hours that I conceived my second attempt at escape. The plan to which I fixed myself definitively came to me from the primitive art of the Indians ranging through the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a great tree, and hollow it out so as to fashion a pirogue of sufficient capacity. With it I would have ascended, by night, the Oyapock for a stretch of fifty miles, and then crossed the Saint-Georges, to land on the territory of Brazil. Having evaded the pursuit of the French Authorities, I would have had to face, in all likelihood, the ferocity of savages, and, beyond doubt, the obstacles, well-nigh insuperable, of unexplored regions and the climate. But the prize was worth the risk!

All of this could not so much as be begun without the assistance of several accomplices. I sounded the spirit of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, satisfied with his position as assistant canteen keeper and in all probability dreading the worst, pretended not to understand me. It was necessary to have recourse to less noble material. By what criteria was it to be chosen? How to acquire over it the ascendancy necessary to bind it to my purposes?

In this case too, my good star, more than my own wisdom, came to my aid.

The penitentiary received its supplies of fresh meat from Brazil. At more or less regular intervals, a sailing vessel arrived with a cargo of cattle. The beach, which shelved gently into the Ocean, afforded insufficient depth for mooring close to the shore, so that the anchor was cast at some distance out, at a distance of about three miles. The guard would then choose one convict from each squad of ten and form them into a special detachment which made its laborious way, with the water above its waist, close up to the vessel. The wild oxen, maddened, were thrown into the sea, and the men drove them, with great shouts and great blows of the whip, as far as a stockaded enclosure within the penitentiary.

Once that work had begun, it proceeded without interruption until the entire cargo was landed, skipping, as need required, even a meal or two. At the Montagne d'Argent, meals were taken by squads of ten. The squad leader received the provisions in his charge, thinned down as they were by the squalid speculation of the officers, and distributed them, on a sort of table, into the ten mess-bowls. Each convict ran to his own and devoured it, seated on some step, or on a tree-trunk, or on the bare ground. On days of disembarkation, the squad leader was obliged to keep the absent convict's ration and hand it over on his return.

Having been chosen to lend a hand at that furious labour, one day I made my return at eight in the evening, when my squad was already snoring in their hammocks. Hunger, sharpened by the incessant exertion, the long immersion, and the sharp sea-breeze, had turned fierce. I shake the squad leader, raise something of a commotion, and he, showing me his fist instead of the mess-bowl: "*Tais-toi, Italien de m...*" (The word of Cambronne).

I chew on nothing and keep silent. The evening of the following day, I take my place in the squad's rank and wait around the table. When the squad leader arrives, bearing a pot brimming over with soup, I lunge forward and deal it a powerful kick. The squad leader stands stupefied, arms outstretched, hands empty. "It takes," I say, "nine Frenchmen to make one Italian go hungry, but one Italian alone suffices to make nine Frenchmen go hungry!"

Now it is the others who raise the commotion. The guard, a certain Cosson, comes running, and having been informed of the matter, demands of me brusquely: "Why, instead of avenging yourself, did you not lodge your report?"

— Because Italians do not inform!

Cosson, borrowing Solomon's trade, sentenced both the squad leader and Count Carlo di Rudio to fifty lashes of the rope. But my resolution and, above all, my proud reply had instantly raised me in the estimation of those wretched men. They surrounded the guard, and said so much, that he consented to swallow his sentence. From that day on, the convicts condescended, in their goodness! to treat me as a brother worthy of their fellowship; from that day on, it proved a comparatively easy undertaking to study the various temperaments, to select eight individuals sufficiently enamoured of liberty to face with a light heart, in the attempt to regain it, whatever danger might arise.

We left our hut by night, creeping, like beasts issuing from their lair, so as to deceive any eyes that might still be awake. We proceeded by different routes as far as a previously appointed spot on the edge of the forest; then, reunited, we advanced into its labyrinths. From the very first night, we had blazed our trail with marks well-nigh invisible to other mortals, thus warding off the danger of losing our way.

A tree of gigantic proportions was felled at its base, with relatively little effort; but in falling, it came down upon the dense undergrowth and remained propped up there. We had to climb upon it and hollow it out while clinging on... a complication that required precious time. We ended by deciding to make use of fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary, and the smoke was rendered invisible by the night; but amid so much resinous vegetation, amid so many dead shrubs, amid so many chips and so much debris accumulated over centuries, the operation demanded great circumspection, or it would have fed a conflagration so rapid and so vast that even the tropical deluges would have had difficulty in mastering it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constitutes for me one of those incidents whose impression only death can efface. One was not in that land as a pioneer of civilisation, and someone might even observe, ironically, that we were not the stuff of missionaries. But the surroundings — oh, the grandeur of the surroundings, rendered ineffable by the silent night — how deeply they penetrated, with their own essence, into ours, and how they transformed us! About us, there was nothing that had not been born and grown in the most boundless freedom, and freedom became almost tangible in every atom of air we breathed. It seemed to me that the muscles, as they toiled, exhaled from their every fibre something luminous, as though they were giving back to things the untainted life that things lent to us. There were moments in which I could have sworn myself as vigorous and as eternal as the virgin forest. And we became so thoroughly a homogeneous part of the surroundings that, after the first nights, countless troops of monkeys, attracted by the glow of the resin, would come to contemplate, and almost to encourage by their enlivening presence, our work.

After a couple of months, or a little more, we succeeded in dragging into a bend of the river, and concealing among the lianas, a canoe seven feet broad and twenty-five feet long. It floated admirably. "And now," I exclaimed, when we parted full of enthusiasm, "to the oars and the sail!"

Unexpectedly, an event to which, in its first manifestation, no one paid serious attention, convulsed the entire penal colony and with it every hope of mine.

In broad daylight, beneath a clear sky that is itself a lyric, in a sun capable of hardening ostrich eggs by its direct rays... a sentry is seized with violent shivering. Replaced as quickly as possible, he was seen making his way at slow steps, trembling, into the antediluvian shelter that bore the pompous name of "Cabinet of decency." He came out of it ashen, distraught. He affirmed that, try as he might, he had been unable to produce a single drop of water. The convicts laughed heartily... the thing seemed to them so comical! By evening, the poor fellow was passing jets of coagulated blood, black as coal... two days later he was a corpse!

But before he had even drawn his last breath, five, seven, ten convicts, while showing a high temperature on the thermometer, were trembling like leaves. The penitentiary had been invaded by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was rapid beyond all telling. In those days, it was held that the seat of the disease lay in the intestines; the remedies, scanty in the extreme and altogether inadequate to the vastness of the mortality, were, to make matters worse, applied according to erroneous principles. The Commandant gave orders that the storehouses and the wine-cellar be opened and left entirely at the disposal of the healthy. From that wine-cellar, flung wide open and dark within, I seemed to feel emanating the very sensation of fatality. If yellow fever was not crouching in there, it had some mysterious ally within. The convicts were inexorably drawn thither by vice and fear. There they brutalised themselves, and passed from drunkenness to delirium; from delirium to oblivion!... with a death all the more sudden, the more robust they were.

The arrangements for the victims, most simple; to cast them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Each evening the head guard went to make his report at the Commandant's house.

One night the Commandant's wife and two children died almost simultaneously, and he ceased to take any interest in my welfare. Not long after, it was the head guard who ceased to make his customary report. He had too readily yielded to the temptations of the wine-cellar, and when he was found stretched out on the beaten earth, it was suspected at first that he was simply drunk. But his gums were covered with bloody froth... he died without having recovered either speech or any glimmer of consciousness, and he was among the many whose feet I was obliged to take hold of. "One, two, three..." Before swinging him in, I would have liked to bid him a "Requiescat!"... he had spared me fifty lashes of the rope. But down there in that pit... it would have seemed like mockery!

I lent a hand to bury, one by one, in that fashion, my eight accomplices in the attempt at escape, and others, and others still... I counted to well over a hundred; then I lost count and counted no more. Some poor wretch, who already seemed to have crossed the threshold beyond which suffering is no more, scarcely thrown into that horrible common grave, was seen to revive and writhe in agony!

When the fever, finding no more flesh to devour, at last disappeared, the French Deputies raised, in Paris, the usual humanitarian clamour. But the Minister replied to them, with an air of triumph, that he had already given the appropriate orders for the transfer of the entire Colony to the Île du Salut. And indeed, one evening we saw taking shape against the twilight glow the black trail of smoke from a vessel proceeding at full steam towards our shore. It was the *Abeille*, bringing us the orders of His Excellency. The following day the entire Colony was on board... Of more than six hundred, comprising condemned men, military, civil employees and servants, we were reduced to sixty-three! The greater part of the survivors had already suffered from yellow fever at other times and in other places; which had rendered them, it appears, resistant if not absolutely invulnerable; many, shadows of their former selves, showed signs of having been touched, or rather smitten, just then, then and there... among these, Antonio Gomez... Oh, how transformed was his face from that of the easy-going priest! Three alone had not contracted the infection of the blood, either then or before. Among the three, the convict di Rudio! From the deck of the *Abeille*, round which the Ocean already foamed and broke, I cast a last glance at the Montagne d'Argent and thought, with profound regret, of my canoe floating solitary and motionless beneath the dense lianas. The landscape shrank rapidly away. The hell that men had accumulated there was no more; there remained one of its most fatal consequences, the vast charnel-pit from which we had miraculously escaped. Nature, indifferent, spread over so much horror enchanting hues, and in the scintillating air there vibrated her mysterious amorous stirrings!

### Chapter Twenty-Second

*La Durance* sailed on, carrying in her wide belly, divided into three decks, about three hundred convicts. Gomez and I were locked in the deepest one. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and must not have harbored excessive antipathy for us, as soon as he learned who we were, had us transferred to the upper deck. Indeed, he pushed his benevolent partiality so far as to permit me to go up on deck from time to time to smoke freely... the Regulations prescribed the trifle of fifty lashes with a rope for anyone who did so in the compartments.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz (Canaries) to take on water and remained there for two days, with windows closed, to remove any possibility of escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale du Salut, where about two hundred convicts were disembarked.

I, Gomez, and another hundred derelicts transferred to a small steamer, and on the 11th of December 1858, after a day and a half of additional sailing, we were anchored before the Montagne d'Argent, which rises at the confluence of the Saint-Georges and the Oyapok, a relatively short distance from the border of Brazil. A free colony once flourished in that place, and free hands cultivated coffee there and every tropical fruit that can tempt the human palate. The French government had acquired it. Its officers occupied the planter's house; numerous thatched roofs, supported by poles, were erected there to shelter the convicts; the earth was pierced here and there, to bury therein, in slow agony, those refractory to discipline; some substantial buildings also arose for warehouses, for defense and for offense... the vanguard of civilization had been transformed into a penitentiary. But the natural beauties wonderfully concealed the hell that men had accumulated there.

The commander, a Major of the marine infantry whom the Republic had retired and the Emperor had reinstated and decorated with honors, hastened to receive us "into his charge" and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it and, eyeing me from head to foot: "Ah, so you are that..." (here followed a word fit for a pigsty!) "I am glad to see you here, and I swear to you that you will leave your skin here!"...

That boorishness was so out of place that even the guards made a grimace of disgust. But I did not lose my composure and, as if flattered by a compliment: "Thank you for the good wish, and I return it with all my heart!" He clenched his jaws; but he had Gallic ferocity, not the French spirit, and he remained silent.

That small incident convinced me that an ill wind was blowing for me at Montagne d'Argent and, from that moment, I resolved to repress my every impulse. The relative liberty that was granted to the disciplined prisoners—I thought—could offer me the opportunity to gain a greater one... the liberty of civilized men. But it was destined that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the hut assigned to me were some fifty convicts... they formed my squad. I spent the first night sleeping in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise we set off, our bones aching, towards the forest. I wore a large straw hat, a pair of large wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, a brown blouse, on the back of which was marked, in large characters, my number... the baptism of the penitentiary!

While crossing a swamp, I had the misfortune of losing one of my clogs, and the stumps of the reeds, cut at water level to facilitate the ford, sliced the sole of my foot. I had to contrive to cover three miles, limping and marking my every footprint with blood, until I arrived, last of my squad, in the heart of the forest... a splendid forest that, for a moment, almost made me forget that I was a convict and in agony.

A pile of logs. The task of the day was to transport them, on our shoulders, to the penitentiary, making five consecutive trips. I spotted a log that, by sight, seemed light; but it was "ironwood" and when I had it at the height of my knees, it fell noisily back onto the pile. Then, instead of retrying the test, I sat down, resolved to rest just long enough to recover my strength. The convict guard stood observing me and when he was certain of my thought: "Get up, you filthy swine! If you don't hurry, I'll kick your arse all the way there. A curse on you and all the Italian seed!"

If he had contented himself with goading me with a simple threat, I would probably have taken the thing with all philosophy; but that contempt for my nationality stirred in my breast the accumulated anger against the bullies who were making a mockery of it. I brandished my *sabre d'abattis* and gave a leap of which, considering my condition, I would not have thought myself capable. "Beware! Italians do not suffer insults! The first... I let pass! At the second, your pumpkin head will no longer see the sun from atop your shoulders!"

My companions, having left one by one, were already far away. We were alone, perfectly alone in a deserted place! He swallowed the pill, turning pale. Nevertheless, to show off his bravery, he asked for my name. "Be content with the number!" and I turned my back on him.

It was a bad start! But as chance would have it, my guard, though he could read more or less correctly, did not know how to write. To make his report, he had to resort to the clerk of the supervising Engineer, who, with the help of the number, verified my name in the registers and who I was. What sentiments animated him with regard to me, I do not know; but I learned that he persuaded my guard not to be cruel. And in his exhortations he deftly insinuated fear: "That man," he said, "fears neither Laws, nor punishments, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later, you are a dead man!"

On his return to the forest, the guard called me aside. "We have certain types at the penitentiary!... But I know how to make my distinctions." And to prove it to me, he asked pardon for his harsh words and granted me leave not to work, neither then nor later. I was to hide myself daily in the thicket... and reappear at the last departure of my squad. And he extended his right hand, as if to seal a pact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours destined for work stretched out amidst a prodigious vegetation, smoking a cigarette, listening to the complicated rustling of the forest. In my eyes, in those hours, there was a fantastic intertwining of patterns never before seen... enormous leaves cut from velvet, trunks as tall and straight as minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their own colors enslaved strange insects to their ill-concealed loves; birds with metallic reflections, emerging from the rustling shadows, passed over my head with wings spread, like lightning. There were grottoes of a deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults, dripping with dew and aromas, from which the Sun gazed placidly, lordly, as if watching that every blade of grass had its due... The Sun which, adored, consciously or unconsciously, in all ages, by all Peoples, had, with secular energy, raised up to itself this temple, worthy of a God. Oh, how beautiful the Earth is when it is not disfigured by the greed of man!... A temple worthy also of Elisa!... Her image, intensely recalled, dominated me as if she were present!

It was in that forest and in those hours that I meditated upon my second escape attempt. The plan I finally settled upon came from the primitive art of the Indians who roamed the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a large tree, to hollow it out so as to fashion a capable pirogue. With it I would ascend, by night, the Oyapok for a distance of fifty miles and then cross the Saint-Georges, to land on the soil of Brazil. Having escaped the pursuit of the French Authorities, I would probably have to face the ferocity of the savages and, undoubtedly, the almost insurmountable obstacles of the unexplored regions and the climate. But the prize was worth the risk!

None of this could even be initiated without the help of several accomplices. I sounded out the soul of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, satisfied with his position as assistant canteen-keeper and probably fearing the worst, pretended not to understand me. It was necessary to resort to less noble material. By what criteria to choose it? How to acquire over it the necessary ascendancy to bind it to my intentions?

In this case too, my lucky star, more than my own wisdom, came to my aid.

The penitentiary received its provisions of fresh meat from Brazil. At more or less fixed periods, a sailing ship would arrive with a cargo of bovine beasts. The beach, which gradually died into the Ocean, did not afford it sufficient depth to moor near the mainland, so the anchor was dropped offshore, at a distance of about three miles. The guard would then choose one convict from each decury and form a special squad that would proceed laboriously, with the water above their waists, up to the vessel. The wild oxen, enraged, were thrown into the sea, and the men pushed them, with great shouts and great lashes, into a stockade in the penitentiary.

Once that work began, it proceeded without interruption until the unloading was complete, even skipping a few meals if necessary. At Montagne d'Argent, we ate by decuries. The chief received the provisions, thinned by the sordid speculation of the officers, and presented them, on a sort of table, in the ten mess-tins. Each convict ran to his own and devoured it, seated on some step, or on a tree trunk, or on the bare earth. On unloading days, the chief of the decury was obliged to guard the ration of the absent convict and to hand it to him upon his return.

Chosen to lend a hand in that devilish work, one day I made my final return at eight in the evening, when my decury was already snoring in their hammocks. Hunger, sharpened by the incessant exercise, the long bath, the harsh sea breeze, had become ferocious. I shake the chief of the decury, I raise a small uproar, and he, showing me his fist instead of the mess-tin: "*Tais toi, Italien de m...*" (Cambronne's word).

I chew on nothing and remain silent. The evening of the following day, I get in line with the decury and wait around the table. When the chief arrives, holding the pot overflowing with soup, I dash forward and give it a mighty kick. The chief remains stupefied, arms outstretched, hands empty. "It takes," I say, "nine Frenchmen to make one Italian fast, but a single Italian is enough to make nine Frenchmen fast!"

Now it is the others who raise an uproar. The guard, one Cosson, runs up and, having inquired about the matter, asks me brusquely: "Why, instead of taking revenge, did you not submit your report?"

"Because Italians do not play the spy!"

Cosson, stealing Solomon's trade, sentenced both the chief of the decury and Count Carlo di Rudio to fifty lashes of the rope. But my resolve and, above all, my proud reply, had suddenly elevated me in the estimation of those wretches. They surrounded the guard and said so much that he agreed to swallow his sentence. From that day, the convicts condescended, of their own goodness! to treat me fraternally as worthy of their brotherhood; from that day it became a relatively easy task for me to study the various humors, to choose eight individuals who loved liberty so much as to cheerfully face any danger in the attempt to regain it.

We would leave our hut at night, crawling like beasts emerging from their lair, to deceive any eyes that might still be awake. We proceeded by different routes to a pre-established location on the edge of the forest; then, united, we would advance into its labyrinths. From the very first night, we had marked our path with signs almost invisible to other mortals, thus warding off the danger of getting lost.

A tree of gigantic proportions was cut at the base with relatively little effort; but, in falling, it landed in the dense growth and remained leaning there. We had to climb it and hollow it out while clinging on... a complication that required precious time. We ended up deciding to resort to fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary and the smoke was rendered invisible by the night; but among so much resinous vegetation, so many dead shrubs, so many splinters, so much detritus accumulated over centuries, the operation required great prudence, or we would have fed a fire so rapid and vast that even the deluges of the tropics would have had difficulty taming it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constitutes, for me, one of those incidents whose impression only death can erase. We were not in that land as pioneers of civilization, and some might even ironically observe that we were not the stuff of missionaries. But the setting—oh, the grandeur of the setting, rendered ineffable by the silent night, how it penetrated, with its own essence, into ours, and how it transformed us! Around us, there was nothing that had not been born and grown in the most boundless liberty, and liberty became almost tangible in every atom of air we breathed. It seemed to me that my muscles, as they toiled, exhaled from their every fiber something luminous, as if they were returning to things the uncontaminated life that things lent us. There were moments when I could have sworn I was as vigorous and eternal as the virgin forest. And we became such a homogeneous part of the environment that, after the first few nights, innumerable troops of monkeys, attracted by the glow of the resin, came to contemplate and almost to encourage our work, cheering it on.

After a couple of months, or a little more, we managed to drag into a bend of the river, and to hide among the lianas, a canoe seven feet wide and twenty-five feet long. It floated wonderfully. "And now," I exclaimed, when we parted full of enthusiasm, "to the oars and the sail!"

Unexpectedly, an event to which at first no one paid serious attention disrupted the entire penal colony and with it all my hopes.

In broad daylight, with a clear sky that is a whole lyric poem, with a sun capable of hardening ostrich eggs with its direct rays... a sentinel is seized by strong tremors. Replaced as quickly as possible, he was seen to walk with slow, trembling steps into the antediluvian shack that bore the pompous name of "Cabinet of Decency." He emerged from it ashen, distraught. He stated that, however much he strained, he had not managed to pass a single drop of water. The convicts laughed loudly... the thing seemed so droll to them! By evening, the poor man had gushes of coagulated blood, black as coal... two days later he was a corpse!

But even before he had drawn his last breaths, five, seven, ten convicts, though showing a high temperature on the thermometer, were trembling like leaves. The penitentiary was invaded by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was swift beyond all telling. In those days, it was believed that the seat of the disease was in the intestines; the remedies, very scarce and wholly inadequate for the vastness of the epidemic, were, for greater misfortune, applied with erroneous criteria. The Commander gave the order to open the warehouses and the cellar and leave them at the full disposal of the healthy. From that cellar, wide-open and black, there seemed to me to issue the sensation of fatality. If the yellow fever was not crouched in there, it had some mysterious ally within. The convicts were inexorably driven there by vice and by fear. They bestialized themselves there and passed from drunkenness to delirium; from delirium to nothingness!... with a death all the more swift, the more robust they were.

The arrangements for the victims were very simple: to throw them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Every evening, the head guard went to report to the Commander's house.

One night, the Commander's wife and two children died almost simultaneously, and he ceased to take an interest in my health. Not long after, it was the head guard who ceased to go to the usual report. He had too easily yielded to the temptations of the cellar, and when he was found stretched out on the beaten earth, it was suspected at first that he was simply drunk. But his gums were covered with bloody foam... he died without having regained either speech or a glimmer of consciousness, and he was one of the many I had to grasp by the feet. "One, two, three..." Before casting him in, I would have liked to say a "Requiescat!"... he had spared me fifty lashes of the rope. But down there in that abyss... it would have seemed an irony!

I lent a hand to bury, one by one, in that manner, my eight accomplices in the escape attempt, and others, and others... I counted to over a hundred; then I became confused and counted no more. Some wretch, who already seemed to have passed the boundary beyond which one no longer suffers, as soon as he was thrown into that horrible common grave, was seen to revive and writhe in agony!
