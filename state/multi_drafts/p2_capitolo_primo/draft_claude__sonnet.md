AS an introduction to this second part, a page essentially of love has its proper place. What I here relate I had, in part, from the Count di Rudio himself, in part from his wife. Nor did I lack the aid of certain intimate letters, yellowed with time, yet still exhaling, fresh as ever, the perfume of the past.

The little politics that peeps in here breathes the soul of Giuseppe Mazzini... but what is Di Rudio, at bottom, if not a reflection of that great conspirator? Setting aside all differences of genius, the only substantial difference between the two, as it seems to me, is this: the one held that without faith in God neither a spirit of sacrifice nor an enduring honesty is possible; the other knew how to sacrifice himself and to live honestly, even setting aside that formidable hypothesis.

In London, di Rudio made for the inn of the host Tressoldi, already of our acquaintance, at whose establishment Fumagalli had preceded him. Tressoldi introduced him to the choirmaster of the Drury Lane, a Milanese who engaged him as a chorister with the Italian Opera company. Thus, as in other times he had drawn his sustenance from flowers, di Rudio now drew it from music... that music, it is understood, sung by others; for in the main he had to content himself with opening his mouth wide and waving his arms. The important thing was to pocket five shillings every evening, and at this he succeeded marvellously well, as any seasoned chorister might.

It should be added that he was, in effect, paid to listen to Madame Glassier and the tenor Armandi; while to be sent into raptures by the high notes of this Bolognese cobbler, the wealthy paid relatively fabulous sums.

When the season ended, the Opera Company returned, one by one, to the fair skies of Italy; but di Rudio remained beneath the leaden skies of London. He persisted in reading the newspaper notices and at length found work at the Wapping Docks, a place situated at the eastern extremity of the metropolis. The work consisted of handling rushes and fashioning wrappers for bottles, using for the purpose a new-model machine invented by a Frenchman.

The excessive distance of the workshop compelled him to relinquish the cordial hospitality of the good Tressoldi. Necessity's bread had, moreover, already thinned out that fine company. To follow its manifold threads, to trace worthily the stubborn, exhausting struggle sustained by our exiles to keep themselves afloat, would require a separate volume.

We should encounter feet peering between sole and upper, testifying to the long and fruitless search for employment; waists compressed by the belt, revealing the enormous disproportion between appetite and the means of satisfying it. I should have to tell of nocturnal hunts across rooftops for innocent cats destined for the pot; to describe the cramped lodgings, partitioned among half a dozen human creatures who, in their sleep, instinctively demanded their share of the blanket — that blanket put together, at times, with a mosaic-maker's art, by pasting together newspapers gathered from the benches of public gardens.

The drama would not fail to sadden us with its icy breath... virile constitutions corroded by want; sensitive natures ruined beyond hope of recovery; poems vanished silently amid the clamour of a thieving and self-indulgent world; the death-rattles of the consumptive; corpses cut to pieces on the cold marble of the mortuary slab... heroisms unwitnessed, unknown; yet no less worthy of history than those which unfolded in the light of great occasions.

Against this backdrop, sketched now in tearful, now in comic, now in sublime strokes, rises the pensive figure of Giuseppe Mazzini who, to feed the generating flame of divine conceptions, of genius and virtue, knocks at the door of the usurer and pledges... his own boots.

Nor should we lack for hearty laughter flung, like a challenge, at misery... dreams of glory frequent; sentimental loves penetrating to the very heart through threadbare elbows; long fasts forgotten in the reading of Byron... and likewise those occupations dripping with grease that take no account of poverty. Angelo Fumagalli, to cite one example, made an excellent living manufacturing sausages.

Count di Rudio had found lodgings in Baldwin's Garden, in the house of one Mancherini, a maker of plaster figures from Lucca. Fumagalli and a certain Leoni were the only exiles with whom, in those days, he maintained relations of close friendship.

He had made Leoni's acquaintance at Tressoldi's inn, and they had become intimate through a characteristic incident which I am pleased to recall.

Austria and the petty tyrants of Italy, who had received so many vexations from the exiles, devised a new means of defence. They granted full liberty of flight to the birds of prey chained in their native gaols, on the condition that these should pass themselves off as political refugees and insinuate themselves among the honest company.

The reasoning was that a change of sky would not change their habits, and that the general public, tarring the good with the blame provoked by the wicked, would cease to commiserate with them and to lend moral support to their noble aspirations.

A veritable flock of these gentlemen descended upon Tressoldi's inn. They were led by a Milanese named Bonacina, a swaggering inciter who, backed by his associates and emboldened by the host's weak character, soon transformed that agreeable place into a dark den of ruffians.

Tressoldi's comic despair and the suspicion he had sniffed out regarding the special mission of these troublemakers induced di Rudio to resort to an extreme measure. He found willing spirits in Leoni, a sort of athlete, and in several other strapping young men; then, having extracted from Tressoldi a promise of the strictest neutrality, he waited for the propitious moment.

One evening Bonacina and four or five figures of the same stamp were demanding to drink at others' expense and threatening to turn everything topsy-turvy if they were not served at the double. Di Rudio stepped forward and ordered them to clear the room... and never to set foot in it again.

"This beardless boy smells of good health!" replied Bonacina, making as if to deal him a backhanded blow. It was the signal for a fiendish brawl... Fumagalli had his head broken; but the ruffians, overpowered and battered, ended by retreating, leaving their captain to extricate himself as best he could. Alone, with one eye shut and the other scarcely open, his mouth bloody, Bonacina backed his way to the window and, swearing that he would have his revenge, leapt out and vanished into the darkness of the street.

Not a surly face, from that evening onward, at Tressoldi's sign!... but the affair had, for di Rudio, an unexpected sequel. One foggy night he was returning from the Opera, his head full of trills and high notes, when, turning a corner into a solitary street, he found himself face to face with Bonacina, fully cloaked, fist raised, and in his fist a large knife.

"Ha, ha!" said the scoundrel mockingly. "Now we shall see whether your liver is sound!" But instead of striking the blow, he drew back... he had seen the gleam of a pistol barrel.

Then di Rudio was seized by a singular impulse: to add, to the mute eloquence of the weapon, that of a little sermon. He reminded Bonacina of the heroism and greatness of his fellow Milanese, so much at odds with his own conduct. And probing that heart, seeking to revive beneath the ashes the gentle sentiments of childhood, he asked him, among other things, whether he did not believe that his mother was ashamed to have brought into the world and nursed and reared a son who was assuredly betraying her finest hopes; who mocked the sufferings of his fellow citizens; who spat upon her grey hair; who repudiated his Fatherland.

Bonacina, hard and contemptuous at first, ended by absorbing, head bowed, his dose of medicine. Then, with a sudden movement, he flung down the knife and fled headlong, sobbing like a child. The love of country had known how to strike a few sparks even in that vulgar and degenerate soul!

Count di Rudio, from his lodgings in Baldwin's Garden, went every evening to exchange a few words with Fumagalli and Leoni. After their customary long walk, they would pool what small coins they had and allow themselves a bottle... of concentrated sunbeams. So they called the product of the Lombard vineyards; the only means within their reach of warming themselves at the sun of Italy.

The first to show himself but rarely at the accustomed gathering was di Rudio.

With regard to these absences of his, Leoni asked him one evening, with an expression that spoke volumes, whether he was lodging in a house of young ladies. "Without a doubt!" replied the Count. "In the Mancherini household there are two. One is called Sara and is the daughter of the family; the other is named Elisa and is their niece. Her parents live in some corner of the County of Surrey and have sent her to London, if what I was told is correct, to train herself in the art of artificial flowers."

Leoni pressed to know more, and Count di Rudio, who had learned at the Drury Lane the language of music, replied:

"Miss Sara has a spirit tuned in the minor key, subdued and gentle; Miss Elisa has, on the contrary, vibrations in the major, impetuous, conquering."

And Leoni, looking Fumagalli fixedly in the eye and winking at him: "Let us recite the *de profundis*. The Moretto is ill... and we shall soon lose him!"

The following evening, and the one after, and several more besides, di Rudio was nowhere to be seen. His two friends grew uneasy and decided to call at Baldwin's Garden to ascertain the matter. They found the door ajar. To ring the bell, or boldly to go in without asking leave? While they stood in doubt, they heard a sweet, low voice say: "In my garden there grows a pear..."

A male voice, that of di Rudio, interrupted: "I have already told you that the names of trees are masculine, the names of fruits feminine. Excepting *dattero*, *limone*, *ananassa*..."

"Excepting *dattero*, *limone*, *ananassa*, and *fico*!" observed another woman's voice, more vibrant than the first.

Leoni drew Fumagalli away. Then he said: "I foresaw it. Our friend is done for!"

Signora Mancherini, a Booth by birth, a distant relation of the founder of the Salvation Army, was of pure English blood; but her husband's occupation had dusted, not to say plastered, her with art of a more or less classical kind. She spoke with evident ostentation of Venuses and Dianas. And with the gods of Olympus she had overcrowded every piece of furniture, every ledge, every projection of her little apartment. She was also accustomed to arrange her hair in a fashion that recalled times long since vanished.

Her daughter Sara was a beautiful flower of eighteen. In Count di Rudio's judgment, she took a little from her father and a little from her mother... a blend of English and Italian. But the Nordic disposition, active and assertive, had, by what seemed an apparent contradiction of nature, ended by remaining somehow atrophied, while the gentle and submissive temper of the Italian woman, developing and almost bursting forth, stamped itself most markedly upon her character. She did not seek to shine, she did not imperiously exact love; she preferred to love without being loved in return to being loved without returning it. Not yet having reached the age at which feeling, fully developed, knows what the tempests of passion are, she contented herself with the tepid breath of friendship and lavished upon her cousin Elisa the tender devotion that, in later years, she was to reserve for her husband; in her cousin she took pleasure, suffered in her reverses, was exalted by her joys. Happy in her own half-shadow, she cared for nothing else, provided that Elisa might triumph.

This latter, as if purposely, was endowed with a temperament that tended to accentuate that disposition. Of the English people it is generally said that they have sound teeth, and that when they succeed in seizing their prey they hold it fast. Beneath the fair hair of Miss Elisa, beneath the rosy skin, behind those blue eyes which even today awaken a sense of sweetness, there lay nestled a tenacity of purpose, a courage altogether out of the common. These qualities, finding occasion to manifest themselves, appeared as a paradox and astonished the superficial observer; but not one who, accustomed to analysis and deduction, had noted the significance of that chin drawn with breadth, and the energy of her gesture, however rare and sparing in every English young lady.

Count di Rudio told me that, as a girl, she did not lack a certain pliability of character and of intelligence; but she would never have been capable, even had she wished it, of carrying it so far as self-negation. To love strong souls, to open to them, like a corolla to the sun's kisses, the most hidden gradations of one's own spirit; but in order to be stronger, greater together. To conclude a treaty of alliance — yes; with complete devotion, and to remain faithful to it to the last; to sign even an honourable surrender, never!

Count di Rudio had resolved to teach Italian to the two young ladies at the solicitation of Signora Mancherini. Why refuse? To study our language becomes fatally synonymous with loving our Fatherland and our friends; and our friends are always a little the enemies of our enemies. The sympathy of the English was greatly coveted by the Italians, and even the arts of peace may serve to win a battle. Besides, every small pebble has its value in great enterprises!

So it came about that Count di Rudio, every evening, after having laboured prosaically among rushes, busied himself in instilling the sweetest and the strongest of modern tongues into the pretty heads of two young ladies born and bred on the foggy banks of the Thames; and perhaps the gods of Olympus, from the height of their ledges, smiled.

Accustomed to the rough frankness of military life, Count di Rudio proved a rather stern teacher, and the lesson hour assumed, so to speak, the rigidity of a geometrical problem. But no sooner had that hour passed than Signora Mancherini set upon the table her steaming *tea service*, and the atmosphere changed as if by enchantment... books and exercise-books disappeared, and animated conversation flowed uninterrupted, punctuated and, as it were, glossed, by sips of the warm beverage. Signora Mancherini, who was stout, occupied with her whole self the large armchair. Sara sat, by a habit congenial to her tendencies, upon a small footstool, resting her forearm on Miss Elisa's lap. Count di Rudio, lean, nervous, dark, amid so much triumph of fair and rosy colouring, like a grain of pepper, sat at first tranquil and composed; then, little by little, he became all gestures and starts, and his most mobile features flashed and flickered.

Before long, at his hands, the titanic figures who had struggled for our *risorgimento* came to be embroidered upon the fibres of those souls, all the more distinctly in proportion as the ground that received them was sensitive and untouched by strong emotions. And they were transformed by it: they felt, they suffered, they hoped, they willed — in the Italian manner.

What had to happen, happened. The heroes were dead or distant; Count di Rudio was near, young, sympathetic. He too had suffered for the sacred cause. He ended by growing vast in the eyes of the two young ladies, who confused about his head the lustre that was his and the lustre that belonged to others.

Angelo Fumagalli had a singular expression for characterising that situation: "Venice," he would say, "had her Moor; Belluno, which is smaller, has only her *Moretto*. But the Moor was loved by a single Desdemona; that rascal di Rudio has two. Perhaps — and why not? — without a shadow of malice, mind you! — perhaps he has three!"

The only one who appeared not to notice was Count di Rudio himself. Love knows no tropics; hearts blaze at the Pole as furiously as at the Equator. It is the manner of expressing feeling that varies according to latitude. The Italian vehemence is unknown to the English girl, who perhaps instinctively senses how ill it would sound in that setting of subdued tones. Free and mistress of herself, she adorns herself, when she loves, with a tranquil dignity... the characteristic of the free. Hence her unjust reputation for coldness. That charm glided over the spirit of the Count, occupied and almost saturated with an altogether different passion.

It is true that in the long hours spent at the workshop, while his practised hands executed mechanically their accustomed task, the mind of di Rudio ran back to the two young ladies. Often the fancy absorbed him so completely that the noise of the tools and the musty smell of the rushes seemed, as it were, suppressed. In such moments — so he himself affirmed to me — a mysterious perfume of youth and health would penetrate his lungs and bestow upon him a subtle intoxication. At times he had caught himself longing for the evening... But was it not in the evenings that, the scales of vulgarity scraped away, he conversed with great men of great things? The explanation seemed to him more than sufficient, and he took no care to probe the matter more deeply.

Unexpectedly, his employer, a Frenchman, retired from business. New proprietor, new methods; foreign labour was dismissed, and Count di Rudio found himself once more without immediate resources, all but destitute. For a while he got on with the small savings he had put by; then he found himself stripped bare. The day he turned out his pockets and pockets within pockets in vain, he reflected, not without dismay, that he already owed a month's rent to Signora Mancherini. For several evenings past he had appeared at the lesson pale and listless. Miss Elisa asked him whether he was unwell. "Not precisely," he replied, "but I must say something that weighs upon me. I have most pressing obligations. Tomorrow evening I shall be engaged elsewhere; nor do I know when we shall be able to resume our lessons!"

The festival of pink and blue was over... there began the *via crucis* of the hero reduced to the level of a pariah, to whom society denies salt and fire. He went out early in the morning and, returning at a very late hour, contrived to slip along the corridor and avoid unpleasant encounters; he had come to call them that! He was of a temper such that he would have preferred to face the mouth of a cannon to the face of a creditor.

Unfortunately one cannot leave hunger on one's own doorstep, nor fling it into the middle of the street like some inconvenient burden. He flung instead, to the usurer, his few superfluous possessions, one by one. He had left his old friends behind, and to seek them out again now, when he stood in extreme need, seemed to him to invite a rebuff deliberately. He would have contented himself with any work whatsoever; but these were times of crisis, and the unemployed formed an army. In the newspapers the "Help Wanted" columns were conspicuous by their absence; the "Situations Wanted" were measured by the yard. He went about soliciting here and there; but to complete his misfortune he had a gentlemanly air. The more human among them contented themselves with answering him with some pleasantry. Others, having barely glanced at him, would grunt the laconic "Go on!" which to Italian ears seems saturated with so much contempt. He walked as long as his legs would carry him; then he had recourse to the resource of those without resources... sitting on a bench in some park and waiting for the late night... the most painful of rests!

Relief came to him unexpectedly, one night by mysterious means, when he had not tasted a morsel for eight-and-forty hours. He had returned home with his customary precautions. He did not light the lamp, and before undressing, by force of habit, turned back the bedclothes. Something heavy rolled across the bed. Presently he was unwrapping a parcel bound with great care, and a delicious fragrance was tickling his nostrils. Before him, at his full and free disposal, lay a marvel of well-cooked viands. And he bit into them with the force of a dog, without reflecting, without thinking. Only when he had wiped his mouth did he, wondering that he had waited so long, ask himself: "Whence has all this abundance rained down upon me? Who in the world has taken care of me?"

His first suspicion fell upon Signora Mancherini and her two gentle creatures, whom she sheltered, after the manner of Providence, beneath her large wings. But he reflected, not without logic, that the Mancherini would have laid the table in the proper fashion, and that a parcel tied with care must have come from a distance. None of his old friends knew of his critical circumstances. What mystery was this, then?

To be brief: he had already stretched himself under the bedclothes and the outer world was already fading, and even the consciousness of his own being had grown dim; yet a small part of his brain was still working mechanically... Silence, the boundless and tenebrous void. Now and again there flashed through it, unresolved, the two questions: "Whence this abundance? Who is taking care of me?"

And since life and the universe do not exist, in practical terms, save in so far as they are reflected in our own minds, we may infer to what minimal proportions they may be reduced inside the skull — be it even the skull of a hero!
