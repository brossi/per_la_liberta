AS an introduction to this second part, a page essentially of love has its due place. What I am about to narrate, I received, in part, from Count di Rudio himself, in part, from his lady. Nor did I lack the aid of a few intimate letters, yellowed by time, but still exhaling, fresh, the perfume of the past.

That little bit of politics that peeps through bears the soul of Giuseppe Mazzini... but what is Di Rudio, in the end, if not a reflection of that great conspirator? The difference in their intellects aside, the only substantial difference between the two, it seems to me, is this: the one held that, without faith in God, neither a spirit of sacrifice nor lasting honesty is possible; the other was able to sacrifice himself and live honestly, even while discarding that powerful hypothesis.

In London, di Rudio made his way to the innkeeper Tressoldi, already known to us, at whose establishment Fumagalli had preceded him. Tressoldi introduced him to the chorus master of Drury Lane, a Milanese who hired him as a chorister in the company of the Italian Opera. Thus, as in other times from flowers, di Rudio drew his sustenance from music... the music sung by others, that is; for, on the whole, he had to content himself with opening his mouth wide and waving his arms. The important thing was to pocket five shillings every evening, and at this he succeeded marvellously, as well as a seasoned chorister.

Let it be added that he got paid to hear Madame Glassier and the tenor Armandi; while, to go into raptures at the high notes of this Bolognese shoemaker, the wealthy paid relatively fabulous sums.

When the season ended, the Opera Company returned, one by one, under the beautiful sky of Italy; but di Rudio remained under the leaden sky of London. He persisted in reading the newspaper advertisements and ended up finding work at the Whapping Docks, a locality situated at the eastern extremity of the metropolis. It was a matter of handling reeds and fashioning wrappers for bottles, using, for the purpose, a new model of machine, invented by a Frenchman.

The excessive distance of the workshop forced him to renounce the cordial hospitality of the good Tressoldi. The need for bread had, for that matter, already thinned out the fine company. To follow its many threads, to worthily sketch the obstinate, exhausting struggle sustained by our exiles to keep themselves afloat, would require a special volume.

We would encounter feet peeking through between sole and upper, attesting to the long, useless hunt for employment; bellies compressed by the belt, revealing the enormous disproportion between appetite and the means to satisfy it. I would have to tell of nocturnal hunts over the rooftops and the meowing of innocent cats destined for the pot; describe the cramped lodgings, shared by half a dozen human creatures, instinctively demanding, in their sleep, their share of the blanket... the blanket sometimes put together, with the art of a mosaicist, pasting together newspapers collected from the benches in the public gardens.

The drama would not fail to sadden us with its icy breath... virile fibers corroded by starvation; sensitive natures ruined without hope of recovery; poems vanished silently, amidst the din of the thieving and pleasure-seeking world; death-rattles of Elysium; cadavers cut to pieces on the cold marble of the mortuary room... heroics without witnesses, unknown; but just as worthy of history as those that unfolded in the light of great occasions.

In the background, with brushstrokes now tearful, now comical, now sublime, stands out the pensive figure of Giuseppe Mazzini who, to feed the generative flame of divine concepts, of genius and of virtue, knocks on the usurer's door and pawns... his own boots.

Nor would we suffer a lack of hearty laughter hurled, as a challenge, at misery... frequent were the dreams of glory; the sentimental loves that penetrated to the heart through tattered elbows; the long fasts forgotten in the reading of Byron... and also the occupations dripping with grease, which know no misery. Angelo Fumagalli, to cite an example, lived excellently by making sausages.

Count di Rudio had found lodging at Baldwin's Garden, in the house of a certain Mancherini, a maker of plaster figurines from Lucca. Fumagalli and a certain Leoni were the only exiles with whom he maintained, in those days, relations of close friendship.

He had met Leoni at Tressoldi's inn, and they had become intimate because of a characteristic incident that I am pleased to recall.

Austria and the petty tyrants of Italy, who had so much trouble from the exiles, devised a new means of defense. They granted the birds of prey chained in the fatherland's galleys full freedom of flight, on the condition that they pass themselves off as political refugees and insinuate themselves among the honest company.

It was argued that a change of sky would not change their habits and that the populace, involving the good in the blame provoked by the wicked, would cease to commiserate with them and to morally support their noble aspirations.

A veritable flock of those masters descended upon Tressoldi's inn. They were captained by a certain Bonacina from Milan, a swaggering instigator who, backed by his men and emboldened by the innkeeper's flabby character, soon transformed the pleasant atmosphere into a gloomy den of hooligans.

Tressoldi's comical desperation and having sniffed out the special mission of the troublemakers, induced di Rudio to resort to an extreme expedient. He found willing spirits in Leoni, a sort of athlete, and in other strapping young men; then, having wrung from Tressoldi the promise of the strictest neutrality, he awaited the propitious occasion.

One evening Bonacina and four or five other figures of the same stamp were demanding to drink for free and threatened to turn everything upside down if they were not served post-haste. Di Rudio stepped forward and ordered them to clear the room... and never set foot in it again.

"This beardless boy must be tired of living!" replied Bonacina, making a motion to give him a backhanded slap. It was the signal for a devilish brawl... Fumagalli had his head broken in it; but the hooligans, overpowered and battered, ended up beating a retreat, leaving their leader to fend for himself as best he could. Alone, with one eye closed and the other barely open, his mouth bloodied, Bonacina retreated to the window and, swearing he would be avenged, he took the leap and vanished into the darkness of the street.

No more tough mugs at Tressoldi's from that evening on!... but the affair had, for di Rudio, an unexpected sequel. One foggy night, he was returning from the Opera, his head full of trills and high notes when, turning the corner into a solitary street, he found himself face to face with Bonacina, all wrapped in a cloak, with his fist raised and in his fist a great ugly knife.

"Ah, ah!" said the scoundrel mockingly. "Now we'll see if you have any courage!" But instead of striking the blow, he drew back... he had seen the glint of a pistol barrel.

Then di Rudio was seized by a singular whim: to add, to the mute eloquence of the weapon, that of a little sermon. He reminded Bonacina of the heroism and greatness of his fellow Milanese, so in contrast with his own conduct. And probing that heart, to revive from beneath the ashes the gentle sentiments of childhood, he asked him, among other things, if he did not think his mother would be ashamed to have brought into the world and nourished and raised a son who was surely betraying her finest hopes; who derided the torments of his fellow citizens; who spat upon her white hair; who denied his Fatherland.

Bonacina, hard and scornful at first, ended by absorbing, with bowed head, his medicine. Then, throwing down the knife with a sudden motion, he fled precipitously, sobbing like a child. The love of the Fatherland had managed to arouse a few gleams even in that vulgar and debased soul!

Count di Rudio, from his lodging at Baldwin's Garden, went every evening to have a chat with Fumagalli and Leoni. After the usual long walk, they would pool some small change and allow themselves a bottle... of concentrated sunbeams. Thus they called the product of the Lombard vineyards; the only means within their reach to warm themselves in the Italian sun.

The first to show himself rarely at the usual meeting-place was di Rudio.

Regarding these absences of his, Leoni asked him one evening, with an expression that revealed the hidden meaning, if he was lodging in a house of young ladies. "Without a doubt!" replied the Count. "In the Mancherini house there are two. One is called Sara and is the daughter of the landlords; the other is named Elisa and is their niece. Her parents live in some little corner of the County of Surrey and have sent her to London, if what I was told is true, so that she might train in the art of artificial flowers."

Leoni insisted on knowing more and Count di Rudio, who had learned the musical jargon at Drury Lane, replied:

"Miss Sara has a soul tuned in a minor key, subdued and caressing; Miss Elisa, instead, has vibrations in a major key, impetuous, conquering."

And Leoni, looking fixedly at Fumagalli and winking at him: "Let us recite the *de profundis*. The 'Moretto' is sick and... we shall lose him soon!"

The following evening, and the next, and for several more, di Rudio did not let himself be seen. His two friends grew worried about it and decided to go to Baldwin's Garden to ascertain the matter. They found the door ajar. Should they ring the bell, or boldly step forward without asking leave? While they were in doubt, they heard a sweet, soft voice that said: "*Nel mio giardino cresce una pera...*" (In my garden grows a pear...)

A masculine voice, that of di Rudio, interrupted: "I have already told you that the name of the tree is masculine, the name of the fruit feminine. Except for *dattero*, *limone*, *ananassa*..."

"—Except for *dattero*, *limone*, *ananassa* and *fico*!" observed another woman's voice, more vibrant than the first.

Leoni dragged Fumagalli away. Then he said: "I foresaw it. Our friend is done for!"

Mrs. Mancherini, a Booth, a distant relative of the founder of the Salvation Army, was a pure-blooded Englishwoman; but her husband's occupation had given her a dusting, not to say a plastering, of more or less classical art. She spoke with evident ostentation of Venuses and Dianas. And with Gods of Olympus she had overloaded every piece of furniture, every shelf, every ledge of her little apartment. She was also wont to do her hair in a way that recalled long-vanished times.

Her daughter Sara was a beautiful flower of eighteen years. According to Count di Rudio's judgment, she had a little of her father and a little of her mother... a mixture of English and Italian. But the Nordic character, active and encroaching, by an apparent contradiction of nature, had ended up remaining as if atrophied, while the sweet and submissive nature of the Italian woman, developing and almost bursting forth, stamped her soul in a very pronounced way. She did not seek to shine, she did not imperiously demand love; she preferred to love without being loved in return, than to be loved without returning it. Not having reached the age in which feeling, completely developed, knows what the tempests of passion are, she kept herself content with the tepid breath of friendship and lavished upon her cousin Elisa the tender devotion that, later, she was to keep for her husband; in her cousin she took pleasure, she suffered at her vexations, she was exalted by her joys. Happy in her half-light, she cared for nothing else, so long as Elisa triumphed.

The latter, then, as if on purpose, was endowed with a temperament that tended to accentuate that disposition of hers. Of the English people it is generally said that they have strong teeth, and that when they manage to seize their prey, they never let it go. Under Miss Elisa's blonde hair, under her rosy skin, behind the blue eyes that arouse, even today, a sense of sweetness, there lay nested a tenacity of purpose, a courage anything but common. Which, finding occasion to manifest themselves, appeared as a contradiction and astonished the superficial observer; but not one who, accustomed to analysis and deduction, had noted the meaning of that her broadly drawn chin and the energy of her gesture, though so rare and spare in any English young lady.

Count di Rudio told me that, as a young girl, she did not lack a certain flexibility of character and intellect; but she could never, even if she wished, have pushed it to the point of self-negation. To love strong souls, to unfold for them, like a corolla to the kisses of the sun, the most recondite gradations of her spirit; but so as to be stronger, greater, as two. To conclude a treaty of alliance... that, yes; with all transport, and to remain faithful to it to the very end; to sign a surrender, even an honorable one, never!

Count di Rudio had decided to teach Italian to the two young ladies at the solicitation of Mamma Mancherini. Why refuse? To study our language inevitably becomes synonymous with loving our Fatherland, and our friends are always a little bit the enemies of our enemies. The sympathy of the English was much yearned for by the Italians, and even the arts of peace can serve to win a battle. Besides, every little stone has its value in great undertakings!

Thus it happened that Count di Rudio, every evening, after having worked prosaically with reeds, applied himself to instilling the sweetest and the strongest of modern languages into the charming heads of two young ladies born and raised on the foggy banks of the Thames, and perhaps the Gods of Olympus, from their high shelves, were smiling.

Accustomed to rude military frankness, Count di Rudio proved to be a rather gruff teacher, and the lesson hour assumed, so to speak, the rigidity of a geometric problem. But as soon as that hour had passed, Mrs. Mancherini would place her steaming 'tea service' upon the table and the atmosphere would change as if by magic... books and notebooks would disappear and the animated conversation would unfold uninterrupted, punctuated and almost glossed by sips of the warm beverage. Mrs. Mancherini, who was plump, filled the large armchair with her person. Sara sat, by a habit in keeping with her tendencies, on a small stool and rested her forearm on Miss Elisa's lap. Count di Rudio, thin, nervous, dark, amidst such a triumph of blonde and rose, like a peppercorn, at first sat quiet and composed; then, little by little, became all gestures and starts, and his most mobile features would flash.

In short, through his work, the titanic figures who fought for our risorgimento were embroidered upon the fibrils of those souls, all the more clearly as the fabric that received them was sensitive and virgin of strong emotions. And they were transformed by it: they felt, suffered, hoped, and willed in the Italian way.

What was bound to happen, happened. The heroes were dead or far away; Count di Rudio was near, young, and likable. He too had suffered for the holy cause. He ended up looming large in the eyes of the two young ladies, who confounded, around that head, his prestige with that of others.

Angelo Fumagalli had a singular expression to sketch that situation: "Venice," he would say, "had the Moor; Belluno, which is smaller, has only the 'Moretto'. But the Moor was loved by a single Desdemona; that rogue di Rudio has two. Perhaps... and why not?... without a shadow of malice, let us be clear!... perhaps he has three!"

The only one who seemed not to notice was Count di Rudio. Love knows no tropics; hearts burn at the Pole as furiously as at the Equator. It is the way of expressing affections that varies according to latitude. Italian impetuousness is unknown to the English young lady, who perhaps instinctively feels how out of place it would sound in that environment of subdued hues. Free and mistress of herself, she adorns herself, when she loves, with a reposed dignity... the characteristic of the free. Hence her unjust reputation for coldness. That charm slid over the Count's soul, occupied and almost saturated with a wholly different passion.

It is true that in the long hours spent at the workshop, while his practiced hands automatically performed their usual work, di Rudio's mind ran to the two young ladies. Often his fantasy absorbed him so much that the noise of the tools and the faint smell of the reeds were as if suppressed. In those moments—so he affirmed to me—a mysterious perfume of youth and health penetrated his lungs, giving him a subtle intoxication. Sometimes he had surprised himself yearning for the evening... But was it not in the evening that, having scraped away the scales of vulgarity, he conversed with his great ones, of great things? The explanation seemed more than sufficient to him, and he did not trouble himself to inquire more deeply.

Unexpectedly, his principal, a Frenchman, retired from the business. New owner, new systems; the foreign labor was dismissed, and Count di Rudio found himself, once again, without immediate resources, almost on the street. He got by for a while with the small change he had saved, then he found himself reduced to nothing. The day he turned out his pockets and fob pockets in vain, he thought, not without dismay, that he already owed a month's rent to Mrs. Mancherini. For several evenings, at the lesson, he had appeared pale and listless. Miss Elisa asked him if he felt ill. "Not precisely," he replied, "but I must say something that weighs on me. I have very serious commitments. Tomorrow evening I will be occupied elsewhere; nor do I know when we will be able to resume our lessons!"

The festival of rose and azure was over... began the *via crucis* of the hero reduced to the level of the pariah, to whom society denies salt and fire. He would leave early in the morning and, returning home at a very late hour, he was able to slip through the corridor, avoiding unpleasant encounters; he had been reduced to calling them that! He was of such a temper that he would have preferred to face the mouth of a cannon than the face of a creditor.

Unfortunately, one cannot leave hunger on one's own doorstep, nor throw it into the middle of the street like a troublesome burden. Instead he threw his few superfluous objects to the usurer, one by one. He had left his old friends, and to approach them again now, when he had extreme need of them, seemed to him like deliberately provoking a rebuff. He would have been content with any work; but these were times of crisis, and the unemployed formed an army. In the newspapers the 'Help wanted' columns were conspicuous by their absence; the 'Situation wanted' ones were measured in yards. He went about soliciting here and there; but to complete his misfortune, he had a gentlemanly appearance. The more humane contented themselves with replying with some witticism. Others, barely glancing at him, would grunt the laconic "Go on!" which to the Italian ear seems saturated with so much contempt. He walked until his legs could carry him no longer; then he would take up the recourse of the resourceless... to sit on a little bench in some park and wait for the dead of night... a most painful rest!

Relief came to him unexpectedly, one night by mysterious ways, when he had not chewed a mouthful for forty-eight hours. He had returned home with his usual precautions. He did not light the lamp and, before undressing, from force of habit, he turned down the bedcovers. Something heavy rolled onto the bed. A moment later he was unwrapping a package tied with great care, and a delicious fragrance tickled his nostrils. He had before him, at his full disposal, a marvel of roasted victuals. And he bit into it with canine force, without reflecting, without thinking. Only when he had wiped his mouth did he, marvelling that he had waited so long, ask himself: "From whence has all this abundance rained down on me? Who on earth has taken care of me?"

His first suspicion fell upon Mrs. Mancherini and her two gentle creatures whom she, in the manner of Providence, protected beneath her great wings. But he thought, not without logic, that Mrs. Mancherini would have set a proper table, and that a carefully tied package must have come from far away. None of his old friends knew of his critical circumstances. So then, what mystery was this?

In short, he had already stretched out under the covers, and the outer world was already fading, and the very consciousness of his own being had grown dark; but a tiny part of his brain still worked automatically... Silence, the boundless and tenebrous void. Now and then, the two questions flashed within it, unresolved: "From whence this abundance? Who is taking care of me?"
