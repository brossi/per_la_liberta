I would not know how to repeat, in perfect chronological order and as if I were reading them from a ship's log, the multiple vicissitudes of our arduous ordeal. I will speak briefly of those which most properly determined its course.

The tempests, invoked by Cousin, came, and so fiercely as to place in jeopardy far greater vessels than our own. It was not only the sea we had to fight; but the sea and the sky conspired against us, hammering us with a pertinacity that almost revealed a purpose.

To comprehend our peril in all its intensity, one would need to be well-versed in the art of navigation. For one who is, any description is superfluous; for others, no description suffices. For two days and three nights, we found ourselves in worse conditions than the wretched convicts condemned to labour in those subterranean dungeons where water penetrates in cleverly measured spurts. Either man the pump without interruption, or drown.

It was then that I understood in its full import the phrase: "And the floodgates of heaven were opened." Whoever did not have every fibre, every atom of his being clinging to the oars that balanced us, worked feverishly to bail out the boat. For this purpose our palmetto hats served us; but the storm had left several of us bare-headed... these men resorted to the only tool left within their reach: their clumsy wooden shoes. In such a predicament, no resource, however minimal, was to be overlooked.

The dense rain, the waves that made it impossible to see any great distance, and above all, the profound darkness, held for us, on the third night, another grave, unexpected peril. Blacker than the night, and almost a phantom rising menacingly from the abyss, we found ourselves facing a large sailing ship. Cousin barely had time to wrench the rudder; but the backwash was so vehement that, for an instant, we believed ourselves overwhelmed and done for. The fearsome apparition passed beside us, gigantic and swift; then it disappeared, as if it had evaporated into the shadows.

Not even in this circumstance did Cousin lose his composure and, after noting that it was, in all probability, a Dutch ship, he added: "Courage, lads! In this darkness, I wager we can dart between the legs of Death without letting him catch us!"

I thought that we owed our salvation to the fever that had confined Morelli to the hospital... had the little craft been but slightly heavier, there was no human strength sufficient to keep it afloat. After that encounter, my companions saw a phantom on the crest of every great wave.

The following day, the storm vanished. Hoisting the sail anew, we could at last think of restoring our exhausted strength. During the long hours of peril, we had swallowed, with difficulty, a few sips of rum, and our vigour had been, more or less, infused by the danger itself. Now that the tempest around us had abated, hunger arose tempestuously. But a terrible disillusionment awaited us: the salt water had partly swept away and partly ruined our provisions, rendering them almost unserviceable.

The horrors of cannibalism then flashed grimly through our imagination, and into the air burst imprecations that would put to shame those attributed to the damned in hell. Cousin deemed it more than opportune, indeed urgent, to change course and turned the prow southwest, our natural direction.

That same day we noticed a long streak of dense smoke in the distance, a few spans above the water's surface... the *Abeille*, perhaps, hurrying westward on the manhunt. We lowered the sail again and, for a long hour, remained in great anxiety, lying flat at the bottom of the dinghy. At twilight, the ill-omened streak disappeared below the horizon. Two days later we were coasting along the Continent.

At what point? Our anxious gazes scanned a hospitable land, or did it hold for us dangers greater than those we had overcome with so much toil?

For a long stretch, we saw no trace of human life; then suddenly, having rounded a point, a small, tranquil bay appeared, spread out before us, and on the beach, a small hut, and around the hut, several men, some labouring to move baskets and barrels; some to spread out nets. Others, seated on the sand, were mending the meshes.

We put our strength to the oars and made land. As we were pulling the boat ashore, a dozen negroes came around us, with attitudes not openly hostile, but of people harbouring some suspicion. Among them was a white man, dressed in the European style, armed with a rifle.

I understood at once that I was among slaves assigned to catch and dry fish, working under the lash of the white man; of the civilized man, of the believer in Christ.

I asked him if, by paying, it was possible to obtain some provisions. He knew some French and understood me. But, for all reply: "Where do you have the pennies?"

I jingled my small coins and, pressing my first question, I added: "What land is this?"

— Dutch Guiana! The land that had driven Pianori's comrades back to certain death. My men no longer felt hunger, no longer felt fatigue. They ran to the dinghy and jumped into it, ready to face any adversity again... such was the power, even in them, of the love of liberty!

We set sail again towards the west: the wind that blew was saturated with the perfumes of the Continent. We thought that the *Abeille* had already passed that way and was leaving us further and further behind with every stroke of its piston. Cousin calculated, moreover, that English Guiana was not far off, and our dinghy sped along so swiftly that to believe the end of our tortures was near seemed more than reasonable.

Suddenly, the boat slowed, its prow dipped, and it moved no more... as if a mysterious hand, having seized it by the keel, had held it back with great force. I instinctively looked at Cousin. He was pale, cadaverous. To my mute question, he replied: "We are lost!" A simple and terrible thing had happened... we were caught in a *vase*. But we were not men to submit to fate without first fighting with all our strength. And Cousin himself, after that first dismay, having recovered all his composure, gave us the order to jump into the water and arrange ourselves around the dinghy, to support it with our shoulders. "If the tide goes out," he said, "commend your souls to God... if you believe you have a soul and if you believe in God. If the tide rises... perhaps... Come, all hope would not be lost!"

For a couple of hours we remained in the tenacious mud; up to our knees at first; then, little by little, up to our waists, the water at the same level. The mosquitoes, fortunately, did not form a swarm; but the crabs did not delay in tormenting us with their pincers. These were moments of impotent rage that bordered on delirium; moments of gloomy phantoms dispelled with difficulty by my strong philosophy. To be eaten alive, in small morsels, or to devour one by one the thousand devouring monsters... What became, in the unequal struggle, of the pride of man? The more the minutes passed, the more the invisible enemy closed in on us. In many and grave perils I found myself exposed both before and after; but it was reserved for the dagger of one of my compatriots and for the filthy crustaceans of Guiana to pierce my flesh so as to leave indelible scars.

It was our good fortune that the tide rose. The boat we had been supporting ended up becoming our support. We climbed into it, muddied, bleeding, exhausted!...
