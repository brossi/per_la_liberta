NON saprei ripetere, con perfetto ordine cronologico e come se le leggessi su un registro di bordo, le molteplici peripezie della nostra ardua prova. Dirò in breve di quelle che ne determinarono più propriamente il corso.

Le tempeste, invocate dal Cousin, vennero e così gagliarde da mettere a repentaglio ben altre imbarcazioni che non fosse quella nostra. Non era solo il mare che dovevamo combattere; ma il mare ed il cielo congiurati; i quali ci martellavano con una pertinacia che quasi rivelava un intendimento.

Per comprendere in tutta la sua intensità il nostro pericolo, bisognerebbe essere rotti nell'arte del navigare. Per chi lo è riesce superflua qualsiasi descrizione; per gli altri non v'ha descrizione che basti. Per due giorni e per tre notti, ci trovammo in peggiori condizioni degli infelici condannati a lavorare in quelle segrete sotterranee ove penetra l'acqua a zampilli sapientemente misurati. O manovrare la pompa senz'interruzione, od affogare.

Fu allora che compresi in tutta la sua portata la frase: "E si persero le cataratte del cielo." Chi non teneva ogni sua fibra, ogni atomo di sé stesso aggrappato ai remi che ci equilibravano, lavorava febbrilmente a vuotare la barca. Ci serviva all'uopo il nostro cappello di palmetto; ma la tempesta aveva lasciato parecchi di noi a testa scoperta... ricorrevano costoro all'unico arnese rimasto a loro portata: le scarpacce di legno. In simile frangente, nessuna risorsa, per minima che fosse, era trascurabile.

La pioggia fitta, le ondate che rendevano impossibile la lunga visuale e, sopratutto, l'oscurità profonda, ci riserbavano, per la terza notte, un altro grave, inaspettato pericolo. Più nero della notte, e quasi fantasma uscito minacciosamente dall'abisso, ci trovammo di fronte un grosso veliere. Cousin ebbe appena il tempo di dare una stratta al timone; ma il rigurgito fu tanto veemente che, per un istante, ci credemmo travolti e spacciati. La paurosa apparizione ci passò d'accanto, gigantesca e veloce; poi scomparve, come se fosse svaporata nelle tenebre.

Neppure in questa circostanza, il Cousin perdette il suo sangue freddo e, dopo aver notato che, con tutta probabilità, trattavasi di una nave olandese, soggiunse: "Coraggio, ragazzi! Con questo buio, mi impegno a guizzare tra le gambe della Morte senza lasciarci afferrare!"

Io pensai che dovevamo la nostra salvezza alla febbre che aveva confinato il Morelli all'ospedale... per poco che la navicella fosse stata più pesa, non v'era forza umana bastante a sorreggerla. Dopo quell'incontro, i miei compagni scorgevano un fantasma sulla cresta d'ogni cavallone.

Il giorno seguente, la tempesta svanì. Issata di bel nuovo la vela, potemmo finalmente pensare a ristorare le forze esauste. Nelle lunghe ore del pericolo, avevamo ingoiato, a stento, qualche sorso di rhum e la vigoria ci era stata, o tanto o quanto, infusa dal pericolo stesso. Sedata ormai intorno la burrasca, sorse burrascosa la fame. Ma una terribile disillusione ci aspettava: l'acqua salsa aveva in parte spazzato via, ed in parte guaste le nostre provvigioni così da renderle pressochè inservibili.

Gli orrori del cannibalismo balenarono allora tristemente nella nostra fantasia e nell'aria scoppiarono imprecazioni da degradare quelle che si attribuiscono ai dannati dell'inferno. Cousin stimò più che opportuno, urgente il cambiar rotta e volse la prua a sudovest, la nostra direzione naturale.

Quel giorno stesso notammo una lunga striscia di fumo denso, in lontananza, a poche spanne sovra il pelo dell'acqua... l'Abeille, forse, che si affrettava verso l'ovest, alla caccia dell'uomo. Ricalammo la vela e, per lunga ora, si rimase, in grande ansietà, appiattati in fondo al canotto. Al crepuscolo, la mala striscia scomparve sotto l'orizzonte. Due giorni dopo costeggiavamo il Continente.

A qual punto? Scorrevano i nostri sguardi ansiosi su una terra ospitale, o ci riserbava essa pericoli maggiori di quelli superati con tanta nostra fatica?

Per lungo tratto, non scorgemmo traccia alcuna di vita umana; poi d'improvviso, girata una punta, ci apparve, spianato d'innanzi, un piccolo seno tranquillo e, sulla spiaggia, una casupola e, attorno alla casupola, parecchi uomini quali affaticantisi a rimuovere cesti e barili; quali a distendere reti. Altri, seduti sull'arena, rammendavano maglie.

Si die forza ai remi ed approdammo. Mentre si tirava la barca in secco, una dozzina di negri ci venne d'attorno, con atteggiamenti non apertamente ostili, ma di gente che covi qualche sospetto. Tra essi era un bianco, vestito all'europea, armato di fucile.

Compresi subito d'essere fra schiavi addetti a pigliar pesci e ad essiccarli, lavorando sotto la sferza dell'uomo bianco; dell'uomo incivilito, del credente nel Cristo.

Gli domandai se, pagando, era possibile ottenere qualche provvigione. Egli sapeva di francese e mi comprese. Ma, per tutta risposta: "Dove hai i quattrini?"

Feci tintinnare i miei spiccioli e incalzando la prima domanda soggiunsi: "Che terra è questa?"

— La Guiana olandese! La terra che aveva risospinto a certa morte i compari del Pianori. I miei non sentirono più la fame, non sentirono più la stanchezza. Corsero al canotto e vi saltarono, pronti a riaffrontare qualunque traversia... tanto poteva, anche in loro, l'amore della libertà!

Rinavigammo verso l'ovest: il vento che spirava era saturo dei profumi del Continente. Noi pensavamo che l'Abeille fosse già passata per là e ci lasciasse sempre più indietro ad ogni suo colpo di stantuffo. Cousin calcolava inoltre che la Guiana inglese non fosse lontana e il nostro canotto scorreva tanto celeramente che il ritenere prossima la fine delle nostre torture appariva più che ragionevole.

Ad un tratto, la barca rallentò, abbassò la prua e più non si mosse... come se una mano misteriosa, afferratala per la chiglia, l'avesse prepotentemente trattenuta. Guardai istintivamente il Cousin. Egli era pallido, cadaverico. Alla mia muta interrogazione, rispose: "Siamo perduti!" Era successa una cosa semplice e terribile... eravamo impigliati in una "vase". Ma non si era uomini da assoggettarci al destino senza prima lottare con tutte le nostre forze. E lo stesso Cousin, dopo quel primo sbigottimento, ritrovato tutto il suo sangue freddo, ci impartì l'ordine di saltare nell'acqua e di disporci attorno al canotto, per sostenerlo colle nostre spalle. "Se la marea cala — disse — raccomandate l'anima a Dio... se credete d'avere un'anima e se credete in Dio. Se la marea monta... forse... Via, ogni speranza non sarebbe perduta!"

Per un paio d'ore rimanemmo nel fango tenace; sino alle ginocchia dapprima; poi, a mano a mano, sino alla cintola, l'acqua allo stesso livello. Le zanzare, fortunatamente, non formavano sciame; ma i granchi non tardarono a tormentarci colle loro tenaglie. Furon momenti di rabbia impotente che confinava col delirio; momenti di lugubri fantasmi dissipati a stento dalla mia forte filosofia. Esser mangiati vivi, a piccoli bocconi, o divorare ad uno ad uno i mille mostri divoratori... Che diventava, nella lotta impari, la superbia dell'uomo? Più i minuti passavano e più l'invisibile nemico ci serrava dappresso. In molti e gravi pericoli mi trovai esposto e prima e poi; ma era riserbato al pugnale di un mio compatriota ed ai luridi crostacei della Guiana il forarmi le carni così da lasciarvi cicatrici indelebili.

Fu nostra ventura che la marea montasse. La barca che avevamo sostenuta, finì per diventare il nostro sostegno. Vi entrammo infangati, sanguinanti, sfiniti!...

CHAPTER TWENTY-SIX

I COULD not recount, in any perfect chronological order and as though reading from a ship's log, the manifold vicissitudes of our arduous trial. I shall speak briefly of those which determined its course most properly.

The storms, invoked by Cousin, came, and came with such fury as to imperil vessels far stouter than our own. It was not the sea alone we had to fight; but sea and sky in league together; which battered us with a persistence that almost betrayed some deliberate intent.

To comprehend our peril in its full intensity, one would need to be broken to the art of navigation. For those who are, any description is superfluous; for all others, no description suffices. For two days and three nights, we found ourselves in worse conditions than the wretched souls condemned to labour in those subterranean dungeons where water seeps in jets of calculated measure. Either work the pump without ceasing, or drown.

It was then that I understood, in its fullest import, the phrase: "And the floodgates of heaven were opened." Whoever was not keeping every fibre, every atom of himself grappled to the oars that kept us balanced, was working feverishly to bail out the boat. Our palmetto hats served us for that purpose; but the tempest had left several among us bareheaded — these had recourse to the only implement still within their reach: their wooden clogs. In such a crisis, no resource, however trifling, was to be despised.

The driving rain, the waves that made any long view impossible, and above all the profound darkness, held in store for us, on the third night, yet another grave and unexpected peril. Blacker than the night itself, and like a phantom risen menacingly from the abyss, a great sailing vessel loomed before us. Cousin had barely time to wrench the tiller; but the surge was so violent that, for an instant, we believed ourselves swallowed up and done for. The fearful apparition swept past us, gigantic and swift; then vanished, as though dissolved into the darkness.

Not even in this extremity did Cousin lose his composure, and having remarked that, in all probability, it was a Dutch vessel, he added: "Courage, lads! In this blackness, I'll undertake to slip between the legs of Death without letting her take hold of us!"

I reflected that we owed our salvation to the fever that had confined Morelli to the hospital — had the little vessel been even slightly heavier, no human force would have been sufficient to keep her afloat. After that encounter, my companions saw a phantom on the crest of every wave.

The following day, the storm subsided. Hoisting the sail once more, we were able at last to think of restoring our exhausted strength. Through the long hours of peril we had swallowed, with difficulty, a few mouthfuls of rum, and what vigour we had was furnished, in some measure, by the danger itself. Now that the tempest had settled around us, a tempestuous hunger arose. But a terrible disillusionment awaited us: the salt water had in part swept away, and in part spoiled, our provisions, rendering them well-nigh useless.

The horrors of cannibalism then cast their grim shadow across our imaginations, and into the air burst imprecations fit to shame those attributed to the damned of hell. Cousin judged it more than opportune — urgent — to change course, and turned the bow to the south-west, our natural heading.

That very day we observed a long streak of dense smoke in the distance, a few spans above the surface of the water — the *Abeille*, perhaps, hastening westward in pursuit of man. We lowered the sail and, for a long hour, remained in great anxiety, pressed flat in the bottom of the canoe. At dusk, the ominous streak disappeared below the horizon. Two days later we were coasting the Continent.

At what point? Did our anxious eyes scan a hospitable shore, or did it hold in reserve for us dangers greater than those we had surmounted with such toil?

For a great distance we could discern no trace of human life; then all of a sudden, rounding a headland, there appeared spread before us a small, tranquil cove, and on the beach a little hut, and around the hut several men — some labouring to shift baskets and barrels, some spreading nets. Others, seated on the sand, were mending meshes.

We bent to the oars and came ashore. As we hauled the boat up the beach, a dozen Negroes gathered about us, their bearing not openly hostile, yet that of men who harbour some suspicion. Among them was a white man, dressed in the European fashion, armed with a musket.

I understood at once that I was among slaves employed to catch and dry fish, toiling under the lash of a white man — of a civilised man, a believer in Christ.

I asked him whether, for payment, it was possible to obtain some provisions. He had some French and understood me. But his only reply was: "Where's your money?"

I made my few coins jingle and, pressing my first question, added: "What land is this?"

— Dutch Guiana! The very land that had driven back to certain death the comrades of Pianori. My men felt their hunger no more, felt their weariness no more. They ran to the canoe and leapt into it, ready to face whatever adversity lay ahead — such was the power, even in them, of the love of liberty!

We sailed on towards the west: the wind that blew was laden with the fragrances of the Continent. We reckoned that the *Abeille* had already passed that way and was leaving us ever further behind with every stroke of her piston. Cousin calculated moreover that British Guiana was not far distant, and our canoe was moving with such speed that it seemed more than reasonable to regard the end of our torments as close at hand.

All at once, the boat slowed, dipped her bow, and moved no more — as though a mysterious hand, having seized her by the keel, had forcibly held her back. I glanced instinctively at Cousin. He was pale, cadaverous. To my mute enquiry he answered: "We are lost!" A thing simple and terrible had occurred — we had become fast in a *vase* [mudbank]. But we were not men to submit to fate without first fighting with every force at our disposal. And Cousin himself, after that first moment of dismay, having recovered all his composure, gave us the order to leap into the water and dispose ourselves around the canoe to support it upon our shoulders. "If the tide ebbs," said he, "commend your souls to God — if you believe you have souls, and if you believe in God. If the tide rises — perhaps — Come now, all hope would not be lost!"

For a couple of hours we remained in that tenacious mud; sunk to the knee at first, then little by little to the waist, with the water at the same level. The mosquitoes, fortunately, did not swarm; but the crabs were not long in tormenting us with their pincers. There were moments of impotent rage bordering on delirium; moments of grim phantasms barely dispelled by my robust philosophy. To be eaten alive, in small morsels, or to devour one by one a thousand devouring monsters — what became, in that unequal contest, of the pride of man? The more the minutes passed, the more closely the invisible enemy pressed about us. In many and grave perils I found myself exposed, both before and after; but it was reserved for the dagger of one of my own countrymen and for the loathsome crustaceans of Guiana to pierce my flesh so deeply as to leave indelible scars.

It was our good fortune that the tide rose. The boat we had been sustaining ended by becoming our sustenance. We climbed back into her — begrimed with mud, bleeding, spent!

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-FIFTH

It was precisely in those days that the frigate *Amazzone* made land at the island of the damned and blessed, bringing with her a wave of news from the old Europe.

Men of retrograde ideas and men of the new order had continued their fierce struggle for the conquest of the future, and great events had pressed upon one another and fixed themselves in History — though Orsini and Pieri and I and so many, so many others had been wholly eliminated from the political stage. What a fine lesson in humility! Magenta had cut its name there in letters glittering and blood-red. And I was saddened by it. Why, why — what did I discern in all this? The King of Piedmont had followed the tactics of his predecessors: to avail himself of the French to beat the Austrians; reserving the option, at a later hour, of allying himself with the Austrians so as to put a brake upon the French — and in either case, what results? A paltry aggrandisement of his Piedmont; a trifling increase in revenue for the old dynasty — that is what I discerned there! Napoleon would never have consented to have, upon the borders of the Empire, a strong Italy capable of withdrawing herself from his influence, from his predominance; Napoleon, who exalted the Church and was made in turn, in the hands of the Church, an instrument of reaction and oppression, would never have said to Italy: "Go, advance triumphantly upon the high road and reclaim thy Rome!"

Vittorio Emanuele II had insinuated, between the Italian People and its Unity, that dissolvent element which would put an end — so I thought — to the heroic times that regenerate character, and usher in those of vulgar opportunism, of craven tergiversations. He had insinuated — I felt it in my deepest presentiment — that profound scepticism which would come to invade the Latin soul, so prone to exaggerated despondency; the Latin soul that cannot shine without enthusiasm, as blood neither blazes nor turns crimson without oxygen. The glorious sign, foretold by so many elect intelligences, the longing and the torment of so many virile consciences, seemed to me fatally receded. In the best of hypotheses, between the present and that sign lay a treacherous waste, wherein liberty could take root only stunted and debased by the ineluctable demands of a Monarchy. Never, as much as in that hour, and with the garb of the outcast clinging to my flesh, did I lament that I had not known how to strike home.

An officer of the *Amazzone*, struck by my melancholy, enquired of me its reason with a kindly air.

"If Italy," I answered him, "in order to rise, however little, to liberty, had absolute need to have recourse to foreign arms, I should have preferred to see descend from the Alps men whose wrists were not, as ours, heavy with chains. Magenta will doubtless mark a memorable date in the annals of Italy; whether equally auspicious I do not know. I know that it would be auspicious if Napoleon had been beaten and destroyed there! Oh, what a provident disaster that would have been! The two Peoples would have extended their hands to one another without hidden meanings, without ulterior aims; the one, after having declared the usurper stripped of his throne; the other, after having freed itself — by the third overthrow of the Monarchy — from the equivocation, from the nightmare that possesses it and well nigh unsettles its very nature. Who could have resisted their impetus? They would have done far more than beat the Austrians and sweep away the petty tyrants who encumber the Peninsula; they would have practically abolished the Alps!"

"A generous dream!" said the officer, bowing his head. Then he leaned close and murmured in my ear words that made me start: "On board, we carry a Decree of amnesty — and it is for the political prisoners!"

An hour of suspense, and we were drawn up in line before the barracks. The Captain of the frigate delivered four sentences, alluding to the new glory with which the French arms had "covered themselves," and following these with the obligatory encomiums upon the lofty wisdom and valour of the august Sovereign. Having extolled his clemency to the skies, he proceeded to the reading of the Decree of amnesty. The Director of the penitentiary pronounced one by one, with a heartrending deliberateness, the names of those upon whom this unexpected dew descended. That of Galli, that of Rossini, that of Tebaldi, my own — were not pronounced. The Decree concerned French subjects only.

Several officers gathered about us and, expressing their regret, suggested that a petition from us, supported by the Director of the penitentiary, would in all probability be favourably received by the Emperor, who would thus remedy what was beyond all doubt an involuntary omission. Why not draw it up at once? The *Amazzone* herself would carry it to France.

Galli, Rossini, and Tebaldi consented and, then and there, informed me that they placed greater faith in their petition than in the escape attempt, which they were naturally renouncing. And this was not, from my own entirely personal point of view, the least grievous of the sorry consequences of the Franco-Piedmontese alliance and the victory of Magenta.

To remedy that defection was a delicate matter, and not one for me, who did not know sufficiently well the other inmates of the house of sorrow. I entrusted the task to the Cousin, the former officer of Louis-Philippe. He set about it with such circumspection that it required a full two months. On the first of December he informed me that all was ready. On the morning of the ninth, I and Morbioli stood at the quay watching the departure of the *Abeille*, bound for Cayenne. Her white wake was still gleaming when I turned to the proud *Trasteverino*:

"Morbioli, today we play the last card resolutely. Warn our companions, with due caution. Who exactly the latest additions are I cannot say with precision; but I know they are not of the same mettle as those we have lost. On this point I must make you a recommendation I consider important."

And I urged him to leave the archpriest aside. True, he had contributed to the expenses; but the cargo he carried in his Bible — no longer so very secretly by now — might have been capable of arousing, in others if not in ourselves, perverse instincts. To abandon him to his fate was, in all probability, to save his skin. Besides, we must not run the risk of dishonouring, by dishonest actions, an enterprise which our very enemies would have been careful, in their hearts, never to censure.

Morbioli rubbed his hands and observed that the archpriest remained a caged bird, precisely on account of that little hoard upon which he had so greatly counted to take wing. "It seems the holy unction has not infused a scrap of prophetic spirit into him." And he added: "The idea suits me well; for, to tell you the truth, that priest, in so hazardous an attempt, struck me as an ill omen. It was as though we had secured him expressly to sing us the *De profundis!*"

I gave him a few further instructions and we parted. At five o'clock, leaving the Workshop, I saw him again, busy about the gunboats. An hour later, while the multitude of the wretched sat in the refectory before their pewter plates, I stood alone in a secluded spot, upright upon the sand, with my feet in the shallows, waiting anxiously; in that low place, hemmed in between the rocks, no one could see me save from the sea.

And from the sea the oarsmen saw me. The desire for their supper lent vigour to their muscles and the little boat darted lightly over the waves, as though barely grazing them. I gave a shout and raised my fist high in the air, displaying, pinched between thumb and forefinger, a five-franc piece.

They cursed; but, induced by the Cousin, who was in command of the little crew, they changed course, and in an instant the keel scraped and stopped. "Quick — quick — what pay is there in it!"

For all answer, a whistle — and from the rocks five surly faces burst forth, such as might have struck awe into a pack of savages. Before the fishermen recovered from their surprise, they found them at their sides, blades raised. But the threat was an almost superfluous precaution. Questioned one by one, the oarsmen had for some time already made clear their ardent desire to flee. They feared their comrades, and not one had dared to utter an explicit yes. The very kernel of my enterprise consisted, therefore, in binding into a single sheaf, by bold initiative, all those pale individual assents; in transforming them into one strong, collective, and dynamic will.

When I had drawn from their hiding-place the provisions and the mast and the sail, the waverers looked one another in the face and smiled. They contented themselves with saying: "Let it be understood that we yield to force!"

It falls out precisely thus in great political and social affairs as well. How many truths are already firmly rooted in the heart of man, how many aspirations already germinating there in luxuriant growth! Yet nothing is realised until the bold arise who dare to be the forerunners. And it is this deplorable inertia, this fatal cowardice, that lends such power to the few to tyrannise over the many.

Four strokes of the oar and we were from the shore as far as a musket-shot. We were eleven. The archpriest was absent; but he was not the only one. Morelli, the Roman tailor, who had likewise rendered us such effective service, seized by a sudden fever, lay delirious in the hospital. I and Morbioli — the only Italians.

Now the rocks no longer intercepted the view between us and the shore, and the unwonted spectacle of a sailing boat revealed in an instant the attempt so jealously concealed. The entire refectory was in uproar, and in the confused symphony of cries, of laughter, of curses, borne to us upon the wind, there rang out the terrible command of an officer: "*Aux canonnières!*" A file of men issued from the refectory and stretched out, with sinister windings, towards the quay.

Our little boat flew on; yet by a manoeuvre I could not comprehend, the Cousin, instead of keeping the prow directed towards the Continent, described a wide circle, as though he intended to round the island. The convicts, who on the rocks were traversing a short chord of that circle, arrived swiftly at the quay and launched a gunboat in time to intercept our way. In other words, we were running at full force, and almost as if by design, straight into the danger.

I cast anxious eyes at the gunboat, which rode the water with an apparently perfect ease; with even greater anxiety I looked at Morbioli. He understood and said: "Trasteverino blood never fails! You see? No one goes on board because the plugs are at the bottom of the sea — they are scrambling now to find new ones at the workshop! And the keel, on the bottom — four deadly bung-holes!" And having said this, he clenched his jaws and pressed with arms outstretched into the void, as though striving to make the gunboat heavier still and to do violence to the sea.

All of a sudden he drove a fist into the air and burst into a great laugh! The four holes had done their duty; the gunboat, swamped, had found those plugs where the light of the Sun does not reach. And the dark swarming mass of men on the shore acclaimed us with a tempest of *evviva*, with a feverish waving of rags and caps.

The tormented were exulting at the defeat of the tormentors!

Now the Cousin completed his mysterious manoeuvre and turned the prow resolutely towards the North. I cried out to him nervously: "What are you doing?"

"Hold your tongue! The *Abeille* will give us chase before long, and it is off the coasts of Guiana that they will expect to intercept us. To the north, to the north! Liberty lies amidst the tempests; our safety is in danger itself!"

We sailed strenuously the whole night, always keeping towards the Pole. Great winds, great waves! The Ocean drenched us with salt spray, roaring in great voice through the darkness.

Was it warning us? Was it claiming us? Our senses, strung to a terrible tension, could not decipher it. Now it flung us upward as though wishing to hurl us beyond its dominion; now we were drawn headlong towards the abyss, as by a powerful, irresistible suction. Our sole assurance lay in the marble impassivity of the captain. No human voice, amidst that roaring fury, save his — measured, calm, cold, metallic.

The dawn found us drenched and shrivelled. But we had left behind the waters muddied by the great rivers that pour into them from the Continent, and we were already cleaving the deep blue. The Sun and reviving hope transformed us within a short hour. The Ocean might well agitate itself, roar its unceasing menace — every wave of it might engulf us! What did it matter? Every pulse of the sail might draw us ever nearer to a final, tragic struggle — for upon every face appeared the steadfast resolution to cut throats, or to have one's own cut, should the fruit of our daring be wrested from us. What did it matter? We felt ourselves free as the wind that ran through the infinite!
