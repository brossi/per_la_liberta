A SAD fatality that seems a chastisement! From the misdeed of Aspromonte down to the days that are now passing, in half a century of History, the House of Savoy, meritorious from various points of view, has no longer been able to lead Italy to victory, which yet smiled repeatedly still upon the glorious victim of its fratricidal lead (1).

In 1866, Venetia was wrested from the hateful yoke of Austria; but by submitting to mysterious defeats, to humiliations that too much recall another murky mystery, that which is coupled with the name of Novara. As at Novara, at Custoza and at Lissa the enemy, perceptibly inferior in strength, marvels at its own victory and, for a moment, scarce gives it credence. In the meantime, a handful of volunteers, premeditatedly left deficient in arms, redeems, on the cliffs of the Tyrol, the honour of Italy. Once again, those who arrogated to themselves the monopoly of genius and seriousness demonstrated their vacuous pomposity, their inferiority before the handful of "fools" who persisted in wanting an Italy whole and glorious. And the peremptory order given to Garibaldi to clear the lands redeemed and made fertile with the blood of his men, reveals all too plainly the royal spite for the glaring contrast.

In 1870, Rome was wrested from the nefarious yoke of the Pope; but the Monarchy did naught else but pluck, reluctantly and almost by imperious injunction, the fruit of the audacity of Mentana. Of that Mentana which saw the royal army stand fast and watch the slaughter of the daring men who sacrificed themselves to clear for the great King the way to the Campidoglio. Where, on that day, were the indignation and the generous impetus that make of the Italians the most genuine, the most winsome among the races of old Europe?

As the Fatherland gradually acquired a strip of its territory, that part of the Nation which had settled into its leaden cope lost a strip of its fine character. Idealism was setting, and in its place came a bastard pragmatism distilled from all that smacks of the most vulgar. "One must be practical!" shouted, until they were hoarse, the wiseacres of Italy who looked on, with their hands on their hips, at the fertile industry of the "disheveled ones." And they did not perceive that for a Nation, to live is less than nothing, when it knows not how to live gloriously. I say that the men who pass themselves off as serious and practical are precisely those who perpetuate the ills and shames that afflict us; for where the effort is wanting which must strike and crumble the sad reality that looms; where one does not constantly aspire, in thought and in deed, to the realization of "dreams," progress becomes a fable; while the utopia of today, nourished by sacrifice and blood, inexorably becomes the reality of tomorrow.

However that may be, the conduct of the royal army at Mentana must have caused a dark light to flash in the breast of the ruler of Italy.

The diseases of the psyche are manifested by actions. To array oneself on the field of battle as on the steps of an amphitheater and to contemplate, motionless, the foreigner who, nestled in the very sanctuary of Italianness, bayonets the generous insurgents to drive them out, as one drives out thieves entering, with a false key, the domestic sanctuary; to count the wounds; to hear the death-rattle of the dying; to see the countryside, all red with our blood, covered with corpses, and to persist in immobility and not level the rifle and not strike the arrogant oppressors, even in the back... In vain do I rummage through my notions of modern and ancient History... I find therein no more complete an eclipse of every patriotic sentiment; no more abject a renunciation of the rights and duties imposed upon virile souls.

And what Sovereign, even one animated by views as clear as the hardest patch of sky, would not have exclaimed: "Your body and your spirit are mine!"

From that servile submission to the fatuous prestige of a crown, the Italian People, taken as a whole, has not since recovered. Italy is no longer a geographical expression, no longer the land of carnival; but neither is it that of Brutus and the Gracchi. "His Majesty... His Holiness"... between the two there is not only the analogy of the rhyme. The one by grace, the other by the will of God; the one sacred and infallible, the other sacred and inviolable; the one inseparable from the Truth, the other from the Good; to speak ill of the one and of the other a crime! The heart bleeds to think of it...

We have cast aside one series of dogmas only to fall into another that is its equal; our Fatherland is now the Peninsula of the two Pontiffs!

The Monarchy, casting the national programme onto the scrap-heap, audaciously placed, after Mentana, a veto on every aspiration of the Italians towards the North. Was it bound to Austria by mysterious pacts even before it thought of tying itself to her with overt alliances? I could not say... but I know that, from then on, the motto "Italy for the Italians" gives it hysterical shivers. It dismisses the old generals who still dare to pronounce it; it prepares manacles for the patriots who do not resign themselves to considering it a mere implication. It would then attempt, by renouncing the principles that were the basis of our resurgence, to revive its own military glory, conquering in faraway Africa the land of the "four robbers." But even with soldiers at its disposal who are the incarnation of dash and sacrifice, it will succeed in nothing other than renewing the shame of its own defeat.

And yet, personally, the Savoys possess in the highest degree the military courage that descended, even unto them, through a long line of ancestors, and at San Martino where there was "glory for all" even the great King had a bounteous harvest!...

Vittorio Emanuele II also knew how to conceal his own weaknesses by assuming, in the sight of an astonished Europe, poses worthy of bronze. Nevertheless, despite his essentially aristocratic nature, his exuberant vitality led him to pleasures bordering on the vulgar. These provoked a smile of gratification both among the refined of high society, fetid and indulgent, and among the multitude which, accustomed to picturing Kings as they are portrayed in tragedies, admired them almost as revelations of a sincerely democratic soul. The times granted him the spectacular part of the triumpher, and his robust individuality seemed artfully cut to play it. He took upon himself the mission of guiding, unscathed, through the stormy present, the Institutions that had survived from the past. A fatal mission... he appears to accomplish it; but by sacrificing the future. The common folk admire him and will admire him as long as they are educated in the school that teaches to prostrate oneself before success. The thinkers who analyze already reproach him for the cunning policy, almost that of a social-climbing banker, which sowed the seeds of dissolution upon the treasure of our enthusiasms.

My sympathies go by preference to his predecessor. Carlo Alberto is embellished in my eyes by the merit that belongs to precursors and by his serene dignity in misfortune. He did not reap the fruit of his good works; of the works of others he reaped the evil fruits. He offered everything and lost everything. Epic and resounding, despite the errors, were his battles; but they become a small thing before those fought silently in his conscience. He betrayed... perhaps; but he was far more betrayed. His weaknesses, his hesitations, his defections, his very sporadic cruelty reveal the profound sufferings of a sick and extremely sensitive soul; so that the instinctive aversion for the head of a system of privilege and reaction yields to the rise of pity for the man. Yields... No, it changes its object and is poured out all the more intensely upon the nefarious sect that, under the shelter of things reputed sacred, succeeds in disfiguring elect natures such as his!

"The Hamlet of Italy"... well defined! His figure is not one for triumphal parades, but for a tragedy of passion, all shadows and flashes. Is there, among my countrymen, one who feels he might attempt it!

It has been written that I do not have the vision of a contemporary Italy, free, united, politically and civilly resurrected; advancing, with a sure step, towards the glorious goal that awaits it... once again loved and envied by all the world.

It has been written that I still have the vision of the Italy of my day, enslaved, wretched and sorrowful; that I am still moved and suffer at the memory of the ills that afflicted her.

He who wrote this shows himself more poet than observer or, at the very least, grasps but the smallest part of the truth.

I have the vision of an Italy as it was glimpsed by Giuseppe Mazzini; as we all dreamed of it when, between it and us, there was such a calvary of suffering. Contemporary Italy too little resembles it... that is why I grieve! Bursts of virile energy are not lacking in the classes that have least to congratulate themselves for the independence acquired. But their gaze is aimed at a height that the times do not permit us, and it surpasses, almost disdaining them, the intermediate claims that would yet be within our reach. One prattles ill of universal brotherhood when the sad plant of servitude takes root, by foreign arrogance, in one's own domestic garden.

And I know that, with the exception of the government of Francesco Crispi, characterized by an energy not always well applied, but virile, we have had, from a long succession of Ministers, naught but manifestations of a neurotic ferocity that reveals fear; or of a myopic and senile cunning that truly commands no respect, nor arouses the envy of anyone.

And I know that insolent Austria still extends her iron hand to this side of the Alps. She could descend, in a single day, into the heart of the Po valley, of the most fertile region of Italy. And while her Emperor, so as not to recede, even in appearance, from the position he was pleased to assume *ab initio*, refuses to return in Rome the visit paid to him by our Sovereign, our Ministers cheerfully sign the alliances that guarantee him the peaceful enjoyment of the usurped lands; that renounce the integrity and greatness of the Fatherland. What a contrast!... and how the fiber of him who holds the destinies of the young Nation shows itself weakened before that of the old reactionary and executioner Monarch!

I ask myself: Who is realizing the aspirations consecrated by the blood of so many martyrs, if one flirts with the enemy who has us by the throat? It has come to this: a special word has been invented—*quarantottate*!—to signify, while deriding it, the desperate heroism of the fathers!

A thought addressed to him who currently reigns, and I am done! For some years our Monarchy has shown signs of undergoing a partial transformation. The acts of regal humanism, more than aiming for theatrical and ephemeral spectacle, seek to leave some lasting trace. Respect for individual rights is more deeply felt therein, and political liberty is extended. Perhaps the young King was taught by the tragic end of his father, violently suppressed for his frequent returns to antiquated and tyrannical systems that should have been the sad and exclusive province of bygone times. The May of 1898, for example, in its means and its effects if not precisely in its end, too much recalls the 2nd of December for the painful doubt not to creep into more than one heart that it was its sad and ill-advised imitation.

Except that while the Monarchy makes concessions to liberalism and shows that it wishes to proceed with it, it slyly and without seeming to, retreats, as if it aimed to maintain its balance by repelling, with the increased power of the Church, the ever-growing demands of the laboring multitudes.

A Government that sits itself down amidst the parties, moderator of them all, superior to all...! This may seem wise to the "pragmatism" that cannot discern the future or that, discerning it, wrongly fears it. I see in it instead the tactic recommended by the little treatises of political philosophy. A "living" Nation is necessarily in perpetual struggle, and the Rulers of a State, monarchical or republican, can temporarily hinder or divert it, but suppress it, never. In the social struggle that looms, equilibrium is a chimera, and progress, which now completes its fatal course rapidly, is the inexorable condemnation of both economic rapine and dogma. The laboring multitude will triumph and will annihilate, in the fever of triumph, not only the hostile elements, but also the Institutions that do not align themselves openly, sincerely with it.

Let it not be thought that I bear grudges. Distance in time and space has blunted the sharp corners, has suffocated many passions. Only love survives, intense. It sparkles in the truth. Nor shall I go to my grave with the remorse of having kept it silent.

As a republican, I would wish for the Monarchy to commit a thousand errors; for, the measure being full, it would precipitate into the grave dug by its own improvidence. But as an Italian educated by my elders to place, before my political ideals, the integrity of the Fatherland, I do not hesitate to revive the last flame that is still in me to admonish:

He who leans upon the past, sets his foot on ground that the tides are daily undermining and swallowing. Trust in the future and move boldly to meet it. It is thus that you will succeed in mastering it. The past evolved from barbarism and is saturated with arrogance and crime; but History tends toward the triumph of Democracy, of equality before rights, duties, and opportunities; toward the triumph of the just. He who does not second the tendencies of History cannot have a long, blessed, and glorious reign. In foreign policy, the traces of the triumph of violence suffered by the Italian people are too plain and drip with too much blood for your mission not to be outlined clearly and precisely. The Monarchy exists because every patriotic party has folded its own banner before its, as the vanquished fold theirs; but by sealing a solemn pact that guaranteed, as compensation for the sacrifice, an Italy independent from the Alps to the extreme southern shores. The Monarchy, now with one pretext, now with another, has evaded full observance of the pact. In the meantime, Austria, despite the infamous alliance, cannot drowse. She renews and multiplies her arms; she sharpens her claws and openly hints at which flesh she yearns to sink them into. It is urgent to act humanely within the borders of the Kingdom, to acquire over the multitude the ascendancy that comes from respect; the respect that is rooted in gratitude and in love; it is urgent to rekindle the slumbering enthusiasm and the virile spirit intolerant of insults, but not with vacuous commemorative festivals, nor with puerile rhetoric of banqueters; it is urgent, I say, so that the Nation might be ready to infuse its soul into the army. The cold and arid discipline is no longer enough for armed hosts that, at the first disaster, fall apart if they are not renewed and sustained by a nobler sentiment.

To Vittorio Emanuele III the highest task of completing and correcting Vittorio Emanuele II. One wears ill the crown on his head that has come to him by the ephemeral right of succession if, in appropriating its privileges and honors, he makes light of its burdens. Every delay is now excessive; to temporize any longer would mean to usurp, by virtue of an equivocation, the throne of Italy!

Count di Rudio closed his eyes before our victories in Libya could dazzle them. What judgment he would have pronounced upon them, the present writer is not in a position to state. But if one considers that he disdained material advantages when they were not coupled with a moral and regenerating triumph, one could wager that they would have torn from him another of those cries of his that reveal a bleeding heart.

After a year and more of exploits, officially sketched for us as worthy of the ancient Romans... a Treaty of peace that recalls the onerous "cession" of Venetia!

But in setting forth the reasons why they were unable to achieve the best, the Counselors of the Crown showed themselves truly unsurpassable!
C. C.
