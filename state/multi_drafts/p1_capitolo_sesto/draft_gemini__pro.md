IN PLACING my own personality among the great men who laboured in that titanic defence, it seems to me I commit an act of irreverence. And I will also say that my soul remains as though overwhelmed by the resurgence of those powerful apparitions.

On April 30th, I too saw the backs of the invaders and surely, profiting from the victory, we would have thrown the French back to the sea, if Giuseppe Mazzini had not deemed it opportune, for his diplomatic aims, to blunt our élan.

On May 9th, I was fighting with Garibaldi at Palestrina against the Bourbon troops of General Lanza. That easy victory was enough to halt the advance of King Bomba, who dislodged from the Alban hills and hastily recrossed the borders a few days later, when Garibaldi, preferring to win rather than to show himself disciplined, incited our vanguard to engage in the battle of Velletri.

On that memorable day, I took part in an episode which I recall with pride. The lancers of Angelo Masina, overwhelmed by an entire enemy column, were falling back at a wild gallop on the road to Valmontone. Giuseppe Garibaldi, having just seen them, spurred his white horse and, reaching a short distance from their front, planted himself across the road to bar their way. Behind him, on a horse the colour of ebony, stood the giant Aguyar, his face just as gleaming and black. Heroism forgot that motion has its laws. The lancers made superhuman efforts to stop dead in their tracks, but to no avail. In less time than it takes to tell, the road was encumbered by a mass of men and quadrupeds... Garibaldi and Aguyar were buried beneath it. The enemy cavalry arrived.

I was fighting along the road, to the right, among the vineyards. We were a handful of beardless youths... But we did not wait for orders and, shouting: "Forward! Forward!" we ran resolutely to the rescue. The Bourbons, assaulted from the front and the flank, took to precipitous flight, leaving a good number of prisoners in our power.

Garibaldi recognized his own salvation in our impetus. In the evening, when by order of General Rosselli our offensive unfortunately came to an end, he came among us. That man, so terrible when the cannon thundered, good-naturedly squeezed my cheek. At this sort of codicil to the episode, I smile now: but then... I felt all that I supposed was heroic in me evaporate. That act of fatherly familiarity reduced me, with an indescribable force, to the humble proportions of a child.

At the end of May, I re-entered Rome, and we were already on the verge of moving against the Austrians when General Oudinot, adding perfidy to perfidy, prematurely broke the armistice concluded after his defeat of April 30th and seized, by surprise, the Villa Pamfili. There was no longer any need to go to Ancona to fertilize, with our blood, the soil of Italy!

That day (June 3rd) saw such proofs of desperate valour as to astonish Europe and the world, no longer accustomed to discerning, among the hills of Rome, Roman greatness. But the hydra of reaction had, that day, an overwhelming sacrifice of our finest men. If only it had been the last!

The French aimed to subjugate Rome by seizing the Gianicolo, which dominates the entire city. To get there, it was indispensable to first occupy our advanced posts. Having taken Villa Pamfili from us, they moved, under the cover of darkness, against Villa Corsini which, due to its elevated and strategic position, could be considered the key to Porta San Pancrazio. And they took it by surprise; but with a furious bayonet assault, it was retaken by us.

Unfortunately, due to the enormous disparity in numbers and for various topographical reasons, on which I deem it superfluous to dwell, the assault of that villa proved more than arduous for those proceeding from the city, but easy for the French, entrenched beyond Villa Pamfili. Our magnanimous contempt for our own lives could lead us, and led us repeatedly, to become its masters again; but to hold it was for us a superhuman and impossible enterprise.

Nevertheless, we showed incredible aggressive tenacity from the earliest hours of the morning until late evening. That superb manifestation of the resurgent, virile Italian character had, as far as I am concerned, a parenthesis. In one of our first reconquests, while crossing with weapon in hand a corridor cluttered with corpses, I saw through the crack of a doorway the red trousers of a Frenchman... an officer who had persisted in the defence and had been unable to retreat with his men. To escape the first rush of the assailants, he had hidden himself in a water closet. When I ordered him to surrender, he handed me his sword, without a word.

I was tasked with accompanying him to the Chiesa Nuova, where the captured officers were being temporarily confined.

The Romans had treated the prisoners taken on April 30th not as enemies, but as guests. Those were the times when the rosy hope of brotherhood between the two Peoples still trembled in the air, and hopes of inducing the French to protect Rome from the Austrian hordes were renewed by the very assurances of the Triumvirs. Carriage rides to the monuments, refreshments, songs, dances... then freedom. The promise never again to turn their arms against the champions of our independence was neither given nor asked.

But on June 3rd, the spirit of the Romans had terribly changed. The bad faith of Oudinot had aroused to a paroxysm the indignation of that populace so averse to all duplicity, so hostile to betrayal. I realized this as soon as I entered through Porta San Pancrazio, and the officer must have been even more convinced of it, for it was not always in my power to shield him from vile insults.

Arriving at the Ponte di San Sisto, I found my way barred by some militiamen of the National Guard who, refusing to listen to reason and following the impulse of the moment, demanded that I hand the prisoner over to them. They wanted to kill him in cold blood and throw his corpse into the river.

I shouted at them: "He who thirsts for French blood, let him run outside the walls, and blessed is he if his hands prove skilful and strong. But not here! As long as I have breath in my body, I will not permit the Italian name to be dishonoured by cowardly acts."

The timely intervention of an officer calmed their spirits and averted any tragedy.

We reached the Chiesa Nuova without further incident, and the grateful Frenchman recounted what had happened to the other prisoners. While, not far away, two peoples, made to understand and love each other, were hurling themselves at one another, thirsting for vengeance, in that relatively tranquil corner I heard the invaders themselves exalt our valour and the wish that, once resentments were dispelled, French blood and Italian blood might flow intermingled, but with fraternal solidarity, but for a common, holy vindication.

A prophetic wish! ... In those moments, however, I deemed it more likely that I would be assumed alive into the glory of the heavens!

As I climbed the narrow and steep road that leads to Porta San Pancrazio, I had to walk against a current... the current of the wounded being carried, or dragging themselves, to the church of San Pietro in Montorio, transformed into a field hospital. Soldiers and citizens applauded them. From the top of the walls, a band scattered, amidst the gunfire, the notes of the Marseillaise, flinging in the faces of the aggressors their own hymn to Liberty. Amidst so much blood, in such an extremity, Rome found a way to be ironic! I had left Villa Corsini in the possession of the Legionaries; I found it reoccupied by the French. Our Bersaglieri, captained by Luciano Manara, were attempting to recapture it. I saw them run up the slopes of the hill, leaving the ground behind them covered with the dead and the barely living. The fire that cut them down was so well-fed that, a short distance from the building, they were stopped as if dead in their tracks by the lead. But their chests, swollen with love of country, had no room for weak sentiments. Exposed as they were, they knelt and continued to discharge their rifles, presenting their dauntless brows to the enemy and to fate. Surely they expected to be reinforced by our men, massed at the Vascello and on the walls; surely they offered themselves, with serene spirit, to death to give them time to arrive, to renew the offence at their side.

What moments, for us, who saw their ranks thinning; for us, pained by their wounds, proud of their greatness! The awaited order never came. They fell back when the officers commanded it; when the sacrifice was forbidden by discipline. But on leaving the enclosure, which ended, towards Rome, in an acute angle, they became a sure target for a converging and terrible fire, and then... My Fatherland, what carnage!

Thus did the proud youth of Lombardy erase the shame of past civil strife, and saturating with their own blood the land of the Gracchi, they traced there, invisible to the eyes, yet flaming for the heart, the fateful words: "One Italy, Rome the capital of Italy!"

It was in that assault that Enrico Dandolo ceased to throb and to fight for his country, for our country. His death, sublimating the courage of his brother Emilio, spurred him to attempt the assault of the villa, leading just twenty of his men. That small handful, thinned along the way by enemy projectiles, was almost lost among the rugged terrain and the orange trees of the slope. They reached the peristyle as thirteen, discharged their weapons, and withdrew in the face of the inevitable. When they took shelter at the Vascello, they were reduced to six... not one of whom was not bleeding.

An enterprise of theirs which cold and calculating minds may well qualify as madness... and so be it! But it was a madness of almost superhuman natures who fought not to win. The defenders of Rome, it is well to clarify, had set out to give the lie to the belief that Italians do not fight; to place, between the Temporal Power and the yearned-for third Italy, the impossibility of an equivocation; to affirm, by dying heroically, our right to life. And we can proclaim it now, aloud: they achieved their arduous goal.

The turn for heroism and sacrifice fell, after Emilio Dandolo, to Angelo Masina of Bologna. By now the upper floors of the villa, shelled by the bastions of Porta San Pancrazio, are half-ruined and in flames; but this does not make the several hundred Frenchmen, unseen and invulnerable unless dislodged by cold steel, any less formidable to the forty lancers of Masina.

They ascend the hill at a gallop, they reach the summit as if nothing could hold them back. Here, to the great astonishment of friends and enemies, Masina, suddenly assuming the proportions of a figure conjured by a magic spell, launches his horse up the grand staircase, dragging behind him, all the way to the terrace, that handful of stalwarts.

And perhaps all of them would have left their bones there, if we soldiers concentrated at the Vascello, exalted by that spectacle, had not feared that our leaders, persisting in their error, would render even so much heroism fruitless. It was as if an electric spark, starting from the heart of Angelo Masina, had suddenly ignited a thousand and a thousand hearts with its own flame. We came out from our shelters and rushed to the assault spontaneously, without waiting for a command from anyone.

An even stranger circumstance! The populace, crowded on the bastions as if at a theatre, intuited our intent and, seized by our fever, precipitated itself, surging like an impetuous wave, between the walls that enclose the narrow road; it spreads out in great confusion and as if driven by its own impetus up the slopes of the hill, bathed in so much blood. It has no weapons, or wholly inadequate ones; but it seems convinced that its very presence will invigorate our arms... It would have, if nothing else, shown that it knew how to face danger, how to die!

Such an infraction of discipline, such a lack of all elementary prudence had an effect contrary to what was logically to be expected. Not only was every Frenchman swept from Villa Corsini; but, pressing the victory, our bayonets also reconquered the Convent of San Pancrazio where the invader had, since morning, entrenched his outposts. And a band of brave men, turning to the right, managed to seize the Villa Valentini, from whose windows the French had been harassing us, shortly before, even inside the Vascello.

Never, as in that hour, did the fortunes of the day seem so propitious to the glory of Rome!... When, from Villa Pamfili, Oudinot pushed forward his battle-hardened and rested Regiments, one after the other, to retake the lost positions, numbers, discipline, and superior arms prevailed once more; but not without enormous sacrifices. Neither our men, exhausted by so many trials, nor the almost unarmed People, fled.

We withdrew to the Casa Giacometti, to the Vascello, to the walls, but with our faces always turned to the enemy, always fighting.

Giuseppe Garibaldi held in his heart the certainty that, with the loss of Villa Corsini, the fate of the young Republic was sealed. The improvident General Rosselli had not taken the trouble, during the armistice, to entrench it, to render it, if not invulnerable, at least stronger. To cap the misfortune, he, Garibaldi, received from Rome only piecemeal and at long intervals the fresh contingents necessary to sustain the battle. Seeing defeat was inevitable, he would not resign himself to it without pushing his tenacity to the extreme.

Night was imminent when he, planting himself before the Unione Regiment and whatever remnants of other corps the French lead had spared, personally captained another furious assault. The valour was identical... but the result was catastrophe!

I had just finished firing my last cartridges when I saw Goffredo Mameli pass by me, supported by his comrades-in-arms, pale, but smiling. They told me he was wounded in the knee... Italy was about to lose its young Tyrtaeus!

Night fell... Despondency, recriminations!? Not even a shadow. There persisted in us, after the defeat, the exaltation that had sustained us, that had multiplied our strength a hundredfold during the desperate and unequal struggle. And as for Garibaldi, we felt we loved him as if he had led us not to slaughter, but to victory. Our spirits felt so little defeated that, having just emerged from that bloodbath, we meditated and attempted to carry out a deed which, in an army made homogeneous not by the overwhelming desire to act but by discipline, would have earned us the iron sentence of a Council of War.

Masina, the heroic Bolognese who for a moment had seemed, to friends and enemies alike, a knight descended among the combatants by a magic spell, had proved to be all too mortal. There was one among us who swore he had seen him fall during our retreat, a short distance from Villa Corsini. We looked at each other, and it was almost enough to say: "Let's go!"

A short while later, we stole out of the Casa Giacometti. There were twelve of us. We crept along the Via della Nocetta, hugging the wall that encircles the villa. The cannon had opened a small breach in it... we entered through it into the enclosure and, still crawling, we climbed the slope. Not a suspicious sound!... we search, we find. But the stalwarts who lay their hands on the glorious remains forget prudence and, thinking to escape danger more quickly, they lift their burden and descend towards the breach at a run. In the night, a volley of rifle shots rang out sinisterly... the three fall in a heap; the others scatter... I leap behind a hedge and throw myself to the ground. Next to me, my elbow touching it, was the corpse of an enemy with his skull shattered.

I was the last to return to the Casa Giacometti... four of our men never came back. And while I was telling my fellow soldiers how I had managed, someone cried out: "Jesus... what a stench!" My uniform sleeve was all plastered with French brains.

June 8th was a torrid day... I stood for a few seconds in silence, thinking of the sad transformation undergone, in a brief hour, by the sublime organ of human thought, under the kisses of the splendid sun of Rome
