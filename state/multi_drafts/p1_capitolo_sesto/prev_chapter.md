CHAPTER FIVE

THE SIEGE OF ROME is indissolubly linked to the name of a man fatal to Liberty: that of Luigi Napoleone. The Carbonari, who had received him into their bosom, regarded him with suspicion as early as 1831; for at that time the rumour had spread that he had made away with his brother (Napoleone Luigi), who, having been born four years before him, stood in his path to the heights of power.

Our revolutionary movements opened, or seemed to open, new horizons for Italy at that time, and the two sons of Hortense, taking part in those movements, sought upon the Peninsula a field propitious to their ambition; a compensation, perhaps, as well, for the loss of the French throne, then warmed by Louis-Philippe. This train of thought lent credence to the dark rumour, which, moreover, could never be thoroughly investigated.

The majority of historians who treat of the period assert simply that Napoleone Luigi died of measles. Others go so far as to admit that the matter is not altogether certain; yet leave it vaguely to be supposed that, if a crime was perpetrated, it is to be attributed to the fanatics of some *Vendita* [lodge of the Carbonari].

Be that as it may, the distrust was tempered by time and by the prince's humanitarian postures. On the other hand, the subversives well understood the immense advantage they might derive from a man whose very name was an entire programme of war against the old order, and who could not have emerged from his semi-obscurity save on condition of assuming full responsibility for that programme.

His hopes of making his fortune in Italy having vanished, Luigi Napoleone turned his eyes toward France. He first attempted the *coup de main* at Strasbourg, which failed chiefly owing to his want of resolution; then that at Boulogne, which led him straight to the prison-cells of the fortress of Ham.

The subversives of France and Italy then schemed to oppose the violent suppression of that political entity. Princess Cristina Belgioioso Triulzio, intimate with the affairs of Young Italy, was induced to employ her own influence at the Court of Louis-Philippe, to obtain an interview with the prisoner.

It is known that the substance of that interview required no lengthy preamble. The gentlewoman offered the support of the liberals of France and Italy to effect an escape and to set the man afloat upon the stormy political sea. Once arrived at power, he would discharge the debt of gratitude by working toward the promulgation of democratic laws and championing our independence.

Luigi Napoleone's hesitancy led the illustrious Italian to grant him two months for reflection. In the meanwhile, so as not to be found unprepared for events, his friends would see to the organisation of an escape plan.

The two months elapsed and a second interview took place. The prisoner let her speak; then expressed his profound sympathy for our Nation; but concluded by declaring that, for the moment, he did not feel disposed to act — whether because he distrusted the comely and most cultivated gentlewoman, or because he hoped for assistance from parties whose ideas were more akin to his own instincts and his secret designs. The plan seemed abandoned when, like a thunderbolt, word spread through our circles that Napoleone, suddenly changing his mind, had given his consent; and, shortly after, with equal swiftness, came the news of his disguise and his flight, from France to Belgium, from Belgium to England.

In London, and precisely at the Brunswick Hotel, he met again with the Belgioioso and sealed, so to speak, the compact: "He would aid Italy, as soon as the affairs of France were settled."

Events rushed headlong. Louis-Philippe was driven from the throne, and Napoleone demanded permission to return to his country. To overcome the parliamentary opposition, Louis Blanc arose and, with a single speech, altered the course of French history as much as ten battles might have done. Napoleone became a candidate for the National Assembly for the Department of the Seine, and set foot triumphantly upon French soil.

A sadly famous epoch! The parties contending for mastery of the Country flew at one another's throats and unleashed upon Paris the tempest of blood that preludes every great political transformation. Cavaignac emerged grimly from the carnage and aspired to the Presidency of the Republic, contested by the vanquished who, in desperation, set against him the candidacy of Ledru-Rollin. The resultant of those two currents, foreseen and facilitated by our secret associations, carried the victory to a third candidacy — that of Luigi Napoleone. On the 20th of December 1848 he became the Supreme Magistrate of the glorious Nation.

What was his first act of note?

That of repaying his friends by working, with arms and with treachery, to restore in the very heart of our Peninsula the most feeble, the most baleful, the most hated of Governments — the misrule of the Pope!

The diabolical design was so well dissembled as to induce the Italians to facilitate the landing of General Oudinot's troops, which appeared without warning in the waters of Civitavecchia. They received them with enthusiastic, fraternal demonstrations.

In France, suspicions arose more promptly. The fierce protests against the suspected annihilation of a sister Republic, to the advantage of the element hostile to all liberty, spread from the halls of Parliament down among the multitudes, assuming here and there the proportions of genuine insurrections. And perhaps popular energy would at last have imposed honest intentions, when word spread of the reverse sustained, on the 30th of April, by the arms of the invader.

A shudder of dark patriotism ran from one end of France to the other. "The prestige of the flag must be restored; national honour must be vindicated at whatever cost." As though the shame of a People derived not from the crime itself, but from its failure to be carried through! Luigi Napoleone thus found, unexpectedly, a willing accomplice in his grim designs — strange to say! — the most generous, the most loyal among the nations of the old Europe!

The French, the soldiery of Re Bomba, the Spaniards, the Austrians, and the underhanded arts of the priests! — how could Rome have escaped utter ruin?

One man alone, Giuseppe Mazzini, foresaw, in that tragic moment, that from death itself might, by virtue of heroism and of martyrdom, be renewed the right, might spring forth life. He strove to transfuse his faith into all who surrounded him, and the men of learning and the humble alike were as if transfigured by it. The "brigands" who defended Rome wrote, in characters of blood, the purest, the most fruitful of the pages of our modern history. The iron resolve that restored its glorious capital to our Fatherland is owed, in great measure, to their defeats!
