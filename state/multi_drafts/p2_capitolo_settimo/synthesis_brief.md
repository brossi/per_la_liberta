LA domane, procedevo a tutto vapore verso il mio destino. Per affrontarlo, avevo la mia valigia a sacco... tra il cuoio e l'assicella del fondo, essa celava il fulminato di mercurio ed una lama che già, nei tentativi insurrezionali del 1853, aveva conosciuto i battiti del mio cuore. Avevo inoltre, un passaporto al nome di Giovanni da Sylva, portoghese, commesso viaggiatore di una fabbrica di birra e una diecina di scellini. Danaro e passaporto mi erano stati consegnati dal dott. Bernard, durante il nostro ultimo colloquio. Avevo, sopratutto, la mia giovinezza ed il mio coraggio. Che sarebbe uscito da tutto questo? La scintilla capace di modificare la storia, o null'altro fuorché la desolazione e la morte?

Lasciate alle spalle le bocche del Tamigi, trovammo un mare agitatissimo, anzi una vera tempesta. I disturbi dello stomaco e lo spavento misero una grande confusione a bordo. Ma l'infinito, che ruggiva d'ogni intorno, a me dava un esaltamento che mi raddoppiava la coscienza della vita. Da tempo il quietismo tentava come di sovrapporsi alla mia natura e quella specie di guazzo nella forza e nel moto pareva mi rigenerasse.

Eppure la tempesta stessa, che io quasi benedicevo, era, a mia insaputa, una risposta delle cose alla domanda che mi ero rivolta e che si affacciava con grande insistenza alla mia mente. Il piroscafo, rallentato dal vento e dai marosi, approdava a Calais alle cinque antimeridiane del nove Gennaio... dodici ore di ritardo! e quando, a Parigi, scesi dal treno, cercai inutilmente, tra il volgo affollato, la nobile figura di Felice Orsini. Questo contrattempo rendeva imperativa una mia visita alla sua dimora, in via Monthabor... circostanza che doveva avere conseguenze più gravi di quanto la sua apparente poca entità mi lasciasse supporre.

Ecco, in breve, ciò che avvenne in quella mia prima visita. L'appartamento dell'Orsini era a pian terreno. Il portinaio mi accompagna sino all'uscio, batte e quando l'uscio si apre, discretamente si allontana. Entro di slancio e sono in procinto d'abbracciare fraternamente l'Orsini, che se ne sta ritto ed in attesa, quando noto la presenza di una terza persona... un giovanotto sdraiato confidenzialmente su una poltrona. Era biondo, aveva le guancie e le labbra completamente rase, la fisionomia di un prete bonaccione. Allora assunsi l'aria ossequiosa del commesso viaggiatore e, declinato il nome del passaporto, mi offersi di sottoporre a "Tomaso Allsop" qualche campione di birra.

Rovistavo nella valigia, già messa sottosopra dagli impiegati della Dogana, quando il finto Allsop diede in una sonora risata. "Siamo tra amici" disse. E mi presentò col mio vero nome, ad Antonio Gomez. Mi domandò poi spiegazione del mio ritardo e, aiutata, disse "Come ti ha accolto la Signorina Cheney! Ti ha consegnato l'affare!"

Rovesciai i miei quattro effetti sul tavolo e posi mano a levare l'assicella che celava il fulminato di mercurio. Poco dopo, esso era spianato su un giornale, "Le Droit", se la memoria non m'inganna. Felice Orsini l'andava carezzando coi polpastrelli, quasi ad accertarne il grado d'umidità. La sua faccia, tanto mobile ed aperta, aveva assunto un'espressione rigida e di profonda melanconia. Finì per dire: "La provvista è ormai completa e non per nulla avremo fuso dodici apostoli." E sospirava.

Colpito da quell'aria melanconica e da quel sospiro, rammentai il suo primo progetto, ispirato dall'orrore del sangue. Evidentemente, egli ci teneva ancora ad affidare all'arma bianca la liberazione della Francia.

"Questa diavoleria è molto umida — riprese — bisognerà ricorrere al caminetto ed al termometro. Ma ciò non ti riguarda. Devo a tua moglie e a tuo figlio di non esporti più di quanto è strettamente necessario."

In quella fu picchiato all'uscio... indubbiamente con un segno convenzionale, perchè l'Orsini non si scompaginò e lasciò il fulminato sul tavolo, in piena vista.

Entrò un uomo della mia statura, col barbone brizzolato, coll'occhio intelligente; la pallidezza del volto rendeva straordinariamente intensa la sua nera pupilla. Rimase interdetto vedendomi e si accontentò di pronunciare un semplice, banale saluto. Ma le sue parole risonarono nella camera in modo straordinario. Una voce, quella, che ricordava il suono monotono di un "bag pipe". Era la prima volta che l'udivo, ma mi sarebbe bastata per riconoscerla fra mille, anche molti anni dopo. E, lì per lì, mi parve il connotato più saliente dell'uomo. Indubbiamente il barbone copriva una gola piena.

Felice Orsini fece la presentazione. Conobbi così personalmente Giuseppe Pieri, col quale avevo già avuto, per posta, una breve corrispondenza. Egli attaccò subito il discorso con grande dimestichezza, come se da lunghi anni avessimo condiviso la buona e la cattiva ventura. Così fu all'atto pratico, quella prudente determinazione dell'Orsini, di riserbarsi la conoscenza esclusiva dei partecipanti all'attentato.

Mi proponevo di parlargliene alla prima occasione, quand'egli, quasi ad accentuare la cattiva piega, interruppe la rumorosa loquela del Pieri: "A proposito! — mi disse — dove hai preso alloggio?"

Gli osservai che ero giunto da breve ora... il tempo indispensabile di mangiare un boccone alla prima trattoria e di percorrer la via. Ero rimasto con pochi soldi in tasca. Siccome l'intesa era di alloggiare, per maggior cautela, in un albergo di primo ordine, così aspettavo le sue istruzioni.

"Sai! — riprese dopo essersi grattato il capo — L'amico Pieri alloggia all'Hotel France et Champagne, via Montmartre. Egli ha una camera con due letti. Va con lui e le cose ne resteranno semplificate."

Non mi lasciò il tempo di sollevare obbiezioni. Aveva piegata la persona all'indietro e, fatto scorrere lo sguardo successivamente su tutti noi, disse: "Un Veneto, un Toscano, un Napolitano, un Romagnuolo. Ed altri, altri lembi di terra italiana saranno rappresentati nella fiera impresa! Non è questo o nell'individuo, non questa o quella regione, ma l'Italia intera che si ridesta, che si adopera per spianarsi la via alla redenzione. Il significato è alto e l'Europa lo comprenderà. E questa unione delle destre che si ricercano, oggi forzatamente in segreto, ci affida dell'altra unione che si compirà al cospetto dei Popoli ed alla luce del Sole... che si compirà, si compirà!" E io abbassai la testa e un fremito prepotente mi corse per l'ossa, come se nell'ambiente fosse improvvisamente passata la grande anima di un profeta!

Capitolo Ottavo

CHAPTER SEVEN

THE next morning, I was pressing forward at full steam towards my destiny. To face it, I had my carpet-bag... between the leather and the wooden panel of the bottom, it concealed mercury fulminate and a blade which had already, in the insurrectionary attempts of 1853, known the beating of my heart. I had moreover a passport in the name of Giovanni da Sylva, a Portuguese, travelling salesman for a brewery, and some ten shillings. Money and passport had been placed in my hands by Dr. Bernard during our last meeting. I had, above all, my youth and my courage. What would come of all this? The spark capable of altering history, or nothing other than desolation and death?

Leaving the mouths of the Thames behind us, we encountered a very rough sea, a veritable tempest. The stomach's distress and the terror cast a great confusion aboard. But the infinite, which roared on every side, filled me with an exaltation that redoubled my consciousness of life. For some time a certain quietism had seemed to impose itself upon my nature, and that kind of immersion in force and motion seemed to regenerate me.

Yet the storm itself, which I almost blessed, was, unknown to me, the answer of things to the question I had put to myself, and which pressed upon my mind with great insistence. The steamship, slowed by wind and billows, reached Calais at five in the morning of the ninth of January... twelve hours late! And when, in Paris, I descended from the train, I sought in vain, among the crowded throng, the noble figure of Felice Orsini. This mishap made a visit to his lodgings in the rue Monthabor imperative — a circumstance which was to have consequences far graver than its apparent trifling nature led me to suppose.

Here, in brief, is what came to pass during that first visit of mine. Orsini's apartment was on the ground floor. The porter accompanied me to the door, knocked, and when the door opened, discreetly withdrew. I entered with a bound and was on the point of embracing Orsini as a brother — he stood erect and waiting — when I noticed the presence of a third person... a young man sprawled with easy familiarity in an armchair. He was fair-haired, with cheeks and lips cleanly shaven, and had the look of a good-natured priest. I thereupon assumed the deferential air of the travelling salesman and, giving the name from the passport, offered to present "Tomaso Allsop" with a few samples of beer.

I was rummaging in the carpet-bag, already turned inside out by the customs officers, when the false Allsop broke into a loud laugh. "We are among friends," he said. And he introduced me, by my true name, to Antonio Gomez. He then asked me to explain my delay, and, assisted, said: "How did Miss Cheney receive you! Did she hand over the matter to you!"

I turned out my few belongings upon the table and set about removing the panel that concealed the mercury fulminate. A moment later it lay spread upon a newspaper — *Le Droit*, if memory does not deceive me. Felice Orsini was running his fingertips over it, as though to judge the degree of its moisture. His face, ordinarily so mobile and open, had assumed a rigid expression of deep melancholy. At last he said: "The supply is now complete, and not for nothing shall we have cast twelve apostles." And he sighed.

Struck by that melancholy air and by that sigh, I recalled his original plan, inspired by his horror of bloodshed. Evidently, he still clung to the idea of entrusting to the blade the liberation of France.

"This devilish stuff is very damp," he resumed, "we shall have to make use of the fireplace and the thermometer. But this does not concern you. I owe it to your wife and your son not to expose you beyond what is strictly necessary."

At that moment there came a knock at the door... undoubtedly a prearranged signal, for Orsini did not stir and left the fulminate on the table, in full view.

There entered a man of my own stature, with a grizzled beard and an intelligent eye; the pallor of his face lent extraordinary intensity to his dark pupils. He stopped short on seeing me and contented himself with uttering a simple, commonplace greeting. But his words resounded in the room in an extraordinary manner. A voice that recalled the monotonous sound of a bag-pipe. It was the first time I had heard it, yet it would have sufficed for me to recognise it among a thousand, even many years hence. And, there and then, it struck me as the most distinctive mark of the man. Undoubtedly the beard concealed a full throat.

Felice Orsini made the introduction. In this manner I came to know personally Giuseppe Pieri, with whom I had already exchanged, by post, a brief correspondence. He at once struck up conversation with great familiarity, as though for long years we had shared good and ill fortune alike. Such, in practice, was the outcome of Orsini's prudent resolve to reserve to himself alone the knowledge of those taking part in the attempt.

I had intended to speak to him of this at the first opportunity, when he, as though to underline the ill-chosen turn of affairs, broke in upon the boisterous chatter of Pieri: "By the way!" he said to me. "Where have you taken lodgings?"

I observed that I had arrived but a short while ago... barely time enough to eat a bite at the nearest eating-house and make my way here. I had been left with few coins in my pocket. Since the understanding had been that I should lodge, as a greater precaution, in a first-rate hotel, I was awaiting his instructions.

"You know!" he resumed, after scratching his head. "Our friend Pieri is lodging at the Hôtel France et Champagne, rue Montmartre. He has a room with two beds. Go with him, and matters will be kept simple."

He gave me no time to raise objections. He leant his person backwards and, letting his gaze pass in turn over each of us, said: "A Venetian, a Tuscan, a Neapolitan, a Romagnole. And other, other shreds of Italian soil shall be represented in this proud enterprise! It is not this or that individual, not this or that region, but all Italy that awakens, that labours to open for herself the road to redemption. The significance is great, and Europe will comprehend it. And this union of hands that seek one another, today compelled to do so in secret, pledges us that other union which shall be accomplished in the sight of Peoples and in the light of the Sun... which shall come to pass, shall come to pass!" And I bowed my head, and a powerful tremor ran through my bones, as though in that place there had suddenly passed the great soul of a prophet!

## Chapter the Sixth

FOR several days, I forced myself to suppress the thoughts that surged tumultuously through my head, renewing old acquaintances. At the same time, I sought some foothold that might put an end to my extremely thorny financial situation, and secure for me work and bread for the future.

One evening, a little before sunset, I was returning, unusually tired, almost dispirited, from my customary excursion, when, turning the corner into Shoah Square, I literally ran into Simon Bernard.

"Bravo! Bravo! I have just come from your lodgings. Signor Orsini has written. Do you know what he has written? That he knows you by proof. That he wants a man of his fullest confidence. Either you, or he sends the whole business to ruin. What do you intend to do?"

This time I answered: "I have given my word to Felice Orsini; I shall keep it."

Instead of going straight home, we walked three or four times round the square. Orsini's letter had dissipated Simon Bernard's coldness and loosened his tongue. I learned that Felice Orsini was already in Paris, gathering again the threads of his conspiracy; that the means definitively chosen for the attempt was that of bombs. That the Baron Egassy di Torocfalda had devised a wholly new type, fashioned in the form of a hedgehog, each spike armed with a percussion cap, so that they would explode in whatever manner they were thrown. The Taylor foundry in Birmingham had cast them piece by piece, in the guise of gas-fittings. The substance destined to make them explode was mercury fulminate. The same Baron di Torrocfalda had taught Orsini how to prepare it, and Orsini had so thoroughly profited from the lesson that he had managed without mishap, scorching but a few fingers.

On taking his leave, Simon Bernard recommended that I hold myself in readiness, since from one day to the next the order of departure might reach me. And I concluded at once that the time had come to seek the cooperation of Giuseppe Mazzini.

To obtain an interview with him was not so easy as it might appear; whether the Maestro, spied upon by every Police in the world, had grown distrustful; or whether, as many maintained, certain persons with whom he had surrounded himself were at pains to keep at a distance all those who might have aided him effectively in some daring enterprise of his. In conversation among friends it was said, sometimes with a smile, but more often not without a touch of resentment, that it was more difficult to obtain an audience with Giuseppe Mazzini than with the Queen of England.

In those days, truth to tell, several individuals had been drawing near me, together with Massarenti, men whom I knew to be in close relations with the Maestro. But my well-founded distrust had led me to play, in their company, the part of the man who listens and does not speak.

Unexpectedly, chance seemed to favour me. I was walking in Cheapside with a certain Gori, formerly a band-leader of the Zambeccari Legion, when I caught sight, at a short distance before me, of Mazzini arm in arm with a stranger. I quickened my pace and caught up with him. Naturally, I could not speak of so important a matter in the presence of strangers, and after the necessary greetings I was obliged to confine myself to telling Mazzini that I desired an interview, and to ask him to name the hour and place. Giuseppe Mazzini gazed into the air, as though seeking inspiration there; then, having asked for my address, said that Massarenti would be able to tell me something in that regard. Massarenti... precisely the fish that stuck in our throat!

We parted. Events came headlong and swept me into their vortex before Mazzini made any sign of life. What would have happened had I been able to speak with him? Would he have dissuaded me from the enterprise, or would he have brought to it the contribution of his influence and his ardent soul? These are questions that were put to me repeatedly. Certainly, to answer them is of no consequence. Yet I observe: the man who wrote that certain exceptional actions, undertaken in vindication of human rights, ought not to be judged by the standard of common morality; the man who pointed out to Gallenga the dagger that should have killed Carlo Alberto; who had a part — not clearly defined, yet certain — in the Tebaldi attempt against Luigi Bonaparte, could not logically have opposed that of Felice Orsini. And the Maestro knew full well where logic has its dwelling!

The Tebaldi attempt had been organised precisely in that year, 1857. Napoleon III frequented the house of the Countess Sommariva for reasons, it was said, better known to him than to the Countess's family. Tebaldi and his two accomplices were to gain entry, by means of a false key, into that house, and take possession of the Emperor's person. The intention appears to have been to force him to abdicate. But the capture of a man so vigilantly guarded could not be accomplished without bloodshed, and in the Maestro's eyes the life of a simple policeman was worth no less than that of an Emperor. However that may be, Tebaldi's two accomplices could not keep the secret, and all three were arrested. Tebaldi was exiled to the Île du Diable; his two companions to Algeria.

The reaction aroused by the misdeeds of Luigi Napoleon was too intense not to manifest itself in violent and personal fashion. Regicide was in the air, and Napoleon knew it so well that, as early as 1852, after the coup d'état and before the proclamation of the Empire, he had the Romagnoles Galli and Rossini deported to Cayenne — guilty of nothing save that of having fallen foul of the infamous Law of Suspects.

The attempt of that other daring Romagnole, Giovanni Pianori, failed because Napoleon, unable to shelter himself

"Beneath the shield of a clear conscience,"

wore, in secret, a coat of chain-mail. Pianori was struck down before he could fire his second shot. He swelled the company of those martyred by the guillotine. His brother, already embittered by political hatred, felt his fury redoubled in his breast at that tragic death. He left Italy, meditating a resounding vengeance. But no sooner had he set foot on French soil than he was arrested and deported to the hell of the tropical islands, like Galli and Redini, without a shadow of a trial.

Fierce tempers, these Pianori brothers! They recognised no duty save the vindication of their own rights and those of others, and toward it they ran resolved, heedless of the Laws, which they held to be a scarecrow contrived by the cunning, for the purpose of domination and plunder; regardless of their own lives, which they said were well spent, if spent in the erasure of an injustice.

Giovanni Pianori, the guillotined, stands forth in all his grim wholeness from yet another tragic incident. He loved his wife to distraction. Compelled by the turbulent vicissitudes of his existence to part from her, he had entrusted her to the care of a close friend who, having insinuated himself into the heart of the comely ward, proved too tender a lover to remain a faithful friend. Some zealous informer breathed the painful truth into the ear of the betrayed man who, braving a thousand dangers, made a sudden return. It was no great difficulty to wring from the terrified woman her imprudent confession. He grew calm again and did not lay a hand upon her; but he imposed absolute silence, with that resoluteness which commands and admits no reply.

The intimate friend arrived. Pianori, dissembling, invited him, after a brief conversation, out of doors. They might discuss certain secret political affairs with greater freedom. All at once, in the darkness, the friend heard himself addressed with this question: "What would you do to a friend to whom you had entrusted your honour, and all that is most precious in the world, if he were to dishonour you, if he were to betray you?"

— You have pronounced your own sentence!

And without another word, he laid him gasping in his own blood.

A crime? I do not say otherwise. I say only that iron times make iron men, and that it is folly to expect mild manners and sheep-like sentiments from people accustomed to the daily spectacle of virtue swinging from a gibbet. And I say further that abstract ideas are fixed in practical life, embodied in History, not by timid bleatings, but by the impetuous action of tempers such as these.

The next letter sent me by Felice Orsini had been written from Paris, but its envelope bore no other postmark than that of London. Evidently some trusted third party had received it and posted it in the latter city. Orsini asked me to call upon Miss Elisa Cheney, residing at Camden Town, who would hand me a travelling-bag and a pair of gold spectacles. What was actually meant by "gold spectacles" I learned shortly afterwards from Simon Bernard. Probably so as not to attract the attention of the customs officials with an excessively bulky parcel, Felice Orsini had carried with him, on his journey to Paris, only a portion of the mercury fulminate he required. He was now entrusting me with the transport of the remainder.

I went to Camden Town that very evening. Miss Elisa Cheney — a woman who appeared to be somewhere in her thirties, slender, slight, dark, not beautiful in the classical sense, but most attractive — received me with exquisite graciousness and a smile upon her lips. The bag she handed me was, more precisely, one of those receptacles the English call a "carpet-bag" — its sides of fabric, its base of leather. Upon the sides a tiger was embroidered. The container of the mercury fulminate was of mahogany and had every appearance of a dressing-case. It stood on the mantelpiece among a few trinkets and a clock. Before stretching out her hand to it, the young lady cast Orsini's letter down upon the marble. She was perfectly calm; so much so that not even the faintest tremor disturbed her persistent smile when she held the box between her fingers. Either she possessed nerves of the most proven temper, or she was ignorant of the terrible power of the elegant article she was handing me. She stood and watched me in silence while I closed it again inside the bag; then all at once, smiling still: "You are going to Paris? A safe journey, and my compliments to Signor Orsini!"

When I returned home and Elisa came, with the child in her arms, to offer me her cheek for the customary kiss, I was obliged to make an effort to carry the bag with an air of unconcern. "Have you been shopping?" she asked. And she reached out her arm. But I said playfully: "Have a care! The tiger is alive and might scratch you!" Then, turning serious again: "These are papers of no interest to you." And I locked away in a cupboard the tiger capable of rending asunder, disguised beneath the other, embroidered and harmless one.

The day after, the 8th of January 1858, Simon Bernard appeared once more upon the threshold. He wore a grave, almost solemn expression, and I, who was now expecting definitive communications, understood at once that a few turns about the square would be most advisable. Nor had I been mistaken.

"You must depart," Bernard told me. "Orsini calculates — indeed he insists — that you set out tomorrow morning. In that case, tomorrow evening, between six and eight o'clock, you would arrive in Paris. He will be at the station... the rest will take care of itself. But in the event that you cannot depart in time, or that Orsini, by some unforeseen mishap, should fail to keep the appointment, take note of his address!" And he dictated it to me.

He left with a vigorous handshake and the assurance: "Au revoir, in a few days, in Paris."

That evening, on returning, I did not give Elisa the usual kiss, as though to prepare her for the parting. While she laid the table for supper, I sat to one side, nervously tormenting my goatee. She caught me with my eyes fixed upon her and, unable to restrain herself, threw her arms about my neck, murmuring softly in my ear: "Carlo, what ails you?"

Then I rose, stroked her hair, kissed her brow, and spoke to her the words that, on another occasion, in a very different moment, I had pressed upon her tender heart as a seal upon a solemn bond: "The conspirator must be solitary even within his family. He must sometimes forget that he has a mother and a wife and children. If his predestined hour arrives, he closes his ears to entreaties, he does not see the tears, and he departs... he departs without confiding his secrets even to the air he breathes. Elisa... that hour has come!"

She grew pale and, at first, found not a word, breathed not a sigh. But it was as though a certain key had been struck; it necessarily calls forth a certain note. She smiled sadly and, with a thread of voice that nonetheless revealed the imperious sovereignty of her spirit: "What can I say, Carlo? I know that you fight for a just cause; that there is nothing you can do of which I need be ashamed. Go, and come back soon — as honest as you are, and more glorious!"

I knelt down and covered her hands with kisses, as on the day we had pledged ourselves to each other.

So as not to prolong the painful hours, I had resolved to depart that very evening from the house, if not yet from London; I should be more alert and more composed in the morning, at the embarkation. We supped in silence; the fullness of our affections we exchanged through glances, through long caresses. At a certain moment I drew out my watch... She understood, and rising, hurried into our other small room. She returned carrying in her arms our firstborn — plump and rosy among his white linens.

He slept, and smiled... dreaming, perhaps, at his mother's full breast. I clasped Elisa's head to me with deep tenderness, whispered to her some counsel... One more kiss with trembling lips... then I fled!
