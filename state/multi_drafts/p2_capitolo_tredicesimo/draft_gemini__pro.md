Here, climbed step by step, is the ladder leading to the abyss that was to swallow both us and our every hope: Pieri's childish trust in Hartmann; the sea storm that prevented my meeting with Orsini at the Paris station; the connection established between the executors of the plot, contrary to the agreement; Pieri's failure to appear and his all-too-easy loquacity immediately after his arrest; Gomez's nerves. To this add (I shall speak of it later) the character of Felice Orsini, little or not at all cut out for the life of a conspirator, and the good faith of a young English lady, blind with love for him. If other causes were brought forth, they were invented by the malignity of men or by the fancy of novelists.

I was forgetting! There was another cause, secondary perhaps, but which must also be taken into account: the defection of Simon Bernard. He did not come to Paris. While others, for having tried to aid his France, were being hurried with great strides toward the scaffold, he gave a fine proof of his courage by strolling peacefully, prating of his own heroism, through the streets of London!

The very morning of my arrest, after a few hours of tormented sleep and as many of wakefulness, spent speculating on the revelations that Gomez might have made, I was taken to the Palazzo di Giustizia.

I had barely set foot in the antechamber and already I had to persuade myself of the ill wind that was blowing. Seated in a row, along the wall, almost as if displayed in a shop window, were four people of my acquaintance: The proprietress of the hotel "France et Champagne"; the Maitre d'Hotel; monsieur and madame Morand, the porters of Felice Orsini's house. All people summoned, without a doubt, for my identification. And, so far, no harm done. I had passed myself off as da Sylva and as da Sylva they would have named me. But I had denied all acquaintance with Felice Orsini: the Pipelet couple would contradict me... my denial was becoming untenable!

But the harshest blow came when I was in the judge's presence. He was the same personage who had interrogated me a few hours before. He received me smiling, as if seeing me again brought him immense pleasure. "Ah, ah! how are you this morning, *signor conte*? No longer da Sylva, this morning; but Carlo dei conti di Rudio!"

There was no longer any doubt: Antonio Gomez had let my name slip. I must say at once, in his defence, that both Pieri and Orsini had always called me, in his presence, either Moretto or di Rudio. He, in all probability, was unaware that I had a Portuguese passport.

I put a good face on a bad situation and replied, smiling no less: "Carlo di Rudio, precisely!"

He became serious: "The devil! A young man like you, getting mixed up in this nasty business. But did you not think of your family? Come now! You must redeem yourself. Tell me with manly frankness how you spent yesterday."

And I repeated, point for point, the little story told at the first interrogation. But, reflecting on the Pipelet couple, who were waiting in the antechamber, I admitted to having made a brief visit to Orsini. No mysterious purpose, moreover. We had conspired together in Switzerland and I had wished to see him again to revive old and dear memories.

— But why did you deny it last night?

— Simply so as not to compromise a man who, from what I could gather, was already in trouble. I believe that none of your Laws obliged me to fail in this duty of chivalry.

— And how is it that you have a false passport?

— As a most natural precaution for a political fugitive who might have Austrian spies at his heels. And how can he reproach me for it, who did the same when he landed at Boulogne with Luigi Napoleone?

The blow landed squarely; but the Judge contented himself with making a grimace. He resumed: "Now tell me everything you know about this plot."

— As much as the guards who arrested me have told me. I know that they wanted to kill the Emperor and nothing more.

— That may be. In any case, I must tell you that your friends have denounced you as their accomplice and that you will be tried with them!

And he gave the order that I be conducted to Mazas.
