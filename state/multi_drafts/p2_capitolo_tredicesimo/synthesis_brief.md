Ecco, percorsa a gradino a gradino, la scala conducente al baratro che doveva ingoiare e noi e ogni nostra speranza: La fanciullesca fiducia del Pieri nella Hartmann; la tempesta di mare che prevenne il mio abboccamento coll'Orsini alla stazione di Parigi; la relazione annodata tra gli esecutori dell'attentato, contrariamente all'intesa; il mancato intervento del Pieri e la sua troppo facile loquela immediatamente dopo l'arresto; i nervi del Gomez. Aggiungasi (ne parlerò più tardi) il carattere di Felice Orsini poco o punto tagliato alla vita del cospiratore e la buona fede di una signorina inglese, cieca d'amore per lui. Se altre cause furono messe in campo, vennero inventate dalla malignità degli uomini o dalla fantasia dei romanzieri.

Dimenticavo! Vi fu un'altra causa, secondaria forse, ma della quale bisogna pur tener calcolo: la defezione di Simon Bernard. Egli a Parigi non venne. Mentre altri, per aver tentato di giovare alla sua Francia, era sospinto a grandi passi verso il patibolo, egli dava bella prova del suo coraggio passeggiando tranquillamente, blaterando del proprio eroismo per le vie di Londra!

La mattina stessa del mio arresto, dopo poche ore di sonno tormentoso ed altrettante di veglia, spesa a fantasticare sulle rivelazioni che il Gomez poteva aver fatte, fui condotto al Palazzo di Giustizia.

Avevo appena messo il piede nell'anticamera e già dovevo persuadermi del vento pessimo che tirava. Sedute in fila, lungo il muro, quasi esposte in vetrina, stavano quattro persone di mia conoscenza: La padrona dell'albergo "France et Champagne"; il Maitre d'Hotel; monsieur e madame Morand i portinai della casa di Felice Orsini. Tutta gente chiamata, senz'alcun dubbio, per la mia identificazione. E, sin qui, nulla di male. Per da Sylva mi ero spacciato e per da Sylva mi avrebbero denunciato. Ma io avevo negato ogni conoscenza con Felice Orsini: i coniugi Pipelet mi avrebbero smentito... la mia denegazione diventava insostenibile!

Ma il colpo più rude mi venne quando fui in presenza del giudice. Era lo stesso personaggio che mi aveva interrogato poche ore prima. Mi accolse sorridendo, come se il rivedermi gli procurasse un immenso piacere. "Ah, ah! come sta, stamane, signor conte? Non più da Sylva, stamane; ma Carlo dei conti di Rudio!"

Non restava più alcun dubbio: Antonio Gomez si era lasciato sfuggire il mio nome. Debbo dire subito, a sua discolpa, che tanto il Pieri quanto l'Orsini, mi avevano sempre chiamato, in sua presenza, o Moretto o di Rudio. Egli, con tutta probabilità, ignorava che io avessi un passaporto portoghese.

Feci buon viso a cattivo giuoco e risposi, non meno sorridendo: "Carlo di Rudio, per l'appunto!"

Egli si fece serio: "Diamine! un giovanotto come voi, mischiarsi in questo affaraccio. Ma non avete pensato alla vostra famiglia? Via! bisogna redimersi. Raccontate con franchezza virile come avete passato la giornata d'ieri".

Ed io rifeci appuntino la storiella narrata al primo interrogatorio. Ma, riflettendo ai coniugi Pipelet, che aspettavano nell'anticamera, ammisi d'aver fatto una breve visita all'Orsini. Nessun scopo misterioso del resto. Avevamo cospirato assieme nella Svizzera e avevo desiderato rivederlo per ravvivare antiche e care memorie.

— Ma perchè la scorsa notte lo avete negato?

— Semplicemente per non compromettere un uomo che da quanto potevo arguire, era già nei pasticci. Credo che nessuna delle vostre Leggi mi imponesse di mancare a questo dovere di cavalleria.

— E come va che avete un passaporto falso?

— Per una precauzione naturalissima in un perseguitato politico che potrebbe avere le spie dell'Austria alle calcagna. E come può rimproverarmelo chi ha fatto altrettanto, quando sbarcò, a Boulogne, con Luigi Napoleone?

La botta era in pieno petto; ma il Giudice si accontentò di fare una smorfia. Egli riprese: "Ora raccontatemi tutto quanto sapete a proposito di questo attentato".

— Quel tanto che me ne hanno raccontato le guardie che mi arrestarono. So che si voleva uccidere l'Imperatore e nulla più.

— Potrebbe darsi. Ad ogni modo debbo dirvi che i vostri amici vi hanno denunciato come loro complice e che verrete processato con loro!

Ed impartì l'ordine che mi conducessero a Mazas.

## Chapter Thirteen

Here, traced step by step, is the staircase leading to the abyss that was to swallow up both us and every hope we harboured: Pieri's childlike trust in the Hartmann; the storm at sea that prevented my interview with Orsini at the Paris station; the association formed between the executors of the attempt, contrary to the understanding; Pieri's failure to act and his all-too-ready tongue immediately after his arrest; Gomez's nerves. To these must be added — I shall speak of it later — the character of Felice Orsini, little or not at all suited to the life of a conspirator, and the good faith of an English young lady, blind with love for him. If other causes have been put forward, they were invented by the malice of men or the fancy of novelists.

I had forgotten! There was yet another cause, secondary perhaps, but one that must nonetheless be taken into account: the desertion of Simon Bernard. He never came to Paris. While others, for having attempted to serve his France, were being driven by great strides toward the scaffold, he gave a fine proof of his courage by strolling tranquilly, babbling of his own heroism, through the streets of London!

On the very morning of my arrest, after a few hours of tormented sleep and as many of wakefulness spent in brooding over the revelations that Gomez might have made, I was conducted to the Palais de Justice.

I had barely set foot in the antechamber before I was obliged to persuade myself of the most ill wind that was blowing. Seated in a row along the wall, almost displayed in a shop window, stood four persons of my acquaintance: the landlady of the Hôtel France et Champagne; the *maître d'hôtel*; and monsieur and madame Morand, the concierges of Felice Orsini's house. All people summoned, beyond any doubt, for the purpose of identifying me. And so far, no great harm. I had passed myself off as da Sylva, and as da Sylva they would denounce me. But I had denied all acquaintance with Felice Orsini: the Pipelet couple would give the lie to that — my denial was becoming untenable!

Yet the heaviest blow came when I was brought before the magistrate. It was the same personage who had questioned me a few hours earlier. He received me with a smile, as though seeing me again afforded him immense pleasure. "Ah, ah! And how are we this morning, Signor Conte? No longer da Sylva this morning; but Carlo of the Counts di Rudio!"

No doubt remained: Antonio Gomez had let my name slip. I must say at once, in his defence, that both Pieri and Orsini had always called me, in his presence, either Moretto or di Rudio. He, in all probability, had no knowledge that I carried a Portuguese passport.

I put a good face on a bad business and answered, smiling no less than he: "Carlo di Rudio, precisely!"

He grew serious: "The devil! A young fellow like yourself, entangled in this wretched affair. Have you given no thought to your family? Come now! One must redeem oneself. Tell me frankly, like a man, how you passed yesterday."

And I went through once more, point by point, the little story I had told at the first interrogation. But, bearing in mind the Pipelet couple waiting in the antechamber, I admitted to having paid a brief visit to Orsini. With no mysterious purpose, for that matter. We had conspired together in Switzerland, and I had wished to see him again to revive old and cherished memories.

— But why did you deny it last night?

— Simply so as not to compromise a man who, from what I could gather, was already in difficulties. I believe that none of your Laws required me to fail in this duty of chivalry.

— And how does it come about that you carry a false passport?

— As a most natural precaution on the part of a political exile who might have Austria's spies at his heels. And how can it be held against me by one who did the very same thing when he landed at Boulogne with Louis Napoleon?

The thrust went full to the chest; but the magistrate contented himself with pulling a grimace. He resumed: "Now tell me everything you know concerning this attempt."

— Whatever the guards who arrested me have told of it. I know that the intention was to kill the Emperor, and nothing more.

— That may well be. In any case I must inform you that your friends have denounced you as their accomplice, and that you will be tried together with them!

And he gave the order that I be conducted to Mazas.

## Chapter the Twelfth

They made me pass through three rooms occupied, despite the hour, by clerks intent at their writing-desks. Having arrived in a fourth, I sat for a long while before a fireplace, upon which two brands burned crosswise. And since, the better to collect my thoughts, I had closed my eyes, I heard a guard say in an undertone: "A false alarm! Were he an accomplice, he would not sleep so soundly!"

At length they drew me into a great hall, whose brilliant light for a moment dazzled me. The first impression I distinctly perceived was that of the door closing behind me; then I saw take shape before me — I must needs lose myself in these particulars — a writing-desk strewn with papers, standing perfectly isolated in the middle of the hall. An old man, apparently in his sixties, of aspect still vigorous, with smooth skin full of bright reflections, was seated at one side of it, frowning over a document. At my appearance he spread it across his knees and raised his face. It was the Prefect of Police.

Another writing-desk stood some four paces off along the right-hand wall. Its supports were set so high as to allow me to discern, of the man seated behind it, only the crown of his skull and nothing more — the skull of the Examining Magistrate. Beside the desk, partly seated, partly standing, were various high dignitaries of the military, their chests laden with medals, and among those gaudy uniforms there darkened sadly several black beetles in civilian dress. Among the latter I recognised a personage of the imperial family — Plon-Plon! There were guards of various ranks and in various attitudes, and as I swept my eyes over those gaudy uniforms, now here, now there, with the intensity of one who peers into the abyss by which he might be swallowed, I saw stand out from the walls of red ground, and as though emerging from them at that very moment, several marble busts that fixed me with their pupilless eyes. And the men, too, fixed me without the flutter of an eyelid, as though they partook of the marmoreal nature of those busts. The Examining Magistrate rose and advanced towards me, and his countenance was not unknown to me. He had a history. In the days when Louis Napoleon was brought low, this man had trampled upon those very Laws of which he was, now, the salaried champion. He owed his position to the old services rendered to the Emperor. At a sign from him I sat down, and two guards who stood beside the door came and placed themselves upright at my sides.

The magistrate then asked me for my particulars, and I replied in accordance with the passport they had confiscated. But when he asked whether I knew my room-mate, and I had answered in the negative, his face grew merry.

"How can that be," he asked, "you sleep with an individual and do not even know who he is? Where did you first encounter him?"

"During my journey from Calais to Paris. It was the first time I had come to this city, and I asked him the address of an hotel. 'Come down to mine,' he replied. 'I have a room with two beds. He understands a little of my language. You will spend less.' I accepted. He told me his name, I told him mine. That is all! Why should I have called him to account for his past?"

"But what were you coming to do?"

"To earn my bread, selling Burton Ale."

"How did you pass yesterday?"

I had already committed my answer to memory, which, moreover, differed but little from the truth. Monsieur Pierey, as one well acquainted with Paris, had taken me in the morning to the Louvre Gallery. Later, Pierey himself had gone to pay a call on some acquaintance of his, and I had awaited him in a tavern. I then recounted the dinner taken at the restaurant in the rue Montmartre.

"At what hour did you dine?"

"Towards seven o'clock. We returned to our hotel. Scarcely arrived, the porter told Monsieur Pierey that a gentleman had come seeking him — he should wait, he would return shortly. And so indeed it fell out. That is all!"

"Who was that gentleman?"

"I do not know. He had come for the other, not for me. Moreover, perceiving that he wished to speak of business, I withdrew out of prudence."

"Where did you go?"

"To the cabinet at the end of the corridor. When I returned, the gentleman had departed."

"But do you not recall his appearance?"

"The room was in darkness. I remember, however, that he wore a full beard."

"Would you recognise him on seeing him?"

"Perhaps yes, perhaps no."

At this point, the magistrate turned to a guard and ordered him to show in "the other prisoner." When the door reopened, on the threshold appeared Felice Orsini. The bandage that swathed his head was soaked in blood. He had the air of a man weak and spent. His first glance was for me. He seemed to address me a mute question, and across his features I saw pass a fleeting unease.

"Is this the man?" the Magistrate asked me.

"The man who came to the hotel wore a full beard. I believe I said as much."

The magistrate then left his desk and, drawing close to Orsini, ran the tips of his fingers under his chin. He wished to assure himself that a razor had not recently passed there. Fortunately, the prisoner's skin must have felt, to his touch, bristling with points.

It was to Orsini that he put his next question. He spoke in English.

"From which part of England do you come?"

"From Kent."

"How far is Kent from London?"

"Thirty leagues."

Those two words sufficed. In England one speaks of miles, not of leagues. Moreover, Orsini had pronounced his "thirty" by converting the sound of the *th* into a *t*, and causing the *r* to ring out vigorously — which is not in the habit of English mouths.

Said the Magistrate: "You are as English as I am a Turk. Indeed, to tell you the full truth, you are not even French."

The pale face of Felice Orsini flashed. He drew himself up, with Roman disdain. He said — nay, he cried: "Ah no, by God! I am Italian and proud of it. My name is Felice Orsini. Ask me nothing further; I shall not answer you."

He had poured his very soul into his words — the soul of the noble and strong man who commands the pygmies of character. They were subdued by it and dared not oppose him. He was forthwith conducted elsewhere. But the Prefect of Police, who throughout the interrogation had remained standing and motionless beside his desk, now fell to rummaging feverishly in the drawers. "Ah, ah!" he said at length, advancing towards the uniformed and civilian personages alike. "Here is a secret circular from Austria. Felice Orsini — the dangerous revolutionary who escaped from the castle of Mantova. We have made a good catch!"

He then returned to his desk, and turning to me: "Monsieur da Sylva, do you know these implements?"

I looked, and it was as though a ray of light illuminated the cells of my brain. Now I understood why Napoleon lived and triumphed! Two of the "implements" which should, by their explosion, have given the signal for awakening to two Peoples, lay idle, like paperweights, upon the Prefect's desk. By what means had they arrived there? There they were — that was sufficient for me. Their dimensions were equal; on both, at the tips of the lower half, the deadly detonating caps still glittered. An inexplicable imprudence!

That sight sufficed to rouse, in my breast, the furies which the predominant thought of one's own preservation had, for a time, lulled to sleep. And my every fibre swelled again with turbulent spirit; my every fibre recovered an impulse of rebellion.

I thought: "Perhaps that which has not been obtained by direct means may be obtained indirectly, by bringing fresh confusion into the enemy's camp, undoubtedly already thrown into disorder — and why not? But in any case — to kill as a human protest, avenging the betrayed and the oppressed!"

I do not say, now, that my thought was that of a measured man; I say that such was my thought. And I say that even today I hold supremely human the cry: "Let Samson perish, and with him the Philistines!"

I rose and bent my body forward, half-closing my eyes, as though I were short-sighted. Two steps — only two steps without arousing suspicion — and I should have made a spring, a rapid movement, a thunderbolt — guards, Prefect, Magistrate, generals, myself — ambitions and hopes, servility and lust for power, crimes and virtues — a few shreds of flesh — nothingness —

A hand, heavy as fate, seized me by the shoulder, and I fell back into the chair. My heart was beating convulsively, but I made an effort and said in a composed and almost perplexed tone: "I do not understand — what is the matter?"

From that moment my interrogation proceeded rapidly and according to my wishes. In giving the final touches to the account of my actions, I took care to transpose, so to speak, a few minutes, and made my entry into the café coincide precisely with the hour of the attempt. I alluded also to the letter I had written there. The game was dangerous; but the proprietress had not seen me enter, and upon that I was counting. The Magistrate whispered a few words into a guard's ear, and the latter departed forthwith. I gathered from this that a verification was to be carried out. No great time passed before the same guard returned and fully confirmed my deposition, recounting even the incident of the four two-sou stamps which I had forgotten. The Magistrate then turned to the Prefect and said to him: "For my part I have no further question to put."

"Very well!" the latter observed, "but Monsieur da Sylva might prove a valuable witness. I would request him to remain at his hotel, at the disposal and expense of the State. His passport will be returned to him later. No objection?"

"None whatsoever — particularly if the State undertakes the expenses. My means are extremely limited!"

And for the third time that night, I heard the French Police lavish apologies upon me. The high personages, uniformed and otherwise, fell to conversing among themselves, glancing at me from time to time as though exchanging their impressions on my account. And the Magistrate, having wished me good night, turned to the guards at my sides. His last words were in Portuguese: "Monsieur da Sylva outside the Prefecture!"

As I was crossing the various rooms, my thoughts retraced my steps and I considered the extraordinary perspicacity of a Magistrate who let the wolf escape from the trap in such a fashion. It would have sufficed for him to have questioned me regarding my place of origin; to have asked me with which clients I had dealt in my capacity as seller of Burton Ale; to have addressed, or caused to be addressed to me, a single word in Portuguese — and my true nature would have been laid bare. From this I concluded that Magistrate and Police found themselves in a state of over-excitement, perhaps also of vague apprehension, which clouded their vision and halved their dynamic power.

Yet he who tonight — I thought — might be awake and on the alert tomorrow… Tomorrow? Let me but set foot out of doors again and shrewd is he who finds me! The Gare d'Orléans is watched; the same must hold of the other stations — but many roads lead out of Paris, and Paris is vast, and never has it lacked for friends and hiding-places.

I had reached the last room and there I found the unexpected. Through the door, which had opened to grant liberty to the wolf, there entered at that very moment, under close escort, Antonio Gomez. I looked away at once, the only means within my power of letting him understand that he must be ignorant of my presence. But his own wits were likewise clouded, and, passing close by me, he murmured in a low voice, in French: "We are lost!"

It was my fate that among his escort there should be an intelligent man. "Ah, ah! You know each other?" And Gomez, driving home the nail that was being hammered into me: "Of course we know each other!"

I had hastened to set foot upon the threshold. "Halt!" a voice thundered behind me. I was invited to sit down and the door closed. Gomez continued on his way to the great hall; I remained in the trap in great anxiety, biting my lips. Ten minutes passed. A clock whirred and struck four strokes. Other guards came. They took me by the arm, without opening their mouths, and led me away. We traversed an underground passage.

I passed the rest of the night in a cell of the Conciergerie!
