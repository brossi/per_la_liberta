SI trattava questa volta, d'impossessarsi dei piroscafi che solcano il Lario e di tentare la presa di Como. Al primo sentore dell'insurrezione di questa città, parecchie colonne armate sarebbero calate dai Grigioni, per sollevare la Valtellina, per dare forza ed ampiezza al moto.

La proposta di pigliarvi parte mi venne direttamente dal pavese Pompei, della Giovine Italia, sezione di Stradella... ma egli mi parlò a nome di Felice Orsini. Noto questo perchè l'Orsini, nelle sue "Memorie" indica Giuseppe Mazzini come capo effettivo e palese di questo tentativo; traendo pretesto dal numero esiguo delle persone che risposero alla chiamata, per dimostrare "la nessuna influenza" di quell'onesto, tra le file del partito che era più propriamente il suo.

Ad onor del vero, tanto io quanto il Fumagalli, abbiamo saputo della partecipazione diretta del Mazzini e della sua presenza nei Grigioni, solo qualche mese dopo, a impresa fallita.

Grande è la mia venerazione per la memoria di Felice Orsini; ma il movente della sua virulenta denuncia di questo e d'altri moti mazziniani, non mi è mai riuscito ben chiaro. Non li aveva egli, partecipandovi ed accettandone talora la parte direttiva, implicitamente approvati? E approvandolo, non ne aveva assunta la piena responsabilità? Ogni parola di biasimo rivolta dall'Orsini al Maestro mi sembra biasimo che ricade sul suo capo. Né mai, come quando lessi la sua autobiografia, ebbi a sospettare non sempre degni dell'eroe i bagliori di quell'animo ardente.

E dirò, incidentalmente, che le stesse frasi di riconoscimento rammentate e messe in ridicolo dal fiero Francesco, sono una variante di quelle da noi realmente pronunciate, secondo le ricevute istruzioni... sia che egli si lasciasse trasportare dall'enfasi della polemica, sia che la memoria gli facesse difetto.

L'uomo appostato sulla via Giulia a noi, che portavamo un fiore nel cappello, non disse: "Olà, galantuomini dove andate?" ma ci richiese se eravamo italiani. E la nostra risposta non fu, come afferma l'Orsini: "Andiamo dal signor Francesco, o dal signor Giuseppe"; ma semplicemente: "Conti". E quando l'altro ci strinse la mano, io soggiunsi, sempre secondo le ricevute istruzioni: "Dov'è Tito Gelsi?" (1)

In quali condizioni io e il Fumagalli giungemmo a San Maurizio, l'Orsini ha rilevato: "stanchi, affamati, laceri, vestiti da accattoni". Avrebbe potuto aggiungere che, ciò malgrado, non eravamo meno impazienti di menar le mani a prò del nostro Paese.

Comunque sia, la persona che ci interrogò ci condusse ad una locanda e ci lasciò soli. Passarono parecchi giorni d'inutile attesa. Benché avessi alquanto migliorato il mio aspetto, non potevo assumere l'aria di un grasso borghese venuto in quei paraggi per godermi le mie rendite oneste. Questa circostanza che poteva dar nell'occhio, mi era causa di qualche apprensione. Né l'abbandono in cui eravamo lasciati tendeva a rassicurarmi.

Imprese come la nostra, vengono segretamente tentate anche dai Governi interessati a suscitare qualche complicazione. E basti, a noi che viviamo in California, rammentare il generale Fremont.

Ma il Fremont doveva avere l'animo relativamente tranquillo; perchè sapeva che alle sue spalle, era una gran forza vigile e pronta ad entrare in azione. E, d'altra parte, l'esito felice del tentativo poco preoccupa quando poco rileva. L'importante era di offrire agli Stati Uniti un pretesto qualsiasi di intervenire: un nonnulla basta al lupo che si disseta nello stesso rivo dell'agnello.

Ben diversamente stavano le cose pei patrioti italiani costretti ad imperniare tutti i loro piani sul felice risultato di una prima, problematica sorpresa. L'esperienza dolorosa degli ultimi anni mi induceva a sospettare, nel nostro abbandono, gli scherzi feroci dell'imprevedibile: una bufera sfuggita ai calcoli, un guado temporaneamente impraticabile, una lettera sviata, un ordine male interpretato, la breve esitazione d'un congiurato, una parola imprudente, l'importuna curiosità di uno sfaccendato, l'abbaiare di un cane... mille cause minime, scaturite come dal nulla... e nulla alle spalle su cui ripiegarsi!

E le conseguenze dell'insuccesso? Il rimprovero dei timidi, lo sprezzo altezzoso degli uomini seri, il veleno dei codardi. Ogni biricchino può pigliarti a sassate, ogni santone della Legge sputarti in faccia, ogni spilorcio vendere la tua testa, ogni sgherro tirarti una fucilata nella schiena, per semplice libidine di strage. E l'odio dei moderni inquisitori che moltiplica i disagi della tua fuga, che trova il modo di farti sbattere ogni uscio sul viso, di levarti il pane di bocca, il guanciale di sotto il capo; che vorrebbe attanagliarti, stroncarti il collo col pretesto di ristabilire l'evo dalle facili digestioni; che, quando non riesce a sbarazzarsi del tuo corpo, ti assassina, colla calunnia, l'anima.

Con quanta intensità dovevano i precursori della terza Italia tenere l'occhio fisso al loro ideale, per preferire, al vivere riposato, la triste odissea del ribelle!...

Una sera il Fumagalli mi comparve dinanzi agitando per l'aria una lettera. Era di Tito Gelsi, che ci comunicava imminente il suo arrivo... ci capitò addosso proprio mentre leggevamo le sue brevi linee. Nel nostro colloquio Felice Orsini non fece alcun nome; si limitò, per quanto riguarda i suoi piani, ad assicurarci che poteva disporre di duemila fucili. Ci consegnò un biglietto di presentazione per un oste di Maloggia e, assicurandoci che avremmo presto ricevuto sue istruzioni, si congedò.

L'oste di Maloggia non era addentro nelle segrete cose, ma ne aveva qualche sentore e le favoriva. Egli ci informò che aspettava una cassa di "ferramenta" proveniente da St. Etienne... giunse e, con essa, le istruzioni di Tito Gelsi. Eran l'armi che avrebbero dovuto servire alla redenzione della Patria; l'Orsini ci indicava una località, non lontana dal ghiacciaio del Muretto, dove dovevamo metterle in assetto. L'oste provvide, sottomano, ai mezzi di trasporto.

La località scelta era propizia. Una rupe a picco da tre lati; una capanna da pastore abbandonata e nascosta fra cespugli e castagni. Di lassù, scorgevamo i giri e rigiri del sentiero che vi metteva capo. Nessun pericolo di sorpresa.

Eravamo alla metà dell'Agosto (1854) e approfittammo delle lunghe giornate per spingere il nostro lavoro alacremente. Sorgevamo dal giaciglio di erbe, al primo albeggiare, e, contemplata l'aurora, ci mettevamo all'opera. Montato un fucile, lo si spianava; se ne provava, con muta manovra, il gioco; poi veniva allineato rasente la parete contesta di fronde. Ve lo appoggiavamo con delicatezza, quasi fosse un gingillo.

In quei giorni commisi un'imprudenza, che, pur non avendo influenza alcuna sulla nostra impresa, ne ebbe una sensibile sulla mia navicella a cui fece mutare la rotta. Avevo finito di mettere in assetto il mio cinquantesimo fucile ed esaminandolo e palpandone le varie parti, mi parve così eccezionalmente perfetto, che mi sarei riputato per gonzo quando non me lo fossi appropriato. Usai il cacciavite a guisa di punteruolo e incisi sul calcio le mie iniziali. Il Fumagalli che mi osservava disse: "Che cosa fai?" Ed io, parafrasando una espressione del vecchio Domenico: "Un arnese come questo e rovino il fegato a mezza Boemia."

La calma della montagna ci riprese. Ma vennero, anche troppo presto, l'agitazione, il crollo.

Ero uscito alla vedetta ed avevo appena fatto visiera della mano, quando, in lontananza tra le macchie, scorsi una nuvola di polvere. La brezza, che spirava verso la montagna, la sospingeva a seconda dei viandanti che la sollevavano; ma essa non riusciva a nascondermi lo scintillio dell'armi. Erano militi ed in buon nerbo; né certo salivan l'erta a caccia di lepri.

Ebbi un gran da fare per persuadere il Fumagalli che bisognava cercare scampo su per l'altura. Egli voleva attendere a piè fermo e "vender cara la pelle"... Strisciammo tra gli arbusti e guadagnammo il monte.

Giunsero, attorniarono la capanna ed intimarono la resa, a fucili spianati... Mezz'ora dopo partivano a piccoli drappelli e si portavano i nostri fucili e le nostre speranze.

Calammo a Maloggia a notte fatta e l'oste, vedendoci, si strinse nelle spalle. Egli ci consigliò a rimanere ritirati e tranquilli nella sua locanda, finché non avesse ben fiutato il vento.

Passarono due giorni. Al terzo ci comparve dinanzi con aria dimessa e ci annunziò che Tito Gelsi era stato arrestato. Soggiunse: "Per quanto mi riguarda, niente paura! Voi potete disporre liberamente della mia locanda finchè c'è polenta e latte per me, ci sarà anche per voi. Ma se fossi nei vostri panni, preferirei piantar le tende ad un miglio e mezzo più a valle e precisamente laggiù, dove avviene il ricambio della diligenza. Le novità vi arrivano prima e se la faccenda si intorbida avrete, a portata di mano, il mezzo di svignarvela. D'altronde in quei paraggi vive un mio fratello a cui vi raccomanderò! Sarà come se foste ancora in famiglia."

Seguimmo il suo consiglio e, la sera stessa del nostro arrivo, trangugiata a stento una forchettata, udimmo il tintinnio della sonagliera e lo schioccare della frusta del postiglione. Usciti con altri curiosi, notai un individuo tutto intabarrato che, sceso allora dalla diligenza, mi squadrava dal capo ai piedi. Lo ricambiai di pari moneta ed egli, fattosi innanzi... il segnale! Trattomi in disparte, mi consegnò un rotolo ed un biglietto... il rotolo conteneva duecento in oro, il biglietto una sola parola: "Salvatevi!"

Con l'aiuto del fratello dell'oste di Maloggia, noleggiammo due cavalli da sella... lasciammo l'ordine di battere al nostro uscio alle tre del mattino; ma fui risvegliato bruscamente molto prima. Ai piedi del letto, disposti in semicerchio, erano parecchi monturati... le canne dei loro fucili convergevano al mio petto.

Fummo trasportati a Coirà e tradotti dinanzi ad un Commissario Federale, venuto appositamente da Berna. L'interrogatorio fu breve. Ci dichiarammo disertori. Non avevamo sentore alcuno di complotti politici e di armi clandestine. Il Commissario ci lasciò dire, poi fece un cenno. Gli portarono un fucile ed egli, sorridendo, me ne mise in evidenza il calcio. Non ebbi bisogno di aguzzare soverchiamente gli occhi. Con caratteri diventati, per una strana illusione, subitamente maggiori, vi risaltavano le mie tre iniziali "C. C. R." Lassù, presso il Muretto, senza saperlo, avevo inciso la mia e l'altrui condanna.

E fummo rinchiusi in domo petri. Il mio primo pensiero fu per papà e per la Luisa che si trovavano nelle carceri di Mantova; né mai ebbi di loro visione più netta, commiserazione più profonda.

Dirò subito che, a Coirà, i sistemi deliziosi dell'Austria erano ignoti. Prigionia, ma rispetto della disgrazia; osservanza dei regolamenti, ma regolamenti umani; qualche privazione, ma non quaresima di Galeazzo.

Il tormento maggiore era per me la forzata inazione... benché, o tanto, o quanto, conoscessi dove stesse di casa la pazienza. Non così il Fumagalli che, a quanto mi narrava il carceriere, girava e rigirava incessantemente nella sua cella, come una belva in gabbia.

Egli era rinchiuso al primo piano, io al terzo. Impossibile il vederci; ma approfittando dei brevi istanti che la sentinella era lontana, ci scambiavamo un saluto. Se la sentinella ci ammoniva; se il guardiano batteva l'uscio, retrocedevo e mi lasciavo andare sulla panca con una calma di cui io stesso mi meravigliavo; il Fumagalli invece riprincipiava rabbiosamente i suoi giri e rigiri. Aveva ai piedi grossi stivali da montagna, rafforzati da certi chiodi che né più massicci, né più laceranti li ostenta il Cristo confitto al legno, nelle nostre chiese di campagna. Il suolo, che era di mattoni, finì per riportarne scanalature da sembrare carreggiate e il guardiano, additandogliele ebbe ad osservargli, tra il serio ed il faceto: "Tu costi troppo alla Repubblica."

La nostra sorte dipendeva dalla decisione che avrebbero preso i signori di Berna. Ma prima che da quella capitale giungesse nuova pur che fosse, i prodromi dell'inverno già si facevano sentire.

Lungo il muro delle carceri, verdeggiava un orticello. Un grosso pero espandeva la sua ampia chioma quasi al livello del mio finestrino. Quando stormiva, la cella si riempiva di un sussurrio che il mio cervello tramutava in melodie indefinite e tristi. Spesso mi domandavo: "Chi può dirmi se sarò ancora qui dentro quando il villano, che coltiva l'orto, coglierà le pere?"

Una mattina del Novembre, messa la faccia all'inferriata, vidi tra le fronde, un uomo dal folto barbone che spiccava i frutti e ne riempiva un suo canestro appeso, col gancio, ad un ramo. Ci guardammo... il villano ciondola il braccio e me ne getta uno, due, tre, mezza dozzina. C'era nebbia, le foglie erano ingiallite. Ed io mi dissi: "Chi sa se sarò ancora qui dentro quando l'albero rimetterà i fiori?"

Proprio in quella, il guardiano spalancò l'uscio, e, agitando il mazzo delle chiavi e sorridendo, mi invitò a seguirlo.

Trovai nel corridoio Angelo Fumagalli, che batteva i piedi ferrati, come un puledro impaziente.

Fummo condotti a Berna, e, per la seconda volta, udii dalle Autorità Federali la predica sui sacrosanti doveri che incombono a chi riceve generosa ospitalità. Come recidivo, venni bandito a perpetuità dai confini della Repubblica. E mi ammonirono a non rimettervi più il piede, o avrei dovuto sottostare a punizioni gravi. Mi usarono la cortesia di leggere gli articoli del Codice che le specificavano.

Alla richiesta di scegliere la parte di Europa o d'America dove desideravo di essere accompagnato, risposi, come in altri tempi: "In Inghilterra".

E così finiva per me, per il Fumagalli e, credo, per un paio di altri partecipanti, quel tentativo che Felice Orsini chiama "piuttosto commedia che tragedia". Il quale Orsini fu pure arrestato, come già notai; ma fuggì dalle mani dei gendarmi e, dopo avere cercato momentaneamente riparo a Coirà ed a Zurigo e tentato, poi, di abbozzare qualche novità a Milano, partì per Vienna, ove intavolò pratiche dapprima per entrare, non so con quale scopo, nell'armata russa; poi, per fare propaganda di idee rivoluzionarie, in quella austriaca. Fallito l'uno e l'altro tentativo, passò in Ungheria ed arrestato ad Hermanstadt, nella Transilvania, venne rinviato a tappe dolorose, sino a Vienna e da Vienna, sino al castello di Mantova che l'inghiottì, come ebbi già occasione di rammentare.

Giuseppe Mazzini, Maurizio Quadrio, Nicola Ferrari e pochi altri, convenuti nei Grigioni per preparare le bande armate, erano riusciti, dopo varie peripezie, a mettersi in salvo.

Delle armi concentrate qua e là ai vari passi, credo che solo quelle affidate a me ed al Fumagalli cadessero in possesso delle Autorità Federali. Le quali, già all'erta dall'epoca del processo Clementi, avevano raddoppiata la propria vigilanza per avvisi venuti dalla Polizia del regno Lombardo-Veneto.

E poiché non si ebbero a lamentare vittime, potremmo anche accettare, senza rancore, la definizione di "commedia" uscita dalla mente irosa di Felice Orsini.

Ma a chi rifletta che, in quei giorni, si meditava, con stranissime alleanze, di modificare la carta d'Europa, quell'affermazione della persistente irrequietezza degli Italiani, quella nuova protesta contro la prepotenza che ci interdica di costituirci a Nazione, non sembrerà né ridicola, né inopportuna, né vana!

(1) È il nome assunto da Felice Orsini per deludere la Polizia Svizzera.

# Chapter the Twenty-Fourth

THE plan, on this occasion, was to seize the steamboats that ply Lake Como and to attempt the capture of the city itself. At the first signal of insurrection there, several armed columns were to descend from the Grisons to raise the Valtellina and lend force and breadth to the movement.

The proposal to take part in it came to me directly from Pompei of Pavia, of the *Giovine Italia*, Stradella section — though he spoke to me in the name of Felice Orsini. I note this because Orsini, in his *Memorie*, names Giuseppe Mazzini as the effective and acknowledged leader of this attempt, seizing upon the paltry number of those who answered the call to demonstrate "the negligible influence" of that honest man among the ranks of the party that was, in the strictest sense, his own.

In all fairness, both Fumagalli and I came to know of Mazzini's direct participation and his presence in the Grisons only some months after the enterprise had failed.

Great is my veneration for the memory of Felice Orsini; yet the motive behind his virulent denunciation of this and other Mazzinian ventures has never been altogether clear to me. Had he not implicitly approved them by taking part in them, and at times accepting a leading rôle? And in approving them, had he not assumed full responsibility for them? Every word of censure that Orsini levels at the Master seems to me censure that falls back upon his own head. And never, as when I read his autobiography, did I have occasion to suspect that the flashes of that ardent spirit were not always worthy of the hero.

I shall add, in passing, that the very phrases of recognition which the fierce Francesco recalls and holds up to ridicule are a variant of those we actually employed, according to the instructions we had received — whether he allowed himself to be carried away by the fervour of polemic, or whether his memory failed him.

The man posted on the Via Giulia did not say to us — as we walked wearing a flower in our hats — "Hola, good fellows, where are you bound?" He asked whether we were Italians. And our reply was not, as Orsini claims, "We are going to Signor Francesco, or to Signor Giuseppe," but simply: "Conti." And when the other man shook our hands, I added, still according to the instructions received: "Where is Tito Gelsi?" *(1)*

In what condition Fumagalli and I arrived at San Maurizio, Orsini has noted: "weary, famished, tattered, dressed like beggars." He might have added that, notwithstanding all that, we were no less impatient to put our hands to work for our Country.

Be that as it may, the man who had questioned us led us to an inn and left us alone. Several days of fruitless waiting went by. Although I had somewhat improved my appearance, I could not assume the air of a prosperous burgher who had come to those parts to enjoy his honest income in peace. This circumstance, which might attract attention, caused me some unease. Nor did the neglect in which we were left tend to reassure me.

Enterprises such as ours are sometimes secretly set in motion by governments with an interest in stirring up some complication. It is enough, for us who live in California, to recall General Frémont.

But Frémont must have had a comparatively easy mind, for he knew that at his back stood a great force, watchful and ready to spring into action. And, moreover, the happy outcome of the attempt matters little when little depends upon it. The important thing was to furnish the United States with some pretext for intervention: a trifle suffices the wolf who slakes his thirst in the same stream as the lamb.

Things stood very differently for the Italian patriots, who were compelled to hinge all their plans upon the fortunate outcome of a first, uncertain surprise. The bitter experience of recent years led me to suspect, in our abandonment, the cruel sport of the unforeseeable: a squall that had escaped all calculation, a ford momentarily impassable, a letter gone astray, an order misunderstood, the brief hesitation of a conspirator, an unguarded word, the meddlesome curiosity of an idler, the bark of a dog — a thousand petty causes sprung as if from nothing — and nothing at one's back to fall back upon!

And the consequences of failure? The reproach of the timid, the haughty contempt of grave men, the venom of cowards. Any rogue may stone you, any oracle of the Law spit in your face, any miser sell your head, any hireling shoot you in the back for the sheer lust of slaughter. And the hatred of the modern inquisitors that multiplies the hardships of your flight, that finds means to slam every door in your face, to take the bread from your mouth, the pillow from beneath your head; that would grip you with pincers, wring your neck on the pretext of restoring the age of easy digestions; that, when it cannot rid itself of your body, assassinates your soul with slander.

With what intensity must the forerunners of the third Italy have kept their eyes fixed upon their ideal, to prefer, over a life of ease, the bleak odyssey of the rebel!

One evening Fumagalli appeared before me brandishing a letter in the air. It was from Tito Gelsi, announcing that his arrival was imminent — and he fell upon us at that very moment as we were reading his brief lines. In our conversation, Felice Orsini mentioned no names; confining himself, in what concerned his plans, to assuring us that he could dispose of two thousand rifles. He gave us a letter of introduction to an innkeeper at Maloggia and, assuring us that we should shortly receive his instructions, took his leave.

The innkeeper at Maloggia was not privy to the secret affairs, yet had some inkling of them and looked upon them with favour. He informed us that he was expecting a crate of "hardware" from Saint-Étienne — it arrived, and with it, the instructions from Tito Gelsi. These were the arms that were to serve for the redemption of the Fatherland; Orsini directed us to a spot not far from the Muretto glacier where we were to put them in order. The innkeeper quietly furnished the means of transport.

The chosen place was favourable. A crag sheer on three sides; an abandoned shepherd's hut hidden among thickets and chestnut trees. From up there we could follow every winding of the path that led to it. No danger of surprise.

It was the middle of August, 1854, and we made the most of the long days to press on with our work briskly. We rose from our bed of straw at first light and, after contemplating the dawn, set ourselves to the task. Having assembled a rifle, one would level it, test the action with a silent motion, then stand it along the wall hung with branches. We leant them there with a kind of tenderness, as though they were delicate objects.

During those days I committed an imprudence which, though it had no bearing whatever on our enterprise, had a very marked bearing on my own small bark and caused it to change its course. I had just finished setting the fiftieth rifle in order and, as I examined it and ran my hands over its various parts, it seemed to me so exceptionally fine that I should have counted myself a fool not to make it my own. I used the screwdriver as a graving tool and carved my initials on the stock. Fumagalli, who was watching me, asked: "What are you doing?" And I, paraphrasing an expression of old Domenico's: "A tool like this, and I'll ruin the livers of half Bohemia."

The mountain calm took hold of us again. But there came, all too soon, the agitation, the collapse.

I had gone out on lookout and had barely shaded my eyes with my hand when, in the distance among the thickets, I caught sight of a cloud of dust. The breeze, which was blowing toward the mountain, drove it in the wake of those who were raising it; but it could not conceal from me the glinting of arms. They were militia men, and in good number; and they were certainly not climbing the slope in pursuit of hares.

I had a great deal of trouble persuading Fumagalli that we must seek safety by making for the heights. He wanted to stand his ground and "sell his skin dearly." We crawled through the undergrowth and gained the mountain.

They arrived, surrounded the hut, and called upon us to surrender, with rifles levelled. Half an hour later they were departing in small detachments, carrying off our rifles and our hopes.

We came down to Maloggia after nightfall, and the innkeeper, on seeing us, shrugged his shoulders. He advised us to remain quietly out of sight at his inn until he had sniffed the wind well enough.

Two days passed. On the third he appeared before us with a crestfallen look and announced that Tito Gelsi had been arrested. He added: "As far as I am concerned, no fear! You are free to make use of my inn for as long as you like — so long as there is polenta and milk for me, there will be for you as well. But if I were in your shoes, I should prefer to pitch my tent a mile and a half further down the valley, precisely there where the coach changes horses. The news reaches you sooner, and if matters grow cloudy you will have, close at hand, the means of slipping away. Besides, a brother of mine lives thereabouts, and I shall commend you to him. It will be as though you were still among family."

We followed his advice, and on the very evening of our arrival there, having barely forced down a forkful of food, we heard the jingling of the harness bells and the crack of the postilion's whip. Going out with the other curious bystanders, I noticed a figure all muffled in a cloak who had just stepped down from the coach and was eyeing me from head to foot. I returned the scrutiny in kind, and he, stepping forward — the signal! Drawing me aside, he handed me a roll and a note: the roll contained two hundred in gold, and the note a single word: "Save yourselves!"

With the help of the Maloggia innkeeper's brother, we hired two saddle-horses. We left word for someone to knock at our door at three in the morning; but I was roused abruptly long before that. At the foot of the bed, arranged in a semicircle, stood several uniformed men — the barrels of their rifles converging upon my chest.

We were conveyed to Chur and brought before a Federal Commissioner who had come expressly from Berne. The interrogation was brief. We declared ourselves deserters. We had no knowledge whatsoever of political conspiracies or of clandestine arms. The Commissioner let us speak; then he made a sign. They brought him a rifle, and he, with a smile, held the stock toward me. I had no need to strain my eyes overmuch. In characters which, by some strange illusion, seemed suddenly to have grown much larger, my three initials stood out clearly: "C. C. R." Up there, near the Muretto, without knowing it, I had carved my own condemnation and another's.

And we were shut up *in domo petri*. My first thought was of my father and of Luisa, who were in the prisons of Mantua; and never did I see them more vividly in my mind, nor feel for them a more profound compassion.

I should say at once that at Chur the delightful methods of Austria were unknown. Imprisonment, but with respect for misfortune; observance of regulations, but humane regulations; some privation, but not the Lent of Galeazzo.

As a recidivist, I was banished in perpetuity from the borders of the Republic. And they warned me not to set foot there again, or I should be made to undergo severe penalties. They did me the courtesy of reading the articles of the Code in which these were specified.

To the question of choosing the part of Europe or of America to which I wished to be conveyed, I replied, as on a previous occasion: "To England."

At the first inkling of this city's insurrection, several armed columns were to descend from the Grigioni to raise the Valtellina, to give strength and breadth to the movement.

The proposal to take part came to me directly from Pompei of Pavia, of the Giovine Italia, Stradella section... but he spoke to me in the name of Felice Orsini. I note this because Orsini, in his "Memoirs," indicates Giuseppe Mazzini as the effective and overt head of this attempt; taking as a pretext the small number of people who answered the call to demonstrate "the nil influence" of that honest man among the ranks of the party that was more properly his own.

In truth's honor, both Fumagalli and I learned of Mazzini's direct participation and his presence in the Grigioni only some months later, after the enterprise had failed.

Great is my veneration for the memory of Felice Orsini; but the motive for his virulent denunciation of this and other Mazzinian movements has never been quite clear to me. By participating in them and at times accepting the leading part, had he not implicitly approved them? And in approving them, had he not assumed full responsibility for them? Every word of blame directed by Orsini at the Master seems to me blame that falls back upon his own head. Nor ever, as when I read his autobiography, did I have cause to suspect that the flashes of that ardent soul were not always worthy of the hero.

And I will say, incidentally, that the very phrases of recognition recalled and ridiculed by the proud Francesco are a variant of those actually pronounced by us, according to the instructions received... whether he allowed himself to be carried away by the emphasis of the polemic, or whether his memory failed him.

The man posted on the via Giulia did not say to us, who wore a flower in our hats: "Ho there, good sirs, where are you going?" but asked us if we were Italians. And our response was not, as Orsini affirms: "We are going to Signor Francesco, or to Signor Giuseppe"; but simply: "Conti." And when the other shook our hand, I added, still according to the instructions received: "Where is Tito Gelsi?" (1)

The condition in which Fumagalli and I reached San Maurizio, Orsini has noted: "tired, hungry, ragged, dressed as beggars." He could have added that, in spite of this, we were no less impatient to raise our hands in aid of our Country.

Be that as it may, the person who questioned us led us to an inn and left us alone. Several days of useless waiting passed. Although I had somewhat improved my appearance, I could not assume the air of a fat bourgeois who had come to those parts to enjoy his honest income. This circumstance, which could attract attention, was a cause of some apprehension for me. Nor did the abandonment in which we were left tend to reassure me.

Enterprises like ours are also secretly attempted by Governments interested in stirring up some complication. And it is enough, for us who live in California, to recall General Fremont.

But Fremont must have had a relatively tranquil soul; for he knew that behind him was a great force, vigilant and ready to enter into action. And, on the other hand, the happy outcome of the attempt is of little concern when it matters little. The important thing was to offer the United States any pretext whatsoever to intervene: a trifle is enough for the wolf who quenches his thirst at the same stream as the lamb.

Things were very different for the Italian patriots, forced to hinge all their plans on the successful outcome of an initial, problematic surprise. The painful experience of recent years led me to suspect, in our abandonment, the ferocious jests of the unforeseeable: a storm that escaped calculations, a temporarily impassable ford, a letter gone astray, an order misinterpreted, the brief hesitation of a conspirator, an imprudent word, the inopportune curiosity of an idler, the barking of a dog... a thousand minimal causes, sprung as if from nothing... and nothing at our backs upon which to fall back!

And the consequences of failure? The reproach of the timid, the haughty contempt of serious men, the venom of cowards. Every rascal can throw stones at you, every holy man of the Law can spit in your face, every miser can sell your head, every thug can shoot you in the back, for the simple lust of slaughter. And the hatred of the modern inquisitors that multiplies the hardships of your flight, that finds a way to have every door slammed in your face, to take the bread from your mouth, the pillow from under your head; that would like to clamp you in tongs, to break your neck on the pretext of re-establishing the age of easy digestions; that, when it fails to be rid of your body, it assassinates your soul with calumny.

With what intensity must the precursors of the third Italy have kept their eyes fixed upon their ideal, to prefer, over a restful life, the sad odyssey of the rebel!...

## Chapter Twenty-Three

I PUSHED on as far as Zurich, where I found a nucleus of compatriots who had escaped, by way of exile, the reactionary storm that had followed the disturbances of February. Among them was Radonitz, who received me with great courtesy. He made not the slightest allusion to the incident at Locarno. I also paid a visit to Caronti. His words could not have been more flattering; but upon first seeing me, his face had assumed an expression of wonder not unmixed with a tinge of unease. Perhaps, I thought, it is my imagination. Giuseppe Mazzini had so thoroughly prejudiced me against him!

That dealer in trinkets, who had gone bankrupt at Como, was living in Zurich in great luxury, at the expense of a wealthy lady who manufactured chocolate. He had, however, contrived to set up a modest felt hat factory, and several Italians found there the means to earn their sustenance. I became one of their number, and it seemed to me a ray of fortune. But sniffing the air, I was not long in discovering something heterogeneous. There were handshakes that resolved themselves into a mere contact of skin; evasive replies; monosyllables, innocent enough in themselves, but which acquired a subtly treacherous flavour from the grimace of the face that accompanied them. In all, a coldness, a coldness! — even at the afternoon meal, when lips exchanged more spontaneous phrases, the mysterious threads that connect souls were wanting.

Of what were they finding fault with me? Perhaps of having come there about their bread and filching from it a small portion?

Things suddenly changed, and so radically as to make me suspect some understanding among them. But what understanding, and why? — The sharp edges were gone; they used the familiar *tu* with me; they clapped me confidentially on the shoulder, offered me tobacco — it seemed they had at last made up their minds to regard me, in every respect, as one of themselves. So much so that one Saturday, they proposed a nocturnal pleasure excursion on the lake. Four strokes of the oar, four tankards, eight perhaps, a patriotic chorus under the starlit sky. It would be like a reliving, beyond the Alps, of that fair land where our sweethearts languished.

So as not to seem discourteous, I accepted. It so chanced that, to remedy some forgotten errand or other, that very evening I returned to the factory. To my great surprise I found it lit up. As I drew nearer I heard distinctly the excited voice of the exile Orioli, saying: "By God, it does not suit me! I should never have the courage to think of my poor old mother again!" I entered, and it seemed as though an iron hand had gagged every mouth. *(1)*

From that day forward, no one spoke to me again either of the lake, or of the tankards, or of the starlit sky. The reason I never learned, nor did I trouble to investigate it. Nevertheless I had the certainty that the sudden cordiality of my companions was feigned; that I found myself, like an intruder, alone and hated in their midst. I ended by feeling thoroughly disgusted, and resolved to depart for Paris.

Without bidding farewell to mine hosts? Not entirely. Caronti, honest or dishonest as he might be, had procured work for me; I considered it my duty to pay him a farewell visit. I thus obtained a new surprise. Upon my arrival in Zurich, he had received me with cheerful and courteous words, but with a face that betrayed his disillusionment; now, having barely learned the reason for my visit, he poured forth a flood of phrases expressing regret; but his entire feeling could not keep itself compressed, and stamped his face with signs which I judged unmistakable. My departure was causing him intense pleasure.

I reached Paris in December of 1853 with two francs in my pocket and a letter of recommendation for a certain Zappa, a Turinese hatter, which had been given me by my foreman at the factory — the one person in Zurich who had shown me any sympathy. With the two francs I did not make much headway; but the letter served me well. Zappa, a man of liberal opinions and generous heart, immediately offered me his hospitality. I had to make do with sleeping on a mattress in the kitchen; but penniless and in that season, the offer was anything but contemptible. The more so as I found a way to repay the debt, by giving Italian lessons to the mistress of the house.

Signora Zappa was French. She had ideas that were more than liberal — they were revolutionary. These were sufficient to establish between us the most cordial relations. She was not young, she was not beautiful; but she preserved a grace whose charm it was not easy to escape. When she spoke of the 2nd of December, in which she had taken an active part, and of the fierce repressions from which, by good fortune, she knew how to extricate herself, she became eloquent, and in the fervour of her evocations forgot even the corsets she manufactured and to which, truth to tell, she owed her popularity rather more than to heroism.

In order not to live at the expense of those good people, I set myself to looking about, and ended by finding employment near the Pont de la Bastille. It earned me two francs a day. What employment? For fourteen unbroken hours I turned, by sheer force of arms, the wheel of a merry-go-round.

Having my hands to the wheel did not prevent me from keeping my eyes on politics. And in that epoch, even for one who was not in France, to occupy oneself with politics was to occupy oneself with Louis Napoleon.

The coup d'état, regarded with execration by uncorrupted consciences, blessed by the Pope, was greeted by the other Sovereigns as a great feat of prowess combined with enviable sagacity. The blood that was spilt was not of the kind deemed blue, and the stupefaction into which liberal France was thrown was, in their eyes, peace restored. And since Louis Napoleon was freeing the reactionary Governments from the spectre of the Republic — too often confused, and artfully so, with that of the Terror — it was the most natural thing in the world that the reactionary Governments should hasten to bend their gaze benevolently towards Louis Napoleon, to recognise his advent, and to expect great things of him.

After the coup d'état, the Dictatorship! Napoleon was too cunning to abuse it without counterposing to odious persecutions a series of measures calculated to seduce the multitude. France was overflowing with hands disposed to raise barricades. Unable to chain them, he provided, with shrewd haste, to win them over. By impulse sent out from Paris, every centre of France, great and small, was seized by the fever of loans and public improvements. And the wave of well-being that spread through the working classes received a sensible increase from the sanitary laws designed to improve their dwellings; from the reordering of taxes, which, lifted from articles of prime necessity, were laid upon luxury goods. To this one must add the generosity of the agricultural loans.

Throughout France, the cannon-shots had thus been succeeded by the blows of the hammer, and the moral effect which naturally sprang from the improved economic conditions was reinforced by the splendid promises that kept hope green and made that unexpected period of extraordinary prosperity seem likely to endure. Louis Napoleon, to hear him tell it, abhorred war. He proposed many conquests — but they were the conquests of Peace: lands to be reclaimed, vast tracts to be opened to cultivation, harbours to be deepened, canals to be dug, rivers to be made navigable. In these useful and glorious undertakings, the sword would yield primacy to the implements of labour, and every peasant, every workman animated by good intentions would become his faithful warrior.

Sentiments worthy of commendation! But in order to place faith in them, it was necessary to wipe from the mind every bitter memory; to lose sight of the fact that that fateful man spent half his day dissembling, with flattering phrases, the evil deeds he reserved for himself to commit in the other half.

Unfortunately, from the multitudes — constrained almost perpetually to consecrate every faculty to the scant satisfaction of the body's needs — it is not reasonable to expect a worship of the ideal that is far-seeing and tenacious. The popular soul does not press forward to its holy vindications save under the impulse of exceptional circumstances, and therefore by fits and starts, necessarily as tumultuous as they are fleeting. And it is a great deal if it does not then attempt to make up for lost time by defiling its magnanimous wrath with ferocious manifestations. Then the ineluctable need drives the herd back to graze — the soul defers its strivings toward the better to happier times, and falls asleep again.

The French people did not understand, or would not understand, that every new prince, risen to power having tasted crime and inclined to perpetuate it, does not venture upon it so long as, craftily practising virtue, he has not allowed the evil seed time to consolidate its roots. The laborious digestion stupefied thought; the rosy hues of the present prevented men from divining the threatening phantoms that were massing on the horizon. And when the servile Senate restored, in the person of Louis Napoleon and his male heirs, the imperial dignity, the multitude hastened to ratify it. Almost eight million Yeas — a meagre minority of two hundred and fifty-three thousand Nays.

On the 1st of December 1852, only a year after having stained his hands and soul in the blood of his fellow-citizens, Louis Napoleon donned, at Saint Cloud, the crown of France; and in assuming it, inscribed in History the name of Napoleon III.

The Empire reconstituted, the Constitution retouched, the various branches of Government assumed, in appearance, their regular function; in reality, France continued to be governed by Dictatorship — veiled as much as one might wish, but no less absolute.

The Prince Consort, who came from London in the spring of 1854 to cement the friendship that was taking shape between England and the new Empire, had occasion to express his own great wonder in this regard. Napoleon himself candidly confided to him that he never permitted his Ministers to assemble together to discuss public affairs. Each individual Minister dealt directly with him on any matter of importance, and what he had deliberated with one was rarely confided to another.

This wholly personal government, deemed pernicious even by the old sovereigns, led us liberals to frame the most sombre prognostications regarding France's future. The fixed idea upon which the politics of Napoleon III were to pivot became manifest at once — to emulate the great intellect that had drawn its house from nothing; that had impressed deep traces upon so great and so elect a portion of the world. He was embarking upon the arduous enterprise with unbridled ambition, a measureless mania for intrigue, an absolute want of faith to the given word and to treaties, a corruption fit to undermine quite other constitutions; the absence of every regenerating principle; a mediocre intelligence, barely so; his own presumptuousness; his great name.

How could the immense disproportion between the means and the object have failed to alarm us? The thought, the driving force of a great State, concentrated in that dark and ambiguous nature, were, in our eyes, the power consecrated to the accomplishment of evil, to propelling, with precipitate steps, a noble Nation toward disgrace and ruin. I must surely be permitted, after half a century, to avail myself of History in order to verify, with the indisputable wisdom of hindsight, how well founded our forebodings were.

Napoleon III had one flash of practical intelligence in allying himself with England. But in crowning his head with the imperial diadem, he had not blown from his soul his natural duplicity. He knew not how to show himself a sincere ally, and his gross machinations so thoroughly irritated the fine diplomacy of Britain as to find it hostile to him in his moments of supreme need. England would indeed have moved declared and open war against him, had the Germanic victories not forestalled her.

Napoleon III knew not how to transform the war with Russia into a glorious uprising of peoples yearning to crush the tyranny of the barbarians. It was reduced to a long and bloody siege, and when Sebastopol fell, it was because General Pelissier manfully refused — and not without pungent phrases — to submit to the orders that came from Paris.

The war for Italy! It is insisted that it was championed by him alone, forgetting the impulse with which the French people subscribed the loan raised to cover its expenses. There are two pitched battles in that war: Magenta and Solferino. The first was won almost in spite of the Emperor, through the disobedience of General Mac Mahon. In the second, the Napoleonic fibre had something of a posthumous gleam, and it was the only victory of the Second Empire owing to the personal merits of Napoleon III. But immediately afterward, his vulgarity regains the upper hand. He tore up, without warning, the articles of the alliance; insulted the King who had heroically fought at his side. And the essential object of the war — that of substituting his own influence in the Peninsula for the Austrian — was destined, thanks to the virile patriotism of the Italian party of action, to fail most miserably. Napoleon III, even as a conqueror, emerged from the bloody struggle irremediably defeated.

The expedition to Mexico, originating in financial speculations that could not be avowed, is an adventure worthy of a filibuster. It began with a betrayal analogous to that which smoothed the way to Rome for his Oudinot, and ended, as had the war for Italy, with the shameful tearing up of solemnly undertaken obligations. The fact that it was Maximilian, and not the Emperor, who fell a victim to Mexican lead, serves to demonstrate how little human or divine Justice governs the affairs of the world.

In the secret negotiations that preceded and followed the defeat of Denmark and the resounding victories of Prussia over Austria, Napoleon, playing a double game and simultaneously offering his alliance to both contending parties, aimed at obtaining a gratuity from the victor and at reclaiming for France the line of the Rhine. But he was disappointed and mocked and covered with ridicule by the sagacity of Bismarck.

What is to be said of the Franco-Prussian war, which he willed, which he undertook without the most elementary knowledge of the enemy's forces or his own?

I do not judge the greatness of men by their success, for I know defeats infinitely more glorious than any triumph. But for that to be so, the ruins must appear to us as irradiated and rendered sacred by the grandeur of the thought, the nobility of the sacrifice, the sublimity of the purpose. The life of the third Napoleon seems to me, from this point of view, arid as a desert.

It is understandable, perfectly understandable! No one could have outrun the times and discerned in the uncertain future the positive catastrophe. But already in the spring of 1854 there abounded tangible reasons that legitimised the mistrust — nay, the profound hatred — of liberals. Louis Napoleon, trampling underfoot the principles he had sworn on pain of death to defend, had, by his crimes, broken France and deprived her of every possibility of those democratic initiatives which are the greatest of her glories. He had shown himself most cowardly and ferocious precisely against the factions that had contributed most to drawing him out of the near-obscurity in which, for so long a time, he had lived. Gratitude weighs heavy upon vulgar souls. He was yet to render a stricter account of all this than those that are balanced upon a page of History — History, too often venal and mendacious, like the consciences of the men who write it.

For Italian liberals it was, in particular, a torment to contemplate the power of that man who had repaid their benefits and their hopes by championing the interests of the ancient enemy of the Fatherland; who had sacrificed the flower of Italian youth to dishonour Rome reborn; to thrust her back into the arms of the papacy; to prostitute her by main force to the most monstrous things that had survived from the barbarism of ages past.

In the spring of 1854, it was already considered more than probable — imminent — that Napoleon III would ally himself with the House of Savoy. And since it was known that the Empire did not desire the unity of Italy, and that Vittorio Emanuele II and his Ministers did not deem it possible, it was inferred that from such a union there would derive, at most, a territorial enlargement of Piedmont. Some French prince substituted for the petty tyrants who infested Italy; but the Pope at Rome, sustained by the imperial arms and subservient, by force of instinct and circumstance, more to the interests of the Empire than to the cause of Italian independence. History would renew its ancient phases; we should have upon our necks, along with the old masters, the new ones.

This train of ideas derived further strength from the marriage of Napoleon III to Eugenia Montijo, Countess of Teba — a Spanish lady of matchless beauty, restless, intelligent, astute, tenacious, and Catholic not by calculation but by deeply rooted convictions. Thanks to her influence, upon the throne of France there sat virtually, alongside Napoleon III, the priest.

The cause of Liberty could not have resumed its fatal course, either in France or in Italy, so long as Napoleon III stood interposed between it and the future. Therefore it was necessary to think of suppressing him — to hasten the return of things to their natural order; to spare, at slight sacrifice, a sequence of tumults and hatreds and rivers of blood.

I meditated long, took my notes, and wrote. And to whom could I write, if not to the most serene conscience I knew — to the man who had been able to surprise the heartbeats of the Fatherland and proclaim her living, and to foretell her greatness, when all believed her inexorably laid out in the tomb?

He was at that time in London. I communicated to him in minute detail a plan of mine — of which I shall have occasion to speak further on — requesting his assistance in carrying it out; disposed to go to the uttermost limit, or to draw back, as his counsel might dictate. Felice Orsini, who at that period was still on the closest terms of friendship with Mazzini, saw my letter, and to it I owe my future relations with the patriot of Meldola.

But neither to that first communication of mine, nor to two further ones that followed, did Giuseppe Mazzini reply. I recalled Pianciani, and in a fourth letter, addressed to him, I poured out all my bitterness at that silence — undoubtedly deliberate — which tasted to me of undeserved contempt.

The reply was not long in coming, and it was brief and terrible. "In London I was accused of having been the voluntary cause of the arrest of Fortunato Calvi and of the failure of the attempts on the Calore. The accusation came from Zurich."

So Austria, powerless to strangle me, had avenged herself for my escape — to isolate me, to annihilate me with calumny. And it was as though a veil had suddenly fallen from my eyes. Now I understood the conduct of my companions in Zurich; nor did it require any great penetration to interpret the secret motive behind the eccentricities of Caronti. The Austrian poison had been diffused by his lips.

I replied to Pianciani without losing a moment, and in my indignation I did not even measure the phrases that alluded to Mazzini — so quick, I observed, to lend his ear to the defamations of a man concerning whose honesty he himself, a few months earlier, had expressed to me the gravest suspicions. I concluded by requesting the means to go and confront my accuser.

Pianciani sent me one hundred and fifty francs, but accompanied by what was almost an injunction to keep quiet and to await my vindication from time. Having pocketed the money, I embraced the Zappa couple with great warmth, and turned my back on Paris. It was a splendid morning in May of 1854 when, suddenly, from the heights, I saw spreading at my feet the blue lake, and glittering in the first rays of the sun, the damp rooftops of old Zurich.

I had myself announced to Caronti, without revealing my name, as an exile who wished to communicate an important message to him. The anteroom was long, but our interview brief. He was still in his dressing-gown and received me standing upright, his two thumbs thrust into his waistband. As soon as he recognised me, he went pale and muttered between his teeth: "What has he come here to do?" — I shall tell you in a moment! — I shut the door, and stepping forward so that our very breath was almost mingled, I satisfied his curiosity to the full, reinforcing my words with a demand for satisfaction. "And mark well: I require it, and I shall have it before I leave this room!"

He peered furtively out of the wide-open window and attempted to gain time, protesting his innocence. On the contrary, he had always entertained for me an unconditional esteem, an almost brotherly affection. Speak ill of anyone? He would have preferred his tongue to fall from his mouth in shreds!

— Very well! But words are carried off by the wind, and writing remains. Set down in black and white your unconditional esteem; furnish me with a formal retraction. That and nothing else will satisfy me!

As I spoke, his lips trembled. I believe he understood, from my expression, that this was no time for evasion. He went to the writing desk, wiped the honest sweat from his brow, and wrote. Then, without turning round, he extended his arm and held out the sheet to me.

I read it, and contented myself with saying: Very well!

Thereupon, as though we were old friends vying with one another in civilities, he fell over himself to offer me his services — in whatever part of the world, at whatever time. I had only to express the wish. If moreover I was making for Italy, and thought a brief halt at Lugano advisable, he would furnish me with a letter of introduction for Count Grillanzoni, who had settled there.

He was evidently seeking to reconnoitre the ground in order to prepare some fresh villainy for me. I cut off his phrases at the mouth. What I would do I did not know; in any case it concerned me alone. Turning the key, I went out.

In the corridor I felt a hand laid upon my shoulder. And a voice whispered in my ear: "How goes it, Moretto?"

It was Angelo Fumagalli, pale and smiling. He put a finger across his lips and added: "Come to the stables. We can talk at our ease!" He took my right hand and, with gentle violence, dragged me along as though I were a child. "I know everything, I heard everything! I saw you cross the garden and recognised you at once. I said to myself: There is something fresh afoot here. What, though? I had some ugly suspicions, and — why hide it from you? — I did a rather shabby thing: I put my ear to the door. But better so! There will be no more misunderstandings. Do you know that that scoundrel even accused you of having stolen a watch from his coachman? That one I never believed. As for the rest — you must forgive us. What would you have? We fell into the trap like a pack of imbeciles. And you had a narrow escape, let me tell you! Do you remember that plan for a nocturnal excursion on the lake? A plot had been hatched to stun you with a blow of a cudgel on the head and to throw you, hands and feet bound, as food for the fishes! Caronti would then have denounced you all, and at one stroke freed Austria of an entire hornets' nest. The very devil! Fortunately Grioli had sense enough for everyone and saved you. God bless him! We thought we were avenging the Fatherland!"

He embraced me and was sobbing. Then, precisely as the other had done, but with different intent: "And now what do you mean to do?"

— My dear fellow, I have tried everything within my power to do my duty, and I have found myself suspected by the very men who should have defended me. One road alone remains to me: to persist, to answer the accusations with silence and with action.

I left Zurich with the intention of taking refuge in Piedmont, and made my halt at Lugano and my visit to Count Grillanzoni. He received me coolly — I understood, and held out to him the retraction signed by Caronti. He thereupon showed me in turn a letter received a few days before. In it that same worthy was urging him to do everything in his power to prevent my return. My irascible character, he said, was the cause of continual vexations and scandals which, for the good name of our emigration and in the interest of the cause "so dear to us all," it was imperative to avoid.

I renounced Piedmont and returned immediately to Zurich. In the meantime Angelo Fumagalli had, with indignant manner, taken his leave of Caronti. From him I learned that Felice Orsini had been making enquiries to discover my address.

A few days later I was engaged in the attempt at Maloggia.

*(1)* This Orioli was the brother of the canon who was martyred at Mantova. When, after the *Risorgimento*, Francesco Giuseppe went to Venice, a Regiment was detailed to form his guard of honour. Orioli, who belonged to it, found his soul revolted by the ties of blood and preferred to be brought before a Council of Discipline rather than pay homage to the gaoler of our Fatherland. And there were those who, for that act of noble pride, called him a fool!
