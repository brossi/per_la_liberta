IT was a matter, this time, of taking possession of the steamships that ply the Lario and of attempting the capture of Como. At the first inkling of this city's insurrection, several armed columns were to descend from the Grigioni to raise the Valtellina, to give strength and breadth to the movement.

The proposal to take part came to me directly from Pompei of Pavia, of the Giovine Italia, Stradella section... but he spoke to me in the name of Felice Orsini. I note this because Orsini, in his "Memoirs," indicates Giuseppe Mazzini as the effective and overt head of this attempt; taking as a pretext the small number of people who answered the call to demonstrate "the nil influence" of that honest man among the ranks of the party that was more properly his own.

In truth's honor, both Fumagalli and I learned of Mazzini's direct participation and his presence in the Grigioni only some months later, after the enterprise had failed.

Great is my veneration for the memory of Felice Orsini; but the motive for his virulent denunciation of this and other Mazzinian movements has never been quite clear to me. By participating in them and at times accepting the leading part, had he not implicitly approved them? And in approving them, had he not assumed full responsibility for them? Every word of blame directed by Orsini at the Master seems to me blame that falls back upon his own head. Nor ever, as when I read his autobiography, did I have cause to suspect that the flashes of that ardent soul were not always worthy of the hero.

And I will say, incidentally, that the very phrases of recognition recalled and ridiculed by the proud Francesco are a variant of those actually pronounced by us, according to the instructions received... whether he allowed himself to be carried away by the emphasis of the polemic, or whether his memory failed him.

The man posted on the via Giulia did not say to us, who wore a flower in our hats: "Ho there, good sirs, where are you going?" but asked us if we were Italians. And our response was not, as Orsini affirms: "We are going to Signor Francesco, or to Signor Giuseppe"; but simply: "Conti." And when the other shook our hand, I added, still according to the instructions received: "Where is Tito Gelsi?" (1)

The condition in which Fumagalli and I reached San Maurizio, Orsini has noted: "tired, hungry, ragged, dressed as beggars." He could have added that, in spite of this, we were no less impatient to raise our hands in aid of our Country.

Be that as it may, the person who questioned us led us to an inn and left us alone. Several days of useless waiting passed. Although I had somewhat improved my appearance, I could not assume the air of a fat bourgeois who had come to those parts to enjoy his honest income. This circumstance, which could attract attention, was a cause of some apprehension for me. Nor did the abandonment in which we were left tend to reassure me.

Enterprises like ours are also secretly attempted by Governments interested in stirring up some complication. And it is enough, for us who live in California, to recall General Fremont.

But Fremont must have had a relatively tranquil soul; for he knew that behind him was a great force, vigilant and ready to enter into action. And, on the other hand, the happy outcome of the attempt is of little concern when it matters little. The important thing was to offer the United States any pretext whatsoever to intervene: a trifle is enough for the wolf who quenches his thirst at the same stream as the lamb.

Things were very different for the Italian patriots, forced to hinge all their plans on the successful outcome of an initial, problematic surprise. The painful experience of recent years led me to suspect, in our abandonment, the ferocious jests of the unforeseeable: a storm that escaped calculations, a temporarily impassable ford, a letter gone astray, an order misinterpreted, the brief hesitation of a conspirator, an imprudent word, the inopportune curiosity of an idler, the barking of a dog... a thousand minimal causes, sprung as if from nothing... and nothing at our backs upon which to fall back!

And the consequences of failure? The reproach of the timid, the haughty contempt of serious men, the venom of cowards. Every rascal can throw stones at you, every holy man of the Law can spit in your face, every miser can sell your head, every thug can shoot you in the back, for the simple lust of slaughter. And the hatred of the modern inquisitors that multiplies the hardships of your flight, that finds a way to have every door slammed in your face, to take the bread from your mouth, the pillow from under your head; that would like to clamp you in tongs, to break your neck on the pretext of re-establishing the age of easy digestions; that, when it fails to be rid of your body, it assassinates your soul with calumny.

With what intensity must the precursors of the third Italy have kept their eyes fixed upon their ideal, to prefer, over a restful life, the sad odyssey of the rebel!...

One evening Fumagalli appeared before me, waving a letter in the air. It was from Tito Gelsi, informing us of his imminent arrival... he descended upon us just as we were reading his brief lines. In our conversation Felice Orsini named no names; he limited himself, as regards his plans, to assuring us that he could dispose of two thousand rifles. He gave us a note of introduction for an innkeeper in Maloggia and, assuring us that we would soon receive his instructions, he took his leave.

The innkeeper of Maloggia was not privy to the secret affairs, but he had some inkling of them and favored them. He informed us that he was expecting a case of "hardware" coming from St. Etienne... it arrived and, with it, the instructions of Tito Gelsi. They were the arms that were to have served for the redemption of the Fatherland; Orsini indicated to us a place, not far from the Muretto glacier, where we were to assemble them. The innkeeper provided, underhandedly, the means of transport.

The chosen location was propitious. A cliff sheer on three sides; an abandoned shepherd's hut hidden among bushes and chestnut trees. From up there, we could discern the twists and turns of the path that led to it. No danger of surprise.

It was the middle of August (1854) and we took advantage of the long days to push our work forward with alacrity. We would rise from our pallet of grass at the first light of dawn, and having contemplated the dawn, we set to work. Once a rifle was assembled, it was aimed; its action was tested with a silent manoeuvre; then it was lined up against the wall interwoven with fronds. We leaned it there with delicacy, as if it were a trinket.

In those days I committed an imprudence, which, though having no influence on our enterprise, had a perceptible one on my own little vessel, causing it to change its course. I had finished assembling my fiftieth rifle and, examining it and feeling its various parts, it seemed so exceptionally perfect that I would have considered myself a fool had I not appropriated it for myself. I used the screwdriver in the manner of an awl and engraved my initials on the stock. Fumagalli, who was observing me, said: "What are you doing?" And I, paraphrasing an expression of old Domenico: "A tool like this and I'll ruin the livers of half of Bohemia."

The calm of the mountain reclaimed us. But then came, all too soon, the agitation, the collapse.

I had gone out to keep watch and had just made a visor of my hand when, in the distance among the thickets, I spied a cloud of dust. The breeze, which blew toward the mountain, pushed it along with the travelers who raised it; but it could not hide from me the glint of arms. They were soldiers, and in good number; nor were they certainly climbing the slope to hunt for hares.

I had a great deal of trouble persuading Fumagalli that we had to seek escape up the heights. He wanted to wait steadfastly and "sell his skin dearly"... We crept through the shrubs and gained the mountain.

They arrived, surrounded the hut, and demanded our surrender, with rifles aimed... Half an hour later they departed in small detachments, carrying away our rifles and our hopes.

We descended to Maloggia after dark and the innkeeper, seeing us, shrugged his shoulders. He advised us to remain withdrawn and quiet in his inn, until he had well scented the wind.

Two days passed. On the third he appeared before us with a downcast air and announced that Tito Gelsi had been arrested. He added: "As for me, have no fear! You may freely dispose of my inn; as long as there is polenta and milk for me, there will be for you as well. But if I were in your shoes, I would prefer to pitch my tents a mile and a half further down the valley, precisely down there, where the stagecoach changes horses. The news arrives there sooner, and if things get murky, you will have, close at hand, the means to slip away. Besides, a brother of mine lives in those parts, to whom I shall recommend you! It will be as if you were still in the family."

We followed his advice and, on the very evening of our arrival, having swallowed a forkful with difficulty, we heard the tinkling of the sleigh-bell and the crack of the postilion's whip. Having gone out with other curious folk, I noticed a heavily cloaked individual who, having just alighted from the stagecoach, was sizing me up from head to foot. I returned his stare in kind and he, coming forward... the signal! Drawing me aside, he handed me a roll and a note... the roll contained two hundred in gold, the note a single word: "Save yourselves!"

With the help of the brother of the innkeeper of Maloggia, we hired two saddle-horses... we left orders to be knocked up at our door at three in the morning; but I was awakened abruptly much earlier. At the foot of the bed, arranged in a semicircle, were several men in uniform... the barrels of their rifles converged on my chest.

We were transported to Coirà and brought before a Federal Commissioner, who had come expressly from Berna. The interrogation was brief. We declared ourselves deserters. We had no inkling of any political plots or clandestine arms. The Commissioner let us speak, then made a gesture. They brought him a rifle and he, smiling, showed me its stock. I had no need to strain my eyes overmuch. In letters that had become, by a strange illusion, suddenly larger, my three initials "C. C. R." stood out. Up there, near the Muretto, without knowing it, I had engraved my own condemnation and that of others.

And we were shut up in a house of stone. My first thought was for papa and for Luisa, who were in the prisons of Mantova; nor did I ever have a clearer vision of them, or a more profound commiseration.

I will say at once that, in Coirà, the delightful systems of Austria were unknown. Imprisonment, but respect for misfortune; observance of regulations, but humane regulations; some privation, but no Lenten fast of Galeazzo.

The greatest torment for me was the forced inaction... though I knew, more or less, where patience made its home. Not so Fumagalli who, as the jailer told me, paced incessantly in his cell, like a wild beast in a cage.

He was locked up on the first floor, I on the third. It was impossible to see each other; but taking advantage of the brief moments when the sentry was away, we exchanged a greeting. If the sentry admonished us, if the guard knocked on the door, I would step back and let myself fall onto the bench with a calm that astonished me myself; Fumagalli, instead, would furiously begin his pacing once more. He had on his feet great mountain boots, reinforced with nails more massive and more lacerating than those displayed on the Christ fixed to the wood in our country churches. The floor, which was of brick, ended up bearing grooves that looked like cart-ruts, and the guard, pointing them out to him, observed, half in jest and half in earnest: "You are costing the Republic too much."

Our fate depended on the decision that the gentlemen of Berna would take. But before any news whatsoever arrived from that capital, the first signs of winter were already making themselves felt.

Along the prison wall, a small vegetable garden grew green. A large pear tree spread its ample foliage almost to the level of my little window. When it rustled, the cell would fill with a whisper that my brain transformed into indefinite and sad melodies. I often asked myself: "Who can tell me if I will still be in here when the peasant who tends the garden gathers the pears?"

One November morning, having put my face to the bars, I saw among the fronds a man with a thick beard who was picking the fruits and filling a basket of his, hung by a hook from a branch. We looked at each other... the peasant swings his arm and throws me one, two, three, half a dozen of them. There was fog, the leaves had yellowed. And I said to myself: "Who knows if I will still be in here when the tree blossoms again?"

Just then, the guard threw open the door, and, jingling his bunch of keys and smiling, invited me to follow him.

I found Angelo Fumagalli in the corridor, stamping his iron-shod feet like an impatient colt.

We were taken to Berna, and, for the second time, I heard from the Federal Authorities the sermon on the sacrosanct duties incumbent upon one who receives generous hospitality. As a recidivist, I was banished in perpetuity from the confines of the Republic. And they admonished me never to set foot in it again, or I would have to be subject to grave punishments. They did me the courtesy of reading the articles of the Code which specified them.

To the request to choose the part of Europe or America where I wished to be accompanied, I answered, as in other times: "To England."

And thus ended for me, for Fumagalli and, I believe, for a couple of other participants, that attempt which Felice Orsini calls "more comedy than tragedy." Which Orsini was also arrested, as I have already noted; but he escaped from the hands of the gendarmes and, after having sought momentary refuge in Coirà and in Zurich and then attempted to sketch out some new enterprise in Milano, he left for Vienna, where he first entered into dealings to join, I know not with what aim, the Russian army; then, to spread revolutionary ideas in the Austrian one. Both attempts having failed, he passed into Hungary and, arrested at Hermanstadt, in Transylvania, he was sent back, by painful stages, to Vienna, and from Vienna, to the castle of Mantova which swallowed him up, as I have already had occasion to recall.

Giuseppe Mazzini, Maurizio Quadrio, Nicola Ferrari and a few others, who had gathered in the Grigioni to prepare the armed bands, had succeeded, after various vicissitudes, in reaching safety.

Of the arms concentrated here and there at the various passes, I believe that only those entrusted to me and Fumagalli fell into the possession of the Federal Authorities. Who, already on alert since the time of the Clementi trial, had redoubled their vigilance due to warnings from the Police of the Lombardo-Veneto kingdom.

And since there were no victims to lament, we could even accept, without rancor, the definition of "comedy" that came from the wrathful mind of Felice Orsini.

But to one who reflects that, in those days, it was being contemplated, with the strangest of alliances, to modify the map of Europe, that affirmation of the persistent restlessness of the Italians, that new protest against the arrogance that forbids us from constituting ourselves as a Nation, will seem neither ridiculous, nor inopportune, nor in vain!
