THE SIEGE of Rome is indissolubly linked to the name of a man baleful to Liberty: that of Louis Napoleon. The Carbonari, who had welcomed him into their fold, regarded him with suspicion as early as 1831; for, at that time, the rumour had spread that he had done away with his brother (Napoleone Luigi); who, having been born four years earlier, barred his path to sublime heights.

Our revolutionary movements were then opening, or so it seemed, new horizons for Italy, and the two sons of Hortense, by participating in those movements, sought in the Peninsula a propitious field for their ambition; a compensation, perchance, for the loss of the French throne, warmed for the moment by Louis Philippe. This line of thought lent credence to the dark rumour that, for the rest, could never be thoroughly investigated.

The majority of historians who treat of the period simply affirm that Napoleone Luigi died of measles. Others go so far as to admit that the matter is not entirely certain; but vaguely suggest that, if a crime was perpetrated, it must be attributed to the fanatics of some *"Vendita"* (a Carbonari lodge).

Be that as it may, this diffidence was attenuated by time and by the prince's humanitarian posturing. On the other hand, the subversives well understood the immense advantage they could derive from a man whose name was an entire programme of war against the old order, and who could not have emerged from his semi-obscurity except on condition of assuming the entire responsibility for that programme.

His hopes of making his fortune in Italy having vanished, Louis Napoleon turned his eyes towards France. He first attempted the *coup de main* at Strasburgo, which failed principally for his lack of resolve; then that of Boulogne, which led him straight to the prisons of the fort of Ham.

The subversives of France and Italy then intrigued to oppose the violent suppression of that political entity. Princess Cristina Belgioioso Triulzio, who was privy to the affairs of Young Italy, was induced to use her influence at the Court of Louis Philippe to obtain a meeting with the prisoner.

It is known that the substantial part of that meeting did not require a long preamble. The gentlewoman offered the aid of the liberals of France and Italy to carry out an escape and to propel the man to float upon the stormy political sea. Once come to power, he would pay the debt of gratitude by working for the promulgation of democratic laws and by championing our independence.

Louis Napoleon's hesitation induced the illustrious Italian lady to grant him two months to reflect. In the meantime, so as not to be found unprepared for events, his friends would see to organizing a plan of escape.

The two months passed and a second meeting took place. The prisoner let her speak; then he expressed his profound sympathy for our Nation; but concluded by declaring that, for the moment, he did not feel disposed to act... whether because he distrusted the comely and most cultivated gentlewoman, or because he hoped for aid from parties with ideas more analogous to his own instincts and his own secret aims. The plan seemed abandoned when, like a thunderbolt, the news spread through our circles that Napoleon, suddenly changing his mind, had given his consent to it; and, shortly after, just as swift as lightning, came the news of his disguise and his flight, from France to Belgium, from Belgium to England.

In London, and precisely at the Brunswick Hotel, he saw the Belgioioso again and sealed, so to speak, the pact: "He would help Italy, as soon as the affairs of France were settled."

Events precipitated... Louis Philippe was driven from the throne and Napoleon asked permission to return to his country. To sway the parliamentary opposition, Louis Blanc arose and, with a single speech, changed the course of French history as much as ten battles might have done. Napoleon became a candidate for the National Assembly, for the Department of the Seine, and triumphantly set foot on French soil.

A sadly famous epoch!.. The parties contending for predominance in the country flew at one another's throats and unleashed upon Paris the hurricane of blood that preludes every great political change. Cavaignac emerged grimly from the carnage and aspired to the Presidency of the Republic, a post contested by the vanquished who desperately opposed him with the candidacy of Ledru Rollin. The resultant between those two currents, foreseen and facilitated by our secret societies, led to the victory of a third candidacy: that of Louis Napoleon. On the 20th of December 1848, he became the Supreme Magistrate of the glorious Nation.

What was his first noteworthy act?

That of rewarding his friends by striving, with arms and with treason, to re-establish in the heart of our Peninsula the most feeble, the most baleful, the most hated of Governments... the misgovernment of the Pope!

The diabolical intent was so well dissembled as to induce the Italians to facilitate the landing of General Oudinot's troops, which had appeared suddenly in the waters of Civitavecchia. They welcomed them with enthusiastic, fraternal demonstrations.

In France, suspicions arose more promptly. Fierce protests over the suspected annihilation of a sister Republic, to the advantage of the element hostile to all liberty, spread from the halls of Parliament down among the multitudes, assuming, here and there, the proportions of veritable riots. And perhaps the energy of the people would have ended by imposing honest intentions, when the news spread of the reverse suffered on the 30th of April by the arms of the invader.

A tremor of grim patriotism ran from one end of France to the other. "It was necessary to restore the prestige of the flag; to vindicate, at all costs, the national honour." As if the shame of a People derived not from the crime, but from its failed perpetration! Louis Napoleon thus had, unexpectedly, as a willing accomplice in his dark aims—strange to say!—the most generous, the most loyal of the entities of old Europe!

The French, the soldiery of King Bomba, the Spaniards, the Austrians, and the subtle arts of the priests!... how could Rome have escaped utter ruin?

One man alone, Giuseppe Mazzini, foresaw in that tragic moment that, from death itself, by virtue of heroism and martyrdom, right could be renewed and life could spring forth. He endeavoured to transfuse his faith into all those who surrounded him, and the intellectuals and the humble were as if transfigured by it. The "brigands" who defended Rome wrote, in letters of blood, the purest, the most fruitful of the pages of our modern history.
