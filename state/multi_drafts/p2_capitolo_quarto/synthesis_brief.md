LA parola al Conte di Rudio: L'anno stesso che mi accasai fui coinvolto in una malaugurata faccenda che fece molto chiasso. Gli esuli che non avevano dovizie di baiocchi, frequentavano certa trattoria di Rupert Street, ove per poco prezzo potevano assaporare qualche piatto cucinato all'usanza nostra. Più di una volta vi ero capitato anch'io e vi avevo stretto conoscenza con un Rossi mantovano, giovanotto che si acquistò presto le mie simpatie per il suo carattere espansivo ed allegro. Il Rossi era soverchiamente corrivo in fatto di relazioni amorose, ma altri vizi in lui non seppi riscontrare e quando il romagnolo Foschini mi avvertì, in tutta confidenza, di stare alla larga da quel "brutto ceffo" perchè era una spia del Governo francese, io gli osservai che il prestare leggermente fede alle calunnie insinuate, con tutta probabilità, per fini loschi, dai nemici del nostro partito, era un prestarsi puerilmente al loro giuoco.

Il Foschini, imbronciato, mi voltò le spalle ed io... strinsi col Rossi maggiore intimità... un pochino anche per l'istinto, in me prepotente, di voler veder chiaro, sino al fondo delle cose. Ebbi a convincermi che il Foschini era realmente in errore. Tale convinzione, a dir vero, non è oggigiorno tanto radicata. Un Rossi, come seppi vari anni dopo, era stato sinistramente implicato nelle congiure di Mantova. Tuttavia il cognome Rossi è molto comune nell'Alta Italia ed io non ebbi mai il modo di accertare se la mia conoscenza di Londra e la spia del Governo austriaco siano l'identica persona. Per chi si interessi d'indagare queste minuzie, dirò che il Rossi di

Londra aveva un fratello prete... circostanza che potrebbe servire di guida a stabilire la verità!

Una sera, ad ora già inoltrata, mi trovavo nell'osteria di via Rupert in compagnia del "brutto ceffo". C'era anche il Foschini, il quale sedeva tutto solo, in disparte; ma dall'occhiatacce che ci aveva rivolte e da certi monosillabi biascicati, avevo potuto arguire che si correva il rischio di navigare in acque torbide.

Gli avventori diradarono e rimanemmo quasi soli. Ad un certo punto, il Foschini si alzò e, passando stecchito d'accanto al Rossi, lo urtò nel gomito e gli disse: "Spia!"

Il Rossi reagì con un complimento peggiore e il Foschini, frustato sul viso, si volse inviperito. Credetti mio dovere l'intervenire e raccomandavo di non far scandali, per non compromettere la nostra immigrazione, quando il romagnolo, tratto un pugnale, diede un balzo. Un secondo... il Rossi sarebbe spacciato! Per prevenire il delitto, afferro una sedia e fo del mio meglio per calarla sull'aggressore. Sfortunatamente colpisco la lampada appesa al soffitto; essa va in frantumi e restiamo al buio. Rammento la sensazione della lama fredda che mi penetrava nel petto, poi... più nulla!

Quando riebbi un barlume di coscienza, compresi d'essere in un ospedale. Non riuscivo a tenere gli occhi aperti; ma udii una voce, quella del medico, sentenziare che non avrei visto il sole della domane. Mi fecero ingoiare non so che preparato e quel breve sforzo bastò perchè smarrissi un'altra volta i sensi.

Ma la mia giovinezza si riaffermò e, malgrado la fosca profezia, la mia prima impressione fu appunto quella dei raggi del sole che mi battevano sul viso. Essi suscitarono in me una volontà ferma d'aggrapparmi alla vita.

Seppi che il dottore, ritenendo il caso disperato, si era appigliato, per arrestare l'emorragia interna, ad un suo rimedio eroico. Considerava l'esito come un portento e, per una lunga sequela di giorni, condusse al mio capezzale ora questo, ora quel collega, intavolando discussioni probabilmente di gran valore scientifico. A me, profano e di nervi irritabili, giungevano sommamente importune.

Prima che ricevessi la visita regolamentare di un Ispettore di Polizia, il dottore mi informò che il Foschini aveva fatto due altre vittime. Ma il Rossi, il solo indubbiamente che egli voleva levare di mezzo, n'era uscito senza una graffiatura.

Sul Foschini, che si era reso latitante, le Autorità misero una taglia di duecento sterline. Ma egli era buon patriota e, benché il suo nascondiglio fosse noto, nessuno fiatò. Anzi, nel lodevole tentativo di coprirlo, si esagerò grandemente il movente politico che l'aveva sospinto ad agire, senza badare troppo pel sottile se, per salvare la riputazione del colpevole, si menomava quella degli innocenti.

Ora si noti la stranezza del destino che in Europa prima, in America poi, ho affrontato pericoli gravissimi e benché fossi più volte ad un capello dal lasciarvi l'ossa, ne riportai sempre la pelle intatta. Era riserbato ai miei compagni di fede il retribuire il mio disinteresse con trentasei pugnalate, due delle quali penetrate in cavità e giudicate mortali!

Il Foschini chiuse splendidamente i suoi giorni combattendo al fianco di Carlo Pisacane, nella sfortunata, gloriosa spedizione di Sapri.

Sapri fu la prima manifestazione grave del nuovo cambiamento di tattica adottato da Giuseppe Mazzini. Terminata la guerra di Crimea, il grande Cavour, malgrado la splendida prova dei nostri soldati e la loro vittoria della Cernaia, nulla ottenne pel suo Re... neppure la sperata annessione di Parma e di Modena, i cui principotti si volevano accollare alla Moldavia ed alla Valacchia. Ma Napoleone III aveva promesso che qualche giorno avrebbe fatto qualche cosa anche pel Piemonte e il Mazzini tenne per certa, sin da quei giorni, l'alleanza dell'Impero con Vittorio Emanuele II. Era però fermamente convinto che da essa non sarebbe derivato altro se non che un ingrandimento degli Stati Sardi o, nella migliore delle ipotesi, una federazione di Stati italiani retti, in buona parte, da principi francesi. Per prevenire tanta iattura, egli abbandonò il vecchio campo d'azione e si adoperò, coi suoi più fidi, per sollevare il regno delle due Sicilie. Contrapporre all'agitazione monarchica del Settentrione, l'insurrezione dell'estremità meridionale d'Italia, imporre da Palermo e da Napoli l'unità della Nazione. Il geniale concetto valse a Giuseppe Mazzini e a Garibaldi una condanna a "morte ignominiosa", ma esso doveva riuscire tra i massimi fattori della nostra futura grandezza.

Lasciato completamente in disparte, probabilmente perché si riputava fossi rammollito dai nuovi affetti, io già mi rassegnavo alla lunga inazione, quando improvvisamente la chiamata mi giunse d'onde meno me l'aspettavo.

THE Count di Rudio speaks: The very year I settled down I was drawn into an ill-starred affair that made a great deal of noise. The exiles who had no abundance of coin used to frequent a certain eating-house in Rupert Street, where for little outlay they could savour some dish prepared after our own fashion. I myself had gone there more than once and had struck up an acquaintance with one Rossi, a Mantuan, a young fellow who soon won my sympathies by his open and cheerful character. Rossi was excessively hasty in the matter of amorous relations, but no other vices could I discover in him; and when the Romagnol Foschini warned me, in strictest confidence, to keep clear of that "ugly customer" because he was a spy in the pay of the French Government, I observed to him that to lend easy credence to calumnies insinuated, in all likelihood for base ends, by the enemies of our party, was to lend oneself most childishly to their game.

Foschini, nettled, turned his back on me, and I... drew closer to Rossi... in part, also, from that instinct, so imperious in me, of wanting to see clearly, to the very bottom of things. I came to be convinced that Foschini was genuinely mistaken. That conviction, to tell the truth, is not so firmly rooted today. A certain Rossi, as I learned several years later, had been sinisterly implicated in the conspiracies at Mantua. Nevertheless the surname Rossi is exceedingly common in Northern Italy, and I never had the means of ascertaining whether my London acquaintance and the spy of the Austrian Government were one and the same person. For anyone interested in probing these minutiae, I will say that the Rossi of

London had a brother who was a priest — a circumstance which might serve as a guide to establishing the truth.

One evening, at a late hour, I found myself in the tavern in Rupert Street in the company of the "ugly customer." Foschini was there too, sitting entirely alone, apart from the rest; but from the black looks he had cast at us and from certain syllables muttered between his teeth, I had been able to gather that there was a risk of sailing in troubled waters.

The other customers thinned out and we were left almost alone. At a certain point, Foschini rose and, passing stiffly beside Rossi, jostled him at the elbow and said: "Spy!"

Rossi retorted with a worse compliment, and Foschini, stung to the face, turned furious. I thought it my duty to intervene, and was urging them to make no scandal, so as not to compromise our whole emigrant community, when the Romagnol, having drawn a dagger, made a lunge. One second more... and Rossi would have been finished! To forestall the deed, I seize a chair and do my best to bring it down upon the assailant. Unfortunately I strike the lamp hanging from the ceiling; it shatters and we are left in darkness. I remember the sensation of the cold blade entering my chest, then... nothing more!

When a glimmer of consciousness returned to me, I understood that I was in a hospital. I could not keep my eyes open; but I heard a voice, the physician's, pronouncing that I should not see the morrow's sun. They made me swallow some preparation or other, and that brief effort was enough for me to lose my senses once again.

But my youth reasserted itself and, in spite of the gloomy prophecy, my first impression was precisely that of the sun's rays beating upon my face. They stirred in me a firm will to cling to life.

I learned that the doctor, considering the case desperate, had resorted, in order to arrest the internal haemorrhage, to a heroic remedy of his own. He regarded the outcome as a wonder, and for a long succession of days brought to my bedside now one colleague, now another, entering upon discussions of presumably great scientific value. To me, a layman and a man of irritable nerves, they were supremely inopportune.

Before I received the regulation visit of a Police Inspector, the doctor informed me that Foschini had made two other victims. But Rossi, the one undeniable target he had wished to remove, had come out of it without a scratch.

Upon Foschini, who had gone into hiding, the Authorities placed a reward of two hundred pounds. But he was a good patriot, and though his hiding-place was known, no one breathed a word. Indeed, in the laudable attempt to shield him, the political motive which had driven him to act was greatly exaggerated, with scant regard for whether, in order to save the culprit's reputation, the reputations of innocent men were being diminished.

Now let one mark the strangeness of destiny: that in Europe first, then in America, I faced the gravest dangers, and though I was more than once within a hair's breadth of leaving my bones there, I always came away with my skin intact. It was reserved for my companions in faith to repay my disinterestedness with six-and-thirty dagger-thrusts, two of which penetrated into cavities and were adjudged mortal!

Foschini closed his days splendidly, fighting at the side of Carlo Pisacane in the ill-fated, glorious expedition of Sapri.

Sapri was the first serious manifestation of the new change of tactics adopted by Giuseppe Mazzini. The Crimean War being ended, the great Cavour, in spite of the splendid showing of our soldiers and their victory at the Cernaia, had obtained nothing for his King — not even the hoped-for annexation of Parma and of Modena, whose petty princes they wished to foist upon Moldavia and Wallachia. But Napoleon III had promised that one day he would do something also for Piedmont, and Mazzini held for certain, even from those days, the alliance of the Empire with Vittorio Emanuele II. He was, however, firmly convinced that from it nothing would derive save an enlargement of the Sardinian States, or at best, a federation of Italian States governed, for the greater part, by French princes. To forestall such a calamity, he abandoned the old field of action and laboured, with his most trusted followers, to raise the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies in revolt. To set against the monarchical agitation of the North the insurrection of the southernmost extremity of Italy, to impose from Palermo and Naples the unity of the Nation — such was the design. The brilliant conception earned for Giuseppe Mazzini and for Garibaldi a sentence of "ignominious death," but it was destined to prove one of the greatest factors in our future greatness.

Left entirely to one side — presumably because I was thought to have been softened by new affections — I was already resigning myself to a long inaction, when suddenly the call came to me from whence I least expected it.

## Chapter the Third

LOVE!... for Miss Sara or for Miss Elisa? The problem, however simple it might appear, stirred within him a singular dismay, followed by a no less singular irritation. “Why torment oneself to resolve it,” he concluded, “when the settling down into marriage is denied me by other ideals?” The Count di Rudio was acting unconsciously, and in a different arena, much as a man acts before new ideas when prejudice has taken root in his brain. Such a man intuits the reasonableness of those ideas, yet — so it is! — he obstinately closes his eyes to the truth, and the very certainty that his idol is crumbling makes him a yet more strenuous champion of old fables and falsehood.

And now — here is how the Count di Rudio’s long drought of purse came at last to an end.

From the day on which the coup d’état had triumphed definitively, Victor Hugo, having taken the road of exile, had settled on the island of Jersey. There he published *L’Homme*, a periodical whose pages blazed forth, with all the intensity lent by genius, the condemnation of the baleful imperial politics. The hero of the Second of December, keenly sensitive to the pricks of criticism, as every criminal who aspires to glory must be, contrived to lodge his complaint adroitly with the Cabinet in London, which, with equal adroitness, fended it off. But when the complications of European politics pointed out to England, in Napoleon III, the sovereign best suited to lend her a strong hand in the meditated enterprise of breaking the Czar’s pride, to humour the whims of that man presented itself as an act of wise diplomacy. And the freedom of thought and the traditional broad hospitality of the English people were, at least in part, curtailed and sacrificed to the true, or supposed, interests of the Nation.

Victor Hugo was surrounded by various brilliant minds, to whose company Pianciani now attached himself. That nest of intellectuals was suddenly thrown into confusion. And the thing seemed such a sacrilege that even the French reactionaries dared not applaud.

Pianciani resolved to repair once again to London. Before leaving the island, he dispatched, together with his other papers, the manuscript of his *Storia dei Papi*, insured for ten thousand pounds sterling. The papers arrived at their destination; the manuscript vanished. And rumour ran that the Jesuits had taken possession of it, most probably to cast it into the flames. A certain colour of truth was lent to this rumour by the fact that the Insurance Company disbursed the considerable indemnity immediately, without initiating the least inquiry, without uttering a single word.

Pianciani and the Count di Rudio met and conferred, and that Roman gentleman, who by his expansive warmth and his generous impulses made men forgive the disfigurement of his face — smallpox had blunted his nose — received the gentleman from Belluno as an equal among equals. At parting, and almost by the way, the conversation fell upon the latter’s financial straits.

“You could not have come to me at a better moment!” said Pianciani. “I have set myself to attempt the *Storia dei Papi* anew. Will you be my amanuensis?”

The man accustomed to the boldness, the labours, the perils of action was confined to an armchair, and made a virtue of what he had always accounted a fatal defect — patience. In recompense, he was brought into contact with those men luminous in genius, in character, in sufferings borne with virile fortitude who frequented the house of Pianciani: rebels, persecuted, slandered, without roof, without bread, unvanquished — great in their loves, their hatreds, their utopias. Noble and proud company!

Most of them had one fixed idea that seemed almost to stamp them. They endeavoured to convince, to draw the young amanuensis into their orbit.

Pianciani discerned in the troubles massing in the East the probable salvation of Italy. Liberolle, the historian, dreamed of the union of the Latin peoples, but under the predominance of France. Victor Hugo could not make his peace with the foolishness of his Fatherland, dazzled by the equivocal splendour of the Court of Napoleon the Little. He had lost all faith in the virtue of a People insensible to great ideals, ready to shoot and to be shot for the lying enchantment of fatuous phrases. And his every outburst closed invariably with the bitter sentence: “The people is a great child that wishes to be deceived!”

And amid those modern figures there passed, now mild and smiling, now flashing with sinister light, the phantoms of History. Nineteen centuries of glories and of squalors went filing across those brief pages, renewing in the Count’s heart the shuddering, the grief, the wrath of the generations that sleep as dust.
Yet in his mind, grown as though swollen with that vast entanglement, there arose, from hour to hour, two simple and smiling figures that stirred him more profoundly than genius; that stripped emperors and pontiffs of every ornament and thrust them back into their vulgarity. They appeared to him constantly adorned with roses, the fair flower that is the emblem of his family, and a sense of serenity pervaded and abided with him: a sense that tempered the stench of Vatican orgies; that stripped the horror from the maledictions which, from every corner of Italy, from every corner of Europe, had ever and always been raised against the brigands who trafficked in their Fatherland, in humanity, in Christ.

The Count di Rudio no longer debated them; they had become as part of himself. Not that his ideas were, in this regard, clear and well defined. Love, not forgetful of its accustomed arts, still dissembled its own vigour behind the shelter of other affections more anciently rooted and in full flower. But he was persuaded, by now, of having found upon his path the beautiful, the good, the chaste, wondrously made flesh. To behold them, to drink in their radiance, to be elevated — to feel himself less unhappy! He surrendered to the wholly novel sensation without the least misgiving. When the revolutionary tempest seized him again, the festival of feeling would be brusquely cut short; but he would carry into the greater trials a richer treasury of dear remembrances — noble affections lend wings to reach the arduous heights!

And he yearned, with an ever more intense anxiety, for the day when, having received the reward of his labours, he might present himself with head held high before Signora Mancherini, pay his debts like a man of honour, resume the course of the interrupted lessons; drink again from that font of the beautiful, the good, the chaste, drawing his two young friends ever more deeply into the cult of martyrs and heroes!

The day came. With what light a step he covered the long road, with what eagerness he mounted the stair! Mamma Mancherini stepped forward and intercepted the threshold with her ample and flourishing person. Whereupon he, extending his right hand without delay — and the sterling — poured forth his apologies for the involuntary delay. But the lady looked at him with a suspicious air. The two young girls came running in, and the Count di Rudio learned, to his great astonishment, that the rent had been paid at the end of every month. Miss Elisa brought up once more the mysterious stranger, and Sara affirmed that she had never delivered the proper receipts to the Signor Conte, because the Signor Conte had not let himself be seen for a great while. But the portly Mancherini appeared not altogether satisfied, and went off with a frown, muttering: “Oh, what a muddle, what a muddle!”
In those days the Crimean War was dragging on, limping forward. From the very first manifestation of the troubles that led to that war, Giuseppe Mazzini had proposed making an appeal to the unitary sentiment of Germany; of seconding the national aspirations of Italy and Hungary; of sending a breath of new life through the Balkans; of forming, to put it briefly, around the Muscovite Empire, a great rampart of free States that would have compressed and paralysed Russia.

This conception was not displeasing to the Cabinet of Turin. But Napoleon III and England were aiming to provoke no hostility from Austria, and indeed to induce her to participate actively in their alliance. And it was precisely because Austria would never have resolved to enter the campaign so long as the terrible hornets’ nest of Italy hung over her neck, that the co-operation of the House of Savoy was accepted with good grace, was in fact solicited. The Piedmontese army was the only strongly organised nucleus; the only one deemed capable of precipitating events in our Peninsula.

Austria would not be moved and thus spared the Italians the humiliation of seeing the heroes of our independence fight side by side with the Croatian hordes, to maintain upon the European Continent “a sorry projection of Asia.”

And the meditated great uprising of peoples was reduced, in practice, to a simple, long, and tedious siege. England, shaken in her military prestige, short of soldiers, resolved to open enlistments, to organise some foreign legions.

No sooner was the appropriate decree promulgated than di Rudio found Fumagalli and Leoni presented before him. The latter had decided to depart for the Crimea and had come to pay a farewell visit. “And if it be for the last time,” he said, pressing the Count’s hand, “then for the last time — farewell!”

They went to pass the few remaining hours before departure at the *Public House* of a certain Shaw, wife of a fellow-countryman of theirs. It was precisely the gathering-place of the recruits. The setting, with a parade-ground elegance, was already exceptionally animated — a confused animation, full of starts and bursts of laughter. Military men and civilians, in clusters, were slapping one another on the shoulder, exchanging nicknames and hearty, good-natured epithets. In secluded corners, a few gentle figures in bonnets, with pale and tear-stained faces. Flowers and green sprigs on every breast; bluish spirals of cigarette smoke from almost every mouth.
But the noise and confusion did not suit di Rudio’s temper; the more so because the sight of so many Italians on the point of shedding their blood for a cause he held foreign to the Fatherland put him in mind of the woeful days of the *soldati di ventura* — soldiers of fortune. And when Leoni, half in earnest, half in jest, said to him: “Come now, Moretto! Make up your mind and embark with us!” he answered, with excessive bluntness: “What harm have the Russians done you, that you should offer your youth as a holocaust, with the intention of swelling their number of widows and orphans?”

And Leoni: “Italian colours are flying in the Crimea!”

— Any flag is a mockery when it does not stand as a symbol of truth and justice!

The conversation was taking a bad turn. Fortunately a Milanese conscript, picking up the thread of a previous discussion, expressed his regret at not having thought to bring with him *I Sepolcri* by Ugo Foscolo, which would have contributed so greatly to shortening the hours of the crossing.

— I have a copy at home, di Rudio told him. — Accept it. It is an inexpensive edition, with the margins full of scribbles, but they do no harm to the substance.

In four bounds he was at Baldwin’s Garden. He mounted the stairs and entered his room, leaving the door ajar. He was already turning the chest of drawers upside down when, from the other side of the corridor, there reached him distinctly the sounds of moaning and sobbing. What could this mean?

Going to the threshold, he found himself face to face with Miss Sara, who, flushing briefly and without waiting to be questioned: “Elisa is weeping because she is persuaded that you are leaving tonight for the Crimea. Love is consuming her. And she has also compromised herself in Mamma’s eyes. It was that business of the receipts — do you remember? Mamma has drawn everything from her heart, and the least that awaits her now is a thorough scolding. The mysterious stranger is there, in despair. It was Elisa who provided out of her own savings — for everything, everything!”

The Count di Rudio gripped the great poet, turned him over and over in his hands, then thrust him furiously into the bottom of his pocket: “Tell Miss Elisa that the di Rudios are not soldiers of fortune. For Liberty — always! Mercenaries of foreign despots — never!”
And he descended the stairs at a run. But the tempest that Miss Sara’s words had roused followed him into the street; entered, through the crack of the door, into Mrs. Shaw’s *Public House*, and swirled about his head the whole evening — so much so that the talk and the literature and the politics and the noise and the chaotic scene of the embarkation and the farewells all reached his brain not as vivid and clearly defined reality, but almost as a pale evocation of images emerging confusedly from among the mists of times gone by.

A second surprise awaited him. Making his way home toward one o’clock, he glimpsed, seated on the landing, the figure of a woman in a white dress — in the darkness she seemed to glow with a faint phosphorescence. A moment of stupefaction seized him... and she rose and, retreating, seemed to vanish. He would have taken her for a phantom, had not a door creaked. Miss Elisa had passed a great part of the night keeping watch, in tears perhaps, to assure herself that the sea and the war had not taken him from her.

And di Rudio, in the tumult that threatened to overwhelm him, clung desperately to this resolve: “Before afternoon, undeceive her and flee!” With that resolution he went to bed; thereupon the thoughts of the night, inspired by the painful remembrance of so many martyrs both living and dead, made him yet more stubborn. When he presented himself, rigid and self-possessed, before the two young girls, Elisa greeted him with: “Are you unwell? You are pale this morning!”

— It seems to me that you are both pale! the cousin observed. And she offered to prepare a cup of warm milk — Mamma too would be glad of some when she came in. As she reached the threshold she turned and, smiling: “Do not be foolish. Every moment lost is stolen from your happiness, and by forever hiding from each other you might end by never finding each other again. You love each other, you were born one for the other — what more is needed?” And she was gone.
The decisive moment had come. Elisa was nervously twisting one of her ribbons, and di Rudio watched her, keeping at a respectful distance. He began by thanking her for all that she had done for him. Any denial was useless — Sara had confided everything to him. The misfortune was that he could demonstrate his gratitude only in words.

And he went on to recount the sad vicissitudes to which one must submit who champions a holy and persecuted cause. With what conscience could he have dared to offer her his hand, to drag her into his tumultuous life, into his poverty? Where find for her a nest that would not, sooner or later, be tainted and destroyed by the fury of men? Would it be wise, humane, honest to reserve an environment haunted by the terror of conspiracies, the snares of arms, the clank of chains, the dread of blood, for children born to perpetuate the gentle virtues of their mother? Infancy yearns for the kisses of smiling lips; it needs to mirror in its candid soul eyes that are serene. And what else could he say to her; what happiness could he promise, without resorting to mendacious phrases? He had chosen, between two evils, the lesser; he had resolved to withdraw — for ever. He implored her to interpret his resolution with that rectitude of feeling that had suggested it to him; to preserve a kindly memory of him.

Here, and here alone, Miss Elisa raised her face and looked him in the eyes. With the acuteness of simple souls, she knew how to sound a soul. She expressed herself as a creature conscious of the power to dispose of her own destiny, with that calm and chaste eloquence which to some of my readers, accustomed to exalting “the opulence of the hip and the quiverings of the flesh,” might seem exhumed from some museum of archaeology... Other times, other men, other loves!

“Toward a soul such as that of di Rudio she could advance without suspicion and without artifice: she would have preferred to see herself definitively rejected over becoming his through simulated virtue. There were Italian women — she was not ignorant of this — who had suppressed their serene aspirations, imposed solitude and silence upon their hearts, so as not to become wives and mothers in calamitous times. She admired their austerity, but did not feel that she could approve of it. The wicked rear the wicked freely, and should the good withdraw themselves from the duty of rearing those who shall perpetuate honest traditions? The surroundings that seemed unfavourable to cultivating, in tender hearts, the gentle virtues he supposed her to possess, would favour the development of the virile paternal virtues. To temper virile characters is a mission that refines a mother’s pride. Or did he perhaps take her, like his ancient Roman women, as fit only to preside over the good order of the kitchen and to spin wool? The anguish of hearts that sweat blood, the terrible effort of muscles that swell to burst their chains, the tormented labour of intellects that seek everywhere for equity and light — were these things to have left her indifferent? She had a deep sympathy for the country he had painted for her so beautiful, so great, so unfortunate. The love he bore his Fatherland did not make her jealous — to distract him from his aims, to blunt his generous impulses? Precisely the contrary! They would work together, and with redoubled energy, for the triumph of Liberty. He had kindled a flame that made her greater than herself — let him try to extinguish it, if his heart were equal to it, if he had the strength!”

The Count thought: “Blessed the rosy mouth that vanquishes the eloquence of so many martyrs, living and dead!” Nevertheless he persisted:

“No, Elisa! The conspirator is alone even in the bosom of his family. He must at times forget that he has a mother, a wife, children. If his predestined hour arrives, he stops his ears to their prayer, he sees not the tears and he departs — departs without confiding his secrets even to the air he breathes. If that hour comes —”

— One last embrace, a wrench of the heart, perhaps — but no indiscreet question. Am I not certain that you can do nothing of which I need be ashamed!

When Miss Sara returned with the cups and the milk, the lover who but a short while before had wished to flee far, far away, had one knee on the ground. Holding Elisa’s hands clasped within his, he was kissing them, kissing them again. On the ninth of December, 1855, a flame was made lawful, upon which misfortune hastened to breathe — in vain! The hard vicissitudes of more than half a century, far from extinguishing it, were only to make it burn ever more brightly!
