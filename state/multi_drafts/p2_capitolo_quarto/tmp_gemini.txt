The word to Count di Rudio: The same year I married, I was involved in an unfortunate affair that caused a great stir. The exiles who had no great wealth of coin would frequent a certain *trattoria* on Rupert Street, where for a small price they could savour some dish cooked in our native style. More than once I had happened upon it myself, and there I had made the acquaintance of a certain Rossi from Mantova, a young man who soon won my sympathies for his expansive and cheerful character. Rossi was excessively disposed to amorous relations, but I could discern no other vices in him, and when the Romagnol Foschini warned me, in all confidence, to steer clear of that 'ugly scoundrel' because he was a spy for the French Government, I observed to him that to lightly give faith to slanders insinuated, in all probability, for nefarious ends by the enemies of our party, was to play childishly into their game.

Foschini, sullen, turned his back on me, and I... grew more intimate with Rossi... a little, also, because of the overpowering instinct in me to wish to see things clearly, down to their very depths. I came to be convinced that Foschini was truly in error. Such a conviction, to tell the truth, is not so deep-rooted today. A Rossi, as I learned several years later, had been sinisterly implicated in the Mantova conspiracies. However, the surname Rossi is very common in Upper Italy, and I never had the means to ascertain if my London acquaintance and the spy for the Austrian Government were one and the same person. For anyone interested in investigating these minutiae, I will say that the Rossi of

London had a brother who was a priest... a circumstance that might serve as a guide to establishing the truth!

One evening, at a late hour, I found myself in the Rupert Street tavern in the company of the 'ugly scoundrel'. Foschini was also there, sitting all alone, apart; but from the dark looks he had shot our way and from certain mumbled monosyllables, I had been able to deduce that we ran the risk of sailing in troubled waters.

The patrons thinned out and we were left almost alone. At a certain point, Foschini stood up and, passing stiffly beside Rossi, he jostled him in the elbow and said: "Spy!"

Rossi reacted with a worse compliment and Foschini, lashed across the face, turned, enraged. I believed it my duty to intervene and was recommending that we make no scandal, so as not to compromise our émigré community, when the Romagnol, drawing a dagger, gave a leap. Another second... and Rossi would have been done for! To prevent the crime, I seize a chair and do my best to bring it down upon the aggressor. Unfortunately, I strike the lamp hanging from the ceiling; it shatters to pieces and we are left in darkness. I recall the sensation of the cold blade penetrating my chest, then... nothing more!

When I regained a glimmer of consciousness, I understood that I was in a hospital. I could not manage to keep my eyes open; but I heard a voice, that of the physician, pronounce that I should not see the morrow's sun. They made me swallow some preparation or other, and that brief effort was enough for me to lose my senses once again.

But my youth reasserted itself and, despite the grim prophecy, my first impression was precisely that of the sun's rays beating upon my face. They awakened in me a firm will to cling to life.

I learned that the doctor, believing the case to be desperate, had resorted to a heroic remedy of his own in order to arrest the internal haemorrhage. He considered the outcome a prodigy and, for a long series of days, he led now this colleague, now that one, to my bedside, initiating discussions of probably great scientific value. To me, a layman with irritable nerves, they were supremely unwelcome.

Before I received the regulation visit from a Police Inspector, the doctor informed me that Foschini had claimed two other victims. But Rossi, the only one he undoubtedly wished to do away with, had escaped without a scratch.

The Authorities placed a bounty of two hundred pounds sterling on Foschini, who had become a fugitive. But he was a good patriot and, although his hiding place was known, no one breathed a word. Indeed, in the laudable attempt to cover for him, the political motive that had driven him to act was greatly exaggerated, without caring too much for the niceties of whether, in saving the reputation of the guilty party, that of the innocent was diminished.

Now, note the strangeness of a destiny that saw me face, first in Europe, then in America, the gravest dangers, and though I was more than once a hair's breadth from leaving my bones there, I always escaped with my skin intact. It was reserved for my comrades in faith to repay my selflessness with thirty-six dagger wounds, two of which penetrated the cavities and were judged to be mortal!

Foschini splendidly ended his days fighting at the side of Carlo Pisacane, in the unfortunate, glorious expedition of Sapri.

Sapri was the first serious manifestation of the new change in tactics adopted by Giuseppe Mazzini. When the Crimean War ended, the great Cavour, despite the splendid performance of our soldiers and their victory at the Cernaia, obtained nothing for his King... not even the hoped-for annexation of Parma and Modena, whose princelings they wished to foist upon Moldavia and Wallachia. But Napoleon III had promised that one day he would do something for Piedmont as well, and from that time on, Mazzini held the alliance of the Empire with Vittorio Emanuele II to be a certainty. He was, however, firmly convinced that nothing else would derive from it but an aggrandizement of the Sardinian States or, in the best of hypotheses, a federation of Italian states governed, for the most part, by French princes. To prevent such a calamity, he abandoned his old field of action and strove, with his most trusted followers, to raise up the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies. To counter the monarchical agitation of the North with the insurrection of the southern extremity of Italy, to impose from Palermo and from Napoli the unity of the Nation. This brilliant concept earned Giuseppe Mazzini and Garibaldi a sentence of 'ignominious death', but it was to prove one of the greatest factors in our future greatness.

Left completely aside, probably because I was reputed to have been softened by my new affections, I was already resigning myself to a long inaction, when suddenly the call reached me from where I least expected it.
