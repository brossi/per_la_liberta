And the little vessel glides on, glides... until before our weary and tormented eyes there appeared, drawn against the blue sky, the black hull of a cutter. It had emerged as if by surprise from the shore, at so short a distance that the mere thought of evading it would have been madness. For a moment we flattered ourselves that those aboard would pay us no heed; but a cannon-shot, fired as a warning, blew away every illusion. The little herring moved to meet the shark, the shark moved to meet the little herring... the distance vanished so rapidly, and before long we were able to make out the flag flying from the stern-mast.

Hearts aloft! It was the symbol of pride and of liberty; of humanity and of progress... the English flag!

Leaning over the rail of his cutter, a Negro captain, surrounded by half a dozen Negro sailors, demanded of us, in a tone of command, who we were and whether we had on board any goods subject to customs. When I had answered him, he drew himself upright in astonishment and, pointing his forefinger: "You cannot mean to tell me that you have covered more than a thousand miles of sea on that hand-span of hollow wood?"

I indicated in turn our trousers which, despite the coat of mud that covered them, revealed here and there the place of our origin. Once persuaded, he expressed a desire to question us at greater length aboard his cutter. It was like touching an electric button... we all sprang to our feet simultaneously. But whether he had no wish to besmirch his vessel with that species of ambulant *vase* [vessel, tub]; or whether our appearance put him on his guard, he cried out: "Not all, not all! You come aboard — you who have been speaking." I was the only one who spoke English.

On board, I had the opportunity to recount our adventures to him in full detail, and to request of him, by way of conclusion, assistance for our most pressing needs. He listened without interrupting me, giving expression, at the dramatic moments, to his emotion in the manner natural to his race: by bursting into unrestrained laughter. When I fell silent, he began: "Well, well, well, well!..." But he broke off and, turning to the sailors, ordered them to lower a sack of ship's biscuit and some fresh water into the dinghy.

I asked him for precise directions to reach the nearest English port and, to my intense relief, was informed that we were not far from the mouth of the Berbice and the town that takes its name therefrom. "Once you have rounded the headland that lies dark over there, the town will rise up suddenly before you. The tide will be going out then. Keep this side of the buoys and you will run no danger whatsoever."

The cutter's crew, made up of sailors who had once been slaves, felt sympathy for us. After the biscuit was lowered they vied with one another in throwing us tobacco and other small gifts, and when the dinghy got under way we were saluted by a chorus of "good bye" and "good luck" that for some moments drowned out even the resonance of the sea itself. We showed our gratitude by breaking the hard crusts on the spot with crocodile teeth.

And the little vessel glides on, glides... until it enters the turbid, yellowish waters that announce the mouth of the Berbice as imminent. My companions, rather than rejoicing at this, began to give voice to the strangest apprehensions. Who could assure us, among other things, that the Negro captain had not taught us how to blunder headlong into some trap?

We fell in with a fishing smack. Two small, sun-bronzed men, Portuguese in appearance, were hauling in their nets. "Which course must we steer to avoid the current and enter the river without danger?"

We heard, in different words, instructions identical to those of the Negro captain. Half an hour later, before our astonished eyes, Berbice, or New Amsterdam, spread itself out on either bank. On one side, low houses set on piles, among which, evidently, the water crept in at high tide; on the other, the broad and solid government buildings. In the middle, several anchored vessels drew the outlines of their yards against the sky.

We did not remain long in doubt as to which direction to steer. We had barely entered when a launch came swiftly towards us, in which stood upright an old man with long white side-whiskers. He was surrounded by a dozen policemen. In an instant he was upon us and the old man, shaking his honest whiskers: "Who are you? Whence do you come?"

As though it wished, for once and for all, to have done with the matter, fate had steered us directly into the path of the Governor. Who, perceiving us so dilapidated, aboard a vessel flying no flag of any kind, and supposing us to be shipwrecked men, had approached to obtain reliable news of us and to render us whatever assistance might be required.

I answered him in four brief words, and he, with that English tact which never permits a man to give a direct contradiction, said:

"You surely cannot mean to say that you come from the Île Royale?" Then he turned to his helmsman, the only one of his following with a white complexion, the only one dressed in plain clothes, and said to him: "What do you make of it? In a shell like that! Old England has set the example!"

He sent one of his guards aboard our dinghy and, having given orders to prepare us a substantial meal at the police station, made a sign, and his launch darted away, nimble as a fish.

The police station was situated on the left bank, near the wharf, and four strokes of the oar sufficed to bring us there. But from the French oaths chewed between my companions' teeth, I could not help noticing that the dinghy was proceeding contrary to their inclinations. True enough, the "substantial supper" was a splendid prospect; but the escort of Negro policemen smacked too much to them of a dismal prelude for them not to suspect that they were about to slide from the table into some large room locked with a double turn of the key. And matters grew worse when, upon arrival, a zealous officer in gold braid launched into formal questions, writing down in a register the answers which I translated for him. Had we then faced death so many times only to tumble so wretchedly out of the frying-pan into the fire?

To convince them they were not prisoners, I made the pretext of wishing to purchase tobacco and went out. Then, lightened of every excessive preoccupation, I had leisure to observe my surroundings more carefully. It was the 15th of December, 1859, and the luxuriant vegetation pressed close about the settlement, as though intending to dominate it and flood it with deep green. Superb, impenetrable forests extended, as far as the eye could reach, along both banks, and the seawater issued from them in little cascades, returning with the ebb-tide to the sea. The sky, a deep blue, had to the west the glare of a thousand conflagrations.

The news of our arrival had spread rapidly and I was soon surrounded by a crowd eager for particulars. My convict's dress, filthy as though I had wallowed in a pigsty, aroused no disgust whatsoever. I was addressed with no questions that were not honourably discreet; I saw none but faces marked by the most genuine sympathy. And there was one man who offered me a thousand duros for the cession of our dinghy.

I cut short the conversations and the handshakes to hasten at the call of a policeman, who announced to me that supper was ready. Before the profusion of fresh bread, of ham, of cheese, and of beer spread out upon the table, every trace of disquiet seemed to melt away from my companions' breasts. Every last crumb was devoured in a deep silence, to the amusement of the policemen, who were astonished and rendered merry by that rapid, phenomenal disappearance.

The dishes were already being cleared away when an unexpected visit plunged us back into painful uncertainty. The old Governor with the long white side-whiskers had entered silently and stood before us, conducting a mute inspection. At his back was a group of citizens, whose sentiments in our regard were perfectly concealed behind impassive faces.

We remained motionless and expectant. The Governor began by expressing, in French, the following thoughts: "You have come to a land that does not turn away those who seek asylum within it. Respect the Laws of the Country and the Country will not fail in its duties of hospitality. But one guarantee is required of you... that of labour. Labour and thrift smooth the path of honest men, whatever depths a man may have fallen to."

He was silent for a moment, then proceeded to interrogate us one by one, asking us not our names, not the nature of our sentence, but the occupation we would be capable of honourably discharging. As each of my companions declared his trade, the Governor would turn to one or another of the citizens who had accompanied him and say to him: "This one I entrust to you... and this other to you."

My turn came. I answered: "My profession is that of the sword."

— What do you mean by that?

— That I was educated at the military college of San Luca, in Milan.

— Where did you learn English?

— In England, where I have a wife and a child.

— You are Italian, you have a family in England, and you have escaped from a French penitentiary. What sort of muddle is this?

Thereupon I revealed my name and the cause of my condemnation. His face grew dark: "I believe you are mistaken. The Count di Rudio is dead." I was about to reply,

I was about to reply, when a young man of handsome appearance, notwithstanding the hue of his complexion which called to mind chocolate — he was a mulatto — interposed and said in perfectly correct French: "If Your Excellency will permit me, I will take this man into my house. I stand surety for him."

That courteous man was the physician of New Amsterdam. He had completed his studies at Brussels, where he had managed to acquire, alongside his degree, the love and hand of a charming Belgian young lady. He was a free-thinker, the proprietor of a local newspaper, and correspondent of the London *Times*... the last of these, perhaps, not the least of the reasons that had prompted him to come to my aid with such eagerness.

He assigned me a comfortable bedroom, where I had at last the opportunity to scrape off — literally! — the crust of the savage from myself, and to restore my natural complexion to the surface.

Thus transformed, I was presented to his wife. She struck me for her very fine figure and for her refinement of culture, which was polished, even though she had been educated in a convent. She spoke Italian with a sweetness unusual on foreign lips, and was more than merely an amateur in drawing melodies from the pianoforte.

The hour preceding the brief tropical dusk I spent in friendly conversation, recounting, at the request of my hosts and of the visitors who had gathered on account of the unusual circumstance, now this, now that adventure of mine. At a certain point the lady, who was probably intent upon extricating me from the excessive importunity of the curious, said aloud: "Count di Rudio, as an Italian, doubtless loves music." And without more ado she seated herself at the piano.

What enchantment!... and how expertly the human heart shows itself in lulling importunate phantoms to sleep and in reviving the rosy phantoms that have been lulled! I still had in my ear the language of the prison-hulk; I still could see, so to speak, at my feet, the chain barely broken; the soul still quivered from the torments endured... many of them, left untold because I shrink from the posture of the martyr... those agile fingers, which materially ran over the keys, were virtually skimming the hidden fibre within me that ached, as lightly as the fingers of a mesmerist. And I tasted the positive sweetness that is coupled with the cessation of all torment. There were notes, too, which, reverberating in the depths, called up an imprecation, a groan, a blood-stained phantom, the roar of a tempest; but all of it appeared softened and rendered harmonious to me by time and by hope...

I believe I shed tears over it; I cannot recall how many. I felt the mastery of art profoundly, because I too belonged to art... I had opened my mouth wide among the choristers of Drury Lane!

That enchantment was of brief duration. An acquaintance of the doctor's came rushing in to announce that a French warship had moored at the wharf. "Let us go and see her!" was the unanimous cry. And we departed. A few minutes later, in the twilight, the menacing outlines of the *Abeille* loomed gigantic before my eyes.

The tactic adopted by Gousin of steering our dinghy northward had produced its effect. When, compelled by the spoilage of our provisions, we turned back towards the South American continent, the *Abeille* had already passed beyond the tangent we had traced. Not finding us at New Amsterdam, she had pushed on as far as the waters of Venezuela, and the captain, disenchanted, was already returning on his course when, passing again before the Berbice, he had fallen in with the smack and the Portuguese. From them he learnt of our arrival and had come up the river to demand my extradition.

There was no doubt of it. At the *Abeille*'s rail stood side by side two odious figures... the warders of my gang. For what reason were they on board, if not to identify me? They caught sight of me and were animated by a sudden ferocity: "There he is, there he is!" exclaimed one of them, pointing at me with his finger. And he added: "Hey, you — the one with the bombs! Before long you will be up here and tasting your medicine again!" This taunt was not enough for him. He came ashore and, advancing towards me with his right hand extended and a diabolical smile on his ugly mouth, congratulated me on the masterliness of our escape. A courtesy which I repaid by turning my back on him.

That same evening, a policeman presented himself to the doctor and announced that the Governor required my immediate presence at Government House. My host consulted with the friends who had remained until that hour, then said: "Let us go to Government House in a body. Some act of weakness... one never knows!"

I traversed the long road with feverish anxiety. Having then climbed the steep stairs, I saw in the ante-chamber — a dismal preamble! — my two warders. A few minutes later I found myself in a great hall in the presence of the Governor.

Beside his writing-desk stood a man in uniform... the captain of the *Abeille*.

The Governor said: "Is this the fugitive you are seeking?"

I protested: I had never sought to conceal my identity!

— I beg you not to speak when you are not being questioned.

And the captain: "I do not know him personally. But I have with me two warders who will be able to reply to Your Excellency in categorical terms."

One of them was shown in. Who, imagining that I had lied about my identity, without waiting for any question, cried out: "It is he! Count di Rudio in person... himself and no other!"

— You may go.

The second warder enters and the denunciation is renewed.

The Governor rose to his feet: "Count di Rudio, I had doubted your word. I ask your pardon for it." Then, turning to the other: "Captain, England does not accord the extradition of political prisoners. I am sorry... but I am unable to give you any other answer!"

I was free, definitively free! And in the excess of the joy which seemed to engulf me, instead of drawing myself up to the full dignity of a man restored to his rightful place, returned to those he loved, to the sacred struggles on behalf of his ideals, I became suddenly a child again. To the captain as he moved away giving me a stern glance, I directed that mischievous act of derision which consists in placing one's thumb against the tip of one's nose and waggling one's fingers.

The following morning... two important pieces of news. The *Abeille* had weighed anchor but, in departing, had left behind at New Amsterdam a spy charged, certainly, with reporting on my movements. Some precaution was necessary; for if the Napoleonic claws were to seize me again, even illegally, there was no diplomatic note powerful enough to save me.

The other piece of news, though it did not directly concern me, made a greater impression upon me. My companions in escape, terrified by the spectre of extradition, had crept back in the night to the dinghy, in order to put out to sea again in secret. It was a grave error... the French authorities, having demanded my person, had taken no account of them whatsoever. According to the doctor, they had beyond all doubt steered their course towards some port of Venezuela. Did they reach it?

I believe that their virile mettle made them worthy of the liberty they had yearned for so ardently. But I never had any further news of them, and for me it was as though the Ocean had swallowed them whole.
