And the little craft slips on, slips on... until, to our harassed and weary eyes, there appeared, outlined against the blue sky, the black hull of a 'cutter'. It had emerged as if by surprise from the shore, at a distance so short that the mere thought of escaping it would have been madness. We deluded ourselves, for a moment, that those on board would pay us no mind; but a cannon shot, fired as a warning, blew away all our illusions. The little herring moved towards the shark, the shark moved towards the little herring... the distance vanished so rapidly, and we were not long in deciphering the flag that fluttered from the mizzenmast.

Hearts aloft! It was a symbol of pride and of freedom; of humanity and of progress... the English flag!

Bent over the rail of his 'cutter', a negro captain, surrounded by half a dozen negro sailors, demanded of us, in a tone of command, who we were and if we had on board goods subject to customs. When I had answered him, he straightened up in amazement and, pointing his finger, said: "You do not mean to tell me that you have crossed more than a thousand miles of sea on that span of hollow wood?"

I, in turn, pointed to our trousers which, despite the coating of mud that covered them, revealed, here and there, our origin. Being persuaded, he expressed the desire to question us at greater length aboard his 'cutter'. It was like touching an electric button... we all shot up at once, simultaneously. But whether he did not wish to befoul his ship with that species of ambulatory sludge, or whether our appearance induced him to be on his guard, he shouted: "Not all of you, not all of you! You come up, you who spoke." I was the only one who spoke English.

On board, I had the opportunity to narrate our adventures to him in detail and to request of him, by way of conclusion, help for our most pressing needs. He listened to me without interruption, expressing his emotion at the dramatic points only in the way natural to his race: by laughing boisterously. When I fell silent, he began: "Well, well, well, well!..." But he broke off and, turning to his sailors, ordered them to lower a sack of ship's biscuit and some fresh water into our boat.

I asked him for precise directions to reach the nearest English port and, to my intense relief, I heard him announce that we were not far from the mouth of the Berbiche and from the city that takes its name. "When you have passed the point that looms black over there, the city will suddenly rise up before you. The tide will be going out then. Keep to this side of the buoys and you will run no danger."

The crew of the 'cutter', composed of sailors who had been slaves, sympathized with us. Once the biscuit was lowered, they vied with one another in throwing us tobacco and other small gifts, and when our boat set off, we were saluted by a chorus of "good bye" and "good luck" that for a few moments overcame the very resonance of the sea. We showed our gratitude by crushing the hard crusts, right then and there, with teeth like a crocodile's.

And the little craft slips on, slips on... until it enters the turbid, yellowish waters that reveal the imminent mouth of the Berbiche. My companions, instead of rejoicing, began to manifest the strangest apprehensions. Who could assure us, among other things, that the negro captain had not shown us the way to run, headlong, into some trap?

We came upon a fishing 'smack'. Two small, sun-tanned men, Portuguese in appearance, were pulling in their nets. "Which way must we take to avoid the current and enter the river without danger?"

We heard, in different words, instructions identical to those of the negro captain. Half an hour later, to our astonished eyes, Berbiche, or New Amsterdam, spread out on either bank. On one side, low houses, resting on stilts, between which, evidently, the water would seep in at high tide; on the other, the large and solid government buildings. In the middle, several anchored ships traced the shafts of their yardarms against the sky.

We did not long remain in doubt as to where to direct our course. We had scarcely advanced when a launch came swiftly towards us, upon which stood an old man with long white side-whiskers. He was surrounded by a dozen policemen. In an instant he was upon us and the old man, shaking his venerable whiskers, asked: "Who are you? From whence do you come?"

As if wishing, once and for all, to be done with it, fate had pushed us directly into the path of the Governor. Who, seeing us so dilapidated, on a boat devoid of any flag, and thinking we were castaways, had approached us to get some definite news and to lend us the appropriate aid.

I answered him with four brief words and he, with the tact of the English, who never permit themselves to issue a direct denial:

"You surely do not mean to say you have come from Île Royale?" Then he turned to his helmsman, the only one in his retinue with white skin, the only one dressed in civilian clothes, and said to him: "What do you think of that? In such a shell! Our old England has set the standard!"

He had one of his guards board our boat and, having given the order to prepare a substantial meal for us at the police station, he made a sign and his launch shot away, as agile as a fish.

The police station was situated on the left bank, near the pier, and four strokes of the oar sufficed to reach it. But from the French curses muttered by my companions, I had to realize that the boat was proceeding against their will. True, the "substantial supper" was a splendid prospect; but the surrounding black policemen smacked too much of a sad prelude for them not to suspect they were to slide from the table to some large cell locked with a double turn of the key. And it was worse when, upon reaching our destination, a zealous, gold-braided official came forth with formal questions, writing in a register the answers that I translated for him. Had we therefore faced death so many times only to fall so miserably from the frying pan into the fire?

To convince them that they were not prisoners, I used the pretext of wanting to buy some tobacco and went out. Then, lightened of all excessive preoccupation, I had leisure to better observe the surroundings. It was the 15th of December (1859) and the luxuriant vegetation closed in upon the settlement, as if intending to dominate it and inundate it with dark green. Superb, impenetrable forests stretched as far as the eye could see along both banks, and the sea-water poured from them in small cascades, to return, with the ebb-tide, into the sea. The sky, profoundly blue, had, to the west, the glare of a thousand fires.

The news of our arrival had spread rapidly, and I was soon surrounded by a multitude anxious for details. My convict's garb, as filthy as if I had wallowed in a pigsty, aroused no revulsion whatsoever. I was not asked any questions that were not honourably discreet; I saw only faces imprinted with the most genuine sympathy. And there was one who offered me a thousand duros for the sale of our boat.

I cut short the conversations and handshakes to hurry to the call of a policeman, who announced that supper was ready. Before the profusion of fresh bread, ham, cheese, and beer spread out on the table, every disturbance seemed to vanish from my companions' breasts. The last crumb was devoured in profound silence, amidst the jests of the policemen, who were amazed and amused by that rapid, phenomenal disappearance.

They were already beginning to clear away the dishes when an unexpected visit plunged us again into painful doubt. The old Governor with the long white side-whiskers had entered silently and was standing before us, in mute inspection. Behind him was a group of citizens, whose feelings towards us were perfectly concealed by their impassive faces.

We remained motionless and in suspense. The Governor began by expressing, in French, these thoughts: "You have happened upon a land that does not reject those who seek asylum in it. Respect the Laws of the Country, and the Country will not fail in the duties of hospitality. But from you a guarantee is required... that of work. Work and thrift smooth the way for any honest man, however low he may have fallen."

He paused for a moment, then proceeded to question us one by one, asking not our names, not our sentences, but the occupation we could honourably perform. As my companions each declared his trade, he would turn to one or another of the citizens who had accompanied him and say: "I entrust this one to you... to you this other one."

My turn came. I replied: "My profession is that of the sword."

— What do you mean to say?

— That I was educated in the military college of San Luca, in Milano.

— Where did you learn English?

— In England, where I have a wife and a son.

— You are Italian, you have family in England, and you escaped from a French penitentiary. What a muddle this is!

Then I revealed my name and the cause of my sentence. His face darkened: "I believe you are in error. The Count di Rudio is dead." I was about to reply,

I was about to reply, when a handsome young man, despite the tint of his face which recalled chocolate (he was a mulatto), intervened and said in perfect French: "If Your Excellency will permit me, I will take this man into my home. I will stand surety for him."

That courteous man was the doctor of New Amsterdam. He had completed his studies in Brussels, where he had managed to acquire, in addition to his degree, the love and the hand of a graceful Belgian young lady. He was a freethinker, owner of a local newspaper, and a correspondent for the *Times* of London... not the least, perhaps, of the reasons that had impelled him to come to my aid with such enthusiasm.

He assigned me a good bedroom, where I finally had the opportunity to scrape off—literally!—the crusts of the savage and restore my natural colour to the surface.

Thus evolved, I was presented to his wife. She struck me for her beautiful figure and for her culture, which was refined, though she had been educated in a convent. She spoke Italian with a sweetness unusual in a foreign mouth and was more than an amateur in drawing melodies from the piano.

The hour preceding the brief tropical twilight I passed in friendly conversation, narrating, at the request of my hosts and of the visitors who had flocked for the unusual circumstance, now one, now another of my adventures. At a certain point the lady, who probably intended to rescue me from the excessive insistence of the curious, said aloud: "The Count di Rudio, as an Italian, undoubtedly loves music." And without another word, she sat down at the piano.

What a charm!... and how expert the human heart shows itself in lulling to sleep the troublesome phantoms and in reviving the rosy phantoms that have slept! I still had in my ear the language of the penal colony; I still saw, so to speak, at my feet, the chain so barely broken; my soul still trembled from the torments endured... many, not recounted because I shrink from the posture of a martyr... those nimble fingers that, materially, ran over the keyboard, virtually brushed against the hidden fibre that ached within me, as light as those of a mesmerist. And I felt the positive sweetness that is coupled with the cessation of all torture. There were also notes that, reverberating in the depths, aroused there a curse, a groan, a bloody spectre, the roar of a storm; but everything appeared to me softened and made harmonious by time and by hope...

I believe I wept at it, I do not recall how much. I felt profoundly the mastery of art, for I belonged to art myself... I had opened my mouth wide amongst the choristers of Drury Lane!

That enchantment was of short duration. An acquaintance of the doctor's came rushing to announce that a French warship had moored at the pier. "Let us go and see it!" was said in chorus. And we left. A few minutes later, in the twilight, the menacing form of the *Abeille* loomed gigantic before my eyes.

The tactic adopted by Gousin of pushing our boat to the North had produced its effect. When, forced by the failure of our provisions, we turned back towards the South American Continent, the *Abeille* had already passed beyond the tangent we had traced. Not finding us at New Amsterdam, it had pushed on as far as the waters of Venezuela, and the captain, disillusioned, was already returning on his route when, passing again before the Berbiche, he had run into the 'smack' and the Portuguese. From them he learned of our arrival and had come up the river to demand my extradition.

No doubt about it. Near the rail of the *Abeille* were, one next to the other, two odious figures... the guards of my squad. For what reason were they on board, if not to identify me? They caught sight of me and were animated by a sudden ferocity: "Look at him, look at him!" exclaimed one of them, pointing his finger at me. And he added: "Hey, you with the bombs! Soon you'll be up here and you'll taste your medicine again!" Such mockery was not enough for him. He came ashore and, approaching me with his right hand extended, a diabolical smile on his ugly mouth, he congratulated me on the mastery of our escape. A kindness which I returned by turning my back on him.

That same evening, a policeman presented himself to the doctor and announced that the Governor required my immediate presence at the Palace. My host consulted with the friends who had remained until that hour, then said: "Let us go to the Palace in numbers. Some act of weakness... one never knows!"

I traversed the long road with febrile anxiety. Then, having climbed the hard stairs, I saw in the antechamber—sad preamble!—my two guards. A few minutes later and I was in a great hall in the presence of the Governor.

Next to his desk was a man in uniform... the captain of the *Abeille*.

The Governor said: "Is this the fugitive you are looking for?"

I protested: I had never tried to hide my identity!

— I beg you not to speak when you are not questioned.

And the captain: "I do not know him personally. But I have with me two guards who will be able to answer Your Excellency in a categorical manner."

One was brought in. Who, imagining that I had lied about my being, without waiting for any question shouted: "Yes, it is he! The Count di Rudio in person... he himself!"

— Be gone!

The other guard entered and the denunciation was renewed.

The Governor rose: "Count di Rudio, I had doubted your word. I ask your pardon." Then turning to the other: "Captain, England does not grant the extradition of political convicts. I am sorry... but I am not in a position to give you any other answer!"

I was free, definitively free! And in the excess of joy that seemed to overwhelm me, instead of rising again in all the majesty of a man reinstated, returned to his affections, to the holy struggles for the sake of ideals, I suddenly became a child again. To the captain who was departing, giving me a severe look, I directed that roguish derision which is expressed by putting the thumb to the tip of the nose and wiggling the fingers.

The next morning... two important new developments. The *Abeille* had weighed anchor but, upon leaving, left behind in New Amsterdam a spy, certainly tasked with reporting on my movements. It was necessary to use some precaution; for if the Napoleonic claws had, even illegally, seized me again, there was no diplomatic note that would be enough to save me.

The other development, though not concerning me directly, made a greater impression on me. My companions in escape, frightened by the bogeyman of extradition, had thrust themselves back into the boat during the night, to mysteriously put out to sea. It was a grave error... the French Authorities, having claimed my person, had not concerned themselves with them at all. According to the doctor, they had undoubtedly directed their course towards some port in Venezuela. Did they arrive?
