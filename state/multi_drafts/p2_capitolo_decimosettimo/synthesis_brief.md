GIUNSE il 25 Febbraio giorno fissato per lo svolgimento del processo. Fui tratto al Palazzo di Giustizia e per la prima volta, dopo la sera dell'attentato, vidi in pieno i miei compagni di sventura.

L'aula del Tribunale era gremita di uomini e di signore. Vi era sfoggio di abbigliamenti... si accorreva al processo come a teatro. Tuttavia, per la percezione di non so quale senso misterioso, compresi subito che l'anima multiforme di quell'uditorio non ci era avverso. Ostili, invece, deliberatamente ostili gli individui seduti alla cattedra e il manipolo di stipendiati che li attorniava. Costoro si immaginavano forse d'essere l'Impero e la sua forza e consideravano il nostro attentato come si considera un'offesa personale.

Il processo fu, nel suo nocciolo, la ripetizione dell'istruttoria... ma riveduta e corretta. Il diritto di difesa esiste pei buoni ed esiste pei tristi ed io sarei uno sciocco se rimproverassi alle Autorità giudiziarie francesi di non aver fatto l'apologia degli accusati; ma altrettanto sciocco si mostrerebbe chi, accecato dal prestigio inerente alla posizione sociale e con istinti da feticcio, pigliasse le opinioni espresse da quelle Autorità come unità di misura per giudicarci.

Dal primo svolgersi della Storia sino ai giorni nostri, il privilegio ha dovuto, per sussistere, rafforzare la prepotenza coll'artificio. La prepotenza è, per la sua stessa natura, intermittente e non arriva dappertutto. Ripara alla sua deficienza una disciplina raffinata dai secoli, che si manifesta in vari modi, a seconda dei tempi e dei costumi; ma che mira costantemente a tratteggiare i capi di uno Stato o di una religione, almeno sinché vivono, quali arche d'ingegno, quali arche di santità; che mira, in pari tempo a tratteggiare i refrattari che si rifiutano di entrare nell'orbita di quei sommi, di quei santi, come esaltati, come creature degenerate e malvagie.

Questa disciplina crea le larve prive di sostanza, ma rosee e fosforescenti, che attraggono le moltitudini sulle vie tortuose; crea i fantasmi spaventevoli che la respingono, con folli paure, dalle vie diritte. Fu essa che ha reso inaccessibile il Gran Lama, perchè l'occhio che vede, analizza e giudica, non scorga, nelle sue carni e nell'animo suo, i difetti inseparabili dall'umana natura. Essa ha indotto i Sultani a farsi precedere e seguire da un branco di sicari roteanti la scimitarra. Gli audaci che ardivano alzare al semidio gli occhi indagatori, provocavano la propria morte fulminea.

Essa ha insegnato, d'altra parte, che il livore contro l'anime ribelli, deve continuare anche dopo il supplizio. Recidere le teste che non vollero piegarsi, non basta; bisogna esporle sanguinolenti al dileggio degli incoscienti. Ardere sul rogo il pensatore, non basta; bisogna pronunciare sulle sue ceneri una fiera maledizione, bisogna scagliarle ai venti!

L'Austria sarebbe stata ingenua se si fosse accontentata d'impiccare i patrioti italiani. Essa li ha sepolti senza esequie e senza feretro, in buche fuori di mano, come si praticava colle carogne dei quadrupedi.

È la funesta disciplina, modificata dai tempi, che gabella e perseguita come crimine l'onesta espressione di un onesto pensiero e, dove certi estremi non sono più possibili, è pur sempre in auge la livida guerra contro la riputazione e la memoria. La quale guerra, se è utile al privilegio più o meno legalmente costituito, diventa una necessità imprescindibile pel privilegio che è diventato tale col sopruso e col delitto.

Alla mia età si rilevano fatti, non si sfogano rancori. Nel processo imbastito contro gli autori dell'attentato del 14 Gennaio, la denigrazione a nostro danno era tanto più necessaria in quanto che l'origine stessa dell'Impero era, più o meno apertamente, in discussione. Noi sedevamo sul banco degli accusati di fronte a Napoleone; ma Napoleone sedeva sul banco degli accusati di fronte alla Francia ed al mondo civile. Ogni sua nobiltà d'animo, ogni sua altezza di proposito diventava un'aggravante per noi; ogni nostra altezza di propositi tendeva a rimpicciolirlo, era una condanna per lui.

Né va taciuto che l'azione violenta suscita, talora, incidenti che la dimostrano più efficace di qualsiasi altra propaganda. Denigrarci, farci passare per malfattori dell'infima specie, diminuire la probabilità di trovare imitatori, doveva perciò essere lo scopo non confessato del processo, ma scopo essenziale. Io riconosco che, dal loro punto di vista, le Autorità giudiziarie francesi agivano saggiamente tentando di decapitare, col nostro corpo, la nostra riputazione. Io riconosco, in pari tempo, che esse si ingolfarono nell'impresa con tutta la fosca destrezza dei servi del secondo Impero.

L'atto di accusa, formulato e letto dal Procuratore imperiale Chaix d'Est-Ange, scivolò destramente sulle prime incertezze e sui raggiri polizieschi; tratteggia le Autorità inquirenti colle pose del genio che guarda, sviscera, vince. Esso sopprime invece ogni nostro impeto che riveli carattere non volgare e, insistendo sui lati più facilmente censurabili e, dando enfasi alle nostre debolezze, ci aggrava con commenti, spesso assolutamente fantastici, che sanno di codardia.

Il Presidente del Tribunale, monsieur Delangle, dal canto suo, lavorò con persistenza viperina a mettere alla creazione semi letteraria del Procuratore, i debiti fregi e ne dissimulò le inverosimiglianze tirando in campo incidenti i quali, anche se veri, non avrebbero, colla nostra congiura, parentela alcuna. Peggio, egli gettò "pietosamente" sull'abito sdrucito del disinteresse, l'ampio mantello del sicario; egli impresse il marchio del ciurmadore sulla moritura virtù.

Compito facile, del resto! Noi abbiamo vissuto in tempi di passioni intense; tempi in cui le attrazioni e le repulsioni degli animi si avvicendavano colla violenza della bufera. Quale uomo, prendendovi, o tanto o quanto, parte attiva non si è visto trattato, con sua stessa meraviglia, ora peggio del diavolo, ora meglio di un santo; oggetto, spesso contemporaneamente, di profonda venerazione e d'immensurabile livore! Si capisce che la Polizia non dovesse avere difetto di melma da rovesciare.

Così a Giuseppe Pieri, il Presidente Delangle riversò sul capo, a Parigi, nel 1858, l'odio dei reazionari toscani che lo perseguitava sin dal 1830; l'odio che, dipingendolo per una belva del liberalismo, era giunto a farlo destituire da colonnello; l'odio che aveva tratto pretesto, ad infamarlo, da una burletta tra scapigliati che si presero, a spese di un altro scapigliato, un orologio di pochi baiocchi.

La generosità d'animo, l'alterezza, gli impeti sdegnosi di Felice Orsini vennero gabellati per una "ridicola ostentazione". E quando l'Orsini persistette a sostenere che la terza bomba non fu gettata da lui, ma dall'Italiano a cui ne aveva fatto consegna pochi minuti prima dell'attentato, il Delangle dimostrò la sua serena imparzialità nella ricerca del vero, prorompendo e tratteggiandolo come un mentitore a cui prendesse di far credere che le sue mani eran pure del sangue dei ragazzi, delle donne, dei vecchi feriti ed uccisi; "perchè l'odore di quel sangue saliva sino a lui e gli dava al cervello".

A compire la benemerita opera sua a prò dei principi alti e morali, il Presidente Delangle accolse poi nell'orecchio compiacente il venticello della sagrestia e Felice Orsini, Commissario della Repubblica Romana, in lontana provincia, che attorniato da nemici stranieri, indebolito da nemici interni, mantiene, colle requisizioni, la sua falange, venne dipinto ai giurati come un ladro volgare, sottrattosi poi, colla fuga, alle delizie dell'ergastolo pontificio.

Per quanto personalmente mi riguarda, il Delangle non ebbe ombra di pretesto per rimproverarmi né rapporti di famiglia riputati incorretti, né appropriazioni indebite vere od apparenti; ma questo non doveva rendermi immune al suo veleno. Io avevo rivelato come e perchè, la sera del 14 Gennaio Felice Orsini ci avesse consegnato la somma di 200 franchi. La mia rivelazione mirava a far comprendere ai nostri Giudici che la venalità non era nell'animo nostro. Ma dovetti accorgermi di non aver fatto entrare nei miei calcoli, la raffinata malizia dei miei Giudici. Quella tenue goccia, in tanto frangente, bastò perchè l'integerrimo Magistrato, atteggiandosi a sdegnosa sorpresa, mi accusasse d'avere, io appartenente ad una famiglia onorata, abbandonata la scuola, abborrito il lavoro sino ad incanagliarmi, a poco a poco, così da diventare assassino mercenario per 200 franchi!

Due parole sui resoconti processuali. La tattica di denigrazione non poteva arrestarsi al margine degli scartafacci, come i favoleggiati nostri progenitori al limitare del paradiso perduto. Essi furono ritoccati e mutilati e distorti, come tutto il resto, ad arbitrio di Sua Eccellenza. Un esempio: Quando il Presidente Delangle ci domandò se nulla volevamo addurre sull'imminente applicazione della condanna, il Gomez e l'Orsini risposero: "Nulla!" Il Pieri osservò che egli non aveva sparso sangue. Io additai Kim, lo spazzino municipale che, sfilando cogli altri testi, aveva fatto la sua falsa deposizione. E lo denunciai ad alta voce, protestando che, nel pronunciare la sentenza, si sarebbe dovuto, per debito di giustizia, non tener calcolo di una deposizione spergiura.

Il resoconto ufficiale afferma che, contrariamente ai miei tre compagni, unanimi nel pronunciare il "nulla", io invocai "la clemenza dei miei giudici".

L'abboccamento di Francesco Crispi coll'Orsini? Mentirei se dicessi che non l'ho negato. Ma bisogna rammentare che l'Orsini stesso aveva, pochi secondi prima dichiarato non essere l'Italiano dalla terza bomba conosciuto dai suoi complici, neppure dal Pieri; che egli non ne aveva rivelato l'esistenza ad alcuno; che nessuno di noi non lo aveva sospettato.

Non era questo un suggerirmi, quasi apertamente, la mia linea di condotta? E non era, d'altronde, nel mio diniego, il mezzo più spiccio per evitare interrogazioni subdole e raggiri che avrebbero potuto compromettere un uomo rimasto insospettato e libero?

Se i ciurmadori dell'Impero non avessero sentito nell'animo lo spavento della verità, non avrebbero imposto ai giornali di attenersi strettamente, nei loro resoconti, a quanto pubblicavano Le Droit e La Gazzetta dei Tribunali. Non è più verità la verità dimezzata e la ragion di Stato che non si perita di adulterarla assottigliandola, non può trattenersi per scrupoli quando giovi l'ornarla come una baldracca, o il soppannarle la veste con qualche menzognetta tendenziosa.

Creda ciecamente ai resoconti ufficiali l'uomo che giura sulla infallibile santità dei potenti, con quella semplicità d'animo che induce altri a credere all'infallibilità dell'ipocrita cui una colomba tuberebbe nell'orecchio il vero. Infinita e multiforme è la scempiaggine umana!

Ma l'accorto che non lascia colare all'intelletto qualsiasi brodo senza filtrarlo, certo non mancherà di riflettere che noi ci troviamo, non foss'altro, sotto la luce sfavorevole di chi ha la propria storia scritta dai propri nemici, in tempi ed in circostanze che facevano lor credere ogni nostra confutazione impossibile.

La condanna venne pronunciata la notte del 26 Gennaio e, quasi ad accrescere la teatralità di quello scioglimento, quando la Corte, concluse le sue deliberazioni, rientrava nella grande aula dell'Udienza, l'orologio del Palazzo di Giustizia batteva i suoi rintocchi.

A Gomez la galera a vita; al Pieri, a me, a Felice Orsini la pena che spetta ai parricidi. Luigi Napoleone era diventato, dopo averlo cannoneggiato, il padre del suo popolo — la Storia è piena di queste sanguinose ironie! E perchè non ci restasse dubbio alcuno sulla natura di quella pena ne fu, per intero, letta la descrizione: "condotti al patibolo, in camicia, a piedi nudi, colla testa coperta da un velo nero; poi la lettura della sentenza poi la decapitazione".

Guardai in viso ai miei compagni di fede e di sventura. Gomez, pallidissimo, manteneva un'immobilità marmorea — il dolore della condanna e la naturalissima soddisfazione d'essersi sottratto al peggio pareva si elidessero reciprocamente nel suo cuore. Il Pieri dondolava la testa e teneva l'occhio fisso al Presidente, con un misto indefinibile di sdegno e di commiserazione. M'accorsi che le sue labbra si muovevano e n'ebbi l'impressione che egli incalzasse, in quel momento, certa frase indirizzata, appunto al Delangle, durante l'interrogatorio: "Lo sapevo bene che non saremmo mai arrivati a nulla di buono, con quel testardo!"

Felice Orsini girava lentamente gli occhi dall'uno all'altro giudice. Pareva dicesse, senz'ombra di spavalderia: "la vostra miseria non mi tange!"

Quanto al mio stato d'animo... che devo dire? Da tempo prevedevo la condanna; ora che la previsione si era avverata, stentavo ad averne la piena coscienza, ad afferrarne la piena portata. Fu per accertarmi che ero ben desto che io avevo guardato in viso i miei compagni. D'improvviso mi sorse vivida nella mente, l'imagine d'Elisa, non florida e sorridente; ma quale l'avevo baciata l'ultima volta riversa sulla sedia, gli occhi spenti, priva di sensi, come se il tribunale della Senna avesse pronunciato la sua, non la mia condanna.

Quell'imagine mi rimase tanto fissa nel cervello da non permettermi di vedere, di pensare ad altro. Percorrendo il sotterraneo che ci riconduceva alle Conciergerie mi scossi e, voltomi all'Orsini, che mi succedeva di pochi passi, gli dissi: "Tu che hai mezzi, rammentati di loro!" Comprese e mormorò "Te lo prometto"! L'uscio delle carceri si richiudeva appena alle mie spalle e riprovavo una strana sensazione, come se venissi tuffato in una quiete quasi famigliare. Saturno e l'altra guardia già tutto sapevano... avevano perso la scommessa, avevano preparato lo champagne. Ma vollero l'opportunità d'una rivincita. Se la sentenza non veniva cassata dalla Corte Suprema, o da un Decreto di grazia, essi avrebbero pagato un'altra bottiglia, altrimenti avrei pagato io. Mai scommettitore desiderò tanto ardentemente di perdere!

Così, per una bizzarria del destino, conversando facetamente e brindando, finii la notte in cui venni condannato alla ghigliottina. Mi sono augurato tante volte di sapere come la finissero i miei Giudici, Napoleone III, lo spazzino Kim e gli altri che avevano contribuito a salvare l'ordine sociale e l'usurpato Impero... ma il mio desiderio rimase sempre insoddisfatto.

THE twenty-fifth of February arrived — the day appointed for the proceedings of the trial. I was brought to the Palais de Justice and for the first time, since the evening of the attempt, I saw my companions in misfortune fully and plainly.

The assembly-hall of the Tribunal was packed with men and ladies. There was a great display of fine apparel... people flocked to the trial as to the theatre. Yet, through the perception of I know not what mysterious sense, I understood at once that the manifold soul of that audience was not opposed to us. Hostile, by contrast — deliberately hostile — were the individuals seated upon the bench, and the handful of hired servants who surrounded them. These men perhaps imagined themselves to be the Empire and its might, and regarded our attempt as one regards a personal affront.

The trial was, at its core, the repetition of the preliminary examination... but revised and corrected. The right of defence exists for the virtuous and exists for the wicked, and I should be a fool were I to reproach the French judicial authorities for not having composed a panegyric upon the accused; but equally foolish would he show himself who, blinded by the prestige inherent in social position and with the instincts of a fetish-worshipper, took the opinions expressed by those authorities as a measure by which to judge us.

From the earliest unfolding of History down to our own days, privilege has been obliged, in order to persist, to fortify oppression with artifice. Oppression is, by its very nature, intermittent and does not reach everywhere. Its deficiency is repaired by a discipline refined across the centuries, which manifests itself in various ways according to the times and the customs, but which aims constantly at portraying the heads of a State or of a religion — at least while they live — as very arks of ingenuity, as very arks of sanctity; which aims, at the same time, at portraying the recalcitrant who refuse to enter the orbit of those supreme ones, those saints, as fanatics, as degenerate and wicked creatures.

This discipline creates the larvæ devoid of substance, yet rosy and phosphorescent, which draw the multitudes along tortuous ways; it creates the fearsome phantoms that drive them back, with wild terrors, from the direct ways. It was this discipline that rendered the Grand Lama inaccessible, lest the eye that sees, analyses and judges, should discern, in his flesh and in his spirit, the defects inseparable from human nature. It induced the Sultans to have themselves preceded and followed by a troop of assassins whirling the scimitar. The audacious who dared raise inquisitive eyes to the demigod provoked their own swift death.

This discipline has taught, on the other hand, that the rancour against rebellious souls must continue even after the punishment. To sever the heads that would not bow is not enough; they must be exposed, bloodied, to the mockery of the unthinking. To burn the thinker at the stake is not enough; a fierce malediction must be pronounced over his ashes, they must be scattered to the winds!

Austria would have been ingenuous had she contented herself with hanging Italian patriots. She buried them without funeral rites and without a bier, in out-of-the-way pits, as was the practice with the carcasses of quadrupeds.

It is this baleful discipline, modified by the times, that brands and persecutes as crime the honest expression of an honest thought; and where certain extremes are no longer possible, the livid war against reputation and memory is ever in fashion. Which war, if it proves useful to privilege more or less legally constituted, becomes an indispensable necessity for the privilege that has attained such standing by violence and by crime.

At my age one records facts; one does not vent rancours. In the trial constructed against the authors of the attempt of the 14th of January, the denigration mounted against us was all the more necessary inasmuch as the very origin of the Empire was, more or less openly, in question. We sat upon the bench of the accused before Napoleon; but Napoleon sat upon the bench of the accused before France and the civilised world. Every nobility of spirit in him, every loftiness of purpose, became an aggravating circumstance for us; every loftiness of purpose in us tended to diminish him — it was a condemnation upon him.

Nor should it pass unmentioned that violent action sometimes provokes incidents which demonstrate it more efficacious than any other manner of propaganda. To denigrate us, to have us passed off as malefactors of the basest sort, to lessen the likelihood of finding imitators — this, therefore, had to be the unconfessed yet essential aim of the trial. I acknowledge that, from their point of view, the French judicial authorities acted wisely in attempting to decapitate, together with our bodies, our reputations. I acknowledge, at the same time, that they plunged into the enterprise with all the sinister dexterity of the servants of the Second Empire.

The bill of indictment, formulated and read by the Imperial Procurator Chaix d'Est-Ange, glided deftly over the initial uncertainties and the police-craft machinations; it portrays the examining authorities in the attitudes of genius that perceives, probes and vanquishes. It suppresses, by contrast, every impulse of ours that might reveal a character above the vulgar; and, dwelling upon the most easily censurable aspects and giving emphasis to our weaknesses, it loads us down with commentaries — often absolutely fantastic — that savour of cowardice.

The President of the Tribunal, Monsieur Delangle, for his part, laboured with viperous persistence to lend the Procurator's semi-literary creation its proper embellishments, dissembling its improbabilities by introducing incidents which, even if true, would have had no connection whatsoever with our conspiracy. Worse still, he cast "compassionately" over the threadbare garment of disinterestedness the ample cloak of the hired assassin; he branded with the mark of the charlatan a virtue in its death-throes.

An easy task, withal! We have lived in times of intense passions — times in which the attractions and repulsions of souls alternated with the violence of the tempest. What man, having taken part in them, to whatever degree, has not found himself treated, to his own astonishment, now worse than the devil, now better than a saint; the object, often simultaneously, of profound veneration and immeasurable hatred! One can well understand that the Police should have been in no want of mud to pour forth.

Thus upon Giuseppe Pieri the President Delangle poured, in Paris in 1858, the hatred of the Tuscan reactionaries which had pursued him since 1830 — the hatred that, portraying him as a beast of liberalism, had succeeded in having him stripped of his colonelcy; the hatred that had seized upon a students' prank, in which a group of young scapegraces had relieved another scapegrace of a watch worth a few *baiocchi* (small coins), as a pretext to defame him.

The generosity of spirit, the haughtiness, the disdainful impulses of Felice Orsini were represented as a "ridiculous ostentation." And when Orsini persisted in maintaining that the third bomb had not been thrown by him but by the Italian to whom he had handed it but a few minutes before the attempt, Delangle demonstrated his serene impartiality in the search for truth by bursting out and portraying him as a liar seeking to make one believe that his hands were clean of the blood of the children, the women, the old men wounded and killed; "for the odour of that blood rose to him and mounted to his brain."

To complete his meritorious work in the service of lofty and moral principles, President Delangle then lent a complaisant ear to the sacristy whisper; and Felice Orsini — Commissioner of the Roman Republic in a remote province, who, surrounded by foreign enemies, weakened by enemies within, maintained his phalanx by means of requisitions — was depicted to the jurors as a common thief, who had then escaped, by flight, the delights of the papal penitentiary.

As for what concerned me personally, Delangle had not the shadow of a pretext for reproaching me with any irregular family connection, nor with any real or apparent misappropriation; but this was not to render me immune to his venom. I had revealed how and why, on the evening of the 14th of January, Felice Orsini had handed us the sum of 200 francs. My revelation aimed at making our Judges understand that venality was not in our hearts. But I was compelled to realise that I had not reckoned with the refined malice of my Judges. That meagre drop, in such a moment of crisis, was sufficient for the upright Magistrate, striking the pose of indignant surprise, to accuse me — I who belonged to an honourable family — of having abandoned school and taken a loathing to work until I had so degraded myself as to become, step by step, a mercenary assassin for the sum of 200 francs!

A word or two upon the official trial reports. The tactic of denigration could not arrest itself at the margin of its papers, as the storied progenitors of our race at the threshold of paradise lost. They were retouched and mutilated and distorted, like all the rest, at the arbitrary pleasure of His Excellency. One example: when President Delangle asked whether we had anything to submit on the imminent application of the sentence, Gomez and Orsini replied: "Nothing!" Pieri observed that he had shed no blood. I pointed out Kim, the municipal street-sweeper who, filing past with the other witnesses, had given his false deposition. I denounced him aloud, protesting that in pronouncing the sentence, justice demanded that no account be taken of a perjured deposition.

The official report states that, contrary to my three companions, unanimous in pronouncing their "nothing," I invoked "the clemency of my judges."

The meeting between Francesco Crispi and Orsini? I should be lying were I to say I did not deny it. But one must remember that Orsini himself had, a few seconds before, declared that the Italian with the third bomb was not known to any of his accomplices, not even to Pieri; that he had revealed his existence to no one; that none of us had suspected him.

Was this not an almost open suggestion to me of my line of conduct? And was there not, moreover, in my denial, the most expeditious means of avoiding insidious questioning and machinations that might have compromised a man who remained unsuspected and at liberty?

Had the charlatans of the Empire not felt within their souls the terror of the truth, they would not have imposed upon the newspapers the obligation to adhere strictly, in their reports, to what was published in *Le Droit* and the *Gazzetta dei Tribunali*. A half-truth is no longer truth, and the reason of State that does not scruple to adulterate it by paring it down cannot restrain itself out of scruples when it is convenient to deck it out like a harlot, or to line its skirts with some tendentious little falsehood.

Let him believe blindly in the official reports who swears upon the infallible sanctity of the powerful, with that simplicity of soul which induces others to believe in the infallibility of the hypocrite into whose ear a dove might coo the truth. Infinite and manifold is human folly!

But the discerning man who does not let any broth drain through his intellect without first filtering it will certainly not fail to reflect that we find ourselves, if nothing else, under the unfavourable light of those whose history has been written by their own enemies, in times and circumstances which made those enemies believe any refutation on our part impossible.

The sentence was pronounced on the night of the 26th of January; and, as if to heighten the theatricality of that denouement, when the Court, having concluded its deliberations, re-entered the great hall of the audience-chamber, the clock of the Palais de Justice was striking its chimes.

For Gomez, imprisonment for life; for Pieri, for me, for Felice Orsini, the penalty reserved for parricides. Luigi Napoleone had become, after having cannonaded it, the father of his people — History is full of such bloody ironies! And lest any doubt remain to us as to the nature of that penalty, its full description was read aloud: "led to the scaffold in a shirt, barefoot, the head covered by a black veil; then the reading of the sentence; then the decapitation."

I looked full in the face of my companions in faith and in misfortune. Gomez, exceedingly pale, maintained a marble immobility — the grief of the condemnation and the entirely natural satisfaction of having escaped the worst seemed to cancel each other out within his heart. Pieri was shaking his head and kept his eye fixed upon the President, with an indefinable mingling of scorn and commiseration. I noticed that his lips were moving, and received the impression that he was pressing home, at that moment, a certain phrase he had addressed to Delangle himself during the interrogation: "I knew well enough that we should never come to any good end with that pigheaded man!"

Felice Orsini was slowly turning his eyes from one judge to another. He seemed to say, without the shadow of bravado: "Your pettiness does not touch me!"

As for my own state of mind... what am I to say? I had long foreseen the condemnation; now that the foresight had been borne out, I struggled to be fully conscious of it, to grasp its full import. It was to assure myself that I was indeed awake that I had looked my companions in the face. All at once there rose vividly before my mind the image of Elisa — not radiant and smiling, but as I had kissed her that last time, fallen back upon her chair, her eyes extinguished, insensible, as though the tribunal of the Seine had pronounced her sentence and not mine.

That image remained so fixed in my mind as to allow me to see or think of nothing else. As I traversed the underground passage that led us back to the Conciergerie I roused myself, and, turning to Orsini, who followed a few paces behind me, I said: "You who have means — remember them!" FEBRUARY 25TH arrived, the day fixed for the unfolding of the trial. I was taken to the Palace of Justice and for the first time, after the evening of the attack, I saw my companions in misfortune in full view.

The courtroom was thronged with men and ladies. There was a great show of apparel... people flocked to the trial as to the theatre. And yet, through the perception of I know not what mysterious sense, I understood at once that the multifaceted soul of that audience was not hostile to us. Hostile, instead, deliberately hostile, were the individuals seated on the bench and the band of hirelings that surrounded them. These men perhaps imagined themselves to be the Empire and its strength, and they regarded our attack as one regards a personal offence.

The trial was, in its essence, a repetition of the preliminary investigation... but revised and corrected. The right to a defence exists for the good and it exists for the wicked, and I would be a fool if I reproached the French judicial authorities for not having made an apology for the accused; but just as foolish would he show himself who, blinded by the prestige inherent in social position and with the instincts of a fetishist, were to take the opinions expressed by those authorities as the unit of measure by which to judge us.

From the first unfolding of History down to our own days, privilege has had, in order to subsist, to reinforce its tyranny with artifice. Tyranny is, by its very nature, intermittent and does not reach everywhere. Its deficiency is repaired by a discipline refined over centuries, which manifests itself in various ways, according to the times and customs, but which constantly aims to portray the leaders of a State or a religion, at least so long as they live, as paragons of genius, as paragons of sanctity; which aims, at the same time, to portray the recalcitrant who refuse to enter the orbit of those great men, of those saints, as fanatics, as degenerate and wicked creatures.

This discipline creates the phantoms devoid of substance, yet rosy and phosphorescent, which attract the multitudes onto tortuous paths; it creates the frightful spectres that repel them, with mad fears, from the straight paths. It was this that made the Grand Lama inaccessible, so that the eye that sees, analyses, and judges should not perceive, in his flesh and in his soul, the defects inseparable from human nature. It has induced the Sultans to have themselves preceded and followed by a pack of assassins brandishing their scimitars. The audacious who dared raise their inquiring eyes to the demigod provoked their own instantaneous death.

It has taught, on the other hand, that the rancour against rebellious souls must continue even after their execution. To sever the heads that would not bow is not enough; one must expose them, bleeding, to the mockery of the unthinking. To burn the thinker at the stake is not enough; one must pronounce a fierce curse over his ashes, one must cast them to the winds!

Austria would have been naive had she contented herself with hanging the Italian patriots. She buried them without funeral rites and without a coffin, in out-of-the-way pits, as was the practice with the carcasses of quadrupeds.

It is this fatal discipline, modified by the times, that labels and persecutes as a crime the honest expression of an honest thought and, where certain extremes are no longer possible, the livid war against reputation and memory is still in vogue. Which war, if it is useful to privilege that is more or less legally constituted, becomes an unavoidable necessity for the privilege that has become such through oppression and crime.

At my age, one notes facts, one does not vent resentments. In the trial concocted against the authors of the attack of January 14th, our denigration was all the more necessary inasmuch as the very origin of the Empire was, more or less openly, under discussion. We sat on the bench of the accused opposite Napoleon; but Napoleon sat on the bench of the accused opposite France and the civilized world. Every nobility of his soul, every loftiness of his purpose became an aggravating factor for us; every loftiness of our purposes tended to diminish him, was a condemnation for him.

Nor should it go unsaid that violent action sometimes gives rise to incidents which show it to be more effective than any other propaganda. To denigrate us, to make us out to be malefactors of the lowest sort, to diminish the probability of finding imitators, must therefore have been the unconfessed, but essential, aim of the trial. I recognize that, from their point of view, the French judicial authorities acted wisely in attempting to decapitate our reputation along with our bodies. I recognize, at the same time, that they plunged into the enterprise with all the grim adroitness of the servants of the Second Empire.

The indictment, formulated and read by the Imperial Prosecutor Chaix d'Est-Ange, glided deftly over the initial uncertainties and the police machinations; it portrays the investigating authorities with the poses of a genius who observes, dissects, and conquers. It suppresses, however, our every impulse that might reveal an uncommon character and, by insisting on the most easily censurable aspects, and giving emphasis to our weaknesses, it weighs us down with commentaries, often absolutely fantastical, that reek of cowardice.

The President of the Tribunal, Monsieur Delangle, for his part, laboured with viperine persistence to add the requisite embellishments to the Prosecutor's semi-literary creation, and he dissimulated its improbabilities by bringing up incidents which, even if true, would have had no kinship whatsoever with our conspiracy. An easy task, moreover! We have lived in times of intense passions; times in which the attractions and repulsions of souls followed one another with the violence of a storm. To complete his praiseworthy work in favour of high and moral principles, President Delangle then received into his compliant ear the whisperings from the sacristy, and Felice Orsini—Commissioner of the Roman Republic in a distant province, who, surrounded by foreign enemies, weakened by internal enemies, maintains his phalanx with requisitions—was depicted to the jurors as a common thief, who then escaped, by flight, the delights of the pontifical life sentence.

They were retouched and mutilated and distorted, like all the rest, at His Excellency's pleasure. And I denounced him in a loud voice, protesting that, in pronouncing the sentence, for the sake of justice, no account should be taken of a perjured deposition.

The official report affirms that, contrary to my three companions, unanimous in pronouncing the word "nothing", I invoked "the clemency of my judges".

The meeting of Francesco Crispi with Orsini? I would be lying if I said I did not deny it. Was this not suggesting to me, almost openly, my line of conduct? And was not my denial, moreover, the swiftest means of avoiding insidious interrogations and schemes that could have compromised a man who remained unsuspected and free?

If the charlatans of the Empire had not felt in their souls the terror of the truth, they would not have required the newspapers to adhere strictly, in their reports, to what was published by *Le Droit* and *La Gazzetta dei Tribunali*. Let the man who swears by the infallible sanctity of the powerful blindly believe the official reports, with that simplicity of soul which leads others to believe in the infallibility of the hypocrite in whose ear a dove might coo the truth. Infinite and multiform is human foolishness!

The sentence was pronounced on the night of January 26th and, almost as if to heighten the theatricality of that denouement, as the Court, its deliberations concluded, re-entered the great Audience Hall, the clock of the Palace of Justice struck its chimes.

Louis Napoleon had become, after having cannonaded it, the father of his people—History is full of these bloody ironies! AND so we come to our chief. I shall call up old impressions; I shall express judgements which fifty years of meditation have not availed to alter.

We find ourselves before a character capable, in the highest degree, of generous impulses. Yet, through the mysterious reactions of his soul, the frequent exaltations of feeling were followed, always or almost always, by profound depressions. A nature excessively nervous and sensitive, he oscillated fatally from one extreme to the other, laying bare strange deficiencies which I regarded, even in those days, as maladies — of what precise nature I could not then define, nor can I now.

The conspiracy against Napoleon III reveals in him a genius adroit in conceiving a design in broad outline. But the success of such undertakings, when analysed, proves to consist in the sum of trifles patiently worked with a chisel. Felice Orsini, in the practical act, showed so little regard for the most elementary precautions that I do not hesitate to affirm that the essential gifts of the conspirator were conspicuous in him by their complete absence.

Giuseppe Mazzini, censured by Orsini to the point of denigration — to the point of allowing him no genius save that of the spoken word — would certainly never have committed the folly of leaving England, of plunging into an enterprise of such magnitude, while forgetting behind him, intact, recoverable, all but in plain sight, the correspondence that revealed every preliminary of the attempt.

This imprudence will appear all the more egregious when it is known that he himself, in writing to me, frequently urged me, urged us, to destroy his letters and to leave not the slightest trace of them.

It weighs upon me to say it, but I must say it in the name of truth: the trouble with Felice Orsini's mind lay in an overflowing imagination that prevented him from discerning the exact contours of things, despite his pretension to being a practical man.

Having shouldered a difficult enterprise, he would construct in imagination the events which ought, through a long chain of consequence, to lead to triumph; and dwelling upon that triumph, he would exalt himself so thoroughly as to regard it as inevitable, savouring it before it had come to pass.

With an irony that seemed cruel, it was observed how Felice Orsini, on the eve of one of his desperate attempts in the Lunigiana, had carried his faith in success to the point of procuring for himself a general's uniform. One recoils, before the majesty of martyrdom, from being compelled to recall how, setting out for Paris to attempt the life of an Emperor, to initiate a revolution, he — who stood in such need of obscurity and silence — purchased a saddle-horse and rode frequently through the streets of the metropolis, as though anticipating therein the emotions of the epic moments to come.

This explains so many other things. The excessive development of one mental tendency is accomplished at the expense of other faculties, which remain all the more deficient as that tendency manifests itself more turgid and overbearing. An immoderate faith in success, which rests in substance upon an exaggerated estimate of one's own powers, is answered, too often, by a corresponding failure to perceive the true strength and character of others.

What reason led Felice Orsini to break so wrathfully with the Maestro? Let historians seek it in this or that incident, and from their point of view they are right to do so. But the primary cause is entirely psychological. Historical incidents are, in reality, nothing other than the expression assumed, in a given environment, by the deplorable flaw of a soul otherwise noble and great.

In a different field and in different measure, yet from the selfsame source, Felice Orsini was destined to treat us also — his companions in danger and in misfortune — in like fashion. The bitter words uttered during the brief absence of the Gomez, in the house in the rue Monthabor, on the evening of the 14th of January; the no less stinging phrase launched, shortly afterwards, at Pieri's address as we were making our way to the Opéra, demonstrate that a dark reaction was already fermenting in his soul even before the catastrophe.

An ill-starred incident provoked by Gomez served to lend some substance to the phantoms that troubled him. Before resolving upon a full confession, Gomez had attempted to persuade the examining magistrate that Orsini, exasperated by his hesitation, had at the moment of the imperial carriage's approach brusquely snatched the bomb from his hands, and that Orsini himself had hurled it against the Emperor. When confronted, he immediately retracted, and the matter had no legal consequence whatsoever. But it certainly did not help to restore Orsini to a just perception of reality.

He was undoubtedly in such a state when he was ensnared and beguiled by the arts of the police. The insinuation contrived against the integrity of his companions made a wide breach in a soul already so open to suspicion. And whereas before that moment, following his generous impulses, he had shown a wish to assume every responsibility for what had occurred, he now repented of this and dictated a detailed account in which, while assigning to each his share in acts already known to the Police, he let fall towards us words full of bitterness.

He then repented of his repentance and attempted, with a vigorous counter-declaration, to take every responsibility back upon himself; but at bottom the doubt remained, and the bitterness found, from time to time, the means to burst forth impetuously — always overcome thereafter, and with equal impetuosity, by feelings more consonant with his greatness.

A pendulum that bewildered us most grievously!

Even today, I could not interpret with any certainty certain of his denials, certain of his disavowals. These might have been inspired as readily by a transient determination to sever himself from us and stand alone, as by an ardent desire to open for us a path to safety, to offer us the means of softening the rigour of the fate about to strike us.

Here, to avoid misunderstanding, I must insist upon one point: the insinuations of the Police were undoubtedly intended to irritate Orsini, in order to wring from him a full confession. Orsini had his faults, but he was not a coward. He did indeed acknowledge everything that the Police showed they knew with certainty; but he took good care not to disclose particulars that were unknown to them, unless those particulars concerned himself alone.

I will say this further: no human soul could be polished and perfect on every facet of its prism. To constitute greatness, one great side sometimes suffices. Felice Orsini had more than one.

It is easy, for example, to display courage in the light of day, upon a field of battle. So easy, that I have known men transformed into heroes by a single spent bullet, who were incapable of standing without trembling before a snarling cur. The disciplined multitude carries along even the most timid and feeble individual — to keep his legs from giving way beneath him, he has, or imagines he has, the eyes of his fellow citizens fixed upon him in their thousands. But Felice Orsini, alone in his room, spreading mercury fulminate before the fireplace and drying it over the flame; with a watch in one hand and a thermometer in the other, watching for the spark that might reduce him to fragments, without leaving of his work one atom to earn him the applause of men — nothing, not even the memory of generous intentions — Felice Orsini, I say, gave proof of a courage whose temper eclipses any noisy and belligerent heroism.

There is furthermore a trait which has escaped, so far as I am aware, all who have written of him hitherto, and which reveals a courage yet more refined and sublime. The seizure of his correspondence had, among other things, revealed him as the centre from which radiated the entire clandestine operation; the point towards which, in turn, converged the concealed hatreds, the subterranean forces working to undermine the Empire. To suppress Napoleon for the mere pleasure of suppressing him would have been the work of madmen. The French political police had well understood that the blow, by its diligent preparation, must be intended to impart an impulse to broad forces, ready to reap its fruit. To lay hold of secrets which it sensed to be of vital importance was a matter to which it attached careers, wealth, power, glory — everything.

Felice Orsini found himself, therefore, in a position not of submission but of power. A single thread of the vast web, secretly surrendered, would have led him to safety, to opulence perhaps. He would have looked again upon the white crests of the mountains, the green fragrant plains, the incomparable sky of Italy; he would have conspired and fought for her still; he might have cherished the hope of witnessing her third rebirth. Nor was his heart deaf to more modest affections, no less intense — he was a father; on the banks of the Thames, a woman hoped and trembled for him. But between that tragic present hour and that rejoicing lay the blood of the generous-hearted, lay the tears of orphans. He chose instead to set forth, pale and silent, towards the scaffold. An austere greatness, worthy of an ancient Roman!

The defence of Felice Orsini arose spontaneously from the circumstances, and Giulio Favre was not the manner of intellect to ruin, through pusillanimous scruple, the splendid material those circumstances offered him. He laid upon it the powerful hand of the artist who knows the mastery of effects; of the man of heart who makes his own the trembling of a glorious and unhappy People.

His masterstroke was the letter addressed by Felice Orsini to Napoleon III. Without revealing anything, without compromising anyone, it permitted him to flash before the Judges, the jurors, and the civilised world the loftiness of our purpose. It flatters the lion who is exercising his claws in our flesh; but amid the blandishments, it insinuates, unperceived, a muffled threat. A single phrase honestly expressed suffices to pierce a wicked heart. In the silent hours, in the hours of remorse, it will reproach the broken oaths; it will exude the undying wrath of a race righteously vengeful, which was his own and which he has betrayed. The impetus of noble passions raised Giulio Favre above petty legal manoeuvres and mean things. Moving among great matters, he was able to accomplish a great defence. He did not snatch a man from the executioner, but he did something better — he restored his character. He transmitted to posterity a spirit, already so virile in itself, transfigured, as it were, by strokes of captivating beauty.
