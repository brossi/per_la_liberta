Toccai Coirà, ove dal Clementi, che trovavasi allora sotto processo come implicato nei moti internazionali, ebbi una lettera pel suo fratello minore. Questi avrebbe, a sua volta, pensato a mettermi in comunicazione coi fratelli Burrasca.

A San Maurizio, nell'Engadina, a Muentzen, il punto di contatto della Svizzera col Wurtemberg e col Tirolo, a Salzburg, a Trento nessun incidente sospetto. In quest'ultima località mi presentai alle Autorità austriache e firmai, col nome fittizio, il mio passaporto... non mi rivolsero una sola occhiata indagatrice.

E da Trento, deviando dalla linea retta, risalgo la vallata solcata dal torrente Trevis, mi abbocco col Clementi minore; mi trovo quella sera stessa a faccia a faccia coi quattro contrabbandieri.

Di loro il Clementi mi aveva detto che erano la disperazione dei cacciatori d'uomini e l'orgoglio della vallata. Gelosi della propria nomea, lasciavano agli infimi le facili imprese e mettevano il loro punto d'onore nel condurre a compimento le più arrischiate. Animi insofferenti d'ogni freno, si prestavano nobilmente anche a raddrizzar torti, a lottare per la libertà altrui. Avevano già reso servigi non indifferenti alla causa italiana.

Non mi occorsero frasi magistrali per piegarli al mio intento, e quando il maggiore dei fratelli, il capitano della piccola e coraggiosa brigata, deposto energicamente il bicchiere da cui sorseggiava, allungò la destra e strinse la mia, compresi d'aver concluso, con quel semplice atto, un patto per la vita e per la morte, ben più tenace di qualsiasi documento notarile.

Di buon mattino, rifiutata ogni guida, partii a piedi, per sentieri poco battuti che si disegnavano, a mano a mano, vividamente nella mia memoria, prima che si distendessero d'innanzi ai miei sguardi. Nei vasti orizzonti, nei subitanei precipizi, nei silenzi solenni, nei profumi alpestri ritrovavo la mia fanciullezza e n'ero così compreso che impallidivano, e quasi dileguavano, i fantasmi dell'avvenire.

Quando, dall'altura, scorsi la mia Belluno, pioveva a grosse goccie; ma il sereno era nel cuore. Stetti, sotto il mio ampio ombrello, a ricercare tra le linee indecise, la casa paterna e trovatala, mi parve che il vecchio papà e mamma e tutti i miei cari dovessero sentirmi vicino e fremente, come io li sentivo.

Giungevo alla riva dell'Ardo sull'imbrunire; ma le pioggie autunnali lo avevano ingrossato così, che il trovare un guado mi riescì impossibile. Era un contrattempo! La casa di don Bastiano sorgeva al lato opposto della città. Per arrivarvi mi vedevo ora costretto ad arrischiarmi sulla via maestra, a passare il ponte, ad attraversare, quant'era lunga, Belluno. Attesi pazientemente la notte, nascosto in una macchia.

Suonavano le dieci quando, calato l'ombrello sul viso, entravo tra l'abitato. Pochi e frettolosi i viandanti. Li scrutavo da lungi, all'incerta luce dei fanali... non uno sbirro, non un conoscente, non anima viva che mostrasse di curarsi di me... avrei detto d'essere, per i miei concittadini, uno spettro invisibile. Ma quando le case diradarono ed ebbi lasciato alle spalle ogni fanale, un subitaneo pensiero venne a turbarmi: "Se la Polizia, occhiuta e felina, mi avesse teso un tranello proprio nell'abitazione solitaria di don Bastiano?"

Entrai nel giardinetto che l'attornia, con tali precauzioni che neppure i mastini se ne accorsero. Il pianterreno... buio; chiuse tutte le griglie; chiuse quelle del piano superiore... una sola finestra sbadigliava uno sprazzo di luce che andava a perdersi tra le chiome di un doppio filare d'alberi. Tesi l'orecchio... la voce dell'Ardo, un lontano stormire di foglie, null'altro! Allora raccolsi un sassolino e lo gettai contro la finestra, mentre con moto istintivo stringevo il calcio d'una pistola.

Ed ecco nereggiare, dietro i vetri, una figura di donna... la domestica di don Bastiano, bella e tondetta dai fianchi procaci e dalle poppe impertinenti. Aperse le imposte e sussurrò nella notte: "Sei tu, Moretto?"

Don Bastiano mi accolse con un abbraccio e singhiozzando. Le sue prime parole furono: "Sia lodato il cielo! Io già disperavo della tua salvezza."

— Se le sevizie degli oppressori si possono dire novità! Da qualche giorno gli arresti inaspettati si succedono. La mia stessa casa è costantemente sorvegliata. Come hai potuto capitarvi senza molestie? Ho un vago sospetto che tu sia piovuto dal cielo!

— Salvi... anno a poche ore or sono. Ma chi potrebbe garantire del presente? Il dormire nel proprio letto è diventato un problema arduo ai tempi che corrono!

A sua richiesta, gli narrai quello che avevo visto e fatto nel mio rapido viaggio. Ed egli:

"La Provvidenza ti protegge. Tanto maggiore è il tuo obbligo di non abusarne. Ascolta, figlio mio: Se io ami la patria, lo sai. Per la sua libertà darei... tranne l'anima, tutto. Ma altro è sacrificare quello che ci appartiene, altro è mettere a repentaglio la sicurezza altrui, la vita di coloro cui si vuol bene. È una responsabilità tremenda di cui dovrei render conto, o tosto o tardi, a Tale che conta le goccie di sangue inutilmente versate e non si appaga di sillogismi. Dà retta: Queste imprese riescono... talvolta, se scoppiano improvvise, come folgore! Ma che il nemico, potentissimo, le intuisca... Carletto! Carletto! a che giovano i generosi propositi, l'ardire di quattro, di trenta, di cento giovanotti, contro un colosso che tiene in soggezione l'intera Europa? Vuoi tu porgere il collo al capestro, così a cuor leggero, per la sterile gloria che te ne potrebbe derivare? E non ha già l'Italia nostra troppi e troppi dei suoi migliori da piangere e da commiserare? Serba la tua energia a tempi più propizi. Come sia non so e non ti saprei dire; ma credo che la Polizia abbia sentore delle nostre trame. Non a casaccio si arresta. Tu non sei sicuro qui. O tempi, o tempi! L'amico ritrova l'amico e deve dolersene e deve scongiurarlo a partire. L'Austria ci fa peggiori dei barbari!"

In quella, fuori a pochi passi, risonò un colpo secco; i mastini ulularono... soffiai sulla candela.

Momenti di terribile ansietà... per don Bastiano. Io mi ero fatto, in punta di piedi, all'uscio e apertone l'occhio, vi avevo piantato la canna delle mie due pistole. Poi, pensando che lo stare in gabbia mi precludeva ogni via di campo, uscii ed andai... Una grande oscurità, un gran silenzio. Feci, per maggior precauzione, il giro della casa... il vento, una griglia sbattuta... ecco tutto! Ma quando rientrai e riaccesi la candela, fui colpito dal pallore mortale di don Bastiano.

Ed io ripensai le belle passeggiate fatte, in sua compagnia, in autunno, lungo l'Ardo, attraverso i pingui vigneti quando egli, per educarmi a sentimenti virili, mi narrava le gesta dei nostri maggiori; quando la sua sottana da sacerdote spariva agli occhi miei e me lo figuravo grave di corazza e d'elmo... tanto le sue parole erano fiere, intenso il suo entusiasmo!

Don Bastiano, avvezzo a leggere i miei pensieri sin dai miei primi anni, disse: "Non annoverarmi tra i codardi. Il mio carattere di sacerdote e questa canizie mi impongono dei doveri. Ma tratto il dado... ritto e sereno sino alla fine!"

La mattina per tempo, mentre io, stanco del lungo viaggio, dormivo ancora saporitamente, egli si recò ad avvertire papà del mio arrivo. Trovai il fiero vecchio ancora vegeto, benché sul suo nobile volto scorgessi rughe che m'erano ignote. Ma il nostro incontro fu diverso da quello che mi aspettavo. È probabile che don Bastiano lo avesse indotto a gettare sulla bilancia tutto il peso dell'autorità paterna per distogliermi da un'impresa tanto arrischiata. Non abbracci, non baci; una stretta di mano poderosa, ma solitaria e subito, rincrudite e fatte quasi sarcastiche, le stesse esortazioni che avevo udito la sera, dalla bocca del buon prete. La nostra discussione si animò a tal segno da riuscirmi penosissima. Decisi di troncarla. "Quando mi venne in uggia — dissi — l'uniforme e il pane dei ladri, non mi aspettavo né feste, né fiori. La sottomissione passiva non si addice all'animo di un Rudio. E poi che tu me la consigli... orbene, io continuerò a battere la mia via, solo. Da questo momento non ho più padre!" Il povero vecchio rimase per un momento con un gesto sospeso, come se si fosse improvvisamente cristallizzato; poi le sue ciglia si riempirono di lagrime. Si lanciò con impeto improvviso e mi abbracciò e mi tenne stretto con una forza di cui non l'avrei creduto capace. Quando potè parlare: "Sei tu, sei tu Carletto!... riconosco il sangue mio. Va'! sinché le forze mi bastino, sarò con te!"

Fu deciso di non disseppellire le armi sinché l'animo degli ardimentosi, che dovevano maneggiarle, non fosse preparato e ben disposto. Papà e don Bastiano si incaricarono di abbordarli. Nel frattempo io avrei, per prudenza, cercato un rifugio meno vigilato dalla Polizia. Nei dintorni di Belluno, verso il settentrione, dove il piano confina colle altare, sorgeva un castagno secolare noto, e quasi famoso, nella città e nel contado per le straordinarie sue dimensioni. Si convenne che io mi sarei recato, col favore delle tenebre, sotto le sue fronde. Persona fidata sarebbe venuta, verso la mezzanotte, ad indicarmi dove avrei potuto posare il capo. Segnale di riconoscimento, tre fischi, ai quali avrei risposto fischiando quattro volte.

Alle nove ero già disteso sull'erba e fantasticavo contemplando la luna attraverso il nero fogliame; poi la luna tramontò e il vento riprincipiò la sua corsa sfrenata. Mi alzai per sgranchirmi e mi appostai dietro il tronco enorme.

Dalla torre di Belluno si incalzarono, nello spazio, dodici rintocchi e l'occhio, avvezzo ormai all'oscurità, corse indagando tra macchie e vigneti. Un'altra lunga ora trascorse... fui preso da tristi presentimenti.

D'improvviso, sommessamente, timidamente, al di là di un gruppo di cespugli, si succedono i tre fischi... poi un bisbiglio... "All'erta, Moretto! Sono parecchi!" Guardai intensamente e scorsi, attraverso i ramoscelli, qualcosa... come il cappotto di uno sbirro. Se non feci fuoco lì per lì, fu per tema di sbagliare il bersaglio e di trovarmi, senza difesa, di fronte al nemico. Risposi al segnale e attesi con ambo le armi puntate...

Oh qual sangue e quanti rimorsi mormora un istante d'esitazione! Un'ombra si fece innanzi a braccia tese... mia madre!

## Chapter the Eighteenth

I touched Coirà, where from Clementi, who found himself at that time under trial as one implicated in the international risings, I received a letter for his younger brother. The latter would, in his turn, have seen to putting me in communication with the Burrasca brothers.

At San Maurizio, in the Engadine, at Muentzen, the point of contact between Switzerland and Württemberg and the Tyrol, at Salzburg, at Trento no suspicious incident. At this last place I presented myself to the Austrian Authorities and signed, under the assumed name, my passport... they did not cast upon me so much as a single searching glance.

And from Trento, deviating from the direct route, I ascend again the valley furrowed by the torrent Trevis, meet with the younger Clementi; and that very same evening find myself face to face with the four smugglers.

Of them Clementi had told me that they were the despair of the man-hunters and the pride of the valley. Jealous of their own *nomea* — their reputation — they left to lesser men the easy enterprises and made it a point of honour to bring to completion the most hazardous. Spirits intolerant of every restraint, they lent themselves nobly also to righting wrongs, to fighting for the liberty of others. They had already rendered services of no small account to the Italian cause.

I had no need of masterly phrases to bend them to my purpose, and when the eldest of the brothers, the captain of the small and courageous band, set down with energy the glass from which he had been sipping, stretched out his right hand and gripped mine, I understood that I had concluded, with that simple act, a pact for life and death, far more binding than any notarial document.

Early in the morning, having refused every guide, I set out on foot, by little-trodden paths which traced themselves, little by little, vividly in my memory, before they spread out before my eyes. In the vast horizons, in the sudden precipices, in the solemn silences, in the alpine fragrances I recovered my boyhood and was so absorbed in it that the phantoms of the future grew pale and almost vanished away.

When, from the height, I descried my Belluno, the rain was falling in heavy drops; but serenity was in my heart. I stood, beneath my ample umbrella, seeking out among the indistinct lines the house of my father, and having found it, it seemed to me that the old papa and mamma and all my dear ones must feel me near and trembling, as I felt them.

I reached the bank of the Ardo at dusk; but the autumn rains had swollen it so that finding a ford proved impossible. It was a setback! The house of don Bastiano stood on the opposite side of the city. To reach it I now saw myself compelled to risk the high road, to cross the bridge, to traverse the whole length of Belluno. I waited patiently for the night, hidden in a thicket.

The clocks were striking ten when, with my umbrella lowered over my face, I entered among the dwellings. Few and hurrying were the wayfarers. I scrutinised them from afar, in the uncertain light of the lanterns... not a spy, not an acquaintance, not a living soul who showed any concern for me... I might have thought myself, in the eyes of my fellow-citizens, an invisible ghost. But when the houses thinned and I had left every lantern behind me, a sudden thought came to disturb me: "What if the Police, sharp-eyed and feline, had laid a trap for me in the very solitary dwelling of don Bastiano?"

I entered the little garden that surrounds it with such precautions that even the mastiffs took no notice. The ground floor... dark; all the shutters closed; those of the upper storey closed also... a single window yawned open upon a gleam of light that was lost among the boughs of a double row of trees. I strained my ear... the voice of the Ardo, a distant rustling of leaves, nothing more! Then I gathered up a small stone and cast it against the window, while with an instinctive movement I gripped the butt of a pistol.

And lo, darkening behind the panes, a woman's figure... don Bastiano's serving-maid, handsome and plump, with ample hips and impertinent bosom. She threw open the shutters and whispered into the night: "Is it you, Moretto?"

Don Bastiano received me with an embrace and with sobbing. His first words were: "Praised be heaven! I had already despaired of your safety."

— If the cruelties of the oppressors can be called news! These past few days the unexpected arrests have followed one upon another. My very house is under constant surveillance. How could you have made your way here without mishap? I have a vague suspicion that you have dropped from the sky!

— Safe... and arrived but a few hours since. But who could vouch for the present? To sleep in one's own bed has become an arduous problem in these times!

At his request, I related to him what I had seen and done in my rapid journey. And he:

"Providence protects you. So much the greater is your obligation not to abuse its protection. Listen, my son: That I love my country, you know. For her liberty I would give... everything save my soul. But one thing is to sacrifice what is our own, another is to imperil the safety of others, the lives of those we love. It is a tremendous responsibility for which I should have to render account, sooner or later, to One who counts the drops of blood uselessly shed and is not appeased by syllogisms. Heed me: These enterprises succeed... at times, if they burst forth suddenly, like a thunderbolt! But let the enemy, all-powerful as he is, but divine them... Carletto! Carletto! what avail the generous resolves, the daring of four, of thirty, of a hundred young men, against a colossus that holds all Europe in subjection? Would you lay your neck in the noose, thus light-heartedly, for the sterile glory that might come of it? And has not our Italy already too many and too many of her best to mourn and to pity? Reserve your energy for more propitious times. How it shall come about I do not know and could not tell you; but I believe that the Police have some inkling of our plots. They do not arrest at random. You are not safe here. O the times, the times! Friend meets friend and must grieve at it and must conjure him to depart. Austria makes us worse than barbarians!"

At that moment, outside a few paces away, a sharp crack rang out; the mastiffs howled... I blew out the candle.

Moments of terrible anxiety... for don Bastiano. I had gone, on tiptoe, to the door and, opening its peephole, had planted the barrels of my two pistols there. Then, thinking that to remain caged would cut off every avenue of escape, I went out and advanced... Great darkness, great silence. As a further precaution I made the circuit of the house... the wind, a shutter banging... that was all! But when I returned and relit the candle, I was struck by the deathly pallor of don Bastiano.

And I thought again of the fine walks taken, in his company, in autumn, along the Ardo, through the rich vineyards, when he, to educate me in manly sentiments, would recount to me the deeds of our forebears; when his priestly cassock disappeared from my eyes and I pictured him grave in breastplate and helmet... so fierce were his words, so intense his enthusiasm!

Don Bastiano, accustomed to reading my thoughts since my earliest years, said: "Do not number me among the cowards. My character as a priest and these grey hairs lay duties upon me. But once the die is cast... upright and serene to the end!"

Early in the morning, while I, wearied from the long journey, still slept soundly, he went to inform papa of my arrival. I found the proud old man still vigorous, though upon his noble face I could discern wrinkles that were unknown to me. But our meeting was other than I had expected. It is probable that don Bastiano had persuaded him to throw into the balance the full weight of paternal authority, to dissuade me from so hazardous an enterprise. No embraces, no kisses; a powerful handshake, but a solitary one, and then at once, sharpened and grown almost sardonic, the same exhortations I had heard the evening before from the lips of the good priest. Our discussion grew so heated as to become most painful to me. I resolved to cut it short. "When the uniform and the bread of thieves grew odious to me," I said, "I expected neither festivities nor flowers. Passive submission does not befit the spirit of a Rudio. And since you counsel it to me... well then, I shall continue to walk my own path, alone. From this moment I have no longer a father!" The poor old man remained a moment with a gesture suspended, as though he had been suddenly turned to stone; then his eyelashes filled with tears. He flung himself forward with a sudden impulse and embraced me and held me close with a strength of which I should not have thought him capable. When he was able to speak: "It is you, it is you Carletto!... I recognise my own blood. Go! So long as my strength holds, I shall be with you!"

It was decided not to unearth the arms until the spirits of the bold men who were to handle them should be prepared and well disposed. Papa and don Bastiano undertook to approach them. In the meantime I should, by way of prudence, seek out a refuge less closely watched by the Police. In the environs of Belluno, towards the north, where the plain borders upon the heights, there stood an ancient chestnut tree, known, and almost celebrated, in the city and throughout the countryside, for its extraordinary dimensions. It was agreed that I should repair, under cover of darkness, beneath its boughs. A trusted person would come, towards midnight, to indicate to me where I might lay my head. The recognition signal: three whistles, to which I was to respond with four.

By nine o'clock I was already stretched upon the grass, musing as I contemplated the moon through the dark foliage; then the moon set and the wind resumed its unbridled course. I rose to stretch my limbs and posted myself behind the enormous trunk.

From the tower of Belluno twelve strokes rang out upon the air, one upon another, and my eye, now grown accustomed to the darkness, ran searchingly among thickets and vineyards. Another long hour passed... I was seized by dark forebodings.

All at once, softly, timidly, from beyond a clump of bushes, the three whistles followed in succession... then a whisper... "On guard, Moretto! There are several of them!" I gazed intently and discerned, through the twigs, something... like the greatcoat of a spy. That I did not fire then and there was from fear of missing my target and finding myself defenceless before the enemy. I answered the signal and waited with both weapons levelled...

Oh what blood and what remorse does a moment's hesitation murmur! A shadow advanced with arms outstretched... my mother!

A friend finds his friend and must grieve for it and must implore him to depart. Austria makes us worse than barbarians!"

At that moment, a few steps away outside, a sharp crack resounded; the mastiffs howled... I blew out the candle.

Moments of terrible anxiety... for Don Bastiano. I had gone, on tiptoe, to the door and, opening its peephole, had planted the barrels of my two pistols there. Then, thinking that being in a cage precluded any way of escape, I went out and... A great darkness, a great silence. As a greater precaution, I made a circuit of the house... the wind, a slamming grille... that was all! But when I re-entered and relit the candle, I was struck by Don Bastiano's deathly pallor.

And I thought back on the beautiful walks taken in his company, in autumn, along the Ardo, through the rich vineyards, when he, to educate me in virile sentiments, would narrate the deeds of our forefathers; when his priest's cassock would vanish from my eyes and I would picture him grave in cuirass and helm... so proud were his words, so intense his enthusiasm!

Don Bastiano, accustomed to reading my thoughts since my earliest years, said: "Do not count me among the cowards. My character as a priest and these white hairs impose duties upon me. But once the die is cast... upright and serene until the end!"

Early in the morning, while I, tired from the long journey, was still sleeping soundly, he went to inform my father of my arrival. I found the proud old man still vigorous, although on his noble face I discerned wrinkles that were unknown to me. But our meeting was different from what I had expected. It is probable that Don Bastiano had induced him to throw upon the scales the full weight of his paternal authority to dissuade me from so risky an undertaking. No embraces, no kisses; a powerful, but solitary handshake, and immediately, exacerbated and made almost sarcastic, the same exhortations I had heard the evening before from the mouth of the good priest. Our discussion became so animated as to be most painful to me. I decided to cut it short. "When the uniform and the bread of thieves became loathsome to me," I said, "I expected neither feasts nor flowers. Passive submission does not befit the soul of a Rudio. And since you advise it of me... very well, I shall continue to walk my path, alone. From this moment I have no longer a father!" The poor old man remained for a moment with a suspended gesture, as if he had suddenly been turned to crystal; then his eyelashes filled with tears. He lunged with sudden impetus and embraced me and held me tight with a strength of which I would not have thought him capable. When he was able to speak: "It is you, it is you Carletto!... I recognize my own blood. Go! So long as my strength suffices, I will be with you!"

It was decided not to unearth the arms until the spirit of the daring ones who were to handle them was prepared and well-disposed. Papa and Don Bastiano took it upon themselves to approach them. In the meantime I would, for prudence, seek a refuge less watched by the Police. In the environs of Belluno, towards the north, where the plain borders the high ground, stood an ancient chestnut tree, known, and almost famous, in the city and the countryside for its extraordinary dimensions. It was agreed that I would go, under the cover of darkness, beneath its boughs. A trusted person would come, towards midnight, to show me where I could lay my head. The recognition signal: three whistles, to which I would respond by whistling four times.

At nine o'clock I was already stretched out on the grass, fantasizing as I contemplated the moon through the black foliage; then the moon set and the wind began its unbridled race anew. I got up to stretch my legs and posted myself behind the enormous trunk.

From the tower of Belluno, twelve chimes pursued one another through space, and my eye, by now accustomed to the darkness, ran searching among thickets and vineyards. Another long hour passed... I was seized by grim premonitions.

Suddenly, softly, timidly, from beyond a group of bushes, the three whistles follow one another... then a whisper... "On your guard, Moretto! There are several of them!" I looked intently and made out, through the small branches, something... like the greatcoat of a constable. If I did not fire then and there, it was for fear of missing the target and finding myself, without defence, before the enemy. I answered the signal and waited with both weapons aimed...

CHAPTER SEVENTEEN

IN LOCARNO, where I had returned to deliver a missive from Mazzini to Calvi, I was arrested and released a few hours later, at liberty, on my word to present myself to the Federal Delegate. I was later tried for conspiracy against a friendly Power, expelled from Switzerland, and put to the alternative of choosing between America and England.

England was closer to the field of probable future enterprises and was preferred by me. I settled in London, where I found not a few of those compromised in the February uprisings, who had escaped Austrian vengeance through adventurous flights... among others, a Milanese Jean Valjean, accompanied out of the city walls in a coffin and with a solemn funeral.

Bound by our common ideal and common sufferings, we lived for some time in community, in the district called Baldwin's Garden, and precisely in the modest inn of the Milanese Tressoldi, a good-natured fellow who possessed, among his other virtues, that of turning a blind eye when we could not settle the bill.

Giuseppe Mazzini arrived in London in April. From him we learned that Pianciani, who was then dictating his "History of the Popes," tempered the fatigues of the intellect by modifying and re-modifying the garden of his villa... a probability of work for several of us. We were presented to his amanuensis, a certain Bardini from Padova, a law student, and he, in turn, presented us to Pianciani who, there and then, entrusted the material labour of those incessant modifications to the young Varisco, granting him ample faculty to avail himself of the work of as many of us as he deemed necessary. I too was immediately engaged, and thus, for some months, I drew my sustenance from the spade and the flowers.

That simple and laborious life was troubled by terrible images. Austria was growing ever more ferocious, and every newspaper, every letter brought us the painful certainty that the phalanx of the generous was being inexorably thinned.

I spoke of it on long walks with Giuseppe Mazzini, and I would fix my gaze upon that olive-hued, intellectual face, as if to say: "What is to be decided? Shall we let them be slaughtered like dogs?" The Master would quicken his pace. His expression was one of ineffable melancholy; but of the thoughts that swirled in his head... not a syllable!

One day in August, he sent for me with urgency. Why ever for! Certainly not to say "good day" or "good evening." So then?..

I planted the spade in the middle of a flowerbed: "Stay there! You are not the only noble instrument!" And off I ran.

But when I stood before the Master, I felt my arms fall to my sides, so sparing of words was his welcome. I was asked to carry a letter to Kossuth and to await the reply. Not another syllable!

Kossuth then held, near Regent's Circus, a sort of Court. At the door I found carriages and servants in livery; up the stairs, a coming and going of staid Englishmen in formal attire; Hungarians in their elegant, militaresque national costume; people with broad scars; uncertain figures, begging under the pretext of the Fatherland. Honest and dishonest, rich or poor, they rarely obtained access to the Dictator who, even beaten and in exile, exercised not only the prestige of his fame, but also that which comes from the habit of command.

While I awaited his pleasure in the antechamber, I tried to pass the time observing the paintings that hung from the walls: Kossuth in the guise of the simple citizen; Kossuth in hunting attire; Kossuth in a general's uniform — now in an armchair, now on horseback, now beside a shattered cannon; but always Kossuth, nothing but Kossuth!

Finally, from among so many simulacra, the great man appeared in the flesh. I must admit that art, in its intent to idealise him, had slandered him. The figure of the illustrious Magyar, more than affected heroism and slaughter, recalled paternal affections and the gentle emotions of the domestic hearth. It is the privilege of artists to alter, at their whim, the colours of History... for this reason Napoleon I is handed down to posterity crossing the Saint Bernard on a prancing colt, with manifest injustice to the poor donkey that lent its back to so great a weight.

The welcome reserved for me was not what the Count di Rudio had expected — nor the Italian patriot, nor the trusted envoy of Giuseppe Mazzini. Kossuth read the letter I handed him while standing on the threshold and, casting it aside, drew a memorandum from his pocket, wrote a few lines, and ordered me, curtly, curtly, to carry them to their destination. Not a word of greeting! And I, descending the stairs, asked myself whether the Cossacks of the Czar and the Croats of Jellacich, by lending their strength to Austria, had not, in substance, spared the brave Hungarians the vexations attendant upon a change of dynasty.

The few lines consigned to me by Kossuth were addressed to a high officer whose name I am now seeking in the recesses of memory. From him I received several rolls of sterling... "The same," he told me, "that we received from Mazzini for our participation in the movements of 1853. May they serve your great, unfortunate country!"

Kossuth, on the morrow of that failure, had publicly denied all complicity in those movements!

Upon my return, the Master opened his soul to me. He began by asking whether I felt disposed to place my life once more in jeopardy for the cause of the Fatherland and, having received the answer from my very bearing, he pointed me to a chair, and we conversed in low voices, the one opposite the other, for over an hour.

He explained to me that, on the counsel of Fortunato Calvi, the future insurrectionary attempts were to unfold in the region of the Alps. The reasons he adduced are the following: Our country is rich in latent forces, ready to burst forth; but whoever takes an initiative must know how to give assurance of serious resistance. The mountain is the natural bulwark of the weak and the oppressed. There it becomes possible for the few to hold their ground against the many; possible to escape pursuit; to descend with the swiftness of the eagle upon the plain; to surprise, to intercept. For the pitched battle, which was denied to us, it was necessary to substitute guerrilla warfare, which is necessarily the daughter of the mountains. If the banner of redemption could be kept upright and flying for three, for two months, there would not be a patriot without fever in his blood, not a corner of Italy where the flame of action would not blaze.

This last thought seemed to lead him into another order of reflection. A shadow spread across his face; then he said, almost in these very words: "I know! Cowardice, masked as prudence, accuses me of driving spirited youth to the gallows, of provoking desperate attempts that offer Austrian tyranny, and that which is home-bred, the opportunity to intensify their cruelties. But they do not reflect that the audacity and sacrifice of the few always end by shaking the torpid from their lethargy and driving the many to action. Nothing great without audacity! Napoleon III would in all probability never have smoothed his path to the Empire without the failures of Strasbourg and of Boulogne. No King of Italy will show spirit so great as to champion the redemption of our Fatherland, so long as Italians show themselves to be basking, in peace, in slavery. Would Carlo Alberto have crossed the Ticino were it not for the magnanimous self-abnegation of the Milanese! It is an imperative necessity, this keeping the great Italian question open and pressing. Europe cannot have peace if our agony endures. She will intervene, sooner or later, in our favour, and the sooner the more we show ourselves intolerant of the yoke and menacing. And what use are sound ideas, vigorous intentions, when want of initiative relegates them to the realm of mere abstractions! Action cleanses our centuries-old faults. Between us and a free Italy lies this baptism of blood. Should we hold back for the slanders of cowards!"

That very evening I procured a passport under an assumed name and set out for Switzerland. Fortunato Calvi was already awaiting me in Zurich. "The plan for the new movements is his," Mazzini had told me. "It falls to him to see to its execution. You will receive the necessary instructions from his own mouth."

In Brussels, where I made a brief halt, I delivered a letter from the Master to dall'Ungaro, the Venetian Catullus. "Where is the Master, what is he doing?" he asked me, almost feverishly. And I, in his own verses:

*"Mazzini è in sul loco ove si trema*
*"Che giunga a' traditor l'ora suprema;*
*"Mazzini è in ogni loco ove si opera*
*"Versar il sangue per l'Italia intera!"*

["Mazzini is in the place where men tremble / lest the supreme hour come upon the traitors; / Mazzini is in every place where men labour / to shed their blood for all of Italy!"]

Having arrived in Zurich, I wished, before going to Calvi, to have my hands free and hastened to pay a visit to de Boni, to whom I likewise delivered a missive; a third I carried to Caronti of Como. Giuseppe Mazzini had charged me to be very much on my guard with this man, of whom he was highly suspicious. He did, however, contribute sums of some considerable amount to the good cause, and this restrained Mazzini from being rid of him.

The brazenly inquisitive questions that Caronti directed at me came to me, already ill-disposed as I was, as a sharp irritant, and when I set foot once more beyond the threshold of his villa, I felt a relief as if I had suddenly been freed from a nightmare.

At last I could restore myself in the embrace of an authentic patriot, of an untarnished conscience... Fortunato Calvi! What joy in seeing him again, still handsome, still proud, still exhaling that indefinable sense of serenity which breathes forth from honest souls!

The substantial part of our colloquy had a beginning which I shall recount without false modesty.

"You are not," he said to me, "and you must not be, an instrument for blindly executing orders you do not comprehend. I shall confide to you the plan I have devised and which the European Revolutionary Committee has fully approved. Thus you will know how to parry, with unity of purpose, the inevitable counterblows. If we succeed in something concrete, you will be my aide-de-camp; if we do not succeed... we shall, in all probability, be made equal in our rewards... martyrdom!" My preliminary charge was to proceed into the valley of Treviso and make contact with certain smugglers, surnamed the Burrasca brothers, so as to induce them to carry a large consignment of munitions into the Agordino. I then had to cross the border, enter Belluno clandestinely, and make contact with Don Bastiano Barozzi, who had returned thither and who was expecting me. Barozzi knew of certain arms hidden in 1848 upon the return of the oppressors. In the Cadore, rebellious spirits abounded who had covered themselves with glory in its defence; the name and exhortation of Fortunato Calvi, who had been the guiding genius of that defence, would have rekindled in their breasts the slumbering enthusiasm. It was necessary to distribute among them the old arms. A band of exiles awaited the signal to descend from the Alps... Fortunato Calvi, leading them, would direct their fortunes.

The Austrian colossus was to be struck precisely at Belluno, and with a threefold surprise: at the Finance Office, at the Gendarmerie, and at the Barracks of the regular troops.

The Finance Office, situated near the old church of Santo Stefano, a solid building erected to serve as a convent, frames a vast courtyard with porticoes. Between one column and the next extends a parapet, so low that a man, creeping along it and on all fours, cannot be spotted by a sentry who, even while keeping watch, patrols the other parts of the arcade. The main gate had no doors and was left unguarded; the custodians of the building, a small body of militia quartered at the far end of the courtyard, to the right... three men in all and a corporal.

In the dead of night, a handful of our men in ambush near the gate; one bold spirit to creep, barefoot, along the parapet and take the sentry from behind... a hushed signal... men come running!... the three militiamen sleeping are rendered powerless. A bold enterprise, not an arduous one!

It was autumn, the season of tax collection; the coffers of the Administration, according to reliable calculations, were to contain a million and a half florins. To seize them... the sinews of war!

Simultaneously, another band of patriots was to conceal themselves near the Gendarmes' Barracks. They were to simulate a brawl and ring the bell of the Gendarmerie, as if to summon assistance. The guard who opens the door is to be dispatched. The lair is to be stormed, and the thirty manaclers sleeping there compelled to pass, by force, into the category of the manacled.

The garrison of Belluno at that time consisted of one hundred and fifty soldiers. The barracks were approached, to the right and to the left, by a circular ramping road — a *tourniquet*, as the engineers call it — flanked by trees and benches, a favourite resort of nursemaids and urchins. As a general rule, when the alarm sounded, the small garrison would emerge, in straggling fashion, from the main gate and draw up on the esplanade, awaiting orders. It was necessary to conceal a body of picked marksmen along the edges of the ramping road, to sound the alarm, and to shoot down as many soldiers as should come running.

The threefold surprise accomplished, the insurgents were to take possession of the civil and ecclesiastical authorities... hostages! Then we seize the means of transport, don the Austrian uniform, and proceed in military fashion to the fortress of Osoppo in Friuli, taking prisoners and compelling all whom we encounter on the road to follow us, so as to forestall any indiscretion.

At this point Fortunato Calvi, who had been speaking throughout in a low voice, almost as though meditating upon his own phrases, grew animated and said vibratingly: "We approach the fortress preceded by the Austrian flag and beating the drum, as friends. Once across the bridge, once the foot is set beneath the archway, the scene changes — the Latin valour bursts forth. On the ramparts, twenty-two of our own, we raise our flag, and woe betide whoever assails it! The hostages remain with us, with us the arms, the money, and the audacity. Osoppo becomes our base of operations, and it is an impregnable stronghold. Through the winter we organise the guerrilla warfare in the Alps, we hold in check the troops that would descend into the Peninsula. In the spring, Lombardy, Venetia, Hungary, France perhaps as well, second the movement... the oppressed and the oppressors measure their forces. The errors of the past are our instruction; the age-old vow is realised; Italy rises again, never to fall again."

I listened with bowed head, thinking what any other man would have thought: "To what extent could we count upon the ability of the European Revolutionary Committee to extend our initiative, to transform it into an international uprising?"

Could I hold back when an entity such as Fortunato Calvi was throwing himself entirely into the breach?

Three days, and I was on my way again.
