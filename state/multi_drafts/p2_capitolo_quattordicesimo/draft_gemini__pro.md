FROM that moment I no longer had any doubt as to the fate that was reserved for me, and if I resolved to struggle to the end, it was solely, as they say, for honor’s sake; so that I might exclaim, upon reaching the bottom of the abyss: "I have done everything humanly possible not to fall into it!"

That very day, I was forced to convince myself that an overwhelming will was working in the opposite direction, strengthening the means honorably granted to it with fraud and with perjury. I was locked in a cell on the ground floor. I was given a companion... an agent of the secret Police, in civilian clothes, who announced to me that he would spend the day and night with me. Certainly not to keep me cheerful—I thought—but to coax from me some word that might escape me, in the travail of my soul, during my sleep.

He seemed to perceive my thought and begged me not to judge by appearances. If he could do me no good, he certainly would do me no harm.

Half an hour later, the bolts shrieked and the heavy door swung on its hinges. Two people entered: the Examining Judge and a man dressed as a municipal sweeper, with an empty eye socket, horribly scarred. Two guards remained standing upright on the threshold.

"Well then!" said the Judge, after a brief silence.

"It is he!"

"Are you quite certain?"

"As certain as I am baptized."

"Repeat your story."

"I was on duty at the Opéra, and I was throwing sand on the entrance reserved for His Majesty. It must have been seven or a quarter past seven, at the most. I saw two gentlemen who were chatting in low voices, gesticulating. They had the air of studying the locality. I warned them that that was not a place for the public and asked them to leave. They told me to mind my own business. The gentleman who is here said: 'Be quiet, you scoundrel!' I made a move to punch him, and both of them cleared off."

"And this happened?"

"Yesterday evening, the fourteenth of January."

"An hour or a little before the attack on His Majesty the Emperor?"

"An hour or a little more."

I had understood, perfectly understood! They wanted to establish, in a way that would seem indisputable, our presence at the scene of the attack. For Orsini there was his wound; for Gomez, the arrest made in front of the Opera house. To cook my goose and Pieri’s, they had put into that unfortunate man’s mouth a little story invented from whole cloth.

Nevertheless, I did not feel that rage which drives one to fury, and I contented myself with asking my strange accuser how much they had promised him in payment for his lies. He turned his remaining greenish eye elsewhere and did not reply.

Late in the evening, a second agent was locked in my cell. As soon as he entered, he greeted me and, offering me his right hand, said that he too would not leave me, day or night. He too was being locked up, as far as he could surmise, to prevent any communication between me and the accomplices I was suspected of having on the outside. He hoped that all three of us would, like good comrades, endeavor not to embitter our imprisonment with useless friction.

Several days of relative quiet passed. No contact with the outside world, from which little air, less sun, and, on rare occasions, the turnkey penetrated my cell. The latter had orders not to speak either with me or with my guards, who thus became the only human beings with whom I was permitted to communicate my thoughts. And I made ample use of them to narrate to them the history of Austria’s horrible persecutions and the sorrows they caused in my Country. One makes propaganda where one can and as the opportunity arises.

But this was not enough to save me from periods of profound prostration, in which the image of Elisa and of the child, for whom I had destroyed the joy of that simple age, the maternal smile, arose vividly before me. And my thoughts then also ran to my companions in misfortune, of whom I knew nothing except that they were, like me, locked up in Mazas; subject to the same snares, plunging inexorably toward the identical destiny. In those moments I would sit apart, on the plank bed, motionless, my eyes on the floor. And here I owe my guards a word of thanks—a trifle late, to be sure—who, so as not to disturb my meditations, walked on tiptoe.

Another of my tormenting preoccupations derived from the certainty that my forced inaction must be matched by a feverish activity on the part of the Police. I tried to put myself, so to speak, in the shoes of the Authorities and, to train myself for the defense. But every effort mocked all my faculties, and the subtle conjectures and complicated reconstructions ended in this: in nervous irritation, in exhaustion. Then nothing was left to me but to resort to the great remedy of youth: either I slept, in whatever way, or I renounced action.

The first certain sign of the inextricable net that was about to envelop me, I received when, led under heavy guard to the Chancellor’s office, I saw placed before my eyes first a true copy, then the original itself, of a letter I had written from London to Felice Orsini. In it, I informed him how a certain Carlotti, from Brescia, had, to my great surprise, spoken to me of our conspiracy. I had good reason to suspect this man of being a spy in the service of the French Government. He had to be set aside. If he already knew too much... either get rid of him in some way, or renounce the action.

Felice Orsini, more a hot-blooded patriot than a prudent conspirator, had kept among his papers so compromising a document, and the English Police, at the urging of Paris, had carried out a search of his home and seized it.

My close connection with the conspirators, my knowledge of their plots, appeared all too well established. Another good stretch down the fatal slope! And when I attempted, for form’s sake, to deny the undeniable, the Examining Judge, for all response, smiled in a mocking way and sent me back to the dark, launching at me, by way of a farewell, the crushing phrase: "Oh, we have plenty of other proofs! and your accomplices have fully corroborated them!"

The following day I was transferred from Mazas, in a closed carriage, to the Conciergerie. They assigned me cell No. 1, and I soon learned that Pieri was locked in No. 2, Gomez in No. 3, Orsini in No. 4.

My new holiday resort had a surface area of, approximately, ten feet by fifteen. The light entered it timidly through a small window with thick iron bars, situated near the ceiling. In the door, at the height of a man, was a circular eye... a five-franc piece would have passed through it with difficulty. It allowed the wardens to watch my every move; it allowed me to observe, up to a certain point, what was happening in the corridor. On the walls, the hand of the prisoners who preceded me had undoubtedly engraved verses and maxims, later scraped inexorably away by the turnkeys’ knives.

What shreds of heart and what reflections of souls refined by sorrow, snatched from my meditation by the petty observance of duty! The story of one of those souls I knew, in all its particulars, from my boyhood. It was precisely from my cell that Marshal Ney had left, to have his patriotic breast shattered by the lead of the Bourbons!

At the Conciergerie too I had two guards. I must say something about them. The more likable was a former petty officer of the navy. The State, having squeezed his youth from him, had saved him from the streets, entrusting him with tasks of a dubious sort which, evidently, were repugnant to his soul. A numerous, poor, and unsupported family kept him bound to his fate; but his resignation was only apparent, and I soon realized that his secret rancor, finding no way to manifest itself, was accumulating, drop by drop, in his heart. Nature had denied him the determination and the impulse that drive men to virile acts; if indeed a foolish discipline, too long endured, had not severed all his willpower. But he still admired in others the virtues that were not in him, and his instincts revealed themselves, in the substratum and perhaps even unknown to himself, as rebellious. The years have erased his surname from my memory. His Christian name was, if I am not mistaken, very common among the people of Paris, a mythological name: Saturno!

The other guard was a former marine infantryman. A rather plump man, of few words, he spent hours upon hours seated, with his hands on his belly, his fingers interlaced, placidly rotating his two thumbs one around the other, now in one direction, now in the other. He was fond of dozing.

At the Conciergerie, I received the first inkling of what was happening in the world. The two guards kept their lips sealed on this subject. But other persons, employed in the service of the prison, provided for my thirst with a very simple expedient: they would plant themselves in front of the cell and converse, in a loud voice, amongst themselves.

I thus came to learn how public opinion, which at first had shown itself to be against us, had rapidly changed in our favor. The substance of entire newspaper articles paraded through the tiny circular eye, to my ear. Among them, those translated from the English press seemed to me especially explicit and almost laudatory.

But why did the staff of the Conciergerie take such trouble? I thought I guessed it on the day I heard a warden state that "in this affair (ours) mysterious and powerful influences were at play." He had added:

"It is not difficult to foresee that none of the four prisoners will be executed." I deduced that he who had spoken thus was not entirely a stranger to the direct inspiration of the mysterious influences to which he had permitted himself to allude.

Now we come to the precipice! From the end of the corridor a voice cries: "Prisoner of cell No. 4!" It was the cell of Felice Orsini. I run, with my eye, to the circular eye. Now see how the vexation of a great enterprise failed and the consciousness of one’s own responsibility can consume the existence of a man, even an undaunted one! Felice Orsini passed rapidly through the brief radius of my vision; but it was enough for me to perceive, on that sculptural head, the traces of the tempest that must have been raging within. It had become almost completely white-haired.

Half an hour of sad reflections passes, and I hear Orsini’s step returning. Immediately, at the end of the corridor, the identical voice: "Prisoner of cell No. 1!"

I was made to enter a long, narrow room. The only window that illuminated it was in front of me. At a desk, near the window, sat the usual Examining Judge and another individual dressed in black, the Imperial Prosecutor. But my attention was violently attracted by a third figure... seated not far from the desk, to the right of the Judge. Her profile stood out sharply against the light from the window. I knew that profile... it was of a small woman, slender, dark-haired, not beautiful in the classical sense, but very attractive, and at the sight of her, the impression of a persistent smile of hers reawakened in my soul. It was Miss Elisa Cheney, the friend of Felice Orsini.

I disguised the turmoil that seized me so intensely as to prevent me from distinguishing the other people around me, and I barely noticed a second lady who, seated at a table near the entrance, was resting her arm upon it and, upon her arm, hiding her face.

The interrogation proceeded with a celerity that seemed to me like lightning. The Judge first addressed the English lady, speaking to her in her language.

"Is this the man who brought the letter found on your mantelpiece and to whom you handed a valise belonging to Mr. Felice Orsini?"

"It is."

"Count, what do you think of that?"

The chivalrous folk, seated at table, in a warm room, with their feet on soft carpets, will perhaps not be able to forgive my boorishness. But it was a matter of saving one's head from the guillotine and of preserving it for a better use. I replied: "How can that woman claim to know me? She must have seen me some night, fleetingly, while she was walking the pavements of Haymarket. The testimony of a dubious female is of no account."

The young lady grew pale and, in a voice broken with anger: "Examine the valise. On the leather, at the bottom... in indelible letters..."

She could not continue. And the Judge: "Calm yourself, calm yourself, Mademoiselle!"

"...in indelible letters, is written the name of Felice Orsini!"

At a nod from the Judge, the Chancellor flashed before me, drawn from I know not where, the valise with the embroidered tiger. Time and dust had blackened its base. But it was immediately washed, and the indelible letters appeared. Simple participation in a conspiracy that had led to bloodshed was enough... those letters were my death sentence.

But I did not give myself up for beaten and attempted a desperate move. I had understood well at that moment that Miss Cheney was acting neither for venality nor for hatred. The spirit that animated her must have been love. "The Police, masters in the art of profiting from the passions"—I thought—"must have promised her the salvation of the man she adored in exchange for a complete revelation." I learned later that I had guessed the truth. To the detective Sanders, who exhorted her to disclose what she knew, she had replied: "Everything, everything to save that man!" And she had allowed herself to be brought to Paris.

Be that as it in may, spurred by my thought, I raised my voice, as I had never done before my judge, and turning to my accuser: "You are trying to save Felice Orsini and, to succeed, you betray the innocent!"

What I had not expected happened. My voice, vibrating through the room, had penetrated a heart, had shaken a creature; it had lifted a head bowed over an elbow in sorrowful languor.

I was clasped by two trembling arms and my muscles had to make an effort to support the weight of a being who was abandoning herself to me. And I, who still did not understand, feel a wave of pink and blue penetrate my depths and temper my storm; while a cloud of hair, blonde and light, caresses my face and encircles it, as if to hide it from profane eyes. A resounding kiss, long...

For a moment, the Judge, the Imperial Prosecutor, the room, the conspiracy, France, the entire world ceased to exist. I was in the arms of my wife.

They separated us. The Judge was the first to break the silence. Oh, how shrill his voice seemed to me! He said: "Is Count di Rudio your husband?" And she, with honest pride: "I would not be here, if he were not!"

Then they granted us ten minutes for a private talk. But when we drew aside, Sanders, the head of the Scotland Yard detectives, who had accompanied my poor Elisa to Paris, followed us and stood right at our heels. I thwarted him by beginning our conversation in Italian.

"The child?"

"A darling! Mama is taking care of him. Carlo, Carlo, what can I do for you?"

And Sanders, brusquely: "Speak in English!"

"Go to hell!"

He protested to the Judge, who, perhaps thinking that speaking the language of Shakespeare was difficult for me, gave an order that a Corsican interpreter should attend our conversation. Sanders moved away. And I then resumed my speech, but in English.

"Elisa, this is not the time to delude ourselves. All is lost and I shall be brought to the scaffold. What can you do? Nothing but bear your fate with fortitude. Unless..."

Here, great protests from the Corsican, who demands that we speak in Italian. And so the Judge orders that both one and the other interpreter stand beside us. But before Sanders had returned, I had already expressed my idea... "unless you manage to interest some English personage of great influence in my, in your, affairs. Then perhaps..."

At this point, the arrival of the second intruder exasperated me and my strong emotions no longer had any restraint. What spurred me on the most? Whether it was the horror of prolonged dissimulation and the need to claim, in that supreme moment, my own part; or whether it was the presence of Elisa that rekindled my pride, the fact is that, raising my voice again, I launched, into the astonished face of the Judge, these words: "Yes, I too tried to suppress the murderer of my Fatherland and of the French People. A single remorse: not having known how to strike. The second bomb was thrown by me!"

The Judge, pale and grave, made to speak. But I forestalled him: "Do not trouble me with useless questions. What I have said is enough for your Justice!"

I saw the gesture that sent me back to prison cut through the air, and I turned to my wife to embrace her again. Alas! the pink had vanished, the blue eyes had lost their vivacity. Elisa, having fainted, had slumped onto a chair, her arms dangling, her head thrown back against the chair. My kiss, the last perhaps, I placed upon her lips, grown pale and insensible but still trembling.
