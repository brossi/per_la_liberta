CHAPTER FOURTEEN

FROM that moment I harboured no further doubt as to the fate that lay in store for me; and if I resolved to struggle to the end, it was solely, as one says, for honour of signature — so that I might cry out, upon reaching the bottom of the abyss: "I have done everything humanly possible to avoid plunging into it!"

That very same day I was compelled to convince myself that an overbearing will was working in the contrary direction, reinforcing the means honourably at its disposal with fraud and perjury. I was confined in a ground-floor cell. A companion was assigned to me — an agent of the secret Police, dressed in civilian clothes, who informed me that he would pass the day and night in my company. Certainly not to keep my spirits up, I thought — but to catch some word that might escape me, in the anguish of the soul, during sleep.

He appeared to divine my thought and begged me not to judge by appearances. If he could do me no good, he would certainly do me no harm.

Half an hour later, the bolts grated and the heavy door swung upon its hinges. Two persons entered: the examining Magistrate and a man dressed as a municipal street-sweeper, with one empty socket, horribly scarred. Two guards remained standing on the threshold.

— Well then! said the Magistrate, after a brief silence.

— It is he!

— Are you quite certain?

— As certain as I am baptised.

— Repeat your account.

— I was on duty at the Opera and was spreading sand at the entrance reserved for His Majesty. It would have been seven o'clock, or a quarter past at the most. I saw two gentlemen who were conversing in undertones and gesticulating. They had the air of studying the locality. I warned them that this was no place for the public and asked them to move along. They told me to mind my own business. The gentleman who is here said: "Shut your mouth, you scoundrel!" I made as if to strike him and both of them cleared off.

— And this took place?

— Yesterday evening, the fourteenth of January.

— An hour, or a little before, the attempt upon the life of His Majesty the Emperor?

— An hour, or a little more.

I had understood — perfectly understood! The object was to establish, in a manner that should appear irrefutable, our presence at the scene of the attempt. For Orsini there was his wound; for Gomez the arrest effected in front of the Opéra theatre. To bring about my own destruction and that of Pieri, they had placed in the mouth of that miserable wretch a tale invented out of whole cloth.

Nevertheless I did not feel that anger which drives a man to outbursts, and I contented myself with asking my strange accuser how much he had been promised in payment for his falsehoods. He turned aside the one greenish eye remaining to him and made no reply.

Late in the evening, a second agent was shut into my cell. Scarcely had he entered when he greeted me and, offering me his right hand, said that he too would not leave me either by day or by night. They were locking him in as well, as far as he could judge, in order to prevent any communication between myself and the accomplices whom I was suspected of having outside. He hoped that all three of us would, like good comrades, endeavour not to embitter our captivity with needless frictions.

Several days of relative quiet passed. No contact with the outside world, of which little air penetrated into my cell, still less sunlight, and the turnkey but rarely. He had orders to speak neither with me nor with my guards, who thus became the only human beings with whom I was permitted to exchange my thoughts. And I availed myself of this largely, telling them the history of Austria's horrible persecutions and of the suffering they visited upon my Country. One makes propaganda where one can and as occasion serves.

But this was not enough to deliver me from periods of deep prostration, in which the image of Elisa and of the child would rise before me, vivid and clear — the child to whose innocent years and maternal smile I had brought ruin. And my thoughts would wander at such times to my companions in misfortune as well, of whom I knew nothing save that they were, like me, confined at Mazas; subject to the same snares, plunging inexorably toward an identical fate. In those moments I would sit apart on the plank bed, motionless, my eyes cast down. And here I owe my guards a word of thanks — somewhat belated, it is true — who, so as not to disturb my meditations, paced on tiptoe.

Another of my tormenting preoccupations arose from the certainty that my enforced inaction must be matched by feverish activity on the part of the Police. I endeavoured to put myself, so to speak, in the place of the Authorities, and to school myself in my own defence. But every effort mocked every faculty I possessed, and the subtle conjectures and elaborate reconstructions ended in this: nervous irritation and exhaustion. There remained, then, nothing but to resort to the great remedy of youth: either I slept, in whatever fashion I might, or I abandoned the effort altogether.

The first certain sign of the inextricable net that was closing about me came when, conducted under heavy guard to the Chancellor's office, I found placed before my eyes, first a certified copy, and then the original itself, of a letter I had written from London to Felice Orsini. In it I informed him how a certain Carlotti, a man from Brescia, had, greatly to my surprise, made mention to me of our conspiracy. I had good reason to suspect this person of being a spy in the service of the French Government. He ought to be left well alone. If he already knew too much — one must either be rid of him by some means, or abandon the enterprise altogether.

Felice Orsini, more ardent patriot than prudent conspirator, had preserved among his papers so compromising a document, and the English Police, urged on from Paris, had carried out a search of his lodgings and taken possession of it.

My close connexion with the conspirators, my knowledge of their plots, appeared only too firmly established. Another good step further down the fatal slope! And when I attempted, pro forma, to deny the undeniable, the examining Magistrate, by way of reply, smiled in a mocking fashion and sent me back to the darkness, launching after me, by way of a parting valediction, the shattering phrase: "Oh, we have far stronger evidence than this! and your accomplices have fully corroborated it!"

The following day I was transferred from Mazas, in a closed carriage, to the Conciergerie. I was assigned cell No. 1, and it was not long before I learned that Pieri was confined in No. 2, Gomez in No. 3, and Orsini in No. 4.

My new lodgings were approximately ten feet by fifteen in extent. The light entered timidly through a small window set with thick iron bars, situated near the ceiling. In the door, at a man's height, was a circular aperture — a five-franc piece would barely have passed through it. It permitted the warders to keep watch over my every movement; it permitted me to observe, within certain limits, what passed in the corridor. On the walls, the hands of prisoners who had preceded me had doubtless engraved verses and maxims, scraped thereafter inexorably away by the warders' knives.

What fragments of human hearts, and what reflections of souls refined by suffering, withdrawn from my meditation by the petty observance of duty! The story of one such soul I had known in all its particulars since my childhood. It was from my very cell that Marshal Ney had walked forth to have his patriot breast shattered by Bourbon lead!

At the Conciergerie I too had two guards. Of them I must say something. The more agreeable was a former petty naval officer. The State, having wrung his youth from him, had rescued him from penury by entrusting him with duties of dubious character that clearly revolted his disposition. A large family, poor and without connexions, kept him in thrall to his own fate; but his resignation was only apparent, and I was not long in perceiving that his secret rancour, finding no outlet, was slowly accumulating, drop by drop, within his heart. Nature had denied him the resolution and the impulse that drive men to virile acts; unless, indeed, a foolish discipline too long endured had severed every thread of his will. But he could still admire in others the virtues that were not in him, and his instincts betrayed themselves, beneath the surface and perhaps without his own knowledge, as rebellious. The years have erased his surname from my memory. His Christian name was, if I am not mistaken, one very common among the people of Paris — a mythological name: Saturno!

The other guard was a former soldier of the marine infantry. A rather stout man of few words, he would sit for hours with his hands upon his belly, fingers interlaced, placidly revolving his two thumbs one about the other, now in one direction, now in the reverse. He was fond of dozing.

At the Conciergerie I received my first intimation of what was passing in the world outside. My two guards kept their lips sealed on this subject. But other persons employed in the service of the prison attended to my thirst by the simplest of expedients: planting themselves in front of my cell and conversing with one another in loud voices.

In this manner I came to learn how public opinion, which had at first shown itself hostile to us, had rapidly shifted in our favour. The substance of entire newspaper articles drifted through the small circular aperture to my ear. Among them, those translated from the English press struck me as particularly explicit and almost laudatory.

But why did the staff of the Conciergerie take such pains? I believed I divined the reason on the day I heard a warder declare that "in this affair" — our affair — "mysterious and powerful influences were at work." He had added: "It is not difficult to foresee that not one of the four prisoners will be executed." I inferred from this that he who had spoken thus was not altogether unconnected with the direct inspiration of the mysterious influences to which he had permitted himself to allude.

And now we approach the precipice! From the far end of the corridor a voice cried: "Prisoner of cell No. 4!" It was Felice Orsini's cell. I ran to press my eye against the circular aperture. Let it now be seen how capable a thing it is of gnawing away a man's very existence — even in the most intrepid — to be tormented by the ruin of a great enterprise and by the consciousness of one's own responsibility! Felice Orsini passed rapidly across the narrow field of my vision; but that was enough for me to discern, upon that sculptural head, the traces of the storm that must have been raging within. His hair had turned almost entirely white.

Half an hour of sad reflection elapsed, and I heard Orsini's step returning. At once, from the far end of the corridor, the same voice: "Prisoner of cell No. 1!"

I was conducted into a long and narrow room. The single window that lit it was directly opposite me. At a writing-desk beside the window sat the usual examining Magistrate and another individual dressed in black — the Imperial Prosecutor. But my attention was violently drawn to a third figure, seated a little way from the desk, to the right of the Magistrate. Her profile stood out sharply against the light of the window. I knew that profile — it was of a woman small, slight, dark, not beautiful in the classical sense, but very attractive; and at the sight of her there awoke in my memory the impression of her persistent smile. It was Miss Elisa Cheney, the friend of Felice Orsini.

I dissembled the agitation that seized me so intensely as to prevent my distinguishing the other persons around me, and barely noticed a second lady who, seated at a table near the entrance, rested her arm upon it and upon that arm buried her face.

The examination proceeded with a swiftness that struck me as lightning-like. The Magistrate addressed himself first to the English young woman, speaking to her in her own language.

— Is this the man who brought the letter found upon your mantelpiece, and to whom you handed a travelling bag belonging to Signor Felice Orsini?

— It is he.

— Signor Count, what have you to say to that?

Genteel people, seated at table in a warm room with their feet upon soft carpets, may perhaps be unable to forgive me my ungallantry. But the question was one of saving my head from the guillotine and preserving it for better employment. I replied: "How can that woman claim to know me? She may have caught a glimpse of me some night, in passing, as she was walking the pavements of Haymarket. The testimony of a woman of doubtful character amounts to nothing."

The young woman turned pale and, in a voice broken by anger: "Let the travelling bag be examined. On the leather, at the bottom — in indelible characters—"

She could not continue. And the Magistrate: "Compose yourself, compose yourself, Miss!"

"— in indelible characters, the name of Felice Orsini is written!"

At a sign from the Magistrate, the Chancellor flashed before my eyes, produced I know not whence, the tiger-skin travelling bag. Time and dust had blackened the bottom. But it was immediately washed, and the indelible characters appeared. Mere participation in a conspiracy that had led to the shedding of blood was sufficient — those characters were my death sentence.

But I did not concede defeat, and attempted a desperate move. I had clearly understood on the spot that Miss Cheney was acting neither from venality nor from hatred. The spirit that animated her must be love. "The Police, past masters in the art of turning passions to account," I thought, "must have promised her the salvation of the man she adored, in exchange for a complete revelation." I learned later that I had guessed the truth. To the detective Sanders, who was urging her to disclose all she knew, she had replied: "Everything, everything, to save that man!" And she had allowed herself to be brought to Paris.

Be that as it may, impelled by my own conviction, I raised my voice as I had never raised it before my judge, and turning to my accuser: "You are attempting to save Felice Orsini, and to that end you are betraying the innocent!"

What I had not expected then occurred. My voice, ringing through the room, had entered a heart, had shaken a creature, had lifted a head bowed upon its arm in painful languor.

I found myself clasped by two trembling arms, and my muscles were forced to make an effort to support the weight of a being who surrendered herself to me. And I, who still did not comprehend, felt something like a wave of rose and blue penetrate to my depths and temper my storm; whilst a cloud of hair, fair and light, caressed my face and circumscribed it, as though to hide it from profane eyes. A sonorous, long kiss —

For a moment, the Magistrate, the Imperial Prosecutor, the room, the conspiracy, France, the whole world ceased to exist. I was in the arms of my wife.

They parted us. The Magistrate was the first to break the silence. How shrill his voice seemed to me! He said: "Is the Count di Rudio your husband?" And she, with honest pride: "I should not be here if he were not!"

We were then permitted ten minutes for a private interview. But when we drew aside, Sanders, the head of the Scotland Yard detectives, who had accompanied my poor Elisa to Paris, followed us and planted himself at our side. I foiled him by opening our conversation in Italian.

— The child?

— A darling! Mamma is taking care of him. Carlo, Carlo, what can I do for you?

And Sanders, brusquely: "Speak in English!"

— Go to the devil!

He lodged a protest with the Magistrate, who, thinking perhaps that speaking the language of Shakespeare presented difficulties for me, gave orders that a Corsican interpreter should be present at our conversation. Sanders withdrew. And I then resumed speaking — but in English.

"Elisa, this is no time for illusions. All is lost, and I shall be brought to the scaffold. What can you do? Nothing, but bear your fate with fortitude. Unless—"

Here, strong protests from the Corsican, who demanded that we speak Italian. And thereupon the Magistrate gave orders that both interpreters should stand at our side. But before Sanders had returned, I had already expressed my meaning — "unless you can manage to engage the interest of some English personage of great influence in our case. In that event, perhaps—"

At this point, the arrival of the second intruder exasperated me, and my overwrought emotions could no longer be contained. Which was the stronger impulse? Whether it was horror at the prolonged dissimulation and the need to vindicate, in that supreme moment, my own share in events; or whether it was the presence of Elisa that rekindled my pride — the fact is that, raising my voice once more, I flung into the astonished face of the Magistrate these words: "Yes, I too attempted to destroy the assassin of my Fatherland and of the French People. I have but one regret: that I did not know how to strike. The second bomb was thrown by me!"

The Magistrate, pale and grave, made as if to speak. But I forestalled him: "Do not trouble me with needless questions. What I have said is sufficient for your Justice!"

I saw the gesture slicing the air that dismissed me back to prison, and I turned to my wife to embrace her once more. Alas! the rose had vanished; the blue eyes had lost their brightness. Elisa, who had fainted, lay abandoned in a chair, her arms hanging loose, her head thrown back against the rail. My kiss — the last, perhaps — I pressed upon those lips grown pale and insensible, yet still trembling.

Then I hurried away, so swiftly that the guards who came after me seemed almost to be giving chase!
