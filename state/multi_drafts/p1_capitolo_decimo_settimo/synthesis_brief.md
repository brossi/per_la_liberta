LOCARNO, dove ero ritornato per comunicare al Calvi una missiva del Mazzini, fui arrestato e rilasciato poche ore dopo, a piede libero, sotto parola di presentarmi al Delegato Federale. Venni poi processato per cospirazione contro una Potenza amica, espulso dalla Svizzera, messo nell'alternativa di scegliere fra l'America e l'Inghilterra.

L'Inghilterra era più vicina al campo delle probabili future imprese e fu da me preferita. Mi stabilii a Londra, dove trovai non pochi compromessi nei moti del Febbraio, che si erano sottratti alla vendetta austriaca con fughe avventurose... tra gli altri, un Jean Valjean milanese accompagnato fuori dalle mura in una cassa da morto e con solenni funerali.

Stretti dall'ideale comune e dalle comuni sofferenze, si visse qualche tempo in comune, nel quartiere detto Baldwin's Garden e precisamente nel modesto albergo del milanese Tressoldi, un bonaccione che possedeva, tra l'altre sue virtù, quella di chiudere un occhio quando non potevamo chiudere il conto.

Giuseppe Mazzini giunse a Londra nell'Aprile. Da lui si seppe che il Pianciani, il quale dettava allora la sua "Storia dei Papi," temperava le fatiche dell'intelletto modificando e rimodificando il giardino della sua villa... una probabilità di lavoro per parecchi di noi. Fummo presentati al suo amanuense, certo Bardini padovano, studente in legge e questi, a sua volta, ci presentò al Pianciani che, lì, per lì, affidava il lavoro materiale di quelle incessanti modificazioni al giovine Varisco, accordandogli ampia facoltà di valersi dell'opera di quanti fra noi stimava necessari. Fui subito assoldato anch'io ed ebbi così, per qualche mese, dalla vanga e dai fiori il mio sostentamento.

Quella vita semplice e laboriosa era turbata da immagini terribili. L'Austria inferociva ed ogni giornale, ogni lettera ci recava la dolorosa certezza che la falange dei generosi veniva inesorabilmente assottigliata.

Io ne parlavo, nelle lunghe passeggiate a Giuseppe Mazzini e fissavo la faccia olivina, intellettuale, come per dire "Che si decide? Li lasceremo sgozzare come cani?" Il maestro affrettava il passo. La sua espressione era d'ineffabile melanconia; ma sui pensieri che gli turbinavano pel capo... non una sillaba!

Un giorno d'Agosto egli mi mandò a chiamare d'urgenza. Perchè mai! Certo non per dirmi "buon giorno" o "buona sera". Dunque?..

Piantai la vanga in mezzo ad un'aiuola: "Sta lì! tu non sei il solo strumento nobile!" E via di corsa.

Ma quando fui d'innanzi al Maestro, mi sentii cascare le braccia, tanto l'accoglienza fu parca di parole. Venni pregato di portare una lettera a Kossuth e d'attendere la risposta. Non una sillaba di più!

Kossuth teneva allora, presso Regent's Circus, una specie di Corte. Trovai alla porta carrozze e servi in livrea; su per le scale un via vai di inglesi compassati, in abito di società; Ungheresi nel costume nazionale, elegante, militaresco; gente con larghe cicatrici; figure incerte, questuanti col pretesto della Patria. Onesti e disonesti, ricchi o poveri, difficilmente ottenevano accesso sino al Dittatore che, anche battuto ed in esilio, esercitava, oltre il prestigio della fama, quell'altro che viene dall'abitudine del comando.

Mentre attendevo nell'anticamera il suo beneplacito, tentavo d'ingannare il tempo osservando i quadri che pendevano dalle pareti: Kossuth sotto le spoglie del cittadino semplice; Kossuth in abito da caccia; Kossuth nell'uniforme del generale ora in poltrona, ora a cavallo, ora d'accanto ad un cannone spezzato; ma sempre Kossuth, nient'altro che Kossuth!

Finalmente fra tanti simulacri, apparve il grand'uomo in carne ed ossa. Devo ammettere che l'arte, nell'intento di idealizzarlo, lo aveva calunniato. La figura dell'illustre Magiaro, più che l'eroismo di maniera e le stragi, rammentava gli affetti paterni e le miti emozioni del focolare domestico. È privilegio degli artisti l'alterare, a loro capriccio, le tinte della Storia... per questo Napoleone I è tramandato ai posteri mentre valica il San Bernardo su un puledro scalpitante, con manifesta ingiustizia al povero asinello che prestò la groppa a tanto peso.

L'accoglienza che mi venne riserbata non fu quale se l'aspettavano e il Conte di Rudio e il patriota italiano e l'inviato di fiducia di Giuseppe Mazzini. Lesse il Kossuth la lettera che gli porsi stando ritto sulla soglia e, gettala, trasse di tasca un memorandum, scrisse poche righe e mi ordinò, secco, secco, di portarle a destinazione. Non un saluto! ed io scendendo le scale, mi domandavo se i Cosacchi dello Czar e i Croati di Jellacich, prestando man forte all'Austria, non avessero, in sostanza, risparmiato ai prodi ungheresi i fastidi inerenti ad un mutamento di dinastia.

Le poche righe consegnatemi dal Kossuth erano dirette ad un alto ufficiale, il cui nome vo cercando nei ripostigli della memoria. N'ebbi parecchi rotoli di sterline... "Gli stessi — mi disse — che abbiamo avuto dal Mazzini per la nostra partecipazione ai moti del 1853. Possano essi giovare al vostro grande, sfortunato paese!"

Il Kossuth, alla domane dell'insuccesso, aveva pubblicamente negato ogni sua complicità in quei moti!

Al mio ritorno, il Maestro aperse l'animo suo. Egli incominciò col domandarmi se mi sentivo disposto a mettere un'altra volta a repentaglio la vita per la causa della Patria e avuta, dal mio stesso contegno, la risposta, mi additò una sedia e ragionammo sommessamente, l'uno di fronte all'altro, per oltre un'ora.

Egli mi spiegò che, per consiglio di Fortunato Calvi, i futuri tentativi insurrezionali si sarebbero svolti nella regione delle Alpi. Le ragioni che ne addusse sono le seguenti: Il nostro Paese è ricco di forze latenti, disposte a prorompere; ma chi si assume un'iniziativa deve saper dare affidamento di seria resistenza. La montagna è il baluardo naturale dei deboli e degli oppressi. Quivi riesce possibile ai pochi il tener testa ai molti; possibile il sottrarsi agli inseguimenti; il calare colla rapidità dell'aquila, al piano; il sorprendere, l'intercettare. Alla battaglia campale, che ci era negata, bisognava sostituire la guerriglia, la quale è necessariamente figlia dei monti. Se la bandiera della redenzione poteva mantenersi ritta e sventolante per tre, per due mesi, non vi sarebbe patriota senza febbre, non lembo d'Italia ove non divampasse l'azione.

Quest'ultimo concetto parve lo inducesse in un altro ordine di pensieri. Sulla sua faccia si stese come un'ombra; poi disse quasi testualmente: "Lo so! La codardia, mascherata da prudenza, mi accusa di sospingere al patibolo la gioventù animosa, di provocare tentativi disperati che offrono alla tirannide austriaca, e a quella nostrana, l'opportunità di rincrudire le loro sevizie. Ma non si riflette che l'audacia e il sacrificio dei pochi finiscono sempre per scuotere i torpidi e trascinare all'azione i molti. Nulla di grande senz'audacia! Napoleone III non si sarebbe probabilmente spianata la via all'Impero senza gli insuccessi di Strasburgo e di Boulogne. Nessun Re d'Italia mostrerà animo tanto grande da caldeggiare la redenzione della Patria nostra, quando gli Italiani dimostrino di crogiolarsi, in pace, nella schiavitù. Forse che Carlo Alberto avrebbe varcato il Ticino se non era la magnanima abnegazione dei Milanesi! Necessità imprescindibile è questo mantenere aperta, incalzante la grande questione italiana. L'Europa non può aver pace se duri il nostro spasimo. Essa interverrà, quando che sia, in nostro favore e tanto più presto quanto più ci mostreremo intolleranti di giogo e minacciosi. E a che giovano le buone idee, i gagliardi propositi, quando il difetto d'iniziativa li rileghi nel campo delle semplici astrazioni! L'azione deterge le nostre colpe secolari. Tra noi e l'Italia libera è questo lavacro di sangue. Dovremmo star di qua per le calunnie dei codardi!"

Quella sera stessa mi procuravo un passaporto con nome assunto e partivo per la Svizzera. Fortunato Calvi già mi attendeva a Zurigo. "Il piano dei nuovi moti è suo — mi aveva detto il Mazzini. — A lui spetta il curarne l'esecuzione. Avrai dalla sua bocca le necessarie istruzioni."

A Bruxelles, ove feci una breve fermata, consegnai una lettera del Maestro al dall'Ungaro, il Catullo veneziano. "Dov'è, che fa il Maestro?" — mi domandò egli, quasi febbrilmente. Ed io, coi suoi propri versi:

"Mazzini è in sul loco ove si trema

"Che giunga a' traditor l'ora suprema;

"Mazzini è in ogni loco ove si opera

"Versar il sangue per l'Italia intera!"

Giunto a Zurigo, volli prima di recarmi dal Calvi, avere le mani libere e mi affrettai a far visita al de Boni, al quale pure consegnai una missiva; una terza portai al comasco Caronti. Giuseppe Mazzini mi aveva raccomandato d'essere molto guardingo con costui che gli era molto sospetto. Contribuiva però somme piuttosto rilevanti alla causa buona e ciò lo tratteneva dal disfarsene.

Le domande sfrontatamente inquisitive che il Caronti mi rivolse, giunsero a me, già mal prevenuto, come un forte irritante e, quando rimisi il piede oltre la soglia della sua villa, provai un sollievo, come se mi fossi improvvisamente liberato da un incubo.

Potevo finalmente ritemprarmi nell'abbraccio di un patriota autentico, di una coscienza intemerata... Fortunato Calvi! Quale gioia nel rivederlo ancor bello, ancor fiero, esalante ancora quell'indefinibile senso di serenità che spira dagli animi onesti!

La parte sostanziale del nostro colloquio ebbe un esordio che narrerò senza false modestie.

"Tu non sei — egli mi disse — e non devi essere uno strumento da eseguire ciecamente ordini che non comprendi. Ti confiderò il piano che ho ideato e che il Comitato Rivoluzionario Europeo ha pienamente approvato. Saprai così parare, con unità d'intenti, gli inevitabili contraccolpi. Se si riesce a qualcosa di concreto, tu sarai il mio aiutante di campo; se non si riesce... saremo, probabilmente, pareggiati come ricompense... il martirio!" Il mio incarico preliminare era quello di recarmi nella vallata del Treviso e d'abboccarmi con certi contrabbandieri, soprannominati i fratelli Burrasca, per indurli a portare nell'Agordino una grossa partita di munizioni. Poi dovevo passare il confine, entrare clandestinamente a Belluno, abboccarmi con don Bastiano Barozzi che vi aveva fatto ritorno e che mi aspettava. Il Barozzi sapeva di certe armi nascoste nel 1848 al ritorno degli oppressori. Nel Cadore, abbondavano gli spiriti ribelli che si erano coperti di gloria nel difenderlo; il nome e l'esortazione di Fortunato Calvi, che fu il buon genio di quella difesa, avrebbero suscitato nel loro petto l'assopito entusiasmo. Bisognava distribuire fra essi le vecchie armi. Un manipolo di esuli attendeva il segnale per scendere dalle Alpi... Fortunato Calvi, guidandoli, ne avrebbe diretto le sorti.

Il colosso austriaco doveva essere vulnerato appunto a Belluno e con triplice sorpresa: all'Ufficio delle Finanze, alla Gendarmeria, alla Caserma delle truppe regolari.

L'ufficio delle finanze, situato presso la vecchia chiesa di Santo Stefano, solido edificio eretto per servire da convento, inquadra un vasto cortile a portici. Tra l'una e l'altra colonna si stende un parapetto al così che un uomo, camminandoci rasente e carponi, non può essere scorto da una sentinella che, pur vigilando, percorra le altre parti del porticato. Il portone non aveva battenti ed era lasciato senza guardia; custodia dell'edificio, un esiguo colpo di militi, di stanza in fondo al cortile, a destra... in tutto tre uomini e un caporale.

Nel cuore della notte, un pugno dei nostri, in agguato presso il portone; un audace strisci, scalzo, lungo il parapetto, sorprenda la sentinella alle spalle... un segnale sommesso... si accorra!... i tre militi addormentati vengono ridotti all'impotenza. Impresa audace, non ardua!

Era l'autunno, l'epoca della riscossione dell'imposte; i forzieri dell'Amministrazione, secondo calcoli attendibili, dovevano racchiudere un milione e mezzo di fiorini. Impossessarsene... il nerbo della guerra!

Contemporaneamente, un altro manipolo di patriotti doveva appiattirsi presso la Caserma dei Gendarmi. Simulare una baruffa e suonare il campanello della Gendarmeria, come per chiedere aiuto. La guardia che apre va freddata. Si irrompa nel covo e i trenta ammanettatori che vi dormono passino, forzatamente, nella categoria degli ammanettati.

La guarnigione di Belluno consisteva allora di centocinquanta soldati. Alla caserma si accedeva, a destra e a sinistra per una via circolare rampante; un "tourniquet" come lo chiamano gli ingegneri, fiancheggiato da alberi e da sedili, ritrovo favorito di bambinaie e di birichini. Regola generale, quando suonava l'allarme, la piccola guarnigione sbucava, alla spicciolata, dal portone e si allineava sulla spianata, in attesa di ordini. Bisognava appiattare un nerbo di tiratori scelti lungo i margini della via rampante, dare l'allarme, pigliare a fucilate quanti militi sarebbero accorsi.

Compiuta la triplice sorpresa, gli insorti si impossessino delle autorità civili ed ecclesiastiche... ostaggi! Poi sequestriamo i mezzi di trasporto, indossiamo la divisa austriaca e procediamo militarmente sino alla fortezza di Osoppo nel Friuli, facendo prigionieri e costringendo a seguirci, per prevenire indiscrezioni, quanti incontriamo per via.

A questo punto Fortunato Calvi, che aveva sempre parlato sottovoce, quasi meditando le sue stesse frasi, si animò e disse vibratamente: "Ci avviciniamo alla fortezza preceduti dalla bandiera austriaca e battendo il tamburo, come amici. Passato il ponte, messo il piede sotto l'andito, la scena cambia, il valore latino prorompe. Sui baluardi, ventidue nostri, innalziamo la nostra bandiera e guai a chi vi attenta! Gli ostaggi restano con noi, con noi l'armi, il danaro e l'audacia. Osoppo diventa la nostra base d'operazione ed è arnese inespugnabile. L'inverno organizziamo la guerriglia sulle Alpi, teniamo in iscacco le truppe che vorrebbero calare nella Penisola. A primavera, la Lombardia, la Venezia, l'Ungheria, la Francia forse anche, assecondano il moto... oppressi ed oppressori misurano le forze. Gli errori del passato ci sono d'ammaestramento, il voto secolare si realizza, l'Italia risorge per non ricadere mai più."

Io ascoltavo a testa bassa, pensando quello che qualsiasi altro uomo avrebbe pensato: "Sino a qual punto potevamo contare sull'abilità del Comitato Rivoluzionario Europeo nell'estendere la nostra iniziativa, nel trasformarla in una sollevazione internazionale?"

Potevo io rallentare quando un'entità come Fortunato Calvi tutta si precipita?

Tre giorni e mi rimettevo in viaggio.

CHAPTER THE SEVENTEENTH

AT LOCARNO, whither I had returned to convey to Calvi a missive from Mazzini, I was arrested and released a few hours later, at liberty, on my word of honour to present myself to the Federal Delegate. I was subsequently tried for conspiracy against a friendly Power, expelled from Switzerland, and placed in the position of choosing between America and England.

England was nearer to the field of probable future enterprises and was by me preferred. I settled in London, where I found no few who had been compromised in the movements of February, and who had escaped Austrian vengeance by means of adventurous flights — among others, a Milanese Jean Valjean who had been escorted beyond the city walls in a coffin, with solemn funeral rites.

Bound together by a common ideal and by common sufferings, we lived for a time in community, in the quarter known as Baldwin's Garden, and more precisely in the modest inn kept by the Milanese Tressoldi, a good-natured soul who possessed, among his other virtues, the virtue of turning a blind eye when we were unable to settle our account.

Giuseppe Mazzini arrived in London in April. From him we learnt that Pianciani, who was then dictating his "Storia dei Papi," was tempering the labours of the intellect by continually altering and re-altering the garden of his villa — a likely source of work for several of us. We were introduced to his amanuensis, one Bardini, a Paduan student of law, and he in turn presented us to Pianciani, who, on the spot, entrusted the manual work of those incessant modifications to the young Varisco, granting him full latitude to avail himself of the services of as many of us as he might deem necessary. I was immediately engaged as well, and thus drew my sustenance, for some months, from the spade and from flowers.

That simple and laborious life was troubled by terrible images. Austria was raging in its fury, and every newspaper, every letter brought us the painful certainty that the phalanx of the generous was being inexorably thinned.

I would speak of it to Giuseppe Mazzini during our long walks, and I would fix my gaze upon that olive-skinned, intellectual face, as if to say: "What has been decided? Shall we leave them to be slaughtered like dogs?" The Master would quicken his step. His expression was one of ineffable melancholy; but of the thoughts that surged through his mind — not a syllable!

One day in August he sent for me urgently. Why ever should he? Certainly not to bid me good morning or good evening. What then?

I planted my spade in the middle of a flower-bed: "Stay there! you are not the only noble instrument!" And off I ran.

But when I stood before the Master, I felt my arms sink to my sides, so spare of words was the reception. I was requested to carry a letter to Kossuth and to wait for the reply. Not a syllable more!

Kossuth kept at that time, near Regent's Circus, a kind of Court. At the door I found carriages and liveried servants; on the staircase, a coming and going of measured Englishmen in formal dress; Hungarians in their national costume, elegant and military in its bearing; men bearing broad scars; uncertain figures, petitioning under the pretext of the Fatherland. The honest and the dishonest, the wealthy and the poor, found it equally difficult to obtain access to the Dictator who, even in defeat and in exile, exercised, beyond the prestige of fame, that further prestige which comes from the habit of command.

While I waited in the antechamber for his permission to enter, I attempted to beguile the time by examining the pictures hanging on the walls: Kossuth in the guise of the simple citizen; Kossuth in hunting dress; Kossuth in the uniform of a general — now in an armchair, now on horseback, now beside a shattered cannon; but always Kossuth, nothing but Kossuth!

At last, amid so many likenesses, the great man appeared in flesh and blood. I must confess that art, in its endeavour to idealise him, had done him a disservice. The figure of the illustrious Magyar was more suggestive of paternal affections and the gentle emotions of the domestic hearth than of heroism in the conventional mode and of carnage. It is the privilege of artists to alter, at their caprice, the colours of History — it is for this reason that Napoleon I is handed down to posterity crossing the Saint Bernard on a rearing colt, in manifest injustice to the poor donkey that lent its back to so great a burden.

The reception accorded to me was not such as the Count di Rudio had expected, nor the Italian patriot, nor the trusted envoy of Giuseppe Mazzini. Kossuth read the letter I presented to him while remaining standing on the threshold, and, having tossed it aside, drew a memorandum from his pocket, wrote a few lines, and ordered me, curtly and coldly, to deliver them to their destination. Not a word of greeting! And as I descended the staircase I found myself wondering whether the Cossacks of the Czar and the Croats of Jellacich, lending their strength to Austria, had not in substance spared the brave Hungarians the vexations attendant upon a change of dynasty.

The few lines consigned to me by Kossuth were addressed to a high-ranking officer whose name I am now seeking in the recesses of memory. I received from him several rolls of sterling — "The very same," said he, "that we received from Mazzini for our participation in the movements of 1853. May they serve your great, unfortunate country!"

Kossuth, on the morrow of that failure, had publicly denied all complicity in those movements!

Upon my return, the Master opened his heart to me. He began by asking whether I felt disposed to risk my life once more for the cause of the Fatherland, and having received his answer from my bearing alone, he pointed to a chair, and we conversed in low voices, seated face to face, for upwards of an hour.

He explained to me that, on the counsel of Fortunato Calvi, the future insurrectionary attempts were to unfold in the Alpine regions. The reasons he advanced were as follows: Our country is rich in latent forces, ready to burst forth; but whoever undertakes an initiative must be able to give assurance of serious resistance. The mountain is the natural bulwark of the weak and the oppressed. There it is possible for the few to hold their ground against the many; possible to escape pursuit; to swoop with the swiftness of the eagle down upon the plain; to surprise, to intercept. In place of the pitched battle, which was denied to us, it was necessary to substitute guerrilla warfare, which is necessarily the offspring of the mountains. If the banner of redemption could be kept upright and flying for three, for two months, there would not be a patriot without fever in his blood, nor a corner of Italy in which the flame of action would not blaze.

This last thought seemed to draw him into another train of reflection. A shadow, as it were, passed across his face; then he said, almost in these very words: "I know it! Cowardice, masked as prudence, accuses me of driving spirited youth to the scaffold, of provoking desperate attempts which afford Austrian tyranny, and that which is home-bred, the opportunity to redouble their cruelties. But it is not considered that the audacity and the sacrifice of the few always end by shaking the torpid from their lethargy and driving the many to action. Nothing great without audacity! Napoleon III would in all probability never have smoothed his path to the Empire without the failures of Strasbourg and of Boulogne. No King of Italy will show spirit great enough to champion the redemption of our Fatherland, when Italians demonstrate that they wallow at their ease in the comfort of slavery. Would Carlo Alberto have crossed the Ticino but for the magnanimous self-abnegation of the Milanese? An imperative necessity it is, this keeping the great Italian question open and pressing. Europe cannot know peace so long as our agony endures. She will intervene, sooner or later, in our favour, and the sooner the more we show ourselves intolerant of the yoke and menacing. And what use are sound ideas, vigorous intentions, when want of initiative confines them within the realm of mere abstractions? Action purges away our centuries-old shortcomings. Between us and a free Italy lies this lavacro — this baptism of blood. Should we remain on this side of it for the slanders of cowards!"

That very evening I procured a passport under an assumed name and set out for Switzerland. Fortunato Calvi was already awaiting me in Zurich. "The plan of the new movements is his own," Mazzini had told me. "It falls to him to attend to its execution. You will receive from his lips the necessary instructions."

In Brussels, where I made a brief halt, I delivered a letter from the Master to dall'Ungaro, the Venetian Catullus. "Where is the Master, what is he doing?" he asked me, almost feverishly. And I, in his own verses:

*"Mazzini è in sul loco ove si trema*
*"Che giunga a' traditor l'ora suprema;*
*"Mazzini è in ogni loco ove si opera*
*"Versar il sangue per l'Italia intera!"*

["Mazzini is in the place where men tremble / lest the last hour come to the traitors; / Mazzini is in every place where men labour / to shed their blood for all of Italy!"]

Having arrived in Zurich, I wished, before going to Calvi, to have my hands free, and I hastened to pay a visit to de Boni, to whom I likewise delivered a missive; a third I carried to Caronti of Como. Giuseppe Mazzini had charged me to be very much on my guard with this man, who was highly suspect to him. He did, however, contribute sums of some considerable amount to the good cause, and this restrained Mazzini from being rid of him.

The brazenly inquisitive questions that Caronti put to me came to me, already prejudiced as I was against him, as a sharp irritant, and when I once more set foot beyond the threshold of his villa, I experienced a relief as if I had suddenly freed myself from a nightmare.

At last I could refresh myself in the embrace of an authentic patriot, of an untarnished conscience — Fortunato Calvi! What joy to see him again still handsome, still proud, still exhaling that indefinable sense of serenity which breathes forth from honest souls!

The substance of our conversation had a beginning which I shall recount without false modesty.

"You are not," he said to me, "and you must not be, an instrument for blindly carrying out orders you do not understand. I shall confide to you the plan I have conceived and which the European Revolutionary Committee has fully approved. You will thus be able to meet, with unity of purpose, the inevitable reverberations. If we succeed in achieving something concrete, you will be my aide-de-camp; if we do not succeed — we shall in all probability be made equal in our reward — martyrdom!" My preliminary charge was to proceed into the valley of Treviso and make contact with certain smugglers, surnamed the Burrasca brothers, so as to induce them to carry a large consignment of munitions into the Agordino. I then had to cross the border, enter Belluno clandestinely, and make contact with Don Bastiano Barozzi, who had returned thither and who was expecting me. Barozzi knew of certain weapons hidden away in 1848 upon the return of the oppressors. In the Cadore, there was no lack of rebellious spirits who had covered themselves with glory in its defence; the name and exhortation of Fortunato Calvi, who had been the guiding genius of that defence, would have rekindled in their breasts the smouldering enthusiasm. It was necessary to distribute among them the old arms. A band of exiles was waiting for the signal to descend from the Alps — Fortunato Calvi, leading them, was to direct their fortunes.

The Austrian colossus was to be struck precisely at Belluno, and with a threefold surprise: at the Finance Office, at the Gendarmerie, and at the Barracks of the regular troops.

The Finance Office, situated near the old church of Santo Stefano, a solid building erected to serve as a convent, encloses a broad arcaded courtyard. Between one column and the next there runs a parapet, so low that a man creeping along it close to the wall and on all fours cannot be seen by a sentry who, though keeping watch, is traversing the other parts of the arcade. The main gate had no doors and was left unguarded; the custodians of the building, a small body of militia quartered at the far end of the courtyard, to the right — three men in all and a corporal.

In the dead of night, a handful of our men lying in ambush near the gate; one bold spirit to creep, barefoot, along the parapet and take the sentry from behind — a muffled signal — men come running — the three militiamen sleeping are rendered powerless. A bold enterprise, not an arduous one!

It was autumn, the time of tax collection; the coffers of the Administration, according to reliable calculations, were expected to contain a million and a half florins. To seize them — the sinews of war!

At the same time, another band of patriots was to conceal themselves near the Gendarmerie Barracks. They were to simulate a brawl and ring the bell of the Gendarmerie, as if to summon assistance. The guard who opens the door is to be shot. The lair is then to be stormed, and the thirty men-at-arms sleeping there compelled to pass, by force, into the category of the manacled.

The garrison of Belluno at that time consisted of one hundred and fifty soldiers. Access to the barracks was to be had, to the right and to the left, by a winding ascending road — a *tourniquet*, as the engineers call it, flanked by trees and benches, a favourite resort of nursemaids and urchins. The general rule was that when the alarm sounded, the small garrison would emerge, in straggling fashion, from the main gate and draw up on the esplanade, awaiting orders. It was necessary to conceal a body of picked marksmen along the edges of the winding road, to give the alarm, and to shoot down as many militiamen as should come running.

The threefold surprise accomplished, the insurgents were to take possession of the civil and ecclesiastical authorities — as hostages! Then we would seize the means of transport, put on Austrian uniforms, and proceed in military fashion to the fortress of Osoppo in Friuli, taking prisoners and compelling all whom we should encounter on the way to accompany us, so as to forestall any indiscretion.

At this point Fortunato Calvi, who had been speaking throughout in a low voice, almost as though meditating upon his own phrases, grew animated and said with intensity: "We approach the fortress preceded by the Austrian flag and beating the drum, as friends. Once past the bridge, once the foot is set beneath the gateway arch, the scene changes, the Latin valour bursts forth. On the ramparts, twenty-two of our own, we raise our flag — and woe betide whoever threatens it! The hostages remain with us, with us the arms, the money, and the audacity. Osoppo becomes our base of operations, and it is an impregnable instrument. Through the winter we organise the guerrilla warfare in the Alps, we hold in check the troops that would descend into the Peninsula. In the spring, Lombardy, Venetia, Hungary, France perhaps as well, second the movement — the oppressed and the oppressors measure their forces. The errors of the past are our instruction; the secular vow is fulfilled; Italy rises again, never to fall again."

I listened with bowed head, thinking what any other man would have thought: "To what extent could we count upon the ability of the European Revolutionary Committee to extend our initiative, to transform it into an international uprising?"

Could I hold back when an entity such as Fortunato Calvi was throwing himself entirely into the breach?

Three days, and I set out on my journey once more.

CHAPTER THE SEVENTEENTH

IN LOCARNO, where I had returned to deliver a missive from Mazzini to Calvi, I was arrested and released a few hours later, at liberty, on my word to present myself to the Federal Delegate. I was later tried for conspiracy against a friendly Power, expelled from Switzerland, and put to the alternative of choosing between America and England.

England was closer to the field of probable future enterprises and was preferred by me. I settled in London, where I found not a few of those compromised in the February uprisings, who had escaped Austrian vengeance through adventurous flights... among others, a Milanese Jean Valjean, accompanied out of the city walls in a coffin and with a solemn funeral.

Bound by our common ideal and common sufferings, we lived for some time in a community, in the district called Baldwin's Garden, and precisely in the modest inn of the Milanese Tressoldi, a good-natured fellow who possessed, among his other virtues, that of turning a blind eye when we could not settle the bill.

Giuseppe Mazzini arrived in London in April. From him we learned that Pianciani, who was then dictating his "History of the Popes," tempered the fatigues of the intellect by modifying and re-modifying the garden of his villa... a probability of work for several of us. We were presented to his amanuensis, a certain Bardini from Padova, a law student, and he, in turn, presented us to Pianciani who, there and then, entrusted the material labor of those incessant modifications to the young Varisco, granting him ample faculty to avail himself of the work of as many of us as he deemed necessary. I too was immediately hired, and thus, for some months, I had my sustenance from the spade and the flowers.

That simple and laborious life was troubled by terrible images. Austria was growing ever more ferocious, and every newspaper, every letter brought us the painful certainty that the phalanx of the generous was being inexorably thinned.

I spoke of it on long walks with Giuseppe Mazzini, and I would stare at his olive-hued, intellectual face, as if to say, "What is to be decided? Shall we let them be slaughtered like dogs?" The Master would quicken his pace. His expression was one of ineffable melancholy; but of the thoughts that swirled in his head... not a syllable!

One day in August, he sent for me with urgency. Why ever for! Certainly not to say "good day" or "good evening." So then?..

I planted the spade in the middle of a flowerbed: "Stay there! You are not the only noble instrument!" And off I ran.

But when I stood before the Master, I felt my spirits sink, so sparing of words was his welcome. I was asked to carry a letter to Kossuth and to await the reply. Not another syllable!

Kossuth then held a sort of Court near Regent's Circus. At the door I found carriages and servants in livery; up the stairs a coming and going of staid Englishmen, in formal attire; Hungarians in their elegant, militaristic national costume; people with large scars; uncertain figures, begging under the pretext of the Fatherland. Honest and dishonest, rich or poor, they rarely obtained access to the Dictator who, even beaten and in exile, exercised not only the prestige of his fame, but also that which comes from the habit of command.

While I awaited his pleasure in the antechamber, I tried to pass the time observing the paintings that hung from the walls: Kossuth in the guise of the simple citizen; Kossuth in hunting attire; Kossuth in a general's uniform, now in an armchair, now on horseback, now beside a shattered cannon; but always Kossuth, nothing but Kossuth!

Finally, from among so many simulacra, the great man appeared in the flesh. I must admit that art, in its intent to idealize him, had slandered him. The figure of the illustrious Magyar, more than affected heroism and slaughter, recalled paternal affections and the gentle emotions of the domestic hearth. It is the privilege of artists to alter, at their whim, the colors of History... for this reason Napoleon I is handed down to posterity crossing the St. Bernard on a prancing colt, with manifest injustice to the poor little donkey that lent its rump to so great a weight.

The welcome reserved for me was not what Count di Rudio, the Italian patriot, and the trusted envoy of Giuseppe Mazzini had expected. Kossuth read the letter I handed him while standing on the threshold and, casting it aside, drew a memorandum from his pocket, wrote a few lines, and ordered me, curtly, to carry them to their destination. Not a word of greeting! And I, descending the stairs, asked myself if the Cossacks of the Czar and the Croats of Jellacich, by lending a hand to Austria, had not, in substance, spared the brave Hungarians the troubles inherent in a change of dynasty.

The few lines given me by Kossuth were addressed to a high officer, whose name I am searching for in the recesses of my memory. From him I received several rolls of sterling... "The same," he told me, "that we received from Mazzini for our participation in the uprisings of 1853. May they aid your great, unfortunate country!"

Kossuth, on the morrow of the failure, had publicly denied any complicity in those uprisings!

Upon my return, the Master opened his soul. He began by asking me if I felt disposed to once again place my life in jeopardy for the cause of the Fatherland and, having received the answer from my very demeanor, he pointed me to a chair, and we reasoned in low voices, one opposite the other, for over an hour.

He explained to me that, on the advice of Fortunato Calvi, future insurrectionary attempts would take place in the region of the Alps. The reasons he adduced are the following: Our country is rich in latent forces, ready to erupt; but whoever takes an initiative must know how to give assurance of serious resistance. The mountains are the natural bulwark of the weak and the oppressed. There it becomes possible for the few to stand against the many; possible to escape pursuit; to descend with the rapidity of an eagle to the plain; to surprise, to intercept. For the pitched battle, which was denied to us, it was necessary to substitute guerrilla warfare, which is necessarily the daughter of the mountains. If the banner of redemption could be kept upright and flying for three, for two months, there would not be a patriot without fever, not a corner of Italy where the action would not blaze forth.

This last concept seemed to lead him to another order of thought. A shadow spread over his face; then he said, almost verbatim: "I know! Cowardice, masked as prudence, accuses me of pushing spirited youth to the gallows, of provoking desperate attempts that offer Austrian tyranny, and our own, the opportunity to intensify their cruelties. But they do not reflect that the audacity and sacrifice of the few always end up shaking the torpid and dragging the many into action. Nothing great without audacity! Napoleon III would probably not have smoothed his path to the Empire without the failures of Strasbourg and Boulogne. No King of Italy will show a soul so great as to champion the redemption of our Fatherland, so long as Italians show themselves to be basking, in peace, in slavery. Would Carlo Alberto have crossed the Ticino were it not for the magnanimous self-denial of the Milanese! It is an imperative necessity to keep the great Italian question open, pressing. Europe cannot have peace if our agony endures. It will intervene, sooner or later, in our favor, and all the sooner the more intolerant of the yoke and menacing we show ourselves to be. And what good are good ideas, bold intentions, when a lack of initiative relegates them to the realm of mere abstractions! Action cleanses our centuries-old faults. Between us and a free Italy lies this bath of blood. Should we hold back because of the slanders of cowards!"

That very evening I procured a passport with an assumed name and left for Switzerland. Fortunato Calvi was already waiting for me in Zurigo. "The plan for the new uprisings is his," Mazzini had told me. "It is for him to see to its execution. You will receive the necessary instructions from his own mouth."

In Bruxelles, where I made a brief stop, I delivered a letter from the Master to dall'Ungaro, the Venetian Catullus. "Where is he, what is the Master doing?" he asked me, almost feverishly. And I, with his own verses:

"Mazzini is in the place where one trembles

"That the supreme hour may come for the traitors;

"Mazzini is in every place where one works

"To shed blood for all of Italy!"

Having arrived in Zurigo, before going to Calvi, I wanted to have my hands free and hurried to visit de Boni, to whom I also delivered a missive; a third I brought to Caronti from Como. Giuseppe Mazzini had recommended that I be very wary of this man, of whom he was very suspicious. However, he contributed rather significant sums to the good cause, and this kept him from getting rid of him.

The brazenly inquisitive questions that Caronti directed at me came to me, already ill-disposed, as a strong irritant, and when I set foot back over the threshold of his villa, I felt a sense of relief, as if I had suddenly been freed from a nightmare.

I could finally restore myself in the embrace of an authentic patriot, of an unsullied conscience... Fortunato Calvi! What joy in seeing him again, still handsome, still proud, still exuding that indefinable sense of serenity that emanates from honest souls!

The substantial part of our colloquy had a beginning which I shall narrate without false modesty.

"You are not," he told me, "and you must not be an instrument for blindly executing orders you do not comprehend. I shall confide in you the plan I have devised and which the European Revolutionary Committee has fully approved. Thus you will know how to parry, with unity of purpose, the inevitable counterblows. CHAPTER THIRTEEN

I KNOW of no Italian insurrectionary movement that brought upon Giuseppe Mazzini so many calumnies, so much bitterness, so many desertions as that of 1853.

The so-called practical men, with that short-sighted practicality which consists in leaving the world to run undisturbed even to its own ruin, maintained that a People recently bled white and broken could not respond with spirit to a call to arms; could not willingly submit to new sacrifices. And they pronounced, with great solemnity, that revolutions come to pass when they ripen of themselves, not when one wills it so.

A singular maxim which, stripped to its core, would lead to this other: "To win one's independence, to vindicate one's rights, nothing is better than to recline in one's armchair, leaving to the Sun the care of ripening events, as it ripens gourds."

The movements organised by Giuseppe Mazzini were never, in truth, either revolution or war; but they indubitably prepared the way for both and rendered them necessary.

Moreover, those of February 1853 were not initiated by the great agitator, but by precisely that People which, were one to trust the serious-minded men, would have had no thought save to make itself comfortable among accomplished facts and its own misery.

And Mazzini himself has said as much, and has written it, and has proved it. Yet even today there is no lack of supermen, the lustre and ornament of our Italy, who, unable to refute the declarations of that great man who never lied, skip over them with masterly ease and continue to extol the wonders of the armchair, persisting in the old calumnies.

The *Fratellanza*, a secret society bent upon insurrection, was already strongly organised in Milan three years after every hope for Italy had seemed to wither. Composed of several thousand workmen, it was subdivided into various sections distinguished by the letters of the alphabet. Its existence was utterly unknown to the Austrian Police, to Mazzini, to the intellectuals, to the aristocratic class, who, for all that, shared with it a common hatred of the foreign yoke.

Its principal organiser, the dyer Assi, entered into communication with Mazzini when he judged that the *Fratellanza* had sufficient strength to act. Assi requested arms, money, and leaders capable of bringing a plan of attack to a successful issue.

Mazzini received the news as proof that the propagation of his ideas had brought forth good seed even among the common multitude; but he did not place full credence in Assi's assurances regarding the numerical strength and the seriousness of purpose of the new organisation, until Major Eugenio Brizzi of Jesi and other informants, sent by him secretly to Milan, had confirmed them in every particular.

Nevertheless, he was urging patience when, in December 1852, Austria, giving fresh vent to her ferocity, crowned the ramparts of Belfiore with gibbets and, having chosen a victim from every rank of citizens, hanged them there.

In the face of this grim provocation, it seemed to the workmen of the *Fratellanza* a crime to deliberate any longer. They notified Mazzini that they had resolved to act, even without his aid and without his consent. Mazzini's inescapable duty then became that of seconding the inevitable, of endeavouring to ensure that this generous initiative should not remain isolated and fruitless.

In the last days of December 1852, charged with a mission received directly from Count Grillanzoni, I departed for Milan, and at midnight of the day on which I arrived, after a brief wait beneath the clock in the Piazza Mercanti, I delivered a packet of instructions to a man who, emerging from the shadow, had given me the agreed signal.

The definitive plan of the insurrection thus reached the hands of Major Brizzi himself, who was charged with overseeing its execution. The plan was, in its principal lines, the work of Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Its fundamental basis was the principle that weapons must be taken from the enemy by surprise, after his forces had been thrown into disorder by the killing of his officers.

By a privilege of regulation, these officers slept, as a general rule, not in barracks but in private lodgings. Note had been taken of their addresses, and their customary meeting-places were, of course, known as well.

The Austrian military establishment was, among other positions, quartered within the walls of Milan at the Palace of the General Command, at that of the Grand Guard, and at the Castello.

The first was guarded — rather than defended — by a picket of twenty-five soldiers. Their weapons stood ranged upon the gun-rack, beneath the passageway of the gatehouse, before which a vigilant sentinel paced unceasingly. The other soldiers whiled away the hours stretching their limbs in the courtyard or smoking in the barrack-rooms. At five in the afternoon, the Governor, the Generals, and the General Staff dined in that palace.

To reduce the sentinel to helplessness by a sudden assault would not have been a particularly difficult surprise. As a small cord draws a rope and the rope hauls the great cable, that first slight success would have put into the hands of the insurgents some thirty rifles, the disarmed picket, and the chiefs of the garrison.

Something similar, but with more powerful means, was to be attempted at the Grand Guard, which housed some hundred of the ruffians; but above all at the Castello, which contained in its armoury twelve thousand muskets. The two armourers who inspected them daily were men of ours. They undertook to leave the doors ajar, so that the time lost in breaking them down should not rob us of a precious moment.

In the premises of the Grand Guard, the insurgents were to entrench themselves and establish their own headquarters, to give a centre to the revolution. And from that centre, clad in the uniforms stripped from the dead and the prisoners, small patrols were to fan out who, beating the drum-roll, would have induced officers to emerge from their dwellings. On the threshold they would find those who would undertake to seal their lips — and for ever.

The sixth of February was chosen for the strike upon the enemy, a day of carnival festivity and of pay for the soldiers. Their quarters would thereby be left almost deserted. The surprise would thus have a far greater probability of success; and our men would have the greater ease to pour into the cafés and taverns, and to renew, though for a more sublime purpose, the blood-tide of the Vespers.

To smooth the path to victory, Giuseppe Mazzini had solicited and obtained from Kossuth, who was on the point of departing for the United States of America, a revolutionary proclamation addressed to his compatriots quartered principally in the barracks of San Francesco; and pledges had already been received that, at the first sign of a serious insurrection, the ill-subdued sons of valiant Hungary would have made common cause with their oppressed Italian brothers.

As the crowning touch of the enterprise — the barricades! The contractor for the city's lighting, in order to protect those who would raise them, had pledged to leave Milan in darkness. And to defend them, Giuseppe Mazzini had caused certain projectiles of a new invention to be smuggled into the city, to be hurled upon the assailants in the manner of bombs.

One must add that the students of Pavia, secretly consulted, had pledged to hasten to the aid of the capital; the agreed signal, a blaze upon the spire of the Cathedral. Acerbi was to have commanded them — and they asked nothing more than weapons. Mazzini had provided these by way of the Po.

Nor was the preparation of simultaneous movements beyond the walls limited to this. In Piedmont, the throng of political exiles kept watch, impatient for action. It was necessary to open them a broad and easy road to descend, in organised formation, upon the offensive. Giuseppe Mazzini entrusted this charge to Fortunato Calvi, who conceived the bold plan of seizing Lake Maggiore, a design I shall explain shortly.

And Aurelio Saffi, after having knocked at the doors of the wealthier patriots — on which occasion Agostino Depretis, the future Prime Minister, contributed twenty-five thousand lire — went to Bologna, defying death, and was fomenting, with fair hopes of success, the insurrection of that city and of the Romagna.

To put it briefly, Giuseppe Mazzini had secured the co-operation of the centres which might have interposed a barrier between the Milan garrison and the various Austrian nuclei scattered about the Peninsula. Fortunato Calvi confided in me later, at Zurich, that he had cherished the hope of turning, within a fortnight, with the revolutionary forces, the Austrian army of Italy, and of cutting it off at the Alps from its base of replenishment. It sufficed, to give spirit and time to Lombardy to rise, that Milan might hold firm and fight in her streets for at least a few days. And Italy would have won her independence by her own strength, as Greece had won hers, as the United States had won theirs, avoiding the fatal aftermath left by hybrid alliances, murky intrigues, and compromises that have clouded her greatness and whose effects, unhappily, will continue to be felt for a long time yet.

But here I do not argue, I narrate.

The workmen of the *Fratellanza*, with the exception of the leaders, were ignorant of the particulars of the insurrectionary plan; but they knew that on the sixth of February the blow would be struck. They had unearthed some hundred pistols and, on Mazzini's advice, had set themselves to fashioning daggers and knives. Even the rivets from the boats of their Naviglio were prised out and fixed upon shafts — everything serves the desperation of a People!

And I, seized by the general fever, was already weighing, in order to forestall them, all the foreseeable adverse contingencies of the assault upon the Grand Guard, to which Brizzi had appointed me, when I received from Count Grillanzoni the order to proceed immediately to Genoa and to await his instructions there.

These instructions were: to meet with a certain affiliated member, to have counted out to me, in Mazzini's name, four thousand lire in gold; to carry them to Sander and Radonitz, who were awaiting me at Locarno. To follow, on the journey, the road along Lake Maggiore and to watch whether anything unusual might be observed there indicating that Calvi's plans had been discovered, or that, by obstructing them, their modification had become necessary. To meet, on my return from Locarno, with Calvi himself, who would be found at Intra, and to report to him punctually all that I had seen and heard.

Austria maintained on the Verbano a flotilla of gunboats which ordinarily lay at anchor opposite Luino. Assigned to the surveillance of the shores and to the passenger service was the steamship *Radetzky*, which plied the lake daily and always had on board a company of soldiers. These, during the crossing, remained below decks, leaving their rifles ranged near the hatchway under the watch of a sentinel. With the sentinel overpowered, it seemed more than probable that one might seize the weapons and shut the other soldiers below in the hold. Sander and Radonitz, formerly officers in the Austrian navy and subsequently passed into the service of the Venetian Republic, would have taken command of the steamship and disposed of it as of an Italian vessel.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi had determined that the *Radetzky*, putting in at Luino well into the evening, should continue to fly the Austrian flag, with myself and a handful of our men on board, clad in the hated uniform.

It was necessary to approach as friends the gunboats unprepared for action; necessary to seize them by surprise. Then, having towed them out into open water, to turn their murderous muzzles upon the small garrison. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, having organised a sound body of patriots, would have assaulted Luino from the landward side. With the Italian flag raised over that strategic point, masters of the lake, the munitions, arms, and armed men coming from Piedmont would have found a comparatively open road to the very walls of Milan.

When I arrived at Arona there was still some hours to go before the departure of the *Radetzky*. I spent them walking along the shore of the lake. I recall that the magnificence of the landscape, all green and Alpine despite the winter, made upon my spirit an impression of such sweetness that, for a moment, it all but unmanned me. And I marvelled that men, being able to enjoy in fraternal ease so much of paradise, should spoil it for themselves by clutching at one another perpetually, as though nothing could take root in their breasts save base and ferocious passions.

But no sooner had the bell of the *Radetzky* announced that departure was imminent than my rebellious spirit reasserted itself. I thought: "Beautiful is our Fatherland. For that very reason they put the knife to our throats and attempt to make it their own. Therefore, all the more holy, as it is the more fierce, the rage that drives us to defend it. Let them recross the Alps and we shall be brothers again."

The steamship stood out into the open water. I lit my pipe and, walking with slow steps, this way and that, I endeavoured to assume the air of one who contemplates, in a kind of rapture, the landscape gliding past. But my mind was fixed upon my secret purpose. I saw the carbines ranged near the hatchway; I scrutinised — weighed, almost — the sentinel who, with German rigidity, with the regularity of a pendulum, trod back and forth across the planking; I heard chaotic voices rising from the depths of the vessel and understood them — they spoke, with coarse candour, of haunches and rumps, punctuating their discourse with coarse laughter. Nor did there emerge at any point during the entire voyage, from that dark recess, the pomaded moustache of a *kaiserlich* officer. Faithful to their orders, those geometric heads!

Our coup de main seemed to me so certain of success that more than once I bitterly regretted being alone and unprepared to attempt it.

But at Locarno I found spirits less willing than my own. Sander and Radonitz received me, not without a certain stiffness, at the house of one Olivieri, a dealer in sundry goods and also in arms. They took and counted carefully the gold which I drew from my money-belt. But when it came to settling the preliminaries of the action, the buts and the ifs grew thick and solid as a wall.

A few days before — I was told — two Hungarians with whom secret dealings had been in train had been thrown into prison; two of our men. "The Authorities kept them in complete isolation, and nothing had transpired regarding the grounds of their arrest. Why such precautions, such mystery? There was cause for alarm at far less than this."

Radonitz was firmly persuaded that the Police had caught some scent of what was being plotted. To attempt new ventures would be to walk headlong into the net. He urged that courage must not be confounded with rashness. It was a matter of the lives, sacred to him, of so many patriots. What would be lost by delay; by ascertaining, with due caution, the temper of an enemy so powerful and so cruel? Wisdom lay not in haste, but in acting in such manner that victory should prove certain and all but inevitable.

To all this I replied that if the arrest of some brother were enough to deflect Italians from their purposes, it would be as well to renounce action for ever. Since '49, not a day had passed without the Austrian baton being at work, without some chain being riveted afresh. The Police could not have suspected a movement as vast as ours without taking extraordinary precautions, which could not have escaped the notice of our brothers scattered everywhere. No alarm had come from any quarter whatsoever. I myself had traversed the lake from end to end, aboard an Austrian vessel, with my eye alert, and had noticed nothing — nothing at all — out of the ordinary. Moreover, the chances of discovery were in proportion to the time spent in organising the conspiracy and to the number of those who took part in it. All the more urgent, therefore, appeared the necessity for swift and almost lightning-like action. Fortunato Calvi must already have departed from Turin; he would be at Intra on the morrow. To him alone belonged the decision as to whether delay was more or less opportune. Our inaction would have robbed him of the possibility of choice and would have amounted to desertion. At all events, I had come to Locarno not to debate but to act. My orders were to report at Intra all that I had seen and heard. I would set out again alone, though it cost me my life.

Present while I spoke was Olivieri's wife. She left without a word and returned shortly after, bearing a packet of revolutionary proclamations and a bundle of daggers. She helped me, with her own hands, to conceal both upon my person, and all argument was thus cut short. On the following morning I embarked on the *Arno*, a Piedmontese steamship, and descended swiftly along the Verbano. Sander and Radonitz had remained ashore!

What a profound change in those few hours! The overcast sky heralded the fall of snow; the lake grey, and almost ashen. From the Alps there blew an icy wind that seemed to have swept away all festive life from the shores and the hillsides.

I found aboard the *Arno* muffled-up passengers with an air of self-absorbed preoccupation; common faces betraying the anxieties of commerce. My attention came at last to fix itself upon the captain, a bony man with a phenomenally large head. He was absorbed in the reading of a newspaper and cast only an occasional glance at the course. Who was he? What sentiments animated him? Could the name and the sufferings of the Fatherland find a place in his heart?

I answered myself with yes, and committed a grave error. He had suddenly risen and, as if following the impulse of a fleeting thought, walked with hurried steps to another part of the deck. He had left his newspaper on a seat. I drew forth a revolutionary proclamation, concealed it within the folds of that newspaper, and returned at a run to my place.

I did not wait long! A face painfully surprised, a knitting of brows, a swaying of that heavy head. He fixed me with a pair of eyes I have never since forgotten. Perhaps they were set in the face of the most immaculate Aristides that Piedmont had ever produced; but to me they seemed steeped, and all but drunk, with idiotic commiseration and bestial menace. I dissembled by whistling a Verdi tune into the wind that roared about me.

I am at Intra. Among the knot of curious idlers gathered to see the steamship arrive, I catch — as one might snatch something from the air — a well-known signal. I followed at a distance the person who had given it and was led to a solitary villa. The unknown brother remained outside and I went in. I found myself face to face with Fortunato Calvi.

What a flood of feeling, and how many things I should have wished to say to him; how many others, I imagine, he would have wished to confide to me! But we had to content ourselves with clasping each other's hand at length. For a moment, neither he nor I could speak a word. When I at last managed to unlock my lips, I said: "Are the sentinels at their posts?"

Having received the assurance that they were, I drew from my person the proclamations and every one of the daggers save one, and laid them on a table. I continued:

— All here and in the neighbourhood. They await the arms and the signal.

— I have had news of him from Milan. (1) It was an imprudence; but his shrewdness stood him in good stead. He is now at Lugano. Let the arms do their duty; the head watches and will do its own.

I then gave him a full account of my journey, and concealed nothing of the strange hesitation of Sander and Radonitz, who had failed to appear at the rendezvous. I had not yet finished when a window opened and in the aperture appeared the face of the same affiliated member who had guided me thither. He said, in an agitated voice: "Run, run! The carabinieri are at the gate. They are looking for you!"

In a single bound I was in the garden, which I crossed at a run, taking care that the view of the gentlemen at the gate should remain intercepted by the house. I climbed a tree and vaulted the enclosing wall. Four strides and I was at the lake-shore. On the shingle, chained to a landing-stage, several small boats lay drawn up in a row. One alone, propelled by two oarsmen, was gliding along close to the shore, as though seeking a place to run in and ground. Before the oarsmen, stupefied, had time to open their mouths, I had leapt aboard and was finding my balance amid that small shell. "Twenty francs for you! Quickly, to Locarno!" They understood and bent to the oars.

And then, followed by a crowd of children, two gendarmes arrived gesticulating on the shore. One jumped onto the shingle and busied himself, but in vain, with the boats. They were locked with padlocks, I believe. The other, flailing his arms like a Dutch windmill, shouted: "Stop, stop!" I judged it prudent not to entrust my salvation to another's compassion and, grasping my double-barrelled pistol: "Either to Locarno, or to the next world!"

From the shore came laughter and applause. The two carabinieri scanned the surface of the lake — no hope of assistance from that quarter. They vented their fury by shaking their fists at the horizon.

(1) That Mazzini was in Milan in 1853 was never stated either by him or by others, so far as I am aware. But the Count di Rudio insists that Fortunato Calvi's assertion admits of no ambiguity.

C. C.
