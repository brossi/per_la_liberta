I wandered, almost adrift, through various cities, until I took refuge in Genova. The "Superb" had ten to twelve thousand political refugees on its hands. It provided for them with generous donations; but this was like throwing water on sand.

The city, saturated with political passions, was disturbed by frequent tumults, and the exiles, made more restless by their sufferings, added no small amount of kindling. A son of my times, far from considering myself above my surroundings, I too did as the others did. Little by little, I fell into great straits. My father, who learned of it, wrote a warm letter of recommendation to Alessandro Lamarmora, and I was unexpectedly offered the rank of second lieutenant in the Bersaglieri. I refused! Why? I refused!

Why?

Sudden conversions were not yet in fashion in Italy, and I did not feel it was for me to begin the series. Mazzinian to the marrow, I would have preferred to serve Satan than to swear fealty to a Monarchy. Let it be added that the Bersaglieri were, at that time, terribly hated in Genova... a fatal legacy of the Piedmontese cannonades!

I continued to live by humble work and by expedients, until an unforeseen event brought me the relief... of changing the nature of my embarrassments. A renowned republican had died. His funeral was memorable for the extraordinary concourse of the agitated multitude and for the no less extraordinary concourse of armed forces, intended to keep it within the prescribed limits. At the cemetery, passions erupted. Acerbic speeches were delivered, with open allusions to the monarchical objection and to the hopes placed in the future. They aroused a torrent of approvals. All at once, the banner with the motto "God and People," hidden on the breast of some Mazzinian, was unfurled high above the surging heads. From the Sun it received its vivid colours, from the sea breeze its tremors; from the generous and impatient, a long, thunderous salute.

That harmless cloth precipitated matters. At its sight, the Bersaglieri charged, as resolute as if it were a matter of avenging an insult. Balilla, ever alive in Genova, greeted them with a volley of stones and enraged them. Then the veins of the rioters stained the precinct of the dead with blood.

Shortly thereafter, mass arrests were carried out and, as always on such occasions, at random. I who, rightly or wrongly, had been among the first to raise my fists, did not wait for the good pleasure of the constables and, that very day, seizing on the fly one of those opportunities that one would say had rained down from heaven, I was sailing toward free America.

I did not reach it. A storm, which forced us to put in at Cartagena, once again changed the course of my existence. Before us, Spain with its multiple attractions: beyond Spain, France... political energy, life! I consulted with a certain Trolli, a Lombard patriot, exiled from Genova under the identical pressure and with the identical purpose as my own. We had a few small coins in our pockets, but we were endowed with nimble legs. We concluded, with a unanimous cry: "To the Pyrenees!"

Trolli belonged to a very good family of Varese, which, intending to make of him something like a Cardinal, had sent him into the priesthood. When the revolution broke out, he cast his cassock to the nettles and took up a rifle. An ardent republican, he brought to his convictions the full exuberance proper to a strong constitution and a flourishing youth. So much so that he would affirm that the most sacred shots he had fired were not against the Germans, but against that poor "execrated Carignano," whom he accused, among other things, of having betrayed the Milanese by abandoning them, without striking a blow, to Austrian vengeance. Which had not prevented him—he would add—from making off with the jewels donated by the Lombard women for the defence of their metropolis.

Our small coins having vanished after a few days of forced marches, the problem of appetite became, for us, as arduous as it was imperious. But my Cardinal-in-the-making had an inspiration. He said to me something to this effect:

"Garibaldian tactics: are provisions lacking? Seize them from the enemy! Do we need bread? The enemy will give us bread and all that goes with it. I studied to be a priest; Latin is my forte. We come from Roma; we are two crows plucked bare by the revolution. I will serve Mass; I will give a few sermons in bad Spanish, but stuffed with excellent Latin. The curias and the monasteries will throw open their doors to us... the doors of the refectory. A comedy with a laden table; star actors on both sides and... may our bellies rejoice!"

My disapproval was tepid. At the sight of the first bell tower, Trolli put an abrupt end to it by taking action.

By this artifice we made our way from Cartagena to Alicante, then to Valencia, then to Saragozza, then to Tarragona. Trolli gave proof of an inexhaustible quickness of wit. The seeds sown in his soul by the Seminary had not fallen on barren ground. With a single glance he could distinguish the simple, good-faith priest from the sly one who, aware of the Church's hidden aims, treated his "mission" as a comfortable trade. His transformations bordered on the phenomenal. Humble, steeped in a seraphic candour with some, he assumed with others an undercurrent of malice that asserted itself even while scrupulously respecting the forms. If he ran into some distrust, he would use and abuse his Latin. Those poor prelates, in their relative ignorance, felt themselves defeated, almost annihilated. Trolli would later say to me, in private: "You see that the language of the dead works miracles in the mouths of the living!"

We did not always live in that manner. In Barcellona, where Trolli was expecting a remittance from home, which unfortunately did not arrive, we took the advice of a certain Piedmontese, a cripple who leaned on crutches and made ends meet by selling saints and madonnas.

And at the Caffè del Liceo, a meeting-place for political refugees, making good use of our brazenness, we planted ourselves in the middle of the room and intoned, in thirds, the hymn of Mameli. For months we had not given legitimate vent to our rebellious souls, and that plunge into clear water had the virtue of imparting to our song an impetus it did not derive from art.

There was some applause. A gentleman with large, grizzled moustaches asked us, in French, who we were.

— Refugees from Roma.

That was enough. He turned to his friends and harangued them in a loud voice. He told them that we were driven from our Fatherland for the crime of having loved and defended it. And, after having painted in vivid colours the sad life of the exile, he added: "Who was the cause of these evils? Who has made the hearts of so many generous men bleed? We, we French, who, being strong, felt no shame in attacking the weak; we who, having risen in defence of a great principle, have promptly disowned it, turning our arms against a people who rose courageously, like us, for the vindication of the identical principle; we who, having barely acquired liberty, have abused it by strangling the liberty of others!" He concluded by recalling the ineluctable duty of every Frenchman to strive individually to relieve the sorrows brought about by the Nation.

And, taking off his hat, he made the rounds of the room, from table to table, soliciting and collecting oblations.

We left Barcellona with twenty-five *duros* in our pockets. With that viaticum, we crossed the Pyrenees and arrived safely in Perpignano.

Here I was forced to agree that, even outside the setting of old-fashioned novels, a good deed can find a greater reward than that which derives from the satisfaction of having performed it.

The remittance from Varese, which we fingered, counted, and re-counted right there in Perpignano, had allowed us to transform ourselves from hat to shoes and to enter a café for a little recreation.

The large room was crowded with soldiers talking loudly about affairs in Algeria. I was listening in silence, sipping a cool drink, when I felt as though I were being stared at by invisible eyes. I turned with a start. At a nearby table sat several officers, one of whom was indeed sizing me up.

— I beg your pardon. By your appearance... you are not French.

— Italian, and not of the worst sort.

— Were you in Roma?

— At the time of your siege.

— And... do you remember the San Siro bridge? Oh, I am not mistaken! Your face, your voice...

And he embraced me.

In the room... a profound silence. I felt myself pushed, with affectionate force, toward the group of officers.

— I wish to present you to my comrades-in-arms. The right hand of a French soldier always cordially clasps that of the valiant, all the more so if, one day, though not hating one another, they found themselves on opposite sides.

It was my prisoner from the Villa Corsini, the officer for whom I had prevented a mortal leap into the Tevere. That evening, we dined merrily in the barracks. And since, in conversation, we revealed our intention of moving further into the territory of the Republic, they insisted that, honoured as guests, we accompany the Regiment, which was the 10th Light Infantry, as far as Marsiglia, where it was heading to embark for Africa.

It was a succession of dinners and receptions that lasted until Montpellier. The mysterious politics that already informed the affairs of France had sent an unexpected counter-order there. The officer from the Villa Corsini gave me a last, affectionate embrace and continued with his men toward Parigi; I, with Trolli, continued my journey on foot to Marsiglia... our paths and our souls never crossed again.

My stay in this latter city is memorable for me, because it was there that I pronounced the inspired oath of Mazzini and was affiliated with the Giovane Italia. At the same time, I became part of the Circolo del Paradiso, a French subversive association that, in many matters, acted in concert with it. Except that, one month later... new troubles! In keeping with the reactionary wind blowing secretly from the capital, the Police surrounded the headquarters of the Circolo. All those they found there did not sleep in their own beds that night. The foreign members were peremptorily expelled from France. To me and to Trolli, the Police handed a passport, with a pre-established itinerary, which was supposed to lead us, stage by stage, to England.

In Digione, with the first train, we slipped through the meshes of the net. The Authorities either did not bother with us or were unable to find our tracks, and, undisturbed, we entered the great vortex of Parigi.

It was June of 1851, and tempestuous days were brewing for France; but to the eye of the superficial observer, this was not apparent. Luigi Napoleone, having assassinated the Roman Republic, had acquired a taste for blood and was preparing for a vaster, if not greater, crime... the assassination of the French Republic. His Presidency expired on March 4, 1852. As the fatal date approached, his hopes, his fears, his hatreds, his cupidities sharpened. But in dissimulating his labours, in dispelling distrust, he appears to us a master. His ideal... to aim for the heights of Napoleon I and to reach them with the arts of Giulio Cesare. A careful study of the life of the great Roman had persuaded him that a new tyranny is not established when, the mask thrown off, it audaciously proclaims itself as such. The favour of the plebs was indispensable to him. How to secure it? He had begun by making them believe in his concern for the disinherited classes by publishing, on the subject of pauperism, pages that did not seem adverse to the doctrines of the socialists. Accused openly of ambitious designs, he responded with his famous comparison between Napoleon and Washington, concluding that, being unable to possess the genius of the first, his sole ambition was to imitate the virtue of the second. And he hammered the point home by affirming, on every propitious occasion, that he would consider as an enemy of his Country anyone who attempted to make himself great by violating the Laws.

Such prattle not only lulled the common multitude; it also had its effect on the National Assembly. This was composed of the most disparate elements who, while exalting the excellence of the then-current regime, championed very different ideals. And since, even in the political field, men easily believe what they wish, the honourable hotchpotch was, by those vanities, easily led to the persuasion that the President of the Republic, more than satisfied with his rapid career, was not thinking of attempting dangerous innovations. On the other hand, Legitimists, Orleanists, reactionaries, and radicals expended their prudence, exhausted their energy, in watching one another, and did not much concern themselves with Luigi Napoleone, who was held, despite the admonitions of his dark past, to be a man too weak and worthless to meditate upon virtuously great enterprises or great crimes.

In the general foolishness, there was no lack of shrewd men who foresaw the danger; but being conscious of their own weakness, they prepared for the surprises of the future by working silently, in the shadows.

Unexpectedly, the National Assembly, disowning its own origin, abolished universal suffrage. Luigi Napoleone had found it!

He could now cut the ground out from under his enemies; plunge into a cowardly crime and find historians to extol its virtue; enslave the multitude and, posturing as its champion, secure for himself, at the same time, its neutrality, perhaps even its benevolence!
