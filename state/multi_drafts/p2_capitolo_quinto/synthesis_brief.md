NEL Settembre del 1857, mentre campavo alla meglio, dando lezioni di lingua a Nottingham, un esule italiano, di passaggio per quella città, venne a farmi visita. Egli trovavasi in circostanze molto più critiche delle mie. Lo sovvenni e, il giorno stesso, proseguì pel suo cammino. Arrivato a Birmingham, si rivolse per soccorsi a Felice Orsini, che gli era, se non m'inganno, compaesano. Nel parlare delle sue traversie, alluse alla visita fattami. "Per Dio! esclamò Felice Orsini. Proprio l'uomo che cerco!" E mi scrisse, dandomi appuntamento alla stazione della Great Northern, a Londra.

Non avevo visto Felice Orsini da lungo tempo. Ma non appena il treno entrò, sbuffando, sotto la tettoia della stazione (Charing Cross) lo scorsi ritto tra la folla, colle sue cassette e i suoi baffi nerissimi. Pochi secondi e lo fronteggiavo. "Dove diavolo sei stato sinora?" mi disse. Ed io glie lo raccontai. Quando allusi al mio matrimonio, il suo viso si rabbuiò. "Allora, addio propositi patriottici!" Lo disingannai. Adesso anzi mi correva obbligo tanto maggiore di cospirare e di battermi... mio figlio non doveva avere una patria serva ed infelice.

Mi domandò se conoscevo il motivo per cui m'aveva dato appuntamento ed alla mia risposta negativa, mi invitò ad uscire dalla stazione. Giunti all'aperto mi rammentò, senza preamboli, la lettera da me scritta a Giuseppe Mazzini e la mia proposta di sbarazzare la Francia sopprimendo Napoleone. Egli aveva in animo di tradurre in atto quella mia vecchia idea. I tempi gli sembravano propizi. Ero ancora dell'istesso parere?

— Che ne pensa il Mazzini?

A questo, egli fece un gesto quasi dicesse: Perchè mi parli di archeologia? E rafforzò il suo concetto affermando che, col Mazzini, non parlava più. D'altra parte Massarenti, l'alter ego del "profeta" sfalsava la razza bolognese e lo avrebbe, quando subodorasse la cosa, indubbiamente denunziato. Anche per questo, bisognava lasciare il Mazzini da parte.

Finii per dirgli che non ero alieno dal partecipare all'impresa. Ma volevo vederci chiaro. Ucciso Napoleone, che cosa accadrebbe? Quali erano i suoi piani per il dappoi?

Ripeto, in riassunto, le sue spiegazioni. Esse rivelano, se non il piano, i fini di una congiura sulla quale si è tanto scritto, ma non mai rivelando, nella sua interezza, il vero. Esse riescono, in pari tempo, una postuma confutazione agli appunti che alcuni storici, pur difettando dei necessari coefficienti di giudizio vollero muovere all'Orsini.

Felice Orsini si era messo in pieno accordo coi Repubblicani francesi. Il colpo di Stato li aveva sorpresi e paralizzati, non domi. Essi congiuravano per preparare una rivoluzione. Un colpo di mano fortunato a Parigi significava la liberazione della Francia e la Francia risorta a libertà, significava l'intera Europa in fermento. Ledru Rollin — mi assicurò l'Orsini — dispera del successo sinché si trovi di fronte la losca figura di Napoleone. Vi è più forza di quanto si creda, in un nome. Se la Francia si agita, Kossuth garantisce dell'Ungheria. Per quanto riguarda l'Italia nostra... soppresso Napoleone, a Parigi si organizzano le barricate. Il nemico, colto all'impensata, senza un capo autorevole, colla compattezza dell'esercito già minata da trame sottili, cade ignominiosamente. Si proclama la Repubblica e, immediatamente, vengo nominato, con pieni poteri, Commissario presso l'esercito francese stanziato a Roma. Sono trentamila uomini agguerriti e ben equipaggiati che guadagno, d'un sol colpo, alla causa italiana. Che faccio? Cancello, in un sol giorno, l'opera e l'onta napoleonica del 1849. Il Papa e i Cardinali diventano miei prigionieri e me li chiudo in Castel Sant'Angelo... il loro fato dipenderà dall'attitudine dell'Europa. Io intanto recido il potere temporale dallo spirituale e proclamo, dal Campidoglio, risorta la Repubblica Romana. La guarnigione francese lascia Roma ed io la spingo rapidamente in aiuto della rivoluzione italiana. Si vince? Tanto meglio! Si subisce uno scacco? La Francia repubblicana, punta sul vivo, si solleva come una furia e non posa più l'armi sinché il prestigio del proprio nome non sia pienamente rivendicato. L'Austria, attaccata da ogni parte, senz'amici in Europa, colla ribellione che divampa entro i suoi propri confini, è spazzata via e non rimette più il piede in Italia...

Certo, resterebbe a stabilire l'assetto politico definitivo della Penisola. Ma vi sono cose che, a dispetto della loro vastità, si accomodano quasi da sé stesse. Il dualismo cieco, cocciuto fra repubblicani e monarchici è già riuscito fatale al Popolo italiano; la lezione lo ha ammaestrato. Il Popolo vuole l'indipendenza e l'unità e mette, ormai, in seconda linea tutto il resto. Se la Monarchia Sabauda si mostrasse capace di dargli la forza che gli manca e di guidarlo alla vittoria, esso si getterebbe, come un sol uomo, ai piedi di Vittorio Emanuele. Se la vittoria gli verrà da Roma repubblicana e da alleati repubblicani, quale sovrano si sentirà da tanto da opporsi alla proclamazione della Repubblica?

Nel momento attuale, la nobile gara è aperta. Dovremmo noi repubblicani, rinunciare spontaneamente alla gloriosa iniziativa?

Tutto dipende dall'audacia! Bisogna saper gettare la prima scintilla. Senz'essa non v'ha incendio, non è possibile l'indipendenza, non è possibile la Repubblica. Tu sai che io sono contrario allo spargimento del sangue. Natura mi ha dotato di un temperamento umano: miro all'alto e i mezzi imposti dai tempi tristi, mi ripugnano. Ma io dico che il sangue di Luigi Bonaparte, dell'uomo che ha scientemente mancato agli impegni solennemente giurati, che ci ha tradito; che ha rimesso in catene la nostra Patria; che fa strame d'ogni nobile aspirazione della Patria sua, non deve pesare sulla bilancia quanto la libertà di settanta milioni d'umane creature. Lo spargerlo, scongiura il sacrificio di migliaia e migliaia di onesti e l'atto crudele diventa opera di pietà. Decidi!

Ed io mi decisi. La correttezza dell'uomo mi era nota; noto, dal più al meno, il sottostrato rivoluzionario francese; le condizioni dell'Italia, note. L'approfondire ritenni superfluo e mi accontentai di domandare il nome degli esecutori materiali del piano. Ma l'Orsini se ne schermì. Il grande pericolo, nelle grandi congiure — mi fece osservare — è appunto la loro vastità. Più numerosi sono coloro che hanno il segreto sul cuore e maggiori diventano le probabilità che qualche labbro se lo lasci sfuggire. Per ovviare, in parte, questo pericolo inevitabile, aveva deciso che i partecipanti all'attentato non dovessero conoscere l'un l'altro.

Per ripararci dal freddo, che quel giorno era piuttosto pungente, entrammo in una "Public House". La conversazione continuò a sbalzi. Orsini doveva essere per l'indomani, a Liverpool e mi pregava di ritornare a Nottingham. "Sta quieto a casa tua e lavora, sinché io mi faccia vivo". Se non poteva venire egli stesso, avrebbe mandato persona fidata, con un suo scritto e colle sue istruzioni. E per maggiore cautela, stabilimmo una parola d'ordine. Gli domandai se, a proposito dell'attentato, avesse già qualche piano ben delineato. Egli aggrottò le sopracciglia e parve contrariato. Orsini non sapeva dissimulare le proprie emozioni. Dopo un breve silenzio, mi rispose che non poteva comunicarmi nulla di preciso. La mia intenzione — soggiunse — sarebbe quella di farci invitare a Corte. V'ha chi è in grado di procurarci quanti inviti desideriamo. L'avvicinare l'usurpatore durante una festa da ballo, è affare di poco momento e si potrebbe, coll'arma bianca, riuscire nell'intento, senza versare una goccia di sangue più del necessario. Ma si teme che gli interessati chiudano le porte del palazzo e impediscano il divulgarsi dell'accaduto, sinché non siano dati tutti gli ordini necessari a scongiurare le conseguenze; finché non abbiano proclamata la Reggenza. Forse bisognerà ricorrere a mezzo più chiassoso. Comunque sia... la tua parola è data. Posso farvi sicuro assegnamento?

Gli risposi che Carlo di Rudio non aveva mai mancato ai propri impegni e poiché, per semplice intuizione, mi pareva di aver compreso, dal più al meno, in che potesse consistere il "mezzo più chiassoso", gli feci osservare che il seguito dell'usurpatore doveva necessariamente essere composto di sostenitori dell'impero e di soldati complici del 2 Dicembre. Più se ne uccidevano e meno ce ne saremmo trovati di fronte, alle barricate della dimane.

Ci lasciammo quella sera stessa. Il giorno dopo, ritornavo a Nottingham e riandando, col pensiero, a cadenza di treno, quello che mi era stato confidato e le probabilità di trarne un moto vasto, che riuscisse salutare alla causa popolare in tutta quanta l'Europa, compresi subito di quale immenso vantaggio sarebbe riuscita la consapevolezza del Mazzini. Io non conoscevo influenza che fosse più profonda e più efficace della sua. Lasciare in disparte un uomo simile era un correre il rischio di perdere il frutto del nostro ardimento. La figura del "bolognese" mi s'affacciò alla mente, ma non mi parve di rilievo. Io ben sapevo come bastasse il narrare con discrezione e richiedere la promessa del silenzio assoluto, perchè il Maestro affrontasse e superasse qualsiasi tortura, senza lasciarsi sfuggire una sillaba compromettente. Il "codardo" della spedizione di Savoia, come lo chiamavano certe gazzette moderate, era indubbiamente tra le individualità più umanamente coraggiose del secolo.

I dissapori tra il Maestro e l'Orsini? Mi sentivo certo che sarebbero svaniti di fronte ai supremi interessi della patria. E da quel momento, mi proposi di aprire al Mazzini l'animo mio, di confidargli quel tanto che bastasse perché gli avvenimenti non lo cogliessero all'impensata.

Mia moglie fu lietamente sorpresa del mio ritorno, ma non azzardò una sola domanda indiscreta e, per parecchi giorni, la nostra vita trascorse nella solita calma, come se nulla fosse nell'aria. Verso la metà del Novembre, mi venne recapitata una lettera, col timbro postale di Birmingham. Essa conteneva queste semplici parole: "Va a Londra e dammi il tuo indirizzo."

Compresi che i nodi si stringevano e riflettendo che, con tutta probabilità, lasciato il suolo inglese, non mi sarebbe più dato di rivedere né mia moglie, né il bimbo mio, dissi seccamente all'Elisa: "Prepara il tuo baule; intendo di stabilirmi a Londra!" Si accinse subito ad assecondarmi. Io l'osservavo. Da ogni suo atto, traspariva l'amorosa persuasione che ella non doveva cercare più in là; che tutto quanto richiedevo da lei era per fin di bene e per causa giusta. Spazzolava, piegava, riponeva, dolce e silenziosa.

Partimmo e fissammo dimora nel Bedman building, sulla piazza dello Shoah. Due giorni d'aspettativa. La mattina del terzo, me ne stavo titillando alla gola il bimbo, che Elisa aveva appena immerso in una catinella d'acqua tiepida, quando una scampanellata mi fece sussultare. Sulla soglia, mi trovai a faccia a faccia con un signore ben vestito, evidentemente straniero; il quale, domandato se io fossi il tale dei tali senz'altro aggiungere, mi porse una lettera aperta. Era di Felice Orsini che mi pregava di avere "piena fiducia in Simon Bernard, medico francese da Carcassonne". A maggior garanzia, chiesi ed ottenni la parola d'ordine. Allora invitai il mio visitatore ad entrare.

Il bimbo sgambettava nella catinella e batteva l'acqua colle manine, spruzzandone il tavolo. A quella vista, Simon Bernard mi fissò in volto, quasi rivolgendomi una muta interrogazione; poi guardò di nuovo il bimbo e finì per gettare alla moglie uno sguardo turbato e perplesso.

Credetti di intuire la causa del suo imbarazzo e gli feci osservare che poteva parlare liberamente in francese; mia moglie non ne avrebbe compreso sillaba.

"Oh non si tratta precisamente di questo!" E ridivenne pensoso. D'improvviso parve si decidesse e, ritrovata tutta la galanteria propria della sua razza, si profuse in complimenti colla mamma per la floridezza della sua creatura; col marito pel suo buon gusto nella scelta della sposa. Parlò di Londra, del suo clima maledetto; della Francia e della gaiezza impareggiabile di Parigi; sfiorò cento soggetti, senza approfondirne alcuno, con un'eleganza di frasi, con una volubilità di pensiero più appropriate in una sala di signori sfaccendati, che nell'umile camera di un cospiratore e tra cospiratori.

Quando si congedò, l'accompagnai sull'uscio ed egli toccandomi il braccio, mi disse sottovoce: "Facciamo quattro passi sulla piazza; avremo agio di parlare con maggiore libertà."

Discesi appena, mi si piantò d'innanzi e dando finalmente la stura al pensiero che l'aveva preoccupato: "Parbleu! non sapevo che eravate padre di famiglia!"

— Felice Orsini lo sa.

— Ma non lo sa, qui a Londra, il Comitato segreto. Comunque, questa visita era pur necessaria per fare la reciproca conoscenza. Stasera il Comitato si raduna e riferirò. Domani ci rivedremo.

E il giorno seguente venne. Sin da quando gli apersi, notai che l'espressione della sua faccia era da uomo ilare. Questa volta si arrischiò a parlare in presenza dell'Elisa, in francese, si intende. Nulla aveva di nuovo a proposito della congiura. Ma era lieto di annunciarmi che il Comitato Segreto aveva deciso di pagare a mia moglie, durante la mia assenza, quattordici scellini la settimana. In caso di disgrazia, il sussidio le verrebbe continuato sinché il mio bimbo avesse raggiunto l'età di quattordici anni.

A torto od a ragione, la preoccupazione massima di poter giovare alla Patria, aveva quasi esclusa dalla mia mente l'altra dell'avvenire. La mamma d'Elisa apparteneva al novero di quelle donne il cui nome non può essere disgiunto dall'idea della venerazione. La certezza che essa non avrebbe lasciato sul lastrico né la figlia, né la creaturina, era bastata a tenermi, su questo riguardo, perfettamente tranquillo. E, a dir vero, la notizia comunicatemi da Simon Bernard, a tutta prima, mi urtò. Ma avevo io il diritto di assecondare l'impeto dell'orgoglio; di imporre a creature deboli ed ignare tutto il peso delle mie azioni? Sarebbe stato onesto il rifiutare?

Ringraziai e Simon Bernard, adducendo a pretesto affari urgenti, grazie ai quali, anzi, non avrebbe potuto farsi vivo per alcuni giorni, mi strinse la destra e se n'andò. Non scarsa fu perciò la mia meraviglia quando, il mattino seguente, me lo ritrovai a faccia a faccia, sulla soglia. Mi espose, senza neppure passarla, il movente di quella visita inaspettata. Il Comitato Segreto aveva riconsiderata la sua decisione e n'aveva concluso che non poteva mettere a repentaglio l'esistenza di un uomo che aveva moglie e che era padre. Venivo quindi dispensato, con mille ringraziamenti, dal pigliar parte all'affare. Mi accontentai di rispondere: "Sta bene. Non parliamone più!"

CHAPTER FIVE

IN September of 1857, while I was making shift to live by giving language lessons in Nottingham, an Italian exile passing through that city came to call on me. He found himself in circumstances far more critical than my own. I came to his aid, and that same day he continued on his way. Arriving in Birmingham, he applied to Felice Orsini for assistance — Orsini being, if I am not mistaken, his fellow-countryman. In the course of recounting his misfortunes, he made mention of the visit he had paid me. "By God!" exclaimed Felice Orsini. "The very man I am looking for!" And he wrote to me, giving me an appointment at the Great Northern station in London.

I had not seen Felice Orsini for a long while. But no sooner had the train puffed in under the station roof (Charing Cross) than I caught sight of him standing erect amid the crowd, with his little cases and his very black moustaches. A few seconds and I was face to face with him. "Where the devil have you been all this time?" he said to me. And I told him. When I touched upon my marriage, his face clouded over. "Then, farewell to patriotic resolutions!" I disabused him. Indeed, I now felt all the greater obligation to conspire and to fight — my son was not to have a servile and unhappy fatherland.

He asked me whether I knew the reason he had given me the appointment, and upon my replying that I did not, he invited me to step outside the station. Once in the open air, he reminded me without preamble of the letter I had written to Giuseppe Mazzini and of my proposal to rid France of her troubles by suppressing Napoleon. He had it in mind to translate that old idea of mine into action. The times seemed to him propitious. Was I still of the same opinion?

— What does Mazzini think of it?

At this he made a gesture as if to say: Why do you speak to me of archaeology? And he strengthened his point by declaring that he no longer spoke with Mazzini. Moreover, Massarenti, the *alter ego* of the "prophet," was a degenerate of the Bolognese breed, and would, the moment he caught wind of the matter, undoubtedly denounce him. For this reason too, it was necessary to leave Mazzini out of it.

In the end I told him that I was not averse to taking part in the enterprise. But I wished to see things clearly. Napoleon once killed, what would happen? What were his plans for the aftermath?

I shall repeat, in summary, his explanations. They reveal, if not the plan, the aims of a conspiracy about which so much has been written, yet without ever fully laying bare the truth of it. They serve, at the same time, as a posthumous refutation of the charges which certain historians, lacking the necessary grounds for judgement, chose to bring against Orsini.

Felice Orsini had come to a full understanding with the French Republicans. The coup d'état had surprised and paralysed them — but had not tamed them. They were conspiring to prepare a revolution. A successful bold stroke in Paris would mean the liberation of France, and France restored to liberty would mean all Europe in ferment. Ledru Rollin — Orsini assured me — despairs of success so long as he finds the sinister figure of Napoleon before him. There is more force than one might suppose in a name. If France stirs, Kossuth vouches for Hungary. As regards our Italy — Napoleon suppressed, Paris throws up barricades. The enemy, caught unawares, without an authoritative leader, with the cohesion of the army already undermined by subtle intrigue, falls ignominiously. The Republic is proclaimed, and immediately I am appointed, with full powers, Commissioner with the French army stationed at Rome. Those are thirty thousand seasoned and well-equipped men that I win, at a single stroke, to the Italian cause. What do I do? In a single day I cancel the work and the shame of the Napoleonic betrayal of 1849. The Pope and the Cardinals become my prisoners and I lock them up in the Castel Sant'Angelo — their fate will depend upon the attitude of Europe. Meanwhile I sever the temporal power from the spiritual, and proclaim from the Campidoglio the resurrection of the Roman Republic. The French garrison quits Rome, and I drive it swiftly forward in support of the Italian revolution. Do we win? So much the better! Do we suffer a reverse? Republican France, stung to the quick, rises like a fury and lays down her arms no more until the prestige of her own name has been fully vindicated. Austria, assailed on every side, without friends in Europe, with rebellion blazing within her very borders, is swept away and never again sets foot in Italy.

Certainly, it would remain to establish the definitive political order of the Peninsula. But there are things which, in despite of their vastness, arrange themselves almost of their own accord. The blind, obstinate dualism between republicans and monarchists has already proved fatal to the Italian People; the lesson has instructed it. The People desires independence and unity, and now places everything else in the second rank. If the Savoyard Monarchy showed itself capable of giving the People the strength it lacks and of leading it to victory, the People would throw itself, as one man, at the feet of Vittorio Emanuele. If victory should come from republican Rome and from republican allies, what sovereign would feel himself great enough to oppose the proclamation of the Republic?

In the present moment the noble contest is open. Should we republicans spontaneously renounce the glorious initiative?

Everything depends upon audacity! One must know how to cast the first spark. Without it there is no conflagration, independence is impossible, the Republic is impossible. You know that I am opposed to the shedding of blood. Nature has endowed me with a humane temperament: I aim high, and the means imposed by these dark times repel me. But I say that the blood of Louis Bonaparte — of the man who has knowingly broken faith with solemnly sworn obligations, who has betrayed us, who has put our Fatherland back in chains, who makes a mockery of every noble aspiration of his own — must not weigh in the balance against the liberty of seventy millions of human creatures. To shed it averts the sacrifice of thousands upon thousands of honest lives, and the cruel act becomes a work of mercy. Decide!

And I decided. The man's rectitude was known to me; the revolutionary undercurrent in France was known to me, more or less; the conditions of Italy were known. To probe further I deemed superfluous, and I contented myself with asking the names of those who were to execute the plan materially. But Orsini fended off the question. The great danger in great conspiracies — he pointed out to me — is precisely their magnitude. The more numerous those who carry the secret in their hearts, the greater become the chances that some lip will let it escape. To guard, in part, against this unavoidable peril, he had decided that the participants in the attempt should not know one another.

To shelter ourselves from the cold, which that day was rather sharp, we entered a public house. The conversation continued by fits and starts. Orsini had to be in Liverpool on the morrow, and he begged me to return to Nottingham. "Stay quietly at home and work, until you hear from me." If he could not come in person, he would send a trusted agent with a letter from him and with his instructions. And as a further precaution, we established a password. I asked him whether, with regard to the attempt, he had any well-defined plan already. He knitted his brows and appeared vexed. Orsini was unable to dissemble his emotions. After a brief silence, he answered me that he could communicate nothing precise. "My intention," he added, "would be to have ourselves invited to Court. There is someone in a position to procure us as many invitations as we wish. To approach the usurper during a ball is a matter of little moment, and it might be possible, with cold steel, to achieve the object without spilling a single drop of blood more than necessary. But it is feared that interested parties would close the doors of the palace and prevent news of what had occurred from spreading, until all the orders necessary to avert the consequences had been given; until they had proclaimed the Regency. Perhaps it will be necessary to resort to a noisier means. However that may be — your word is given. Can I count upon it with certainty?"

I replied that Carlo di Rudio had never failed in his undertakings, and since, by simple intuition, I believed I had understood, more or less, what the "noisier means" might consist in, I observed to him that the retinue of the usurper must necessarily be composed of supporters of the empire and of soldiers complicit in the coup of the 2nd of December. The more of them one killed, the fewer we should find before us on the morrow's barricades.

We parted that same evening. The following day I returned to Nottingham, and turning over in my mind, to the rhythm of the train, what had been confided to me and the prospects of drawing from it a broad movement that might prove salutary to the popular cause throughout the whole of Europe, I understood at once what an immense advantage Mazzini's awareness of it would prove. I knew no influence more profound or more efficacious than his. To leave such a man aside was to run the risk of losing the fruit of our daring. The figure of the "Bolognese" presented itself to my mind, but did not strike me as a matter of consequence. I knew well enough that it sufficed to narrate the affair with discretion and to exact a promise of absolute silence for the Maestro to face and endure any torture whatsoever without letting fall a single compromising syllable. The "coward" of the Savoy expedition, as certain moderate newspapers called him, was incontestably among the most humanly courageous individuals of the century.

The ill-feeling between the Maestro and Orsini? I felt certain that it would vanish in the face of the supreme interests of the fatherland. And from that moment I resolved to open my heart to Mazzini, to confide to him as much as would suffice that events should not catch him unawares.

My wife was pleasantly surprised at my return, but did not venture a single indiscreet question, and for several days our life passed in its usual calm, as though nothing were in the air. Towards the middle of November, a letter was delivered to me bearing the Birmingham postmark. It contained these simple words: "Go to London and send me your address."

I understood that the knot was drawing tight, and reflecting that, in all likelihood, once I had quitted English soil, it would be given me no more to see again either my wife or my child, I said curtly to Elisa: "Pack your trunk; I intend to settle in London!" She set about complying at once. I watched her. From her every movement there shone the loving conviction that she ought not to seek beyond; that everything I asked of her was for a good end and for a just cause. She brushed, folded, and put away — gentle and silent.

We departed and took up lodgings in Bedman Buildings, in Shoah Square. Two days of waiting. On the morning of the third, I was tickling the child under the chin — Elisa had just lowered him into a basin of warm water — when a ring at the bell made me start. On the threshold I found myself face to face with a well-dressed gentleman, evidently a foreigner; who, having asked whether I was such and such a one, without adding anything further, handed me an open letter. It was from Felice Orsini, begging me to place "full confidence in Simon Bernard, a French physician from Carcassonne." By way of greater surety, I asked for and received the password. I then invited my visitor to enter.

The child was kicking in the basin and beating the water with his little hands, spattering the table. At that sight, Simon Bernard fixed his eyes upon my face as if putting to me a mute question; then he looked at the child again, and ended by casting at my wife a troubled and perplexed glance.

I believed I could divine the cause of his embarrassment, and observed to him that he might speak freely in French; my wife would not understand a syllable.

"Oh, that is not precisely the point!" And he became thoughtful again. Suddenly he appeared to make up his mind, and having recovered all the gallantry natural to his race, he lavished compliments upon the mother for the thriving appearance of her little one, and upon the husband for his good taste in the choice of a wife. He spoke of London and its accursed climate; of France and the matchless gaiety of Paris; he touched upon a hundred subjects without going deep into any of them, with an elegance of phrase and a volatility of thought more suited to a drawing-room of idle gentlefolk than to the humble lodging of a conspirator and among conspirators.

When he took his leave, I accompanied him to the door, and he, touching my arm, said to me in an undertone: "Let us take a few turns about the square; we shall have more freedom to talk."

Arriving in Birmingham, he turned for help to Felice Orsini, who was, if I am not mistaken, a fellow townsman of his. In speaking of his adversities, he alluded to the visit paid to me. "By God!" exclaimed Felice Orsini. "The very man I am looking for!" And he wrote to me, asking me to meet him at the Great Northern station in London.

I had not seen Felice Orsini for a long time. But no sooner did the train enter, puffing, under the station's roof (Charing Cross) than I spied him standing amidst the crowd, with his cases and his jet-black mustache. A few seconds and I stood before him. "Where the devil have you been until now?" he said to me. And I told him. When I alluded to my marriage, his face darkened. "Then, farewell to patriotic plans!" I disabused him of that notion. Now, on the contrary, I had an even greater obligation to conspire and to fight... my son was not to have an enslaved and unhappy fatherland.

He asked me if I knew the reason for which he had arranged to meet me and, upon my negative reply, he invited me to leave the station. Once in the open he reminded me, without preamble, of the letter I had written to Giuseppe Mazzini and my proposal to deliver France by eliminating Napoleon. He had a mind to put that old idea of mine into action. The times seemed propitious to him. Was I still of the same opinion?

— What does Mazzini think of it?

At this, he made a gesture as if to say: Why do you speak to me of archaeology? And he reinforced his point by affirming that he no longer spoke with Mazzini. On the other hand Massarenti, the *alter ego* of the "prophet," was a discredit to the people of Bologna and would, when he got wind of the matter, undoubtedly denounce him. For this reason, too, it was necessary to leave Mazzini aside.

I ended by telling him that I was not averse to participating in the enterprise. But I wanted to see things clearly. Once Napoleon was killed, what would happen? What were his plans for the aftermath?

I repeat, in summary, his explanations. They reveal, if not the plan, the aims of a conspiracy about which much has been written, but without ever revealing the whole truth. They serve, at the same time, as a posthumous refutation of the criticisms that some historians, though lacking the necessary elements for judgement, wished to level at Orsini.

Felice Orsini had come to a full accord with the French Republicans. The *coup d'état* had surprised and paralyzed them, but not subdued them. They were conspiring to prepare a revolution. A successful bold stroke in Paris meant the liberation of France, and France risen again to liberty meant all of Europe in a ferment. Ledru Rollin—Orsini assured me—despairs of success so long as he finds himself facing the sinister figure of Napoleon. There is more power than one might think in a name. If France is stirred, Kossuth guarantees Hungary. As for our Italy... once Napoleon is eliminated, barricades are organized in Paris. The enemy, caught unawares, without an authoritative leader, with the cohesion of the army already undermined by subtle intrigues, falls ignominiously. The Republic is proclaimed and, immediately, I am named, with full powers, Commissioner to the French army stationed in Rome. These are thirty thousand battle-hardened and well-equipped men whom I win over, at a single stroke, to the Italian cause. What do I do? In a single day, I erase the work and the Napoleonic shame of 1849. The Pope and Cardinals become my prisoners and I lock them up in Castel Sant'Angelo... their fate will depend on the attitude of Europe. I, meanwhile, sever the temporal power from the spiritual and proclaim, from the Campidoglio, the Roman Republic risen again. The French garrison leaves Rome and I dispatch it rapidly to the aid of the Italian revolution. Do we win? So much the better! Do we suffer a setback? Republican France, stung to the quick, rises up like a fury and does not lay down its arms again until the prestige of its name is fully vindicated. Austria, attacked from every side, without friends in Europe, with rebellion flaring up within her own borders, is swept away and never again sets foot in Italy...

The blind, stubborn dualism between republicans and monarchists has already proved fatal to the Italian People; the lesson has taught them. ## Chapter the Fourth

THE Count di Rudio speaks: The very year I took a wife I was drawn into an ill-starred affair that made a great deal of noise. The exiles who had no abundance of coin used to frequent a certain eating-house in Rupert Street, where for little outlay they could savour some dish prepared after our own fashion. I myself had gone there more than once and had struck up an acquaintance with one Rossi, a Mantuan, a young fellow who soon won my sympathies by his open and cheerful character. Rossi was excessively hasty in the matter of amorous relations, but no other vices could I discover in him; and when the Romagnol Foschini warned me, in strictest confidence, to keep clear of that "ugly customer" because he was a spy in the pay of the French Government, I observed to him that to lend easy credence to calumnies insinuated, in all likelihood for base ends, by the enemies of our party, was to lend oneself most childishly to their game.

Foschini, nettled, turned his back on me, and I... drew closer to Rossi... in part, also, from that instinct, so imperious in me, of wanting to see clearly, to the very bottom of things. I came to be convinced that Foschini was genuinely mistaken. That conviction, to tell the truth, is not so firmly rooted today. A certain Rossi, as I learned several years later, had been sinisterly implicated in the conspiracies at Mantua. Nevertheless the surname Rossi is exceedingly common in Northern Italy, and I never had the means of ascertaining whether my London acquaintance and the spy of the Austrian Government were one and the same person. For anyone interested in probing these minutiae, I will say that the Rossi of

London had a brother who was a priest — a circumstance which might serve as a guide to establishing the truth!

One evening, at a late hour, I found myself in the tavern in Rupert Street in the company of the "ugly customer." Foschini was there too, sitting entirely alone, apart from the rest; but from the black looks he had cast at us and from certain monosyllables muttered between his teeth, I had been able to gather that there was a risk of sailing in troubled waters.

The other customers thinned out and we were left almost alone. At a certain point, Foschini rose and, passing stiffly beside Rossi, jostled him at the elbow and said: "Spy!"

Rossi retorted with a worse compliment, and Foschini, lashed across the face, turned furious. I thought it my duty to intervene, and was urging them to make no scandal, so as not to compromise our whole emigrant community, when the Romagnol, having drawn a dagger, made a spring. One second more... and Rossi would have been finished! To forestall the deed, I seize a chair and do my best to bring it down upon the assailant. Unfortunately I strike the lamp hanging from the ceiling; it shatters and we are left in darkness. I remember the sensation of the cold blade entering my chest, then... nothing more!

When a glimmer of consciousness returned to me, I understood that I was in a hospital. I could not keep my eyes open; but I heard a voice, the physician's, pronouncing that I should not see the morrow's sun. They made me swallow some preparation or other, and that brief effort was enough for me to lose my senses once again.

But my youth reasserted itself and, in spite of the gloomy prophecy, my first impression was precisely that of the sun's rays beating upon my face. They stirred in me a firm will to cling to life.

I learned that the doctor, considering the case desperate, had resorted, in order to arrest the internal haemorrhage, to a heroic remedy of his own. He regarded the outcome as a wonder, and for a long succession of days brought to my bedside now one colleague, now another, entering upon discussions of presumably great scientific value. To me, a layman and a man of irritable nerves, they were supremely inopportune.

Before I received the regulation visit of a Police Inspector, the doctor informed me that Foschini had made two other victims. But Rossi, the one undeniable target he had wished to remove, had come out of it without a scratch.

Upon Foschini, who had gone into hiding, the Authorities placed a reward of two hundred pounds. But he was a good patriot, and though his hiding-place was known, no one breathed a word. Indeed, in the laudable attempt to shield him, the political motive which had driven him to act was greatly exaggerated, with scant regard for whether, in order to save the culprit's reputation, the reputations of innocent men were being diminished.

Now let one mark the strangeness of destiny: that in Europe first, then in America, I faced the gravest dangers, and though I was more than once within a hair's breadth of leaving my bones there, I always came away with my skin intact. It was reserved for my companions in faith to repay my disinterestedness with six-and-thirty dagger-thrusts, two of which penetrated into cavities and were adjudged mortal!

Foschini closed his days splendidly, fighting at the side of Carlo Pisacane in the ill-fated, glorious expedition of Sapri.

Sapri was the first serious manifestation of the new change of tactics adopted by Giuseppe Mazzini. The Crimean War being ended, the great Cavour, in spite of the splendid showing of our soldiers and their victory at the Cernaia, had obtained nothing for his King — not even the hoped-for annexation of Parma and of Modena, whose petty princes they wished to foist upon Moldavia and Wallachia. But Napoleon III had promised that one day he would do something also for Piedmont, and Mazzini held for certain, even from those days, the alliance of the Empire with Vittorio Emanuele II. He was, however, firmly convinced that from it nothing would derive save an enlargement of the Sardinian States, or at best, a federation of Italian States governed, for the greater part, by French princes. To forestall such a calamity, he abandoned the old field of action and laboured, with his most trusted followers, to raise the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies in revolt: to set against the monarchical agitation of the North the insurrection of the southernmost extremity of Italy; to impose from Palermo and Naples the unity of the Nation. The brilliant conception earned for Giuseppe Mazzini and for Garibaldi a sentence of "ignominious death," but it was destined to prove one of the greatest factors in our future greatness.

Left entirely to one side — presumably because I was thought to have been softened by new affections — I was already resigning myself to a long inaction, when suddenly the call came to me from whence I least expected it.
