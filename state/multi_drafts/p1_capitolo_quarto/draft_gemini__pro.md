Having found refuge and safety in Verona, the old Marshal offered the cadets entry, as non-commissioned officers, into the regular army, granting them the privilege of choosing their Regiment. No one accepted. We were interned in Gratz and incorporated into that Military Academy; until the Minister of War, acceding to our petition, permitted us to return to our homes.

September came. The valiant Piedmontese army, abandoned to its fate by the defection of the Italian princes: its assaults blunted by the mental frailties of its own King, vanquished more by the deficiency of its own organization than by Austrian valour, had been forced to withdraw from Venetia, from the Duchies, from Lombardy; to retreat behind the Ticino line. Milano had fallen once more into the possession of the oppressors... The royal war being virtually over, there continued, according to the expression then in vogue, that of the People.

Among the individuals unadorned by any official insignia who most distinguished themselves that year, for valour and for integrity of spirit, was a patriot and a very dear friend of my family: Pietro Fortunato Calvi...

With a few mountaineers and with almost primitive weapons, he had defended the Cadore from the beginning of May to the beginning of June, inscribing in the annals of our epic the names of Ampezzo, of Termine, of Rivalgo, of Chiusa di Venas, of the passo della Morte, of Selva, of the Rindimera and the ill-fated Mauria.

In this astonishing campaign, which certain historians skip over with an even more astonishing rapidity, Pietro Fortunato Calvi repulsed the attacks—separate at first, then combined—delivered by the Austrians at the three natural gates of the Cadore. When, in the third phase of the war, he laid down his arms, it was a necessity created by the complete exhaustion of provisions and munitions. Nor in that era, so quick to attribute reversals to treason, did a single voice arise to diminish the hero's glory. He took refuge, by unusual routes, in Venice and, wherever he appeared, he was welcomed, not as one vanquished, but as a victor.

In his footsteps, by way of Portogruaro, the only route that remained open, I too entered Venice, together with my brother Achille. Calvi at once admitted us into that legion of the Cacciatori delle Alpi, whose name became synonymous with heroism and glory.

The end of 1848 and the first months of the following year, I thus had the opportunity to witness a marvellous spectacle. In my long existence I have known no other more potent in exalting the human soul... A people who, by force of will, re-tempers its own fibre; who cleanses itself in hardship, in the pangs of hunger, in the horrors of disease, in the washing of its own blood, of the shame of centuries of wantonness, of the cowardly resignation to servitude.

In destruction, the glimmers of life being renewed, of greatness reasserting itself.

In those months, besides my share of the common sufferings, I felt an intimate, intense sorrow. My brother Achille had his saintly resolutions, his warm hopes, inexorably cut short by disease. I came to kiss his icy brow and it seemed to me that, at the contact of my lips, a remnant of affection stirred in the inert corpse. I believe that only from that moment did the young patriot cease entirely to stir.

Personally, I took part in only one important action: the sortie towards Mestre which supplied the city with fodder and meat. On that occasion I saw, for the first time, without speaking to him, a man who was to have a great influence on my future: Felice Orsini.

Two grave infractions of discipline removed me prematurely from that theatre of sacrifice and greatness. I had been assigned to the defence of the Marghera forts, lunette number thirteen. A tedious task; for Cavedalis, the Minister of War, on the pretext that munitions were lacking, had ordered us not to open fire. We indolently watched the feverish work of the Austrians who, concentrated at Mestre, were digging their parallels and preparing for the bombardment. That inaction of ours had made them so arrogant as to induce them, one day, to advance along the canal to within a rifle shot of the forts... and from there, they wounded the sentry of my lunette in the arm.

The Minister's orders and the Minister himself vanished from my mind, not to say from the face of the globe... I snatched the linstock from the gunner's hands and the shot went off. Immediately, as if my fury had spread with the speed of electricity, all the cannons of the forts thundered... the few enemies who remained standing took to their heels.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi in person pleaded my cause before the Council of War. He adduced, in my defence, ignorance of the orders, my age, love of Country, and an irresistible impulse, and, for the time being, I was saved. But at the second infraction, political animosities made my situation untenable.

The fatal dualism between republicans and monarchists, which in 1848-49 was so great a part of our reversals, necessarily had its repercussion in Venice as well. Republican at first, then annexed to monarchical Piedmont, she had again assumed a republican government when the royal war seemed irretrievably lost. The upper bourgeoisie, which, even then, arrogated to itself the exclusive possession of practical good sense, did not willingly accept the popular verdict, and several Ministers backed them.

Hence, hybrid measures that irritated the multitude and frequently drove it to assemble in Piazza San Marco and to protest loudly.

In March of 1849 (I do not recall the day) the exasperation, having reached its peak, seemed on the verge of degenerating into open revolt. To prevent it, Cavedalis gave the order to Pietro Fortunato Calvi to disperse the crowd at any cost, and Calvi, having detached several patrols from his legion, laid the thankless duty upon the non-commissioned officers. However, at the moment of departure, he called us aside and, certain of being understood, pronounced these exact words: "Remember that in Venice, the Croats no longer command."

I crossed a good stretch of the city at the head of my patrol without encountering obstacles; but having scarcely emerged into the Piazza San Marco, I found myself before a tempestuous sea. Our uniform of green trimmed with white and our drooping plume immediately attracted general attention, and a vibrant voice cried out: "Look at them! They are coming to shoot us!"

I put my kepi on the point of my dagger and, waving it by way of a salute, shouted at the top of my lungs: "We are here to defend the People, not to tyrannize them!"

Farewell, repression! The crowd opened to let us pass freely and, its sentiments suddenly changed, shouted from thousands upon thousands of throats: "Long live the Cacciatori delle Alpi!" Lost and as if absorbed by that stormy sea, we ended by becoming an integral part of it.

Fortunately, Daniele Manin made up for our arms with his authority and eloquence. I still have before my eyes his face, resembling, as one drop of water resembles another, that of Cavour, although his soul was so alien to all diplomatic duplicity. The ideas he expressed I can still repeat today, in almost his identical words. He exhorted them not to embitter the miseries of the Fatherland, which demanded, in its straits, the concord of all its sons. To divide it, to diminish its strength, was a crime.

The urgent task, the supreme task, was that of defeating the foreigner, of breaking the chains being prepared for the Republic. One could think later of settling the internal dissensions; when the glorious banner would wave, once more, over free lands and free men. What could not a people conscious of its own rights, drunk with enthusiasm, emboldened by victory, then accomplish? What schemes, what flattery would have been able to rob it, even in part, of the fruit of so many sacrifices? "Return to your homes, restore yourselves with the caresses of your mother, your wife, your anxious children... poor great People, so harshly tested in the school of adversity! Think that the cannon may, from one moment to the next, tear you from those caresses, demand your presence there where men fight, where men die. No Italian worthy of the name must refuse to present himself, uncontaminated and without remorse, for the supreme sacrifice!"

He fell silent and that strong People and that good People, little by little, dispersed. With how much greater glory would the wise, humane word succeed even today for those Rulers who so lightly set it aside for fratricidal arms!

The following day, Colonel Calvi sent for me. I found him more sad than stern. "Minister Cavedalis," he said to me, "is in a fury. Venice is full of informers, as in the days of the Signoria, and he has learned, down to the last detail, what... what has happened. He demands the names of the non-commissioned officers who commanded the patrols; especially the name—do you understand me?—of a beardless sergeant who uttered subversive phrases and whom he wants punished. The escapade is covered by military regulations..."

I began to patch together a defence; but he interrupted me with a gesture. The superior officer would not and could not enter into that discussion; but the patriot extended his right hand to the patriot and gave me a clasp that spoke an entire volume.

"It is the second of its kind," he continued, "and there would be no remission. Sergeant di Rudio, you must leave Venice!.. you will find enemies to fight in every corner of Italy. Rome needs defenders and the Government of the Republic, at the solicitation of those Triumvirs, is sending there the legionaries of the Unione, who came among us after the about-face of Pope Pius. Venice has more need of bread than of soldiers, and Colonel Ferrara, who leads the legionaries, will set sail tomorrow, in fact, from Chioggia. Here is a letter for him... you will be well received. Go, I command you!"

I gave the military salute and departed. The unforeseen awaited me. Minister Cavedalis had already sent to Chioggia the order to arrest, and bring back to Venice, any soldiers who did not belong to the Unione, or who could not show a permit signed by the garrison commander. I did not walk for long through the streets of Chioggia.

The barracks in which they locked me up flanked a street that leads to the seafront. A second was enough to dash from the main gate and slip away down that street; four squirrel-like leaps were enough to traverse it and reach the shore. In the courtyard of the barracks, legionaries of every region and every uniform were strolling about, all jumbled together... on the wide-open gate stood a sentry, or rather a great lout who, judging roughly, seemed to be dreaming of the good soups of his village. Slowly, slowly, as if I had no other care than to amuse myself a bit by observing the passers-by, I came up beside him. Then I waved my arms and cried to him in a choked voice: "Look! He's escaping... escaping from that window!"

He looks to the left, I leap to the right, slip around the corner, and am out of range. Oh, the blue waters of the Adriatic, oh, the brown *trabiccoli*, rocking gently on the waves, light as shells!.. the *trabiccoli* grazed by the wings of seagulls, resounding with labours and songs! I had about forty of them before me, already crowded with legionaries, already maneuvering for the imminent departure.

I concealed myself in a small boat which, propelled by two sturdy rowers, cut through the waves as swift as an arrow. But, as I was climbing up the side of a ship, an officer from the shore spotted the green uniform trimmed with white. The shifting maneuver of the mobile flotilla proved providential for me at that juncture. The officer had undoubtedly fixed his eye upon the *trabaccolo* on which I had sought refuge; but, in the passage, he became confused and went aboard, for his search, where I was not. He persisted in inspecting a second ship, then a third, and finally arrived at mine... but I had already found a way to put on a uniform of the Unione. I placed myself in rank with the other legionaries and he, passing a hand's breadth from my nose, scarcely deigned to look at me.

However, I was not to set foot in Rome without making a wide circuit. Numerous combatants had, like me, left Venice by subterfuge, not out of cowardice, but because they were drawn, by various considerations, toward the heart of Italy. Except that, upon arriving in Ravenna, Colonel Ferrara, pleading I know not what peremptory orders, refused to enlist us and, organizing us into a detachment, had us escorted to Bologna under strict surveillance, as if we were Austrian prisoners. Our final destination: Venice. But I was conscious of having taken up arms to break chains, not to have them imposed upon me... I decided to face the vicissitudes of another flight.

As the only non-commissioned officer, I had been entrusted with the task of procuring the rations for the entire detachment... one fine morning I took advantage of this to bid farewell to the barracks and to San Petronio. The soldier Angeli, whom I had induced to follow me, placed at my disposal some small change so that I might procure a disguise; but Venetian currency, unfortunately, was not accepted in Bologna, and an extreme expedient imposed itself: to take off my green uniform trimmed with white, which I had put on again in Ravenna, and barter it for a suit of fustian. Profound was the sense of bitterness that I felt; but I was finally consoled by the idea that the garb of the field labourer, the honest and unfortunate pariah of my Country, is also an honourable one. Besides, the heart that yearns for Liberty, the heart that wins battles, I always carried that, unchanged, within my breast.

At one o'clock that very night, we arrived, unmolested, at the walls of ancient Ferrara. The gates were closed; in the deep darkness, on the other bank of the Po, in the distance, shone the fires of the Austrian encampment. With the exception of the fortress, the city was in the power of our men. I knocked at the gate.

— Who goes there?

— Italians deserted from the Austrian camp. We wish to fight for the cause of independence.

We were admitted, welcomed with open arms, escorted to the general headquarters, bombarded with questions. Our information, gleaned from newspapers along the road, was found plausible... besides, we had passed ourselves off as simple soldiers and no one expected revelations of high strategy. It ended with our being sent on to Cento, where Major Mambrini enlisted us in the Legione Garibaldi.

The most beautiful sun in Italy was shining when, from the heights of Civita Castellana, I saw Michelangelo's dome stand out powerfully against the blue. My weariness vanished as if by magic, and our arms stretched out toward the plain, there where pulsed the metropolis that, with its strength and its wisdom, had filled the world. In those days, among the vestiges of its ancient civilization, among the superb creations of the middle ages, the red shirt blazed, a symbol of the new idea.
