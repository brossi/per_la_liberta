# Preface

THE PRESENT volume might make some contribution to History; but it is not history in the sense in which that word is today understood and written... Something less, for those who would demand the methodical concatenation of facts and the cold judgment upon men and events; something more, for those who, regarding events as a mere resultant, might seek to grasp “the soul of the times,” rummaging among the passions that stirred them.

The Count di Rudio, so far removed, in time and in space, from the events he narrates, frequently protests that the miseries of politics no longer move him... Doubtless he deceives himself. Like a Minos who

*“Giudica e manda secondo che avvinghia”*

he might have, in the estimation of many of my countrymen, a value altogether relative; but he acquires a great one as a “human document,” and it is precisely as such that he is especially to be considered.

Why did I resolve to write of him?

Consider: In this farthest West, in this land of vulgar commercialism... to find an exemplar of that epoch, now long swept away by the centuries, in which the third Italy had its gestation; to find him pulsing with intense life, still distilling a potent poetry, still capable of indignation and of tears... How could I not have been tempted, and all but mastered, by him? I wrote because my existence was thereby intensified by edifying and pleasurable sensations.

And I did not cast the manuscript to the bottom of a trunk, specially if not exclusively, out of regard for the Count di Rudio, whom I know to be either calumniated or forgotten. In the great outpouring of men and events that sprang from the European revolution which preceded my times, he possessed virtues that predestined him to be swept away. The triumph of political men is almost always a consequence of their most wretched qualities. It neither dazzles nor moves me. My sympathies are for those who manfully succumb.

I sensed that this book would see the light when di Rudio would be no more. That no doubt whatsoever might arise as to its authenticity, not only did I procure the approval, in writing, of every single chapter, but I also requested and obtained that every page liable to presumable controversy should be authenticated by di Rudio with his own signature.

I might have softened certain jarring notes, rendered the more strident by so much lofty and virile language; might have erased certain heterogeneous imprints that insinuated themselves into the volume through the influence of those authors whom di Rudio most cherished... I did not. To adulterate the word issued from lips silenced for ever may be a venial sin for a Minister of Public Instruction; for a simple mortal it is cowardice.

I have nothing further to premise. *Salute!*

Cesare Crespi.
