I KNOW of no Italian insurrectionary movement that earned Giuseppe Mazzini so many calumnies, so much bitterness, so many defections as that of 1853.

The so-called practical people, with that short-sighted practicality which consists in letting the world run its course undisturbed, even to its own ruin, maintained that a People recently bled dry and broken could not respond with spirit to a call to arms, nor willingly take on new sacrifices. And they would pronounce, in all seriousness, that revolutions happen when they mature spontaneously, not when they are willed.

A singular maxim that, when thoroughly examined, would lead to this other: "To acquire one's independence, to reclaim one's rights, there is nothing better than to lie back in an armchair, leaving to the Sun the task of ripening events, as it ripens pumpkins."

The movements organized by Giuseppe Mazzini were never the true revolution, nor the war; but they undoubtedly prepared for them and made them necessary.

Furthermore, those of February 1853 were not initiated by the great agitator, but precisely by that People who, if one were to believe the serious men, would have cared for nothing other than to make a bed for themselves in the *faits accomplis* and in their own misery.

And Mazzini himself has said it and written it and proved it. But even today supermen abound, the pride and glory of our Italy; who, being unable to refute the declarations of that great man who never lied, masterfully leap over them with both feet and continue to exalt the portents of the armchair, to persist in the old calumnies.

The "Fratellanza," a secret society aimed at insurrection, was already strongly organized in Milano, three years after every hope for Italy seemed to have withered. Composed of several thousand workers, it was subdivided into various sections marked by the letters of the alphabet. Its existence was absolutely unknown to the Austrian Police, to Mazzini, to the intellectuals, and to the aristocratic class, which nevertheless shared with it a hatred of the foreign yoke.

Its principal organizer, the dyer Assi, communicated with Mazzini when he deemed that the "Fratellanza" had sufficient strength to act. Assi asked for arms, money, and leaders capable of bringing a plan of attack to a successful conclusion.

Mazzini welcomed the news as proof that the propaganda of his ideas had developed good seeds even among the popular multitude; but he did not give full credence to Assi's assurances about the numerical importance and seriousness of purpose of the new organization, until Major Eugenio Brizzi of Jesi and other informants, sent by him secretly to Milano, had confirmed them in every respect.

Nevertheless, he was advising to play for time when in December of 1852, Austria, giving new vent to its ferocity, crowned the ramparts of Belfiore with gallows and, choosing a victim from every class of citizen, hanged him there.

Faced with the grim provocation, it seemed a crime to the workers of the "Fratellanza" to debate any longer. They notified Mazzini that they had decided to act, even without his help and without his consent. It became Mazzini's inescapable duty to support the inevitable, to strive so that the generous initiative should not remain isolated and sterile.

In the last days of December 1852, on a commission received directly from Count Grillanzoni, I left for Milano, and at midnight on the day I arrived, after a brief wait under the clock in piazza Mercanti, I delivered a packet of instructions to a man who, emerging from the shadows, had given me the agreed-upon signal.

The definitive plan for the insurrection thus came into the hands of that same Major Brizzi tasked with overseeing its execution. In its main lines, it was the work of Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Its fundamental basis was the principle that the arms were to be taken by surprise from the enemy, who would be disorganized by the killing of his officers.

By regulatory concession, these officers generally slept not in the barracks but in private lodgings. A note had been taken of their addresses and, naturally, the haunts where they usually gathered were also known.

The Austrian military structure was, among other places, housed within the walls of Milano at the Palace of the General Command, at that of the Gran Guardia, and at the Castello.

The first was guarded, more than defended, by a picket of twenty-five soldiers. Their arms stood aligned on the rifle rack, under the entranceway of the main gate, before which a vigilant sentinel paced incessantly. The other soldiers passed the hours stretching their limbs in the courtyard, smoking in the barracks rooms. At five in the afternoon, the Governor, the Generals, and the General Staff dined in that palace.

To render the sentinel powerless with a surprise attack was not a feat of great difficulty. As the small cord pulls the rope, and the rope drags the thick hawser, that first tenuous success would have delivered into the hands of the insurgents some thirty rifles, the unarmed picket, and the leaders of the garrison.

Something similar, but with more powerful means, was to be attempted at the Gran Guardia, where a hundred or so henchmen were quartered; but especially at the Castello, which held, in its armoury, twelve thousand rifles. The two armourers who inspected them daily were with us. They pledged to leave the doors ajar so that breaking them down would not rob us of precious time.

In the premises of the Gran Guardia, the insurgents were to entrench themselves and establish their own headquarters to give the revolution a center. And, from that center, small patrols, dressed in the uniforms taken from the dead and the prisoners, were to diverge, beating the drum-roll, which would induce the officers to come out of their dwellings. On the threshold they would find those who would take it upon themselves to seal their lips, and forever.

The 6th of February was chosen to strike the enemy, a day of Carnival revelry and of pay for the soldiers. Their quarters would be left almost deserted. The surprise would thus have a greater probability of success; our men would have greater opportunity to rush into the cafés, into the taverns; to renew, and for a more sublime purpose, the bloodbath of the Vespers.

To pave the way for victory, Giuseppe Mazzini had solicited and obtained from Kossuth, who was about to depart for the United States of America, a revolutionary proclamation addressed to his countrymen quartered especially in the San Francesco barracks, and assurances were already had that, at the slightest sign of a serious insurrection, the ill-tamed sons of brave Hungary would make common cause with their oppressed Italian brothers.

As the crowning of the work, the barricades! The director of the city lighting, to protect those who raised them, had pledged to leave Milano in darkness. And to defend them, Giuseppe Mazzini had certain newly invented projectiles brought into the city, to be thrown at the assailants like bombs.

Add to this that the students of Pavia, secretly consulted, had pledged to rush to the aid of the capital; the agreed-upon signal, a blaze on the spire of the Duomo. Acerbi would command them... and they asked for nothing but arms. Mazzini, by way of the Po, had provided them.

Nor was the preparation of simultaneous movements beyond the walls limited to this. In Piemonte, the host of political refugees lay in wait, impatient for action. It was advisable to open a wide and easy path for them to descend, organized, on the offensive. Giuseppe Mazzini entrusted the task to Fortunato Calvi, who planned to seize Lago Maggiore, with a spirited plan that I shall explain shortly.

And Aurelio Saffi, after knocking on the doors of the wealthiest patriots—on which occasion Agostino Depretis, the future President of Ministers, bestowed twenty-five thousand lire—went, defying death, to Bologna and fomented, with good hopes of success, the insurrection of that city and of the Romagna.

In short, Giuseppe Mazzini had secured the cooperation of the centers that could have placed a barrier between the Milan garrison and the various Austrian nuclei scattered throughout the Peninsula. Fortunato Calvi later confided to me, in Zurich, that he flattered himself that he could, in about fifteen days, with the revolutionary forces, encircle the Austrian army of Italy and cut it off, at the Alps, from its base of reinforcement. It was enough, to give Lombardia the courage and time to rise up, that Milano could hold firm and fight in its streets for at least a few days. And Italy would have conquered its independence by its own strength, like Greece, like the United States, avoiding the fatal trail left by hybrid alliances, by shady intrigues, by the transactions that have obscured its greatness and which, for a long time yet, will, alas, make themselves felt.

But here I am not arguing, I am narrating.

The workers of the "Fratellanza," except for the leaders, were ignorant of the details of the insurrectional plan; but they knew that on the 6th of February, action would be taken. They had unearthed a hundred or so pistols and, on Giuseppe Mazzini's advice, set about preparing daggers and knives. Even the nails from the boats of their Naviglio were pulled out and fixed on shafts... Everything serves the desperation of a People!

And I, seized by the general fever, was already pondering, to prevent them, all the foreseeable sinister eventualities of the assault on the Gran Guardia, of which Brizzi had put me in charge, when I received from Count Grillanzoni the order to go immediately to Genoa and await his instructions there.

Which were: to confer with a certain affiliate, to have him count out to me, in Mazzini's name, four thousand lire in gold; to take them to Sander and Radonitz, who were waiting for me in Locarno. To adhere, on the journey, to the Lago Maggiore route and to watch if anything unusual there might indicate that Calvi's plans had been discovered or, by obstructing them, might require their modification. To confer, on my return from Locarno, with Calvi himself, who would be in Intra, and to report to him punctually what I had seen and heard.

Austria maintained on the Verbano a flotilla of gunboats which usually lay at anchor off Luino. Assigned to the surveillance of the shores and to passenger service was the steamship "Radetzky," which plied the lake daily and always had a company of soldiers on board. These, during the passage, stayed below deck, leaving their rifles aligned near the hatchway, under the watch of a sentinel. With the sentinel suppressed, it became more than probable that we could seize the arms and lock the other soldiers in the hold. Sander and Radonitz, former officers of the Austrian navy, who had then passed into the service of the Venetian Republic, would take command of the steamship and would command it as an Italic vessel.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi had arranged that the "Radetzky," upon landing at Luino late in the evening, would continue to fly the Austrian flag, and a handful of our men on board would be dressed in the hated uniform.

It was necessary to approach the gunboats as friends, unprepared for action; it was necessary to seize them by surprise. Then, having towed them out into the open lake, to turn their homicidal muzzles against the small garrison. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, having organized a good backbone of patriots, would assault Luino from the countryside. With the Italian flag raised on that strategic point, masters of the lake... the munitions, the arms, and the armed men coming from Piemonte would have found a relatively easy path right up to the walls of Milano.

When I arrived in Arona, there were a few hours to go before the "Radetzky's" departure. I spent them walking on the shore of the lake. I recall that the magnificence of the landscape, all green and alpine despite the winter, made such a sweet impression upon my soul that, there and then, it almost weakened it. And I marvelled that men, being able to enjoy so much paradise in brotherhood, should spoil it by perpetually tormenting each other, as if in their breasts nothing but base and ferocious passions could ever sprout.

But as soon as the bell of the "Radetzky" announced its imminent departure, my rebellious spirit regained the upper hand. I thought: "Beautiful is our Fatherland. For this very reason they put a knife to our throat and try to appropriate it. Therefore, the more intense the rage that drives us to defend it, the more sacred it is. Let them cross back over the Alps and we shall be brothers again."

The steamship put out into the lake. I lit my pipe and walking with slow steps, now here, now there, I tried to assume the poses of one who contemplates, ecstatically, the landscape that glides before him. But my mind was fixed on my secret purpose. I saw the carbines aligned near the hatchway; I scrutinized, almost weighed, the sentinel who, with Germanic rigidity, with the regularity of a pendulum, trod upon the planking as he walked; I heard the chaotic voices coming from the deep hold and understood them... they spoke, with lubricious frankness, of hips and hindquarters, punctuating their speech with coarse laughter. And never, during the entire journey, did the waxed moustaches of a *kaiserlich* emerge from that dark cavern. Faithful to their posts, those geometric heads!

Our surprise attack seemed to me of such certain success that I several times regretted being alone and unprepared to attempt it.

But in Locarno, I did not find souls as well disposed as my own. Sander and Radonitz received me, not without haughtiness, in the house of a certain Olivieri, a merchant of various goods and also of arms. They took and carefully counted the gold that I pulled from my money belt. But when it came to settling the preliminaries of the action, the 'buts' and 'ifs' became as thick and rigid as a wall.

A few days before—I was told—two Hungarians with whom secret dealings had been held had been put in jail; two of our men. "The Authorities were keeping them completely segregated and nothing had transpired about the reasons for the arrest. Why so many precautions, so much mystery? There was cause to be suspicious for less."

Radonitz held it for certain that the Police had some inkling of what was being plotted. To attempt anything new would be to rush headlong into the net. He exhorted not to confuse courage with recklessness. It was a question of the lives, sacred to him, of so many patriots. What would be lost in delaying; in ascertaining, with due caution, the mood of an enemy so powerful and so cruel? Wisdom did not consist in being in a hurry, but in acting in such a way that victory would be certain and as if inevitable.

To this I observed that if the arrest of a few brothers were enough to deter the Italians from their purposes, one might as well renounce action forever. From '49 onwards, not a day had passed that the Austrian stick had not been at work, that some chain had not been riveted. The Police could not suspect a movement as vast as ours without taking extraordinary precautions, which would not have escaped the brothers scattered everywhere. No alarm had come from any quarter. I myself had traversed the length of the lake, on an Austrian vessel, with a watchful eye, and I had noted nothing, absolutely nothing, unusual. Besides, the chances of being discovered were in proportion to the time spent organizing the conspiracy and the number of those who participated in it. All the greater appeared the necessity for prompt and almost lightning-fast action. Fortunato Calvi must have already departed from Torino; he would be in Intra the next day. It was for him alone to decide on the greater or lesser advisability of procrastinating. Our inaction would deprive him of the possibility of choice and would be tantamount to a defection. In any case, I had come to Locarno not to discuss, but to act. My orders were to report in Intra what I had seen and heard. I would depart again even if alone, cost me my life.

Present while I spoke was Olivieri's wife. She went out without a word and, shortly after, returned carrying a packet of revolutionary manifestos and a bundle of daggers. With her own hands, she helped me hide them on my person, and thus all discussion was cut short. The following morning, I embarked on the Arno, a Piedmontese steamship, and swiftly descended the Verbano again. Sander and Radonitz had remained ashore!

What a profound change in those few hours! The murky sky heralded the falling of snow; the lake was grey and almost ashen. A freezing wind blew from the Alps that seemed to have swept away the festive life from the shores and the slopes.

I found on the Arno people bundled up, with a selfishly pensive air; vulgar faces that betrayed the preoccupation of the marketplace. My attention finally came to concentrate on the captain, a bony man, with a phenomenally large head. He was absorbed in reading a newspaper and cast, only from time to time, a glance at the course. Who was he? What sentiments animated him? Could the name and the sufferings of the Fatherland have an effect on his heart?

I answered myself in the affirmative and committed a grave error. He had suddenly risen and, as if following the impulse of a fleeting thought, with hurried steps, was going elsewhere. He had thrown his newspaper on a seat. I drew out a revolutionary proclamation, hid it in the folds of that newspaper, and returned, running, to my post.

I did not wait long! A painfully surprised face, a frowning of the brows, a wagging of the heavy noggin. He fixed me with two eyes I have never forgotten. And perhaps they were set in the face of the most immaculate Aristides of Piemonte; but to me they seemed steeped, and almost drunk, with cretinous commiseration and bestial menace. I dissembled by whistling a cabaletta by Verdi to the wind that roared around me.

I am in Intra. Among the cluster of onlookers gathered for the steamship's arrival, I catch, as if on the fly, a well-known signal. I followed from afar the person who made it and was led to a solitary villa. The unknown brother remained outside and I entered. I found myself face to face with Fortunato Calvi.

What a flood of affections and how many things I would have wished to tell him; how many others, I imagine, he would have wished to confide in me! But we had to content ourselves with shaking hands for a long time. There and then, neither I nor he could utter a word. When I managed to unbind my lips, I said: "Are the lookouts at their posts?"

Having received assurance, I took from my person the proclamations and all the daggers, save one, and placed them on a table. I resumed:

— Everyone here and in the surroundings. They are waiting for the arms and the signal.

— I had news of him from Milano. (1) It was an imprudence; but his cunning served him well. Now he is in Lugano. Let the arms do their duty, the brain is watching and will do its part.

Then I gave him an account of my journey and did not conceal from him the strange hesitation of Sander and Radonitz, who had failed to appear at the rendezvous. I had not yet finished, when a window opened and in the frame appeared the face of the same affiliate who had guided me thus far. He said to me, in an agitated voice: "Escape, escape! The carabinieri are at the gate. They are looking for you!"

With one leap, I was in the garden, I crossed it at a run, taking care that the line of sight of the sirs at the gate remained blocked by the house. I climbed a tree and scaled the surrounding wall. Four leaps and I am at the lake. On the shore, chained to a piling, were several small boats lined up. Only one, pushed by two oarsmen, was gliding along the shore, as if looking for a place to run aground. Before the astonished oarsmen had time to open their mouths, I had made the jump and was seeking my balance in the middle of that small shell. "Twenty francs for you! Quick, to Locarno!" They understood and put their strength to the oars.

And there, followed by a swarm of children, two gendarmes arrive, gesticulating, at the shore. One jumped onto the bank and busied himself, but in vain! around the boats. They were, I believe, secured with a padlock. The other, waving his arms like a Dutch windmill, was shouting: "Stop, stop!" I deemed it prudent not to entrust my salvation to the pity of others and, gripping my double-barrelled pistol: "Either to Locarno, or to the next world!"

On the shore, laughter and applause resounded. The two carabinieri scan the surface of the lake... no hope of help from that quarter. They vented their tantrum by showing their fists to the horizon.

(1) — That Mazzini was in Milan in 1853 was never stated either by him or by others, as far as I know. But the Count di Rudio insists that Fortunato Calvi's statement admits no ambiguity.
