NON conosco moto insurrezionale italiano che sia valso a Giuseppe Mazzini tante calunnie, tante amarezze, tante defezioni quanto quello del 1853.

La gente così detta positiva, di quel positivismo miope che consiste nel lasciare il mondo correre indisturbato magari alla propria rovina, sosteneva che un Popolo di recente dissanguato e rotto, non poteva rispondere animosamente ad un appello alle armi; sobbarcarsi volonteroso a nuovi sacrifici. E sentenziava, con tutta serietà, che le rivoluzioni si fanno quando spontaneamente maturano, non quando si vuole.

Massima singolare che, sviscerata, condurrebbe a quest'altra: "Per acquistare la propria indipendenza, per rivendicare i propri diritti, nulla di meglio dello sdraiarsi in poltrona, lasciando al Sole la cura di maturare gli eventi, come matura le zucche."

I moti organizzati da Giuseppe Mazzini non furono mai né la rivoluzione vera, né la guerra; ma essi indubbiamente le prepararono e le resero necessarie.

D'altra parte, quelli del Febbraio 1853 non vennero iniziati dal grande agitatore; ma appunto da quel Popolo che, se si prestasse fede agli uomini seri, di null'altro sarebbe stato curante se non di acconciarsi un giaciglio nei fatti compiuti e nella propria miseria.

E lo ha pur detto il Mazzini e lo ha scritto e lo ha provato. Ma anche oggigiorno abbondano i superuomini, lustro e decoro dell'Italia nostra; i quali, non potendo confutare le dichiarazioni di quel grande che non ha mai mentito, le saltano maestrevolmente di piè pari e continuano ad esaltare i portenti della poltrona, a persistere nelle vecchie calunnie.

La "Fratellanza", società segreta mirante all'insurrezione, era già fortemente organizzata, a Milano, tre anni dopo che ogni speranza d'Italia pareva avvizzita. Composta da alcune migliaia d'operai, era essa suddivisa in varie sezioni contrassegnate dalle lettere dell'alfabeto. La sua esistenza era assolutamente ignorata dalla Polizia austriaca, dal Mazzini, dagli intellettuali, dalla classe aristocratica, che pure aveva comune con essa l'odio del giogo straniero.

Il suo organizzatore principale, il tintore Assi, si mise in comunicazione col Mazzini quando stimò che la "Fratellanza" avesse forza sufficiente ad agire. L'Assi chiese armi, danari e capi atti a condurre a buon fine un piano d'attacco.

Il Mazzini accolse la nuova come una prova che la propaganda delle sue idee aveva sviluppato buoni germi anche tra la moltitudine popolare; ma non prestò piena fede alle assicurazioni dell'Assi sull'importanza numerica e sulla serietà d'intenti della nuova organizzazione, sinché il maggiore Eugenio Brizzi da Jesi ed altri informatori, da lui inviati segretamente a Milano, non glie le ebbero confermate in tutto e per tutto.

Tuttavia, egli consigliava di temporeggiare, quando nel Dicembre del 1852, l'Austria dando nuovo sfogo alla propria ferocia, coronava di forche gli spalti di Belfiore e, scelta una vittima tra ogni classe di cittadini, ve l'appendeva.

Di fronte alla truce provocazione, parve agli operai della "Fratellanza" delitto il discutere più a lungo. Essi notificarono al Mazzini d'aver deciso di agire, anche senza il suo aiuto e senza il suo consenso. Dovere imprescindibile del Mazzini divenne quello di assecondare l'inevitabile, di adoperarsi perchè la generosa iniziativa, non rimanesse isolata e sterile.

Gli ultimi del Dicembre 1852, per incarico avuto direttamente dal Conte Grillanzoni, io partivo per Milano e la mezzanotte del giorno in cui arrivai, dopo breve attesa sotto l'orologio di piazza Mercanti, consegnavo un plico di istruzioni ad un uomo che, sbucato dall'ombra, mi aveva fatto il segnale convenuto.

Il piano definitivo dell'insurrezione perveniva così nelle mani dello stesso maggiore Brizzi incaricato di curarne l'esecuzione. Esso era dovuto nelle sue linee principali, a Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Sua base fondamentale il principio che le armi dovevano essere tolte di sorpresa al nemico, disorganizzato dall'uccisione dei suoi ufficiali.

Per concessione regolamentare, questi dormivano, generalmente, non in quartiere ma in alloggi privati. Si era preso nota dei loro indirizzi e conosciuti, naturalmente, erano anche i ritrovi dove, di solito, si radunavano.

La compagine militare austriaca era, tra l'altro, insediata, entro le mura di Milano, al Palazzo del Comando Generale, a quello della Gran Guardia ed al Castello.

Il primo veniva guardato, più che difeso, da un picchetto di venticinque soldati. Le loro armi stavano allineate sulla fuciliera, sotto l'andito del portone, d'innanzi al quale passeggiava incessantemente una vigile sentinella. Gli altri soldati ingannavano l'ore stirando le membra nel cortile, fumando nelle camerate. Alle cinque del pomeriggio, il Governatore, i Generali e lo Stato Maggiore pranzavano in quel palazzo.

Il ridurre, con un colpo di mano, la sentinella all'impotenza, non era sorpresa di grande difficoltà. Come la cordicella tira la fune e la fune trascina la grossa gomena, quel primo tenue successo avrebbe dato in potere degli insorti una trentina di fucili, il picchetto inerme, i capi della guarnigione.

Qualcosa di simile, ma con mezzi più poderosi, doveva essere tentato alla Gran Guardia, ove avevano stanza cento sgherri giù di lì; ma specialmente al Castello che racchiudeva, nell'armeria, dodici mila fucili. I due armaioli che, giornalmente li ispezionavano, erano dei nostri. Essi si impegnarono a lasciare le porte socchiuse perchè, l'abbatterle, non ci derubasse un tempo prezioso.

Nei locali della Gran Guardia, gli insorti dovevano trincerarsi e stabilire il proprio quartier generale per dare un centro alla rivoluzione. E, da quel centro, vestite delle uniformi tolte d'addosso ai morti ed ai prigionieri, dovevano divergere piccole pattuglie che, battendo il rullo, avrebbero indotto gli ufficiali ad uscire dalle loro dimore. Sulla soglia troverebbero chi si sarebbe incaricato di suggellare le loro labbra e per sempre.

Si scelse, a dar nel nemico, il 6 Febbraio, giorno di baldoria carnevalesca e di paga pei soldati. I loro quartieri ne sarebbero rimasti quasi deserti. La sorpresa avrebbe avuto così maggiore probabilità di riuscita; agio maggiore i nostri di precipitarsi nei caffé, nelle osterie; di rinnovare, e per intento più sublime, il lavacro di sangue dei Vespri.

A spianare la via alla vittoria, Giuseppe Mazzini aveva sollecitato ed ottenuto dal Kossuth, in procinto di partire per gli Stati Uniti d'America, un proclama rivoluzionario diretto ai suoi connazionali acquartierati specialmente nella caserma di San Francesco e già si avevano affidamenti che, al minimo indizio d'insurrezione seria, i figli mal domi della prode Ungheria avrebbero fatto causa comune cogli oppressi fratelli italiani.

Come coronamento dell'opera, le barricate! L'impresario dell'illuminazione, per proteggere chi le innalzava, si era impegnato a lasciare Milano nel buio. E per difenderle, Giuseppe Mazzini fece penetrare nella città certi proiettili di nuova invenzione, da lanciare sugli assalitori a mo' di bombe.

Aggiungasi che gli studenti di Pavia, segretamente interpellati, si erano impegnati ad accorrere in aiuto della capitale; segnale convenuto, una fiammata sulla guglia del Duomo. Li avrebbe comandati l'Acerbi... ed altro non chiesero se non l'armi. Il Mazzini, per la via del Po, le aveva provvedute.

Né a questo si limitava la preparazione dei moti simultanei, oltre le mura. In Piemonte spiava, impaziente d'azione, la turba dei rifugiati politici. Conveniva aprirle una via ampia e facile per calare, organizzata, all'offesa. Giuseppe Mazzini ne affidò l'incarico a Fortunato Calvi ch'i meditò di impossessarsi del Lago Maggiore, con un piano animoso che spiegherò tra breve.

Ed Aurelio Saffi, dopo aver battuto alla porta dei patrioti più facoltosi — in tale occasione Agostino Depretis, il futuro Presidente dei Ministri, elargì venticinquemila lire — si recò sfidando la morte, a Bologna e fomentava, con buone speranze di successo, l'insurrezione di quella città e della Romagna.

Per dirla in breve, Giuseppe Mazzini si era assicurata la cooperazione dei centri che avrebbero potuto frapporre una barriera tra il presidio di Milano e i vari nuclei austriaci sparsi per la Penisola. Fortunato Calvi mi confidava più tardi, a Zurigo, che egli si lusingava di girare, in una quindicina di giorni, colle forze rivoluzionarie, l'esercito austriaco d'Italia e di tagliarlo, all'Alpi, dalla sua base di rinsanguamento. Bastava, per dare animo e tempo alla Lombardia di insorgere, che Milano potesse tener fermo e combattesse nelle sue vie almeno qualche giorno. E l'Italia avrebbe conquistato l'indipendenza per forza propria, come la Grecia, come gli Stati Uniti, evitando lo strascico fatale lasciato dalle ibride alleanze, dai loschi intrighi, dalle transazioni che ne hanno offuscata la grandezza e che, per lungo tempo ancora, si faranno, pur troppo, sentire.

Ma qui non discuto, narro.

Gli operai della "Fratellanza" eccetto i capi, ignoravano i particolari del piano insurrezionale; ma sapevano che il 6 Febbraio si sarebbe agito. Essi avevano dissotterrato un centinaio di pistole e, per consiglio di Giuseppe Mazzini, si diedero a preparare pugnali e coltelli. Persino i chiodi delle barche del loro Naviglio vennero divelti e infissi su aste... Tutto serve alla disperazione di un Popolo!

Ed io, preso dalla febbre generale, già ponderavo, per prevenirle, tutte le sinistre eventualità prevedibili dell'assalto alla Gran Guardia, a cui il Brizzi mi aveva preposto, quando ricevetti dal Conte Grillanzoni l'ordine di recarmi immediatamente a Genova e di attendervi le sue istruzioni.

Le quali furono: abboccarmi con certo affiliato, farmi conteggiare, a nome del Mazzini, quattro mila lire in oro; portarle a Sander e Radonitz, che mi aspettavano a Locarno. Attenermi, nel viaggio, alla via del Lago Maggiore e vigilare se nulla di insolito fosse lì che indicasse scoperti i piani del Calvi o che, intralciandoli, richiedesse la loro modificazione. Abboccarmi, al mio ritorno da Locarno, col Calvi stesso che si sarebbe trovato ad Intra e riferirgli puntualmente quanto avessi veduto e udito.

L'Austria manteneva sul Verbano una flottiglia di cannoniere che, solitamente, stavano ancorate di fronte a Luino. Alla sorveglianza delle rive ed al servizio passeggieri era addetto il piroscafo "Radetzky" che solcava il lago giornalmente ed aveva sempre a bordo una compagnia di soldati. Questi, durante il tragitto, se ne stavano sotto coperta, lasciando i loro fucili allineati presso il boccaporto, sotto la sorveglianza di una sentinella. Soppressa la sentinella, riesciva più che probabile l'impossessarsi dell'armi e il rinchiudere nella stiva gli altri soldati. Sander e Radonitz, già ufficiali della marina austriaca, passati poi al servizio della Repubblica Veneta, avrebbero preso il comando del piroscafo e n'avrebbero disposto come di nave italica.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi aveva stabilito che il "Radetzky", approdando a Luino a sera inoltrata, continuasse a sventolare la bandiera austriaca io ed una mano dei nostri a bordo, vestiti dell'odiata uniforme.

Bisognava avvicinarsi come amici alle cannoniere impreparate all'azione; bisognava impossessarsene di sorpresa. Poi, rimorchiatele al largo, rivolgere le bocche omicide contro la piccola guarnigione. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, organizzato buon nerbo di patrioti, avrebbe assaltato Luino dalla parte della campagna. Inalberata la bandiera italiana su quel punto strategico, padroni del lago... le munizioni, l'armi e gli armati provenenti dal Piemonte, avrebbero trovato via relativamente facile sin sotto le mura di Milano.

Quand'io giunsi ad Arona mancava qualche ora alla partenza del "Radetzky." Le trascorsi passeggiando sulla riva del lago. Rammento che la magnificenza del paesaggio, tutto verde e alpino malgrado l'inverno, mi fece sull'animo un'impressione di tanta dolcezza che, lì per lì, quasi me lo infiacchiva. E meravigliai che gli uomini, potendo godere fraternamente tanto paradiso, se lo guastassero attanagliandosi perpetuamente, come se nel loro petto non potessero germogliare se non passioni basse e feroci.

Ma non appena la campana del "Radetzky" annunciò imminente la partenza, il mio spirito ribelle ripigliò il sopravvento. Pensai: "Bella è la Patria nostra. Per questo appunto ci mettono il coltello alla gola e tentano d'appropriarsela. Dunque, tanto più santa quanto più intensa la rabbia che ci sospinge a difenderla. Ripassino l'Alpi e torneremo fratelli."

Il piroscafo prese il largo. Accesi la pipa e camminando a passi lenti, or qua or là, procurai d'assumere le pose di chi contempla, estatico, il paesaggio che gli sfila d'innanzi. Ma la mente era fissa al mio segreto intento. Vidi le carabine allineate presso il boccaporto; scrutai, pesai quasi, la sentinella che, con tedesca rigidezza, con regolarità da pendolo, calpestava, camminando, l'assito; udii le voci caotiche uscire dal legno profondo e le compresi... parlavano, con lubrica franchezza, d'anche e di poppe, punteggiando il loro dire con grasse risate. Né mai, durante l'intero viaggio, sbucarono da quell'antro oscuro, i baffi cosmeticati di un "kaiserlich". Fedeli alla consegna, quelle teste geometriche!

Il nostro colpo di mano mi parve di riuscita tanto sicura, che più volte mi rammaricai d'esser solo e impreparato a tentarlo.

Ma a Locarno, non trovai animi ben disposti quanto il mio. Sander e Radonitz mi accolsero, non senza sussiego, in casa di un certo Olivieri, negoziante di generi vari ed anche di armi. Essi presero e contarono accuratamente l'oro che mi cavai dalla cintola. Ma quando si trattò di concretare i preliminari dell'azione i ma e i se divennero fitti e rigidi quanto una muraglia.

Pochi giorni innanzi — mi si disse — erano stati messi al buio due Ungheresi coi quali erano corse pratiche segrete; due dei nostri. "Le Autorità li tenevano completamente segregati e sui motivi dell'arresto nulla era trapelato. Perché tante precauzioni, tanto mistero? C'era da adombrarsi anche per meno."

Il Radonitz riteneva per certo che la Polizia avesse qualche sentore di quanto si tramava. Il tentare novità sarebbe stato un dare nella rete a capo fitto. Egli esortava a non confondere il coraggio colla temerità. Si trattava della vita, sacra per lui, di tanti patrioti. Che cosa si sarebbe perso nell'indugiare; nell'accertare, con debite cautele, l'umore d'un nemico tanto potente e tanto crudele? La saggezza non consisteva nell'aver fretta; ma nell'agire in modo che la vittoria riescisse certa e come inevitabile.

A tanto osservai che se l'arresto di qualche fratello dovesse bastare a distogliere gli Italiani dai loro propositi, tanto valeva il rinunciare per sempre ad agire. Dal '49 in poi, non era passato giorno che il bastone austriaco non lavorasse, che qualche catena non fosse ribadita. La Polizia non poteva sospettare moto tanto vasto quanto il nostro senza prendere precauzioni straordinarie; le quali non sarebbero sfuggite ai fratelli sparsi per ogni dove. Nessun allarme era giunto da qualsivoglia parte. Io stesso avevo percorso il lago quant'era lungo, su nave austriaca, coll'occhio all'erta e nulla, proprio nulla, avevo notato d'insolito. D'altronde, le eventualità d'essere scoperti erano in proporzione del tempo impiegato ad organizzare la congiura e del numero di coloro che vi partecipavano. Tanto maggiore appariva la necessità di un'azione pronta e quasi fulminea. Fortunato Calvi doveva essere già partito da Torino; egli si sarebbe trovato ad Intra alla domane. Solo a lui spettava il decidere sulla maggiore o minore opportunità di procrastinare. La nostra inazione gli avrebbe tolta la possibilità della scelta e sarebbe equivalsa ad una defezione. Ad ogni modo, ero venuto a Locarno, non per discutere, ma per agire. Mia consegna il rapportare ad Intra quanto avevo visto ed inteso. Sarei ripartito anche solo, mi costasse la vita.

Era presente, mentre parlavo, la moglie dell'Olivieri. Essa uscì senza far parola e, poco dopo, ritornava portando un pacco di manifesti rivoluzionari ed un fascio di pugnali. Mi aiutò, colle proprie mani, a nascondermi addosso gli uni e gli altri ed ogni discussione rimase così troncata. La mattina seguente, mi imbarcavo sull'Arno, piroscafo piemontese e ridiscendevo celermente il Verbano. Sander e Radonitz erano rimasti a terra!

Che profondo cambiamento in quelle poche ore! Il cielo torbido preludiava il fioccar della neve; grigio e quasi cinereo il lago. Dalle Alpi soffiava un vento diacciato che aveva come spazzata via la vita festosa, dalle rive e dalle pendici.

Trovai sull'Arno gente imbaccuccata, dall'aria egoisticamente pensosa; facce volgari che tradivano la preoccupazione del mercato. La mia attenzione finì per concentrarsi sul capitano, un uomo ossuto, dalla testa fenomenalmente grossa. Era assorto nella lettura di un giornale e gettava, solo di quando in quando, uno sguardo alla rotta. Chi era? quali sentimenti lo animavano? Potevano, sul suo cuore, il nome e le sofferenze della Patria?

Risposi a me stesso di sì e commisi un grave errore. Egli si era improvvisamente alzato e, come seguendo l'impulso di un pensiero fugace, con passi affrettati, recavasi altrove. Aveva gettato il suo giornale su un sedile. Trassi un proclama rivoluzionario, lo nascosi tra le pieghe di quel giornale e ritornai, correndo, al mio posto.

Non aspettai a lungo! Una faccia penosamente sorpresa, un aggrottar di ciglia, un dondolare del pesante testone. Mi fissò con due occhi che non ho più scordato. E forse erano incastonati nella faccia del più immacolato Aristide del Piemonte; ma a me parvero imbevuti, e quasi ubriachi, di commiserazione cretina e di bestiale minaccia. Dissimulai fischiando una cabaletta del Verdi, al vento che mi ruggiva d'attorno.

Sono ad Intra. Fra il crocchio dei curiosi accorsi all'arrivo del piroscafo, afferro, come chi dicesse al volo, un segnale ben noto. Seguii da lungi la persona che me lo fece e fui condotto ad una villa solitaria. L'ignoto fratello restò fuori ed io entrai. Mi trovai a faccia a faccia con Fortunato Calvi.

Quale piena d'affetti e quante cose avrei voluto dirgli; quante altre, mi imagino, egli avrebbe voluto confidarmi! Ma dovemmo accontentarci di stringerci a lungo la destra. Lì per lì, né io, né lui potemmo pronunciar parola. Quando giunsi a svincolare le labbra, dissi: "Sono le vedette al loro posto?"

Avutane l'assicurazione, trassi d'addosso i proclami e tutti quanti i pugnali, tranne uno, e li deposi su un tavolo. Ripresi:

— Tutti qui e nei dintorni. Aspettano l'armi ed il segnale.

— Ebbi sue nuove da Milano. (1) Fu un'imprudenza; ma la sua scaltrezza gli valse. Adesso è a Lugano. Faccian le braccia il dover loro, il cervello veglia e farà il suo.

Allora gli diedi conto del mio viaggio e non gli tacqui della strana esitazione di Sander e di Radonitz, mancati al convegno. Non avevo ancor finito, quando una finestra si aperse e nel vano apparve il viso dello stesso affiliato che mi aveva guidato sin là. Mi disse, con voce concitata: "Scappa, scappa! I carabinieri sono al cancello. Ti cercano!"

D'un balzo, fui in giardino, lo attraversai di corsa, ponendo cura che la visuale dei messeri, che si trovavano al cancello, rimanesse intercettata dalla casa. Mi arrampicai su un albero e scavalcai il muro di cinta. Quattro salti e sono al lago. Sul greto, incatenate ad una palafitta, erano allineate varie barchette. Una sola, spinta da due rematori, scivolava a randa a randa, quasi cercando un posto di dare in secco. Prima che i rematori, stupefatti, avessero il tempo di aprir bocca, avevo spiccato il salto e cercavo, in mezzo a quel piccolo guscio, il mio equilibrio. "Venti franchi per voi! Presto, a Locarno!" Compresero e fecero forza di remi.

Ed ecco, seguiti da una torma di fanciulli, due gendarmi giungere, gesticolando, alla riva. L'uno saltò sul greto e si affaccendò, ma indarno! attorno alle barche. Erano, credo, assicurate a lucchetto. L'altro, agitando le braccia come un mulino olandese, vociava: "Ferma, ferma!" Stimai prudenza il non affidare la mia salvezza all'altrui commiserazione e, impugnata la mia pistola a due canne: "O a Locarno, o all'altro mondo!"

Sulla riva risuonavano risate ed applausi. I due carabinieri scrutano la superficie del lago... nessuna speranza d'aiuto da quella parte. Sfogarono la loro bizza mostrando i pugni all'orizzonte.

(1) — Che il Mazzini fosse a Milano nel 1853, non fu mai detto né da lui, né da altri, per quanto io sappia. Ma il Conte di Rudio insiste nel ritenere che l'affermazione di Fortunato Calvi non ammette equivoci.

C. C.

CHAPTER THIRTEEN

I KNOW of no Italian insurrectionary movement that brought upon Giuseppe Mazzini so many calumnies, so many afflictions, so many desertions as that of 1853.

The so-called practical men, with that short-sighted practicality which consists in leaving the world to run undisturbed even to its own ruin, maintained that a People recently bled white and broken could not respond with spirit to a call to arms; could not willingly submit to new sacrifices. And they pronounced, with great solemnity, that revolutions come to pass when they ripen of themselves, not when one wills it so.

A singular maxim which, stripped to its core, would lead to this other: "To win one's independence, to vindicate one's rights, nothing is better than to recline in one's armchair, leaving to the Sun the care of ripening events, as it ripens gourds."

The movements organised by Giuseppe Mazzini were never, in truth, either revolution or war; but they indubitably prepared the way for both and rendered them necessary.

Moreover, those of February 1853 were not initiated by the great agitator, but by precisely that People which, were one to trust the serious-minded men, would have had no thought save to make itself comfortable among accomplished facts and its own misery.

And Mazzini himself has said as much, and has written it, and has proved it. Yet even today there is no lack of supermen, the lustre and ornament of our Italy, who, unable to refute the declarations of that great man who never lied, skip over them with masterly ease and continue to extol the wonders of the armchair, persisting in the old calumnies.

The *Fratellanza*, a secret society bent upon insurrection, was already strongly organised in Milan three years after every hope for Italy had seemed to wither. Composed of several thousand workmen, it was subdivided into various sections distinguished by the letters of the alphabet. Its existence was utterly unknown to the Austrian Police, to Mazzini, to the intellectuals, to the aristocratic class, who, for all that, shared with it a common hatred of the foreign yoke.

Its principal organiser, the dyer Assi, entered into communication with Mazzini when he judged that the *Fratellanza* had sufficient strength to act. Assi requested arms, money, and leaders capable of bringing a plan of attack to a successful issue.

Mazzini received the news as proof that the propagation of his ideas had brought forth good seed even among the common multitude; but he did not place full credence in Assi's assurances regarding the numerical strength and the seriousness of purpose of the new organisation, until Major Eugenio Brizzi of Jesi and other informants, sent by him secretly to Milan, had confirmed them in every particular.

Nevertheless, he was urging patience when, in December 1852, Austria, giving fresh vent to her ferocity, crowned the ramparts of Belfiore with gibbets and, having chosen a victim from every rank of citizens, hanged them there.

In the face of this grim provocation, it seemed to the workmen of the *Fratellanza* a crime to deliberate any longer. They notified Mazzini that they had resolved to act, even without his aid and without his consent. Mazzini's inescapable duty then became that of seconding the inevitable, of endeavouring to ensure that this generous initiative should not remain isolated and fruitless.

In the last days of December 1852, charged with a mission received directly from Count Grillanzoni, I departed for Milan, and at midnight of the day on which I arrived, after a brief wait beneath the clock in the Piazza Mercanti, I delivered a packet of instructions to a man who, emerging from the shadow, had given me the agreed signal.

The definitive plan of the insurrection thus reached the hands of Major Brizzi himself, who was charged with overseeing its execution. The plan was, in its principal lines, the work of Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Its fundamental basis was the principle that weapons must be taken from the enemy by surprise, after his forces had been thrown into disorder by the killing of his officers.

By a privilege of regulation, these officers slept, as a general rule, not in barracks but in private lodgings. Note had been taken of their addresses, and their customary meeting-places were, of course, known as well.

The Austrian military establishment was, among other positions, quartered within the walls of Milan at the Palace of the General Command, at that of the Grand Guard, and at the Castle.

The first was guarded — rather than defended — by a picket of twenty-five soldiers. Their weapons stood ranged upon the gun-rack, beneath the passageway of the gatehouse, before which a vigilant sentinel paced unceasingly. The other soldiers whiled away the hours stretching their limbs in the courtyard or smoking in the barrack-rooms. At five in the afternoon, the Governor, the Generals, and the General Staff dined in that palace.

To reduce the sentinel to helplessness by a sudden assault would not have been a particularly difficult surprise. As a small cord draws a rope and the rope hauls the great cable, that first slight success would have put into the hands of the insurgents some thirty rifles, the disarmed picket, and the chiefs of the garrison.

Something similar, but with more powerful means, was to be attempted at the Grand Guard, which housed some hundred of the ruffians; but above all at the Castle, which contained in its armoury twelve thousand muskets. The two armourers who inspected them daily were men of ours. They undertook to leave the doors ajar, so that the time lost in breaking them down should not rob us of a precious moment.

In the premises of the Grand Guard, the insurgents were to entrench themselves and establish their own headquarters, to give a centre to the revolution. And from that centre, clad in the uniforms stripped from the dead and the prisoners, small patrols were to fan out who, beating the drum-roll, would have induced officers to emerge from their dwellings. On the threshold they would find those who would undertake to seal their lips — and for ever.

The sixth of February was chosen for the strike upon the enemy, a day of carnival festivity and of pay for the soldiers. Their quarters would thereby be left almost deserted. The surprise would thus have a far greater probability of success; and our men would have the greater ease to pour into the cafés and taverns, and to renew, though for a more sublime purpose, the blood-tide of the Vespers.

To smooth the path to victory, Giuseppe Mazzini had solicited and obtained from Kossuth, who was on the point of departing for the United States of America, a revolutionary proclamation addressed to his compatriots quartered principally in the barracks of San Francesco; and pledges had already been received that, at the first sign of a serious insurrection, the ill-subdued sons of valiant Hungary would have made common cause with their oppressed Italian brothers.

As the crowning touch of the enterprise — the barricades! The contractor for the city's lighting, in order to protect those who would raise them, had pledged to leave Milan in darkness. And to defend them, Giuseppe Mazzini had caused certain projectiles of a new invention to be smuggled into the city, to be hurled upon the assailants in the manner of bombs.

One must add that the students of Pavia, secretly consulted, had pledged to hasten to the aid of the capital; the agreed signal, a blaze upon the spire of the Cathedral. Acerbi was to have commanded them — and they asked nothing more than weapons. Mazzini had provided these by way of the Po.

Nor was the preparation of simultaneous movements beyond the walls limited to this. In Piedmont, the throng of political exiles kept watch, impatient for action. It was necessary to open them a broad and easy road to descend, in organised formation, upon the offensive. Giuseppe Mazzini entrusted this charge to Fortunato Calvi, who conceived the bold plan of seizing Lake Maggiore, a design I shall explain shortly.

And Aurelio Saffi, after having knocked at the doors of the wealthier patriots — on which occasion Agostino Depretis, the future Prime Minister, contributed twenty-five thousand lire — went to Bologna, defying death, and was fomenting, with fair hopes of success, the insurrection of that city and of the Romagna.

To put it briefly, Giuseppe Mazzini had secured the co-operation of the centres which might have interposed a barrier between the Milan garrison and the various Austrian nuclei scattered about the Peninsula. Fortunato Calvi confided in me later, at Zurich, that he had cherished the hope of turning, within a fortnight, with the revolutionary forces, the Austrian army of Italy, and of cutting it off at the Alps from its base of replenishment. It sufficed, to give spirit and time to Lombardy to rise, that Milan might hold firm and fight in her streets for at least a few days. And Italy would have won her independence by her own strength, as Greece had won hers, as the United States had won theirs, avoiding the fatal aftermath left by hybrid alliances, murky intrigues, and compromises that have clouded her greatness and whose effects, unhappily, will continue to be felt for a long time yet.

But here I do not argue, I narrate.

The workmen of the *Fratellanza*, with the exception of the leaders, were ignorant of the particulars of the insurrectionary plan; but they knew that on the sixth of February the blow would be struck. They had unearthed some hundred pistols and, on Mazzini's advice, had set themselves to fashioning daggers and knives. Even the rivets from the boats of their Naviglio were prised out and fixed upon shafts — everything serves the desperation of a People!

And I, seized by the general fever, was already weighing, in order to forestall them, all the foreseeable adverse contingencies of the assault upon the Grand Guard, to which Brizzi had appointed me, when I received from Count Grillanzoni the order to proceed immediately to Genoa and to await his instructions there.

These instructions were: to meet with a certain affiliated member, to have counted out to me, in Mazzini's name, four thousand lire in gold; to carry them to Sander and Radonitz, who were awaiting me at Locarno. To follow, on the journey, the road along Lake Maggiore and to watch whether anything unusual might be observed there indicating that Calvi's plans had been discovered, or that, by obstructing them, their modification had become necessary. To meet, on my return from Locarno, with Calvi himself, who would be found at Intra, and to report to him punctually all that I had seen and heard.

Austria maintained on the Verbano a flotilla of gunboats which ordinarily lay at anchor opposite Luino. Assigned to the surveillance of the shores and to the passenger service was the steamship *Radetzky*, which plied the lake daily and always had on board a company of soldiers. These, during the crossing, remained below decks, leaving their rifles ranged near the hatchway under the watch of a sentinel. With the sentinel overpowered, it seemed more than probable that one might seize the weapons and shut the other soldiers below in the hold. Sander and Radonitz, formerly officers in the Austrian navy and subsequently passed into the service of the Venetian Republic, would have taken command of the steamship and disposed of it as of an Italian vessel.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi had determined that the *Radetzky*, putting in at Luino well into the evening, should continue to fly the Austrian flag, with myself and a handful of our men on board, clad in the hated uniform.

It was necessary to approach as friends the gunboats unprepared for action; necessary to seize them by surprise. Then, having towed them out into open water, to turn their murderous muzzles upon the small garrison. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, having organised a sound body of patriots, would have assaulted Luino from the landward side. With the Italian flag raised over that strategic point, masters of the lake, the munitions, arms, and armed men coming from Piedmont would have found a comparatively open road to the very walls of Milan.

When I arrived at Arona there was still some hours to go before the departure of the *Radetzky*. I spent them walking along the shore of the lake. I recall that the magnificence of the landscape, all green and Alpine despite the winter, made upon my spirit an impression of such sweetness that, for a moment, it all but unmanned me. And I marvelled that men, being able to enjoy in fraternal ease so much of paradise, should spoil it for themselves by clutching at one another perpetually, as though nothing could take root in their breasts save base and ferocious passions.

But no sooner had the bell of the *Radetzky* announced that departure was imminent than my rebellious spirit reasserted itself. I thought: "Beautiful is our Fatherland. For that very reason they put the knife to our throats and attempt to make it their own. Therefore, all the more holy, as it is the more fierce, the rage that drives us to defend it. Let them recross the Alps and we shall be brothers again."

The steamship stood out into the open water. I lit my pipe and, walking with slow steps, this way and that, I endeavoured to assume the air of one who contemplates, in a kind of rapture, the landscape gliding past. But my mind was fixed upon my secret purpose. I saw the carbines ranged near the hatchway; I scrutinised — weighed, almost — the sentinel who, with German rigidity, with the regularity of a pendulum, trod back and forth across the planking; I heard chaotic voices rising from the depths of the vessel and understood them — they spoke, with coarse candour, of haunches and rumps, punctuating their discourse with coarse laughter. Nor did there emerge at any point during the entire voyage, from that dark recess, the pomaded moustache of a *kaiserlich* officer. Faithful to their orders, those geometric heads!

Our coup de main seemed to me so certain of success that more than once I bitterly regretted being alone and unprepared to attempt it.

But at Locarno I found spirits less willing than my own. Sander and Radonitz received me, not without a certain stiffness, at the house of one Olivieri, a dealer in sundry goods and also in arms. They took and counted carefully the gold which I drew from my money-belt. But when it came to settling the preliminaries of the action, the buts and the ifs grew thick and solid as a wall.

A few days before — I was told — two Hungarians with whom secret dealings had been in train had been thrown into prison; two of our men. "The Authorities kept them in complete isolation, and nothing had transpired regarding the grounds of their arrest. Why such precautions, such mystery? There was cause for alarm at far less than this."

Radonitz was firmly persuaded that the Police had caught some scent of what was being plotted. To attempt new ventures would be to walk headlong into the net. He urged that courage must not be confounded with rashness. It was a matter of the lives, sacred to him, of so many patriots. What would be lost by delay; by ascertaining, with due caution, the temper of an enemy so powerful and so cruel? Wisdom lay not in haste, but in acting in such manner that victory should prove certain and all but inevitable.

To all this I replied that if the arrest of some brother were enough to deflect Italians from their purposes, it would be as well to renounce action for ever. Since '49, not a day had passed without the Austrian baton being at work, without some chain being riveted afresh. The Police could not have suspected a movement as vast as ours without taking extraordinary precautions, which could not have escaped the notice of our brothers scattered everywhere. No alarm had come from any quarter whatsoever. I myself had traversed the lake from end to end, aboard an Austrian vessel, with my eye alert, and had noticed nothing — nothing at all — out of the ordinary. Moreover, the chances of discovery were in proportion to the time spent in organising the conspiracy and to the number of those who took part in it. All the more urgent, therefore, appeared the necessity for swift and almost lightning-like action. Fortunato Calvi must already have departed from Turin; he would be at Intra on the morrow. To him alone belonged the decision as to whether delay was more or less opportune. Our inaction would have robbed him of the possibility of choice and would have amounted to desertion. At all events, I had come to Locarno not to debate but to act. My orders were to report at Intra all that I had seen and heard. I would set out again alone, though it cost me my life.

Present while I spoke was Olivieri's wife. She left without a word and returned shortly after, bearing a packet of revolutionary proclamations and a bundle of daggers. She helped me, with her own hands, to conceal both upon my person, and all argument was thus cut short. On the following morning I embarked on the *Arno*, a Piedmontese steamship, and descended swiftly along the Verbano. Sander and Radonitz had remained ashore!

What a profound change in those few hours! The overcast sky heralded the fall of snow; the lake grey, and almost ashen. From the Alps there blew an icy wind that seemed to have swept away all festive life from the shores and the hillsides.

I found aboard the *Arno* muffled-up passengers with an air of self-absorbed preoccupation; common faces betraying the anxieties of commerce. My attention came at last to fix itself upon the captain, a bony man with a phenomenally large head. He was absorbed in the reading of a newspaper and cast only an occasional glance at the course. Who was he? What sentiments animated him? Could the name and the sufferings of the Fatherland find a place in his heart?

I answered myself with yes, and committed a grave error. He had suddenly risen and, as if following the impulse of a fleeting thought, walked with hurried steps to another part of the deck. He had left his newspaper on a seat. I drew forth a revolutionary proclamation, concealed it within the folds of that newspaper, and returned at a run to my place.

I did not wait long! A face painfully surprised, a knitting of brows, a swaying of that heavy head. He fixed me with a pair of eyes I have never since forgotten. Perhaps they were set in the face of the most immaculate Aristides that Piedmont had ever produced; but to me they seemed steeped, and all but drunk, with idiotic commiseration and bestial menace. I dissembled by whistling a Verdi tune into the wind that roared about me.

I am at Intra. Among the knot of curious idlers gathered to see the steamship arrive, I catch — as one might snatch something from the air — a well-known signal. I followed at a distance the person who had given it and was led to a solitary villa. The unknown brother remained outside and I went in. I found myself face to face with Fortunato Calvi.

What a flood of feeling, and how many things I should have wished to say to him; how many others, I imagine, he would have wished to confide to me! But we had to content ourselves with clasping each other's hand at length. For a moment, neither he nor I could speak a word. When I at last managed to unlock my lips, I said: "Are the sentinels at their posts?"

Having received the assurance that they were, I drew from my person the proclamations and every one of the daggers save one, and laid them on a table. I continued:

— All here and in the neighbourhood. They await the arms and the signal.

— I have had news of him from Milan. (1) It was an imprudence; but his shrewdness stood him in good stead. He is now at Lugano. Let the arms do their duty; the head watches and will do its own.

I then gave him a full account of my journey, and concealed nothing of the strange hesitation of Sander and Radonitz, who had failed to appear at the rendezvous. I had not yet finished when a window opened and in the aperture appeared the face of the same affiliated member who had guided me thither. He said, in an agitated voice: "Run, run! The carabinieri are at the gate. They are looking for you!"

In a single bound I was in the garden, which I crossed at a run, taking care that the view of the gentlemen at the gate should remain intercepted by the house. I climbed a tree and vaulted the enclosing wall. Four strides and I was at the lake-shore. On the shingle, chained to a landing-stage, several small boats lay drawn up in a row. One alone, propelled by two oarsmen, was gliding along close to the shore, as though seeking a place to run in and ground. Before the oarsmen, stupefied, had time to open their mouths, I had leapt aboard and was finding my balance amid that small shell. "Twenty francs for you! Quickly, to Locarno!" They understood and bent to the oars.

And then, followed by a crowd of children, two gendarmes arrived gesticulating on the shore. One jumped onto the shingle and busied himself, but in vain, with the boats. They were locked with padlocks, I believe. The other, flailing his arms like a Dutch windmill, shouted: "Stop, stop!" I judged it prudent not to entrust my salvation to another's compassion and, grasping my double-barrelled pistol: "Either to Locarno, or to the next world!"

From the shore came laughter and applause. The two carabinieri scanned the surface of the lake — no hope of assistance from that quarter. They vented their fury by shaking their fists at the horizon.

(1) That Mazzini was in Milan in 1853 was never stated either by him or by others, so far as I am aware. But the Count di Rudio insists that Fortunato Calvi's assertion admits of no ambiguity.

I KNOW of no Italian insurrectionary movement that earned Giuseppe Mazzini so many calumnies, so much bitterness, so many defections as that of 1853.

The so-called practical people, with that short-sighted practicality which consists in letting the world run its course undisturbed, even to its own ruin, maintained that a People recently bled dry and broken could not respond with spirit to a call to arms, nor willingly take on new sacrifices. And they would pronounce, in all seriousness, that revolutions happen when they mature spontaneously, not when they are willed.

A singular maxim that, when thoroughly examined, would lead to this other: "To acquire one's independence, to reclaim one's rights, there is nothing better than to lie back in an armchair, leaving to the Sun the task of ripening events, as it ripens pumpkins."

The movements organized by Giuseppe Mazzini were never the true revolution, nor the war; but they undoubtedly prepared for them and made them necessary.

Furthermore, those of February 1853 were not initiated by the great agitator, but precisely by that People who, if one were to believe the serious men, would have cared for nothing other than to make a bed for themselves in the *faits accomplis* and in their own misery.

And Mazzini himself has said it and written it and proved it. But even today supermen abound, the pride and glory of our Italy; who, being unable to refute the declarations of that great man who never lied, masterfully leap over them with both feet and continue to exalt the portents of the armchair, to persist in the old calumnies.

The "Fratellanza," a secret society aimed at insurrection, was already strongly organized in Milano, three years after every hope for Italy seemed to have withered. Composed of several thousand workers, it was subdivided into various sections marked by the letters of the alphabet. Its existence was absolutely unknown to the Austrian Police, to Mazzini, to the intellectuals, and to the aristocratic class, which nevertheless shared with it a hatred of the foreign yoke.

Its principal organizer, the dyer Assi, communicated with Mazzini when he deemed that the "Fratellanza" had sufficient strength to act. Assi asked for arms, money, and leaders capable of bringing a plan of attack to a successful conclusion.

Mazzini welcomed the news as proof that the propaganda of his ideas had developed good seeds even among the popular multitude; but he did not give full credence to Assi's assurances about the numerical importance and seriousness of purpose of the new organization, until Major Eugenio Brizzi of Jesi and other informants, sent by him secretly to Milano, had confirmed them in every respect.

Nevertheless, he was advising to play for time when in December of 1852, Austria, giving new vent to its ferocity, crowned the ramparts of Belfiore with gallows and, choosing a victim from every class of citizen, hanged him there.

Faced with the grim provocation, it seemed a crime to the workers of the "Fratellanza" to debate any longer. They notified Mazzini that they had decided to act, even without his help and without his consent. It became Mazzini's inescapable duty to support the inevitable, to strive so that the generous initiative should not remain isolated and sterile.

In the last days of December 1852, on a commission received directly from Count Grillanzoni, I left for Milano, and at midnight on the day I arrived, after a brief wait under the clock in piazza Mercanti, I delivered a packet of instructions to a man who, emerging from the shadows, had given me the agreed-upon signal.

The definitive plan for the insurrection thus came into the hands of that same Major Brizzi tasked with overseeing its execution. In its main lines, it was the work of Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Its fundamental basis was the principle that the arms were to be taken by surprise from the enemy, who would be disorganized by the killing of his officers.

What would be lost in delaying; in ascertaining, with due caution, the mood of an enemy so powerful and so cruel? Wisdom did not consist in being in a hurry, but in acting in such a way that victory would be certain and as if inevitable.

To this I observed that if the arrest of a few brothers were enough to deter the Italians from their purposes, one might as well renounce action forever. From '49 onwards, not a day had passed that the Austrian stick had not been at work, that some chain had not been riveted. The Police could not suspect a movement as vast as ours without taking extraordinary precautions, which would not have escaped the brothers scattered everywhere. No alarm had come from any quarter. I myself had traversed the length of the lake, on an Austrian vessel, with a watchful eye, and I had noted nothing, absolutely nothing, unusual. Besides, the chances of being discovered were in proportion to the time spent organizing the conspiracy and the number of those who participated in it. All the greater appeared the necessity for prompt and almost lightning-fast action. Fortunato Calvi must have already departed from Torino; he would be in Intra the next day. It was for him alone to decide on the greater or lesser advisability of procrastinating. Our inaction would deprive him of the possibility of choice and would be tantamount to a defection. In any case, I had come to Locarno not to discuss, but to act. My orders were to report in Intra what I had seen and heard. I would depart again even if alone, cost me my life.

Present while I spoke was Olivieri's wife. She went out without a word and, shortly after, returned carrying a packet of revolutionary manifestos and a bundle of daggers. With her own hands, she helped me hide them on my person, and thus all discussion was cut short. The following morning, I embarked on the Arno, a Piedmontese steamship, and swiftly descended the Verbano again. Sander and Radonitz had remained ashore!

What a profound change in those few hours! The murky sky heralded the falling of snow; the lake was grey and almost ashen. A freezing wind blew from the Alps that seemed to have swept away the festive life from the shores and the slopes.

I found on the Arno people bundled up, with a selfishly pensive air; vulgar faces that betrayed the preoccupation of the marketplace. My attention finally came to concentrate on the captain, a bony man, with a phenomenally large head. He was absorbed in reading a newspaper and cast, only from time to time, a glance at the course. Who was he? What sentiments animated him? Could the name and the sufferings of the Fatherland have an effect on his heart?

I answered myself in the affirmative and committed a grave error. He had suddenly risen and, as if following the impulse of a fleeting thought, with hurried steps, was going elsewhere. He had thrown his newspaper on a seat. I drew out a revolutionary proclamation, hid it in the folds of that newspaper, and returned, running, to my post.

I did not wait long! A painfully surprised face, a frowning of the brows, a wagging of the heavy noggin. He fixed me with two eyes I have never forgotten. And perhaps they were set in the face of the most immaculate Aristides of Piemonte; but to me they seemed steeped, and almost drunk, with cretinous commiseration and bestial menace. I dissembled by whistling a cabaletta by Verdi to the wind that roared around me.

I am in Intra. Among the cluster of onlookers gathered for the steamship's arrival, I catch, as if on the fly, a well-known signal. I followed from afar the person who made it and was led to a solitary villa. The unknown brother remained outside and I entered. I found myself face to face with Fortunato Calvi.

What a flood of affections and how many things I would have wished to tell him; how many others, I imagine, he would have wished to confide in me! But we had to content ourselves with shaking hands for a long time. There and then, neither I nor he could utter a word. When I managed to unbind my lips, I said: "Are the lookouts at their posts?"

Having received assurance, I took from my person the proclamations and all the daggers, save one, and placed them on a table. I resumed:

— Everyone here and in the surroundings. They are waiting for the arms and the signal.

— I had news of him from Milano. (1) It was an imprudence; but his cunning served him well. Now he is in Lugano. Let the arms do their duty, the brain is watching and will do its part.

Then I gave him an account of my journey and did not conceal from him the strange hesitation of Sander and Radonitz, who had failed to appear at the rendezvous. I had not yet finished, when a window opened and in the frame appeared the face of the same affiliate who had guided me thus far. He said to me, in an agitated voice: "Escape, escape! The carabinieri are at the gate. They are looking for you!"

With one leap, I was in the garden, I crossed it at a run, taking care that the line of sight of the sirs at the gate remained blocked by the house. I climbed a tree and scaled the surrounding wall. Four leaps and I am at the lake. On the shore, chained to a piling, were several small boats lined up. Only one, pushed by two oarsmen, was gliding along the shore, as if looking for a place to run aground. Before the astonished oarsmen had time to open their mouths, I had made the jump and was seeking my balance in the middle of that small shell. "Twenty francs for you! Quick, to Locarno!" They understood and put their strength to the oars.

And there, followed by a swarm of children, two gendarmes arrive, gesticulating, at the shore. One jumped onto the bank and busied himself, but in vain! around the boats. They were, I believe, secured with a padlock. The other, waving his arms like a Dutch windmill, was shouting: "Stop, stop!" I deemed it prudent not to entrust my salvation to the pity of others and, gripping my double-barrelled pistol: "Either to Locarno, or to the next world!"

On the shore, laughter and applause resounded. The two carabinieri scan the surface of the lake... no hope of help from that quarter. They vented their tantrum by showing their fists to the horizon.

(1) — That Mazzini was in Milan in 1853 was never stated either by him or by others, as far as I know. But the Count di Rudio insists that Fortunato Calvi's statement admits no ambiguity.
