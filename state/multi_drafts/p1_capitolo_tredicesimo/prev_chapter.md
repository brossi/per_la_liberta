## Chapter Twelve

Of my excursion, which extended, on two separate occasions, as far as the remote Udine, I shall recount in brief some few episodes.

Having baffled the police at Milan, at Como, at Bergamo, at Brescia, I was drawing near to Mantova with a great confidence of spirit... I was reassured by the certainty of not being known there by a living soul. But at the very moment I was passing through the customs-post, violent as a battering-ram's blow, I felt my full name hurled squarely at my chest.

In that instant I believed myself discovered and lost. But the man who stood before me wore a smiling countenance, and his right hand, extended, invited mine. It was none other than the chief of the guards himself. Where I had made his acquaintance I can no longer recall... he was a patriot deep in secret affairs.

When he learned that I was not in Mantova for a change of air, his face darkened. The very day before, the police had carried out a number of arrests, the reason for which remained unknown... he glimpsed a connection between those unexpected severities and my arrival. He advised me to take refuge immediately in his house and remain shut within it. He would endeavour to sound out the ground, and in the evening, over a bite to eat, we should deliberate on what was to be done.

Provoked by the conspiracy in which the gentle figure of the priest Tazzoli plays a leading part, there was beginning at that very moment, in Mantova, the series of refined tortures that drove to an inhuman death the little band of the martyrs of Belfiore. But, returning late in the evening, my host could bring me nothing save vague tidings, and with them a breath of the terror that hung over the city. He had not encountered a single brother who felt secure of being able to pass the night in his own bed.

I lay down in the attic as best I could, without undressing... ready to attempt, at the slightest alarm, a flight across the rooftops.

Early the following morning, after a long conversation with my host, I learned that among the garrison there was an officer who had been educated at the Military Academy of Milan and had been a fellow-pupil of mine: the bonds of earliest youth do not grow worn with the passing of years. Half an hour later I was in the Castello, exchanging a fraternal embrace, or something very like it. I was under the protection of the double-headed eagle.

To explain my presence, I gave it out that I was bound for Belluno to attend upon my father, weighed down by years... I had made a detour to Mantova expressly to see my old friend again; to evoke, in his company, the age of happy carelessness. As by tacit understanding, we each took care not to touch upon subjects of a political nature; yet in the half-festivity that was organised in my honour, I never lost sight of the purpose of my journey, and gleaned, in snatches and by degrees, all the information I was seeking. Two hours before sunset, the officer himself courteously offered to accompany me, by carriage, beyond the city walls. And under his protection I passed through the customs-post without hindrance, receiving a military salute.

However, having escaped one danger that was in all likelihood imaginary, I was compelled to face one all too real at Verona.

I had taken lodgings at the "Scudo di Francia," and scarcely had I shut myself into my room than I made, by instinct, a small inspection of it. It was situated on the third floor; the single window gave onto a courtyard. Opposite me lay the rear wall of another hotel, the "Regina d'Inghilterra." Craning my face downward, I made out a broad penthouse roof that sloped toward the centre of the courtyard. A smell of the stable rose up to that height; I heard the stamping of a horse.

I sat down at the little table beside the lit fireplace and began to write my report on Mantova. All at once, measured footsteps resounded in the corridor. I blew out the candle and cast my letter into the fire, where it turned to ash and flew up through the flue.

There came a knock at the door and an obsequious voice, that of a servant: "Signor Moretti, if you please!" After a brief silence, another voice, gruff and imperious: "In the name of the Law, open up!"

I had already swung myself over the parapet and was clinging there with arms fully extended, so as to diminish the distance between myself and the penthouse roof. The gendarmes began to beat the door with the butts of their rifles... I loosened my grip and let myself drop, close against the wall. The shock was severe, but the damage fell to the roof-tiles. Along them I slid, and reaching the edge, availed myself of a projecting beam and of such gymnastics as my aching limbs permitted, in order to place the gutter between myself and the constables. Crouching beneath it, I heard the door of my room give way with a crash... then a hasty trampling... then silence... then a ringing voice: "Sergeant, I'll wager he jumped from the window!" And the sergeant, who knew better: "Fool! One doesn't jump from a third floor!"

I doubt they even leaned over the sill. The courtyard was deserted. From the half-open stable door a strip of light was cast. It revealed to me a dung-heap. I leapt into it...

A little later I walked resolutely into the kitchen of the "Regina d'Inghilterra:" scullions and servants, busy about their work, did not so much as raise their heads. A cook, his great paunch wrapped in a white apron, a white cap on his head, was blowing on his ladle at that very moment... He stared at me in stupefaction; but he had not yet recovered from his surprise when I was already making for the door of the dining room. The waiters took me in all probability for some scullion's friend. In a few seconds I found myself in the open air.

Where to pass the night? The police, rendered more suspicious still by my disappearance, would not have permitted me to find rest in any hotel. I wandered adrift until I found myself in front of the Post. Several stage-coaches stood there in a row with their doors flung wide... into one of them I threw myself at full length and crouched there until the approach of dawn. More than once, between sleep and waking, I heard the night-watch pass beneath my feet. Had I snored, in all probability I should not be here to tell that adventure of mine.

A yet more devilish peril I brought upon myself, through excess of zeal, on my return to Milan. On the advice of a certain Valentini, a student of chemistry, I had lodged, during my stays in the Lombard capital, at the home of his sweetheart; a woman who lived from the proceeds of a little dairy-shop she kept, and by taking in boarders. Several finance guards attended her table, as did a Hungarian sergeant, a relentless tormentor of the pianoforte. I felt as though I were entrenched within the very enemy's camp.

The dairywoman, a fleshy creature with a face like a fresh *panettone*, was of the most ardently Italian sympathies, so much so that on my previous visit I had entrusted to her, for destruction, a bundle of revolutionary pamphlets that had become superfluous. Now she informed me that she had preserved it intact, at the bottom of a chest of drawers. I rejoiced at the news... before returning to Piedmont I might, with a good posting of bills, put the finishing touch to my work.

I spoke of it to the Revolutionary Committee, which, exasperated in those days by the wretched news arrived from Mantova, approved my resolution. And among those patriots I found one who, of his own accord, offered to lend me his assistance... a stocky workman, with a broad scar upon his face. He told me not his name; he was an upholsterer, like the poor Antonio Sciesa.

A cold, rainy night, the streets deserted. We began at the locality known as the Carrobbio; I on one pavement, he on the other... we pasted up our proclamation with those small wafers which the Milanese call *obiadìtt*. We were heading toward the Duomo, and already I could see the façade of the magnificent temple gleaming white before me, when from the far side of the square, and precisely beneath the Coperto dei Figini, there rang out in the silence that terrible warning: "Run, run!"

I saw the stocky figure of the upholsterer flee at full speed toward the Piazza Mercanti, and a patrol of policemen, bursting forth from some dark alleyway, gave chase with shouts. Had it not been for his fraternal solicitude, I might perhaps have slipped away without attracting notice; but by launching that cry of his, he had put the constables on the alert as well; and a goodly number of them advanced toward me at a run.

There were no alternatives. I flung down the pamphlets and fled headlong through a labyrinth of narrow streets, until, breathless, I found myself confronted with the line of the city walls rearing up before me. I climbed the ramp on all fours; but with my pursuers at my heels and a customs-post, with its respective guard-house, to right and to left, I might consider myself trapped. Momentary shelter I found behind one of those great horse-chestnut trees, and for an instant I flattered myself that, uncertain of the direction I had taken, the wretches would turn back, crestfallen, upon their steps; or that, having gained the rampart, they would all veer to the right, leaving me free to steal away silently to the left. But they were not so simple-minded.

Having reached the top, they divided into two groups which, turning their backs to one another, advanced simultaneously on either side, peering with caution behind every tree. As at Verona, I found myself in the terrible alternative of submitting, sooner or later, to a small jump from which, by virtue of the noose, there would be no recovery, or of braving the immediate dangers of a great jump, which still left me some measure of a chance. I resolved upon the great jump.

How long I lay stunned down there at the bottom, in truth I never knew. Fortunately I had fallen upon soft ground, and perhaps it was the rain, which fell as though poured over me bodily, that served to bring me back to my feet. I set out, painfully and soaked to the bone, toward Pavia.

In what condition did I arrive there? It is not difficult to imagine. Three days under covers and I was once again in fine fettle. The brother of the Giovine Italia who had tended me with such care was desirous that I should remain longer; but the land of salvation was too near for me not to feel the fever to set foot on it once more. He accompanied me as far as the bridge that crosses the Ticino and joins the Lombard bank to the Piedmontese, and there we parted.

And then — two, three, a crowd of wild-eyed people came running noisily to the mouth of the bridge. From the phrases they exchanged at full voice, I understood that an unknown man, some smuggler or some hot-head pursued by a patrol, had thrown himself from the parapet. They had struck him mid-flight with a gunshot in the back, and were now endeavouring to fish him out.

I skirted a wide berth, and, having found a way to get myself ferried across by passing myself off as a deserter, I arrived, as midnight was approaching, at Stradella. The Casa Winkler was still lit. I entered and found before me a group of patriots who received me as though I had fallen from the clouds. A secret message, arrived from Pavia, had brought the news that I had been discovered, pursued, and mortally wounded while seeking escape by leaping into the Ticino. Fortunato Calvi had already been informed of it by telegraph.

I departed immediately and found Calvi still in bed. Barely recovered from his surprise, his first thought was for don Bastiano Barozzi, with whom he had already arranged to meet at nine o'clock at the Caffè del Pantheon. The good priest entered it pale and almost beside himself. His short-sightedness prevented him from seeing me, and Calvi went to meet him. Catching sight of Calvi, he exclaimed: "Ah, what a calamity! What will my benefactor say when he learns that I repaid him by driving his son to ruin?"

— And what would you say, if Carlo were to walk in here, sound and smiling?

The face of Barozzi underwent convulsive movements. All at once, the knot that was strangling him loosened, and he burst into a flood of tears.

Then the atheist threw himself upon the neck of the priest. Which tends to demonstrate that the hypothesis of God is not necessary to men in order that they may love one another as brothers!
