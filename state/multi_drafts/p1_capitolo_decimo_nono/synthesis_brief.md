Nella EFFICACIA di qualunque squarcio oratorio ad incitare i nobili impulsi ribelli giungono al cuore di un figlio, i singhiozzi della madre. Quelli della mia, nel silenzio della notte, risonarono spaventosamente.

Mi affrettai a sorreggerla con un braccio e le posi la destra sulle labbra come per soffocarli. "Calmati, calmati!" volevo dire, ma la voce non venne e, vinto da un impeto di tenerezza, io pure singhiozzai.

Il primo a dir sillaba coerente fu mio fratello Giustiniano, rimasto in disparte, quasi inosservato. Disse: "Tristi nuove. I nostri amici, i migliori, sono arrestati; don Bastiano è anche lui arrestato. Tiriamo la somma: il povero Achille ha lasciato l'ossa a Venezia; tu sei indubbiamente ricercato dalla Polizia."

E continuando, mi narrò i particolari della catastrofe. Don Bastiano aveva steso serenamente i polsi alle manette "felice di contribuire, col proprio sacrificio, a compiere la serie di patimenti richiesti dalla Provvidenza al riscatto del suo Paese." Ma il papà voleva rompere la sua canna di bambù sulle spalle degli sgherri e le carezze de' suoi a stento avevano potuto calmarlo.

Meditai un istante e ne conclusi: "Adesso l'importante è di condurre a casa la mamma. Penseremo poi al resto."

Ma la poverina, affranta dalla terribile prova, resa ancora più impotente dall'oscurità, inciampava ad ogni passo. Consegnai una delle mie pistole a Giustiniano e gli mormorai all'orecchio: "Fa da battistrada. Pensa a chi difendi... ai di Rudio non si pestano i calli impunemente. Mi hai compreso?"

Mi chinai e, prima che la mamma avesse tempo di avvedercene e di reagire, l'avevo tolta sulle mie braccia e procedetti, fiero di sostenere chi m'aveva portato nel suo grembo e con ben altro travaglio.

Alla prima, incerta luce dei fanali, deposi la santa donna e abbracciatela le impressi sulla fronte un bacio lungo. Non presentivo che sarebbe l'ultimo! "Arrivederci, mamma!" E le promisi che, per quanto avversa si mostrasse la fortuna, sempre avrei agito in modo che, rivedendola, o pensando all'amor suo, non mi avrebbe rimorso la coscienza di sentirmene indegno.

Nella località dove avevamo fatto sosta si costruiva una casa di Domenico. Quei travi e mattoni accatastati lungo la strada mi offrivano un ottimo schermo. Dissi a Giustiniano che l'avrei aspettato a quel posto. Mi riportasse l'arme. Se scopriva novità me ne avrebbe informato. Le mie intenzioni? Le saprebbe al suo ritorno.

Non aspettai a lungo. Mamma e Giustiniano erano giunti a casa senza imbattersi in anima viva. Mia sorella Luisa le aveva preparato una buona tazza di caffè; la poverina si mostrava, se non rassegnata, calma.

"Ed ora a noi!" — soggiunse Giustiniano. "Ai di Rudio non si pestano i calli impunemente... è massima tua; ma è anche la mia! Tiriamo la somma: il povero Achille ha lasciato l'ossa a Venezia; tu sei indubbiamente ricercato dalla Polizia, inseguito come un malfattore nelle vie della notte." Questo si insinuava profondamente nei miei pensieri; l'animo mio finì per rispecchiare la pittoresca maestà della montagna.

Riconobbi la casupola di Domenico, addossata alla rupe, l'uscio nereggiante sotto il pergolato, spoglio ormai di grappoli e di foglie... il pergolato che si allungava sino alla strada e divideva in parti uguali la spianata dell'orticello. Dal piede del vecchio noce che, colle fronde tremolanti, titillava il tetto dell'abituro, due bracchi si lanciarono abbaiando; ma rimisero tosto del loro impeto all'udirsi chiamare per nome. Essi seguirono le mie peste, allungando il muso e annusandomi ansiosamente i polpacci.

Una, due, tre martellate, a discreto intervallo... il sonno si abbarbica tenacemente ai lavoratori della montagna. Una quarta, una quinta rimbombarono nel vano, e finalmente dalle sconnessure del vecchio uscio, trapelò la luce; due zoccoloni si avanzarono rumorosamente.

"Apri, Domenico. Sono Carletto."

L'uscio cigolò ed io rimasi come abbagliato dalla lanterna che, con un balzo, si sollevò da terra e mi lanciò uno sprazzo di luce in viso.

"Carletto!.. più alto, più fiero, più abbronzato, col pizzo... ma Carletto! Ed io lo facevo in capo al mondo! Dicevano bene i nostri vecchi: solo i monti stanno al posto. Ma come qui a quest'ora! Si è smarrito per l'erta?"

Non risposi, ma accennando alla destra del buon vecchio, che stringeva una roncola: "Mi volevi tagliare la testa!"

Nella cucina dalle pareti ingommate di fumo, sotto l'ampia cappa, crepitò presto un fuoco sufficiente ad arrostire un capretto. Sedemmo l'uno di fronte all'altro, nelle nicchie laterali, scavate nel grosso muro, e mentre gli facevo piana la situazione, egli tormentava le braci colle corna del soffietto a canna. Quando udì che il babbo era in catene, assestò, con quel ferreo arnese, una puntata formidabile al ceppo, che sfavillò. Dallo stringere delle mascelle e dall'ira, avrei detto che il colpo era, intenzionalmente, diretto alla testa di qualche croato. Mi disse:

"Ha fatto bene a salire quassù. Dalla nostra altura, si domina la valle per oltre cinque miglia. Mettono appena il piede fuori di Belluno e già sappiamo dove vorrebbero tender la rete. Con cinque miglia di vantaggio, non c'è mangiacandele che batta gambe d'Italiano; lasciando anche stare che la montagna è tutta sparsa dei nostri. Che se il diavolo li aiuti ci capitassero addosso all'impensata... c'è rimedio anche per questo! Se la ricorda la vecchia caverna, dove lo cacciavo in castigo, quando mi rovinava i mandorli! Ad un salto dalla casa, l'entrata stretta e coperta così, che non te n'accorgi neanche a battervi del naso. In caso disperato, un uomo solo con un vecchio fucile, un arnese arrugginito, vi rovina il fegato a mezza Boemia. Non c'è che dire, ha mostrato sale in zucca, a riparare quassù."

Stette un momento come sospeso, poi aggiunse: "Io dundolo e Lei ha bisogno di riposo. Corro a risvegliare la vecchietta. Mezz'ora più, mezz'ora meno, che importa? Tanto deve preparare il beverone alla vacca che è da vitello. Per questa volta si rassegni a coricarsi nel nostro giaciglio... penseremo poi ad allestire un letto un po' più da cristiani."

E poiché io protestavo d'essere rotto a ben altre fatiche; di preferire la veglia accanto al fuoco, egli si atteggiò a persona punta sul vivo e disse: "E scusa: nel nostro giaciglio... ma non dubiti!... Le metteremo lenzuola da bucato!"

Quel giorno stesso, nel pomeriggio, Giustiniano capitava lassù. Aveva lasciato la città col pretesto di andare alla caccia di fringuelli e di cardellini; aveva seco la civetta, il paletto, la pania. Nulla di sospetto nel suo lungo giro; ma le notizie che recava erano molto tristi. Un'invasione della polizia, una perquisizione in piena regola; mamma in terribile orgasmo... tanto più che la Luisa conservava una lettera molto compromettente. Fortunato Calvi aveva, per la Luisa, un'ammirazione sincera; un'amicizia che lo induceva a confidarle, come a sorella, i suoi intimi pensieri. In quei giorni egli aveva inviato le sue istruzioni al babbo, indirizzandole, a meglio deludere la Polizia, appunto alla Luisa. Erano stese in linguaggio velato, ma non tanto che non dovessero destare i sospetti di chi già subodorasse la nostra trama. Inoltre, cedendo agli impulsi del suo cuore, il Calvi, in un breve poscritto, esprimeva la speranza che, "liberata la Patria, nulla più si sarebbe frapposto alla sua unione con Teresa Duodo", una gentile signorina da Padova che, adorata, l'adorava.

Fortunatamente mia sorella, colto il destro, gettò prestamente la lettera alla chitarra del babbo... gran pizzicatore di corde, il babbo! E i perquisitori, che pure avevano frugato ogni cantuecio della casa ed ogni angolo, non si erano curati di quel umile strumento, appeso alla parata.

Già essi si accingevano ad andarsene, già le due donne incominciavano a respirare liberamente, quando l'ispettore, che aveva indubbiamente ordini perentori, chiamò la Luisa in arresto... Un lungo abbraccio... poi la mamma era caduta riversa sulla poltrona, in preda ad una terribile crisi nervosa.

All'udire quelle notizie, mi parve che l'aria greve del piano fosse salita al monte e mi soffocasse. A quale scopo indugiare maggiormente nel Cadore? Mi trattenne un senso cavalleresco che mi fece riputare codardia il cercare la mia sicurezza, mentre i miei cari erano perseguitati ed esposti ad ogni pericolo; mi trattenne anche la speranza, sia pur vaga ed effimera, che Fortunato Calvi e Giuseppe Mazzini, saputa la mala piega presa dai loro piani, si adoperassero per raddrizzarli e avessero bisogno di me. Ma gli avvenimenti, precipitando, dovevano ben presto soffiar via anche quegli scrupoli.

Una mattina passeggiavo a breve distanza dal casolare e, per istinto, studiavo con occhio tattico il terreno, quando, girando un dirupo, mi trovai a faccia a faccia con un ufficiale austriaco. Non credo si accorgesse del mio turbamento, perché mi salutò in modo inappuntabilmente cortese e mi narrò d'esser partito da Belluno, con altri graduati, prima che il sole sorgesse. Si era sbandato per inseguire una volata di pernici. Mi domandò, concludendo, se avessi visto passare, per quei paraggi, i suoi compagni di caccia.

Ottenuta risposta negativa, mi offerse un sigaro poi, con una certa meraviglia: "Vive in questi luoghi solitari? Ci vuol del genio per non annoiarvisi!" Prima che io potessi rispondere, echeggiarono, di balza in balza, alcuni colpi di fucile. Egli rovesciò il pollice e disse: "Sono i miei amici!" Salutò e si allontanò a grandi passi.

Poche ore dopo, giungeva lassù Giustiniano. Compresi, al solo vederlo, che qualche altra brutta nuova gli premeva il cuore. Mi narrò che, la mattina per tempo, uno scritturante della Polizia, a cui il mestiere non aveva paralizzato ogni fibra onestamente sensitiva, aveva battuto all'uscio di casa nostra. Confidò alla mamma come al suo ufficio fosse giunta notizia positiva che io mi trovavo a Belluno, o nei dintorni e per scopi che non potevano giungere graditi alle regie imperiali autorità. Queste avevano emanato ordine perentorio di ghermirmi, o vivo o morto. Ci regolassimo e con sollecitudine. Aveva compiuto, da buon Italiano, il proprio dovere... fidava che, uscito di là dentro, noi sapremmo compiere il nostro, sostenendo, anche sotto giuramento, di non averlo mai neppur visto.

Nella mia mente, il fortuito incontro con l'ufficiale austriaco e quanto mi aveva narrato Giustiniano, si collegarono spontaneamente. Se la Polizia mi vuole o vivo o morto, avrà comunicato i miei connotati alle sue ramificazioni dirette e indirette. E se l'ufficiale, spiegando ai commilitoni la propria assenza, ha parlato di me...

Il filo era tenue; ma sarebbe bastato ad intrecciare un capestro... il mio!

Mi rivolsi a Giustiniano: "Assicura la mamma che stasera non dormirò in casa di Domenico. Domani, all'albeggiare, sarò lontano."

Il sole era appena tramontato ed io già cercavo rifugio nell'antica spelonca, d'accanto alla quale non passavo, fanciullo, senza un senso di profonda apprensione. Vi entrai prudentemente, senza lume; poi rimisi al posto l'edera che ne celava la bocca. Da quel nascondiglio, spiando attraverso il fogliame, mi trovai di fronte la finestra, senza griglie e senza imposte, della cucina di Domenico; la quale si spiegò ai miei occhi distintamente, come se vi fossi. Domenico, in quel momento, affettava del cacio... egli preparava il mio viatico pel pellegrinaggio della domane. La Caterina sedeva in una nicchia del focolare e sgranava la corona del rosario.

D'improvviso i cani abbaiarono. Non era cosa insolita. La sera, le volpi vagano per la montagna e i bracchi, che le fiutano da lontano, con incessanti giri e rigiri, le tengono in soggezione. Senonché, questa volta, al loro abbaiare, fattosi a mano a mano più rabbioso, successero i guaiti e, circostanza estremamente sospetta, invece di scorrere pei campi, essi ripiegavano verso la casa. Avevano dunque di fronte un avversario capace di incutere rispetto.

Tesi l'orecchio e non tardai a notare un calpestio cadenzato, un cozzare secco di metalli; poi... tra i miei occhi e la finestra luminosa, sfilarono in punta di piedi parecchi fantasmi. Pennacchi e carabine cancellarono dal mio animo sin l'ultime tracce del dubbio... gli sgherri dell'Austria avevano circondato la casa.

Dentro, le due oneste creature si mostravano ignare affatto della tempesta che si era addensata attorno al vecchio nido. A un tratto, i manigoldi calarono il calcio del fucile sul fragile uscio che con gran fracasso si spalancò. Vidi Domenico dare un balzo a ritroso e istintivamente la sua destra, armata del coltellaccio, si ritrasse per acquistare lo slancio necessario a colpire. Ma quattro fucili spianati gli scacciarono, collo spavento della realtà, quello messogli in corpo dall'immaginazione.

Uno spilungone, dai baffi tirati a punta e il muso da mangiacandele, fece un gesto e due gendarmi si lanciarono su quello sbigottito, lo ammanettarono, lo legarono ad una gamba del tavolo.

Nessuno aveva parlato. Lo spilungone strappò la lanterna cieca ad un soldato, ne riverberò la luce all'ingiro e accortosi di Caterina che, lasciata cadere ai suoi piedi la corona del rosario era rimasta come paralizzata, le disse: "Alzati ed accompagnami!" Ella non rimise della sua rigidezza e parve non comprendesse, sinché lo spilungone, afferratala per una spalla, la scosse, la fece alzare e le assestò, tra scapola e scapola, un pugno poderoso. Vidi distintamente Domenico digrignare i denti.

Lo spilungone, un sergente, si dileguò per l'uscio di fronte, preceduto dalla Caterina e seguito da quattro dei suoi uomini. Lo udii dar l'ordine di cacciar quattro once di piombo nello stomaco a chiunque tentasse fuggire: poi un rumore di passi pesanti sulla scaletta di legno; poi un rimuovere sordo di mobili, uno sbattere di cassetti sul mattonato. Frugarono in ogni stamberga, scesero persino in cantina.

Quando ricomparve a capo de' suoi, il sergente si asciugava, col rovescio della mano, i baffi a punta... si era industriato di rendere meno tediosa la sua perquisizione spillando da qualche botte. Ma evidentemente il vino non aveva fatto generoso il suo cuore. Afferrò una sedia e piantatela presso il prigioniero, vi si pose risolutamente a cavalcioni.

Incominciò a parlare con una calma ipocrita e così sottovoce che, or sì or no, mi giungevano le parole. Udii a intervalli, in un italiano infarcito di dialetto veneto ed incrostato di sali croati, queste frasi anche troppo significative: "Non ci siamo arrampicati sin qui pel gusto di lasciarci infinocchiare. — Il sorcio c'è e Sua Maestà l'Imperatore e Re... — el garon o un falò de la tua casa e di to straèsi. — La segreta, el baston sul... I ferri corti e la forca!"

Dopo quest'ultima frase, appoggiò il pugno allo schienale della sedia, il mento nella fossetta del pugno e stette in attesa. Domenico non mosse palpebra.

Il sergente gridò: "Orsù! bisogna farla bella!" E trinciò nell'aria uno di quei suoi gesti che avevano diritto d'infondere un impeto di vita negli automi impalati che gli stavano alle spalle.

Domenico venne slegato e tratto, coi pollici avvinghiati dietro le reni, in mezzo alla cucina. Io men che non si dica, uno sgherro gli calò un nodo scorsoio attorno al collo; un altro sgherro gettò l'altro capo della corda a cavalcioni d'un trave. Nel frattempo il sergente, ridiventato estremamente calmo, si era industriato di accendere uno sigaro e, tormentandone la posta tra le dita e soffiando, ad un tempo, parole e fumo, disse quasi sillabando: "O canta... o ti appendo come una salsiccia!"

Il montanaro impallidì. Tuttavia la sua faccia, comune se non volgare, apparve trasfigurata da una nobile fierezza. Disse: "Hai torto. Non c'è nessuno qui. Ma sei il più forte... fa quello che ti piace."

Aveva appena finito, quando due manigoldi, afferrato il capo della corda, lo tirarono su lentamente, lentamente. Le braccia del poverino tentarono, con uno sforzo supremo, di svincolarsi, il petto gli si gonfiò orribilmente, la faccia divenne livida. Quando la lingua già gli spuntava dalla bocca storta, d'un tratto lo lasciarono precipitare. Gli zoccoli batterono violentemente al suolo e le ginocchia gli si piegarono sotto, come se egli fosse un fantoccio di stracci. Il laccio venne subito allentato.

Successe un breve silenzio, durante il quale la faccia di Domenico divenne di un pallore cadaverico.

"Che te ne pare della mia medicina? T'ha rinsavito? Sei tu ora disposto a palesarmi la verità?"

Domenico fece col capo un cenno affermativo.

"Alla buon'ora!" continuò il sergente. "Dategli un sorso d'acqua." E soffiò una boccata di fumo lunga e sottile — Era un modo sbrigativo per esprimere la sua intera soddisfazione.

Ma Domenico si raddrizzò e fattosi innanzi, per quanto gli fu concesso dagli sgherri che trattenevano la corda, ringhiò a denti stretti: "A te, a quante carogne hanno insozzato il mio Paese!" E lanciò un largo sputo sul petto del suo sgozzino.

Questi ebbe uno scatto spasmodico ed uno scroscio di bestemmie esotiche. Ma con uno sforzo supremo, contenne l'ira intensa e la dissimulò colla lentezza del gesto e col tono glaciale. Disse: "Impiccatemi quel rospo!"

Il capo della corda venne tirato lentamente, lentamente...

Dalla mia spelonca osservavo e meditavo. Più volte avevo tormentato il calcio della pistola, ma una voce interna, imperiosa m'aveva gridato: "Non precipitare!" Un cadavere, due, una strepitosa vendetta... a qual pro? Decisi di giocare disperatamente l'ultima carta solo quando la partita fosse irremmissibilmente perduta. Il mio dolore per la tortura di Domenico era colmo di apprensione. Conoscevo la fibra del fiero vecchio e intuivo che non mi tradirebbe. Ma Caterina?.. l'orribile spettacolo, la certezza che una sola parola poteva scongiurare l'atroce agonia... Come avrebbe resistito il suo cuore di donna, il suo cuore di compagna?

Caterina corse forsennata dall'una all'altra parete; invocò, agitando le braccia, la Vergine ed i santi. E quando, sopraccolta da repentino furore, fece atto di lanciarsi, colle unghie, sugli impassibili tormentatori e il braccio ferreo di un gendarme l'ebbe respinta, quasi atterrata, ella stridendo si strappò, a ciocche, i capelli. Ma il segreto custodito nel profondo del petto, più la tempesta dell'animo infuria e più si avvinghia alle carni e nel profondo rimase.

Domenico venne, per la seconda volta, calato. Era tramortito così che le domande, rinnovate, giunsero probabilmente troppo indistinte al suo cervello perché egli potesse rispondere. Il sergente chiamò a consulta i suoi sgherri. Devono aver concluso che il montanaro nulla sapeva, che la Polizia regia e imperiale aveva preso un granchio. Nell'aria tranquilla risonarono ordini; nella cucina, attorno al casolare si udirono i passi cadenzati della "forza armata" che si disponeva alla partenza. Pochi minuti e tutto era silenzio...

Domenico volse all'ingiro i suoi occhi indagatori e il suo petto si gonfiò di sospiri. Poi si rizzò, guardò la sua compagna con uno sguardo lungo, intenso e le accarezzò la testa. Poco dopo, ripreso il coltello, ritornava ad affettare il suo cacio, come se nulla di straordinario fosse accaduto. Caterina raccolse, d'accanto al focolare, la corona del rosario e riprincipiò a sgranarlo... questa volta in ginocchio.

Lasciai passare tanto tempo quanto poteva bastare ad infondermi la certezza che la pesta maledetta fosse realmente giù per la china; poi uscii d'infra l'edera e comparvi sulla soglia. Caterina non si volse neppure. Domenico si accontentò di aprire lo sparato della camicia e di additarmi il cerchio incisogli dal capestro. Lo strinsi tra le mie braccia e cercando colle mie labbra quel solco livido, ripetutamente glie lo baciai.

Quanto sto per narrare ricavai dalle lettere scrittemi da Giustiniano. Io le ho impresse nella memoria a caratteri indelebili.

A rassicurare la mamma, Giustiniano le tratteggiava la situazione stemperandovi buona dose d'ottimismo. Luisa subirebbe qualche interrogatorio, ma verrebbe presto rimessa in libertà. Papà era in odore di vecchio patriota, di uomo irriducibile e non avrebbe potuto cavarsela tanto facilmente. Ma come condannarlo senza prove? Le autorità giudiziarie erano rigorose oltre ogni dire; ma ci tenevano a parere integerrime e non oserebbero commettere una furfanteria palese. Quanto a me, egli stesso mi aveva accompagnato sino alle miniere di Agordo. Da quel punto, avevo scelto vie che io solo e i camosci sapevamo percorrere. Già dovevo essere arrivato oltre i confini e la Polizia mi credeva ancora a Belluno ed a Belluno mi cercava. Fra breve, avrebbero ricevuto mie lettere dalla Svizzera, o da qualche altro paese ove il giallo e nero non sventolava. Tutto stava dunque a non accasciarsi, per poter conservare la salute, tanto necessaria in quella burrasca.

Ma la sicurezza che egli si industriava di istillare nell'animo della mamma, era ben lungi dall'essere nel suo. I giornali, soggetti a rigorosa censura, parevano concordi nel trattare l'ultima congiura colla congiura del silenzio. Ma le notizie sanno fare di molto cammino anche senza l'aiuto dei giornali. Ora dalle labbra dei fidati, in cui si imbatteva lungo la via; ora al caffè, d'attorno al tavolino, tra l'una e l'altra frase sul bel tempo, o sul prezzo dei prodotti agricoli, scivolavano, nel suo orecchio, le parole che non potevano essere apertamente pronunciate senza pericolo, e da esse, come sempre in simili casi, i lieti affidamenti apparivano delineati appena; fortemente pennellate le sinistre previsioni.

Una sera, queste parve avessero, dai fatti, un tragico scioglimento. "Tra le gole dell'Alpi, una pattuglia aveva sbarrato il sentiero al patriota fuggitivo... l'intimazione d'arrendersi messa in non cale... il nobile cuore spezzato dal piombo austriaco... la serie degli impeti generosi troncata per sempre. Quella notte stessa, il cadavere giungerebbe a Belluno per le constatazioni di Legge."

Giustiniano si affrettò a rincasare, per impedire che la triste nuova giungesse alla mamma da bocca malcauta. La trovò coricata. Svegliarla? a qual pro? Avrebbe avuto tempo anche soverchio per piangere. Passeggiò in lungo e in largo la camera sino ad ora tardissima; poi, vinto dalla stanchezza, si lasciò cadere su una poltrona e si addormentò.

Dalle imposte socchiuse, la prima luce del crepuscolo spiava appena e già la mamma, impaziente di informazioni, gli portava la consueta tazza di caffè. Lo vide e, indovinando la notte insonne ed angosciosa, invece di svegliarlo, si fece al letto intatto, ne tolse una coltre e glie la stese sulle ginocchia e sul petto. Proprio in quella, il campanello suonò fortissimamente.

Giustiniano si svegliò di soprassalto, vide, si rammentò, comprese. Egli gettò le braccia al collo della poverina e le disse: "Mia povera mamma, sii forte e preparati al peggio."

Partirono in carrozza, un gendarme a cassetta, un ispettore ai fianchi. Non attacchi d'isterismo, non singhiozzi, non lagrime silenziose. Giustiniano teneva stretta la destra all'addolorata che non gli badava, che restava immobile, coll'occhio fisso a qualche punto lontano, percettibile a lei sola.

Fu condotta in una camera ampia, fredda. ## Chapter Nineteen

IN THE EFFICACY of any oratorical passage in stirring noble impulses, there is nothing that reaches the heart of a son like a mother's sobbing. My mother's sobs, in the silence of the night, resounded with terrible force.

I hastened to support her with one arm and placed my right hand over her lips as if to stifle them. "Calm yourself, calm yourself!" I wished to say, but the voice would not come and, overcome by a surge of tenderness, I too broke into sobs.

The first to utter a coherent syllable was my brother Giustiniano, who had remained to one side, almost unnoticed. He said: "Bitter news. Our friends, the best of them, have been arrested; don Bastiano has been arrested as well. Let us reckon the sum: poor Achille has left his bones at Venice; you are beyond doubt being sought by the Police."

And continuing, he recounted the particulars of the catastrophe. Don Bastiano had calmly held out his wrists for the manacles, "happy to contribute, through his own sacrifice, to completing the series of sufferings required by Providence for the redemption of his Country." But father had wished to break his bamboo cane over the shoulders of the ruffians, and the caresses of his family had with difficulty managed to calm him.

I reflected for a moment and concluded: "For now the important thing is to bring mother home. We shall think of the rest afterwards."

But the poor woman, shattered by that terrible ordeal and rendered still more helpless by the darkness, stumbled at every step. I handed one of my pistols to Giustiniano and murmured in his ear: "Go ahead as scout. Think of whom you are defending... one does not tread upon the toes of the di Rudio with impunity. Do you take my meaning?"

I stooped and, before my mother had time to notice and protest, had lifted her in my arms and pressed forward, proud to carry the one who had once carried me in her womb and with far greater travail.

At the first uncertain light of the street-lamps, I set the saintly woman down and, embracing her, pressed a long kiss upon her brow. I did not foresee that it would be the last! "Until we meet again, mamma!" And I promised her that, however adversely fortune might show herself, I would always conduct myself in such a manner that, when we met again, or when thinking of her love, my conscience would not torment me with the sense of being unworthy of it.

In the spot where we had halted, Domenico was building a house. Those beams and bricks stacked along the road offered me an excellent screen. I told Giustiniano that I would await him at that place. He was to bring back my weapon. Should he discover any fresh news, he would inform me. My intentions? He would know them upon his return.

I did not wait long. Mother and Giustiniano had reached home without encountering a living soul. My sister Luisa had prepared a good cup of coffee for her; the poor woman appeared, if not resigned, at least calm.

"And now to our business!" added Giustiniano. "One does not tread upon the toes of the di Rudio with impunity... it is your maxim; but it is mine as well! Let us reckon the sum: poor Achille has left his bones at Venice; you are beyond doubt being sought by the Police, hunted like a criminal through the streets of the night." This thought pressed itself deep into my mind; my spirit at last came to mirror the picturesque majesty of the mountain.

I recognised Domenico's little cottage, leaning against the cliff, its dark doorway beneath the pergola, bare now of grapes and leaves — the pergola that stretched as far as the road and divided the little kitchen-garden plot into equal halves. From the foot of the old walnut tree whose trembling fronds tickled the roof of the dwelling, two hounds launched themselves forth barking; but they soon checked their ardour upon hearing themselves called by name. They followed in my footsteps, stretching out their muzzles and anxiously sniffing at my calves.

One, two, three blows of the knocker, at decent intervals... sleep clings tenaciously to the labourers of the mountain. A fourth, a fifth resounded in the void, and at last through the cracks of the old door, light filtered through; two heavy clogs advanced noisily.

"Open, Domenico. It is Carletto."

The door creaked, and I remained as if dazzled by the lantern which, with a jolt, was raised from the ground and sent a flash of light into my face.

"Carletto!.. taller, more proud-looking, more bronzed, with a beard... but Carletto! And I had taken you for the other end of the world! The old folk were right: only the mountains stay in their place. But how come you are here at this hour! Did you lose your way on the slope?"

I did not answer, but gesturing toward the right hand of the good old man, which gripped a billhook: "You meant to cut off my head!"

In the kitchen with its smoke-blackened walls, beneath the wide chimney-hood, a fire crackled up soon enough to have roasted a kid. We sat facing one another in the lateral niches carved into the thick wall, and while I laid out the situation plainly for him, he tormented the embers with the ends of a reed-handled bellows. When he heard that father was in chains, he drove that iron implement with a formidable thrust into the log, which sent up a shower of sparks. From the clenching of his jaws and the fury in his face, I would have said the blow was intentionally aimed at the head of some Croatian. He said:

"You did well to come up here. From our height, one commands the valley for more than five miles. They have barely set foot outside Belluno and we already know where they would like to cast their net. With five miles' advantage, there is no candle-chewing foreigner that can outrun Italian legs; leaving aside altogether that the mountain is scattered all about with our own people. And should the devil help them and they chance upon us unawares... there is a remedy for that too! Do you remember the old cavern, where I used to put you in punishment when you ruined my almond trees! A single leap from the house, the entrance narrow and so well-covered that you would not notice it even if you knocked your nose against it. In a desperate case, one man alone with an old musket, a rusted contraption, could make half Bohemia wish it had never been born. There is no denying it, you showed good sense in taking refuge up here."

He paused a moment as if suspended in thought, then added: "I am dithering and you need rest. I shall run and rouse the old woman. Half an hour more, half an hour less, what does it matter? She has to prepare the calf-feed for the cow that is still with calf anyway. For this once, you must resign yourself to sleeping in our bed... we shall think later of making up something a bit more fit for a Christian."

And as I protested that I was broken to far harder fatigues; that I preferred to keep watch by the fire, he assumed the air of one touched on the quick and said: "And why not? In our bed... but have no fear!... We shall put on freshly-laundered sheets!"

That very day, in the afternoon, Giustiniano appeared up there. He had left the city on the pretext of going hunting for finches and goldfinches; he had with him the decoy-owl, the perch-pole, and the bird-lime. Nothing suspicious about his long circuit; but the news he brought was very gloomy. A raid by the police, a search in full form; mother in a terrible state of agitation... all the more so because Luisa was keeping a highly compromising letter. Fortunato Calvi had for Luisa a sincere admiration; a friendship that led him to confide to her, as to a sister, his most intimate thoughts. In those days he had sent his instructions to father, addressing them, the better to deceive the Police, to Luisa herself. They were written in veiled language, but not so veiled that they would fail to arouse the suspicions of one who already had some inkling of our plot. Moreover, yielding to the impulses of his heart, Calvi had, in a brief postscript, expressed the hope that, "the Fatherland once liberated, nothing more would stand in the way of his union with Teresa Duodo," a gentle young lady from Padova who adored him, and was adored by him in return.

Fortunately my sister, seizing the moment, swiftly thrust the letter into father's guitar — a great plucker of strings, father was! And the searchers, who had rummaged through every corner of the house and every nook, had taken no notice of that humble instrument, hanging upon the wall.

Already they were preparing to take their leave, already the two women had begun to breathe freely, when the inspector, who had beyond doubt received peremptory orders, placed Luisa under arrest... A long embrace... then mother had fallen back into the armchair, in the grip of a terrible nervous seizure.

On hearing that news, it seemed to me that the heavy air of the plain had climbed up to the mountain and was suffocating me. To what end linger any longer in the Cadore? I was held back by a chivalrous feeling that made me regard it as cowardice to seek my own safety while my loved ones were persecuted and exposed to every danger; I was held back also by the hope, however vague and fleeting, that Fortunato Calvi and Giuseppe Mazzini, having learned of the ill turn taken by their plans, would exert themselves to set them right and might have need of me. But events, in their headlong course, were soon enough to blow away even those scruples.

One morning I was walking a short distance from the farmstead and, by instinct, studying the terrain with a tactical eye, when, rounding a crag, I found myself face to face with an Austrian officer. I do not think he noticed my agitation, for he greeted me in a manner of faultless courtesy and told me he had set out from Belluno, with other officers, before sunrise. He had gone astray in pursuing a covey of partridges. He then asked me whether I had seen his hunting companions pass through those parts.

Having received a negative reply, he offered me a cigar and then, with a certain surprise: "You live in these solitary places? One needs a certain genius to avoid being bored by them!" Before I could answer, several rifle-shots echoed from crag to crag. He turned his thumb back and said: "Those are my friends!" He saluted and moved off with long strides.

A few hours later, Giustiniano arrived up there. I understood, from the mere sight of him, that some other bad news was pressing upon his heart. He told me that, early that morning, a clerk of the Police, in whom the trade had not deadened every honestly sensitive fibre, had knocked at our door. He confided to mother that positive word had reached his office that I was to be found in Belluno, or in the surrounding area, and for purposes that could not be welcome to the royal imperial authorities. These had issued a peremptory order to seize me, alive or dead. We were to govern ourselves accordingly and with dispatch. He had done his duty as a good Italian... he trusted that, once he was out of that office, we would know how to do ours, maintaining, even under oath, that we had never so much as laid eyes on him.

In my mind, the chance encounter with the Austrian officer and what Giustiniano had told me connected themselves spontaneously. If the Police want me alive or dead, they will have circulated my description to their direct and indirect ramifications. And if the officer, in explaining his absence to his fellow-soldiers, has spoken of me...

The thread was slender; but it would have been enough to braid a noose... my own!

I turned to Giustiniano: "Assure mother that tonight I shall not sleep in Domenico's house. By dawn tomorrow, I shall be far away."

The sun had barely set and I was already seeking refuge in the ancient cavern, past which I had never walked as a child without a feeling of profound apprehension. I entered it cautiously, without a light; then replaced the ivy that concealed its mouth. From that hiding-place, watching through the foliage, I found myself directly facing the window of Domenico's kitchen, which was unbarred and unshuttered; it lay open before my eyes as clearly as if I had been inside. Domenico was at that moment cutting slices of cheese... he was preparing my *viatico* — provisions for the pilgrimage of the morrow. Caterina sat in one of the chimney-niches telling the beads of her rosary.

All at once the dogs barked. This was not unusual. In the evening, foxes roam the mountain and the hounds, scenting them from afar, with their ceaseless circlings keep them in check. On this occasion, however, their barking, growing ever more furious, gave way to whimpering and — a circumstance in the highest degree suspicious — instead of coursing through the fields, they were falling back toward the house. They had before them, then, an adversary capable of commanding respect.

I strained my ears and was not long in detecting a measured footfall, a sharp clank of metal; then... between my eyes and the lit window, several phantoms filed past on tiptoe. Plumes and carbines erased from my mind even the last traces of doubt... the henchmen of Austria had surrounded the house.

Within, the two honest souls gave every appearance of being utterly unaware of the storm that had gathered about the old nest. All at once, the villains brought the butt of a rifle down on the frail door, which flew open with a great crash. I saw Domenico leap back and instinctively his right hand, armed with the large knife, drew back to gather the impetus needed to strike. But four levelled rifles drove away, with the fright of reality, that which his imagination had planted in him.

A lanky man, with waxed pointed moustaches and the face of a candle-chewing foreigner, made a gesture and two gendarmes threw themselves upon the bewildered man, manacled him, and bound him to a leg of the table.

Not a word had been spoken. The lanky man snatched the dark-lantern from a soldier, cast its light around, and catching sight of Caterina who, having let her rosary fall at her feet, had remained as if paralysed, said to her: "Get up and come with me!" She did not relax her rigidity and seemed not to understand, until the lanky man, seizing her by the shoulder, shook her, forced her to her feet and dealt her a powerful blow between the shoulder-blades. I could see Domenico grind his teeth distinctly.

The lanky man, a sergeant, disappeared through the opposite door, preceded by Caterina and followed by four of his men. I heard him give the order to put four ounces of lead in the stomach of anyone who attempted to flee; then a noise of heavy footsteps on the wooden staircase; then a dull shifting of furniture, a banging of drawers upon the flagstones. They searched every room, went even down to the cellar.

When the sergeant reappeared at the head of his men, he was wiping his pointed moustaches with the back of his hand... he had taken the trouble to make his search less tedious by drawing from some cask. But evidently the wine had not made his heart generous. He seized a chair, planted it near the prisoner, and straddled it resolutely.

He began to speak with a hypocritical calm and so quietly that only here and there his words reached me. I heard at intervals, in an Italian larded with Venetian dialect and encrusted with Croatian saltiness, these phrases all too full of meaning: "We haven't scrambled all the way up here for the pleasure of being made fools of. — The mouse is here and His Majesty the Emperor and King... — *el garon o un falò de la tua casa e di to straèsi* [the gallows or a bonfire of your house and your rags]. — The dungeon, the rod on... Short irons and the gallows!"

After this last phrase, he rested his fist on the chair-back, his chin in the hollow of his fist, and waited. Domenico did not move an eyelid.

The sergeant cried: "Come now! We must have it out!" And he cut through the air with one of those gestures of his that had the right to instil a burst of life into the ramrod-stiff automata standing at his back.

Domenico was untied and hauled, with his thumbs lashed behind his back, into the middle of the kitchen. Before one could say a word, a ruffian dropped a running noose around his neck; another ruffian threw the other end of the rope over a beam. Meanwhile the sergeant, having regained perfect calm, had been at pains to light a cigar and, worrying its tip between his fingers and blowing words and smoke in equal measure, said, almost syllable by syllable: "Either you sing... or I hang you up like a sausage!"

The mountain man turned pale. Nevertheless his face, plain if not coarse, appeared transfigured by a noble fieriness. He said: "You are mistaken. There is no one here. But you are the stronger... do as you please."

He had barely finished when two ruffians, having seized the end of the rope, drew it up slowly, slowly. The poor man's arms strove with a supreme effort to wrench free, his chest swelled horribly, his face turned livid. When his tongue was already protruding from his contorted mouth, they suddenly let him drop. His clogs struck the floor with violence and his knees buckled under him as though he were a rag puppet. The noose was immediately slackened.

A brief silence followed, during which Domenico's face became of a cadaverous pallor.

"What do you think of my medicine? Has it brought you to your senses? Are you now prepared to reveal the truth to me?"

Domenico gave an affirmative nod of his head.

"That is more like it!" continued the sergeant. "Give him a mouthful of water." And he blew a long, thin mouthful of smoke — it was his expeditious way of expressing his entire satisfaction.

But Domenico drew himself up and, pressing forward as far as the ruffians holding the rope permitted, snarled through clenched teeth: "At you, and at every carrion that has defiled my Country!" And he launched a broad spit upon his torturer's chest.

The latter gave a spasmodic start and a torrent of exotic blasphemies. But with a supreme effort he contained his fierce anger and dissimulated it with the slowness of his gestures and an icy tone. He said: "Hang me that toad!"

The end of the rope was drawn up slowly, slowly...

From my cavern I watched and deliberated. More than once I had tormented the butt of my pistol, but an inward voice, imperious, had cried to me: "Do not be rash!" One corpse, two, a resounding act of vengeance... to what purpose? I resolved to play the last card in desperation only when the game was irretrievably lost. My anguish at Domenico's torment was filled with apprehension. I knew the fibre of the proud old man and intuited that he would not betray me. But Caterina?.. the horrible spectacle, the certainty that a single word could avert that atrocious agony... How would her woman's heart withstand it — the heart of his companion?

Caterina ran frantically from one wall to the other; she invoked the Virgin and the saints, throwing her arms wide. And when, seized by a sudden fury, she made as if to fling herself, nails first, upon the impassive tormentors and the iron arm of a gendarme had thrust her back, nearly knocking her to the ground, she screamed and tore her hair out in handfuls. But the secret kept fast in the depths of her breast — the more the tempest of the soul rages, the more it clings to the flesh — and in the depths it remained.

Domenico was lowered for the second time. He was so utterly stunned that the renewed questions must have reached his brain too indistinctly for him to be able to reply. The sergeant called his ruffians to a brief council. They must have concluded that the mountain man knew nothing, that the royal and imperial Police had taken hold of the wrong end of the stick. In the still air, orders rang out; around the kitchen and the farmstead, one heard the measured footsteps of the "armed force" disposing itself for departure. A few minutes and all was silence...

Domenico turned his searching eyes about the room and his chest swelled with sighs. Then he raised himself, looked at his companion with a long, intent gaze and stroked her head. A little later, having taken up his knife again, he returned to cutting his cheese, as though nothing out of the ordinary had occurred. Caterina gathered up the rosary from beside the hearth and began telling it again... this time upon her knees.

I allowed as much time to pass as was needed to give me certainty that the accursed troop was truly making its way down the slope; then I stepped out from under the ivy and appeared upon the threshold. Caterina did not even turn. Domenico was content to open the front of his shirt and show me the ring the noose had carved into his neck. I clasped him in my arms and, seeking those livid furrows with my lips, kissed them again and again.

What I am about to recount I have drawn from the letters written to me by Giustiniano. They are impressed upon my memory in indelible characters.

In order to reassure mother, Giustiniano sketched the situation for her, tempering it with a good measure of optimism. Luisa would undergo a few interrogations, but would soon be released. Father was known as an old patriot, a man of unbending temper, and would not be able to extricate himself quite so easily. But how were they to condemn him without evidence? The judicial authorities were severe beyond measure; but they set great store by appearing beyond reproach, and would not dare commit an openly brazen iniquity. As for myself, he had personally accompanied me as far as the mines at Agordo. From that point, I had taken paths that only I and the chamois knew how to travel. I must by now have arrived beyond the frontier, and the Police believed me still in Belluno and were searching for me there. Before long, they would receive letters from me from Switzerland, or from some other country over which the black and yellow did not fly. Everything therefore depended on not losing heart, so as to preserve their health, so necessary in that storm.

But the assurance he was at pains to instil into mother's heart was far from dwelling in his own. The newspapers, subject to rigorous censorship, seemed unanimous in treating the latest conspiracy with the conspiracy of silence. But news knows how to travel a great distance even without the aid of newspapers. Now from the lips of trusted men he encountered along the way, now at the café, gathered about a table, between one remark about the fine weather and another about the price of farm produce, words that could not be spoken openly without danger would slip into his ear, and from them, as always in such cases, the cheerful assurances appeared barely sketched in, while the sinister forecasts were painted in heavy strokes.

One evening these seemed to have received a tragic confirmation from events. "Among the Alpine gorges, a patrol had barred the path of the fleeing patriot... the order to surrender disregarded... that noble heart shattered by Austrian lead... the series of generous impulses cut short for ever. That very night, the body would arrive in Belluno for the legal formalities."

Giustiniano hastened home to prevent the grim news from reaching mother through some careless mouth. He found her already retired to bed. Wake her? To what end? She would have more than enough time to weep. He paced the room back and forth until very late; then, overcome by fatigue, let himself fall into an armchair and fell asleep.

Through the half-closed shutters, the first light of dawn was barely peering in, when mother, impatient for news, brought him his customary cup of coffee. She looked at him and, divining the sleepless and anguished night, instead of waking him, went to the untouched bed, took a blanket from it and spread it over his knees and chest. Just then, the doorbell rang with great violence.

Giustiniano awoke with a start, looked, remembered, understood. He threw his arms about the poor woman's neck and said to her: "My poor mamma, be strong and prepare yourself for the worst."

They departed in a carriage, a gendarme on the box, an inspector at their side. No hysterical attacks, no sobbing, no silent tears. Giustiniano held tightly to the right hand of the grieving woman, who paid him no heed, who remained motionless, her gaze fixed upon some distant point visible to her alone.

She was conducted into a large, cold chamber. Detached from the opposite wall was a board, one end resting on a trestle; upon the board a sheet was laid; beneath the sheet, with a sinister uncertainty, the forms of a man in the rigidity of death were outlined. A soldier, rifle at the order, stood stiffly at his side. Another, in his shirtsleeves, cuffs rolled back, his left hand plunged deep into a large boot upon which his eyes were intently fixed, was furiously working the brush and polishing it. Four officers, arranged in a circle, were conferring in low voices. A glowing brazier adulterated the limpid morning light with its flickering.

At the mother's entrance, the officers broke off their conversation and turned their faces toward that severe apparition. She seemed not to hear; her whole spirit was concentrated in the eyes that blazed beneath the black veil.

The soldier in shirtsleeves blew warm breath onto the boot and drove the brush down with blows more furious than ever... perhaps at that moment he wished that the barracks and the Empire were concentrated in that muddy toecap. The soldier standing stiffly beside the corpse did not venture to draw breath.

One of the officers stepped forward, drew back the sheet, perhaps with a sudden gesture. Then a sob resounded in the sepulchral silence, one only!.. but it was enough to reveal the depth of a heart.

I had to exert myself to calm that youthful fever and with a voice in which the muffled threat of an entire people vibrated, I said: "No, this is not a small matter. I have no intention of leaving the neighbourhood of Belluno until I have seen clearly into the heart of these entanglements. And where find a more trustworthy person than yourself, to serve me as informant and, if need be, as intermediary?"

"Come then!" I added, to declare his triumph. She moved toward the exit. The soldier in shirtsleeves blew warm breath onto the boot and drove the brush down with blows more furious than ever... perhaps at that moment he wished that the barracks and the Empire were concentrated in that muddy toecap. The soldier standing stiffly beside the corpse did not venture to draw breath. The situation was uniform, awaiting a command that admits of no delay. "I choose Domenico's house as my refuge. That peasant has the right stuff in him and will not betray us. I expect you tomorrow. Come by a wide circuit and make certain no one follows you. I shall judge by your cunning whether you are worthy of greater enterprises."

And having pressed his hand, I turned my back and left the shadowy plain. In the fields, in the brisk open air, I rediscovered that purity of a glittering sky which lends strength in the face of fierce emotions. I had not set down any burden along the climb; yet my weariness had suddenly vanished. I mounted with great strides and with every step the serenity of the night pressed itself deep into my thoughts; my spirit at last came to mirror the picturesque majesty of the mountain.

CHAPTER EIGHTEEN

I touched Coirà, where from Clementi, who found himself at that time under trial as one implicated in the international risings, I received a letter for his younger brother. The latter would, in his turn, have seen to putting me in communication with the Burrasca brothers.

At San Maurizio, in the Engadine, at Muentzen, the point of contact between Switzerland and Württemberg and the Tyrol, at Salzburg, at Trento — no suspicious incident. At this last place I presented myself to the Austrian Authorities and signed, under the assumed name, my passport... they did not cast upon me so much as a single searching glance.

And from Trento, deviating from the direct route, I ascend again the valley furrowed by the torrent Trevis, meet with the younger Clementi; and that very same evening find myself face to face with the four smugglers.

Of them Clementi had told me that they were the despair of the man-hunters and the pride of the valley. Jealous of their own reputation, they left to lesser men the easy enterprises and made it a point of honour to bring to completion the most hazardous. Spirits intolerant of every restraint, they lent themselves nobly also to righting wrongs, to fighting for the liberty of others. They had already rendered services of no small account to the Italian cause.

I had no need of masterly phrases to bend them to my purpose, and when the eldest of the brothers, the captain of the small and courageous band, set down with energy the glass from which he had been sipping, stretched out his right hand and gripped mine, I understood that I had concluded, with that simple act, a pact for life and death, far more binding than any notarial document.

Early in the morning, having refused every guide, I set out on foot, by little-trodden paths which traced themselves, little by little, vividly in my memory before they spread out before my eyes. In the vast horizons, in the sudden precipices, in the solemn silences, in the alpine fragrances I recovered my boyhood and was so absorbed in it that the phantoms of the future grew pale and almost vanished away.

When, from the height, I descried my Belluno, the rain was falling in heavy drops; but serenity was in my heart. I stood, beneath my ample umbrella, seeking out among the indistinct lines the paternal house, and having found it, it seemed to me that the old papa and mamma and all my dear ones must feel me near and trembling, as I felt them.

I reached the bank of the Ardo at dusk; but the autumn rains had swollen it so that finding a ford proved impossible. It was a setback! The house of don Bastiano stood on the opposite side of the city. To reach it I now saw myself compelled to risk the high road, to cross the bridge, to traverse the whole length of Belluno. I waited patiently for the night, hidden in a thicket.

The clocks were striking ten when, with my umbrella lowered over my face, I entered among the dwellings. Few and hurrying were the wayfarers. I scrutinised them from afar, in the uncertain light of the lanterns... not a spy, not an acquaintance, not a living soul who showed any concern for me... I might have thought myself, in the eyes of my fellow-citizens, an invisible spectre. But when the houses thinned and I had left every lantern behind me, a sudden thought came to disturb me: “What if the Police, sharp-eyed and feline, had laid a trap for me in the very solitary dwelling of don Bastiano?”

I entered the little garden that surrounds it with such precautions that even the mastiffs took no notice. The ground floor... dark; all the shutters closed; those of the upper storey closed also... a single window yawned open upon a gleam of light that was lost among the boughs of a double row of trees. I strained my ear... the voice of the Ardo, a distant rustling of leaves, nothing more! Then I gathered up a small stone and cast it against the window, while with an instinctive movement I gripped the butt of a pistol.

And lo, darkening behind the panes, a woman’s figure... don Bastiano’s serving-maid, handsome and plump, with ample hips and impertinent bosom. She threw open the shutters and whispered into the night: “Is it you, Moretto?”

Don Bastiano received me with an embrace and with sobbing. His first words were: “Praised be heaven! I had already despaired of your safety.”

— If the cruelties of the oppressors can be called news! These past few days the unexpected arrests have followed one upon another. My very house is under constant surveillance. How could you have made your way here without mishap? I have a vague suspicion that you have dropped from the sky!

— Safe... and arrived but a few hours since. But who could vouch for the present? To sleep in one’s own bed has become an arduous problem in these times!

At his request, I related to him what I had seen and done in my rapid journey. And he:

“Providence protects you. So much the greater is your obligation not to abuse its protection. Listen, my son: That I love my country, you know. For her liberty I would give... everything save my soul. But one thing is to sacrifice what is our own, another is to imperil the safety of others, the lives of those we love. It is a tremendous responsibility for which I should have to render account, sooner or later, to One who counts the drops of blood uselessly shed and is not appeased by syllogisms. Heed me: These enterprises succeed... at times, if they burst forth suddenly, like a thunderbolt! But let the enemy, all-powerful as he is, but divine them... Carletto! Carletto! what avail the generous resolves, the daring of four, of thirty, of a hundred young men, against a colossus that holds all Europe in subjection? Would you lay your neck in the noose, thus light-heartedly, for the sterile glory that might come of it? And has not our Italy already too many and too many of her best to mourn and to pity? Reserve your energy for more propitious times. How it shall come about I do not know and could not tell you; but I believe that the Police have some inkling of our plots. They do not arrest at random. You are not safe here. O the times, the times! Friend meets friend and must grieve at it and must conjure him to depart. Austria makes us worse than barbarians!”

At that moment, outside a few paces away, a sharp crack rang out; the mastiffs howled... I blew out the candle.

Moments of terrible anxiety... for don Bastiano. I had gone, on tiptoe, to the door and, opening its peephole, had planted the barrels of my two pistols there. Then, thinking that to remain caged would cut off every avenue of escape, I went out and advanced... Great darkness, great silence. As a further precaution I made the circuit of the house... the wind, a shutter banging... that was all! But when I returned and relit the candle, I was struck by the deathly pallor of don Bastiano.

And I thought again of the fine walks taken, in his company, in autumn, along the Ardo, through the rich vineyards, when he, to educate me in virile sentiments, would recount to me the deeds of our forebears; when his priestly cassock disappeared from my eyes and I pictured him grave in breastplate and helm... so fierce were his words, so intense his enthusiasm!

Don Bastiano, accustomed to reading my thoughts since my earliest years, said: “Do not number me among the cowards. My character as a priest and these grey hairs lay duties upon me. But once the die is cast... upright and serene to the end!”

Early in the morning, while I, wearied from the long journey, still slept soundly, he went to inform papa of my arrival. I found the proud old man still vigorous, though upon his noble face I could discern wrinkles that were unknown to me. But our meeting was other than I had expected. It is probable that don Bastiano had persuaded him to throw into the balance the full weight of paternal authority, to dissuade me from so hazardous an enterprise. No embraces, no kisses; a powerful handshake, but a solitary one, and then at once, sharpened and grown almost sardonic, the same exhortations I had heard the evening before from the lips of the good priest. Our discussion grew so heated as to become most painful to me. I resolved to cut it short. “When the uniform and the bread of thieves grew odious to me,” I said, “I expected neither festivities nor flowers. Passive submission does not befit the spirit of a Rudio. And since you counsel it to me... well then, I shall continue to walk my own path, alone. From this moment I have no longer a father!” The poor old man remained a moment with a gesture suspended, as though he had been suddenly crystallised; then his eyelashes filled with tears. He flung himself forward with a sudden impulse and embraced me and held me close with a strength of which I should not have thought him capable. When he was able to speak: “It is you, it is you Carletto!... I recognise my own blood. Go! So long as my strength holds, I shall be with you!”

It was decided not to unearth the arms until the spirits of the bold men who were to handle them should be prepared and well disposed. Papa and don Bastiano undertook to approach them. In the meantime I should, by way of prudence, seek out a refuge less closely watched by the Police. In the environs of Belluno, towards the north, where the plain borders upon the heights, there stood an ancient chestnut tree, known, and almost celebrated, in the city and throughout the countryside for its extraordinary dimensions. It was agreed that I should repair, under cover of darkness, beneath its boughs. A trusted person would come, towards midnight, to indicate to me where I might lay my head. The recognition signal: three whistles, to which I was to respond with four.

By nine o’clock I was already stretched upon the grass, musing as I contemplated the moon through the dark foliage; then the moon set and the wind resumed its unbridled course. I rose to stretch my limbs and posted myself behind the enormous trunk.

From the tower of Belluno twelve strokes rang out upon the air, one hard upon another, and my eye, now grown accustomed to the darkness, ran searchingly among thickets and vineyards. Another long hour passed... I was seized by dark forebodings.

All at once, softly, timidly, from beyond a clump of bushes, the three whistles followed in succession... then a whisper... “On guard, Moretto! There are several of them!” I gazed intently and discerned, through the twigs, something... like the greatcoat of a spy. That I did not fire then and there was from fear of missing my target and finding myself defenceless before the enemy. I answered the signal and waited with both weapons levelled...

Oh what blood and what remorse does a moment’s hesitation murmur! A shadow advanced with arms outstretched... my mother!
