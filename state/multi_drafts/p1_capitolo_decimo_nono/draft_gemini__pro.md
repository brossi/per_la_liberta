MORE EFFECTIVE than any oratorical outburst in stirring the noble impulses of rebellion, a mother's sobs reach a son's heart. Hers, in the silence of the night, resounded frightfully.

I hastened to support her with one arm and placed my right hand on her lips as if to stifle them. "Calm yourself, calm yourself!" I wanted to say, but my voice failed me and, overcome by a surge of tenderness, I too began to sob.

The first to speak a coherent syllable was my brother Giustiniano, who had remained apart, almost unnoticed. He said: "Sad news. Our friends, the best of them, have been arrested; Don Bastiano has been arrested too. Let us sum it up: poor Achille has left his bones in Venezia; you are undoubtedly wanted by the Police."

And continuing, he recounted to me the particulars of the catastrophe. Don Bastiano had serenely extended his wrists to the manacles, "happy to contribute, with his own sacrifice, to completing the series of sufferings required by Providence for the redemption of his Country." But Papa wanted to break his bamboo cane on the shoulders of the ruffians, and the caresses of his family had scarcely been able to calm him.

I meditated for an instant and concluded: "The important thing now is to get Mamma home. We will think about the rest later."

But the poor woman, broken by the terrible ordeal and rendered even more helpless by the darkness, stumbled at every step. I handed one of my pistols to Giustiniano and murmured in his ear: "You lead the way. Think of whom you are defending... one does not tread on the toes of the di Rudios with impunity. Have you understood me?"

I bent down and, before Mamma had time to realize and react, I had lifted her into my arms and proceeded onward, proud to support the one who had carried me in her womb, and with far greater travail.

At the first, uncertain light of the streetlamps, I set the sainted woman down and, embracing her, I impressed a long kiss upon her forehead. I did not foresee that it would be the last! "Goodbye, Mamma!" And I promised her that, however adverse fortune might prove, I would always act in such a way that, upon seeing her again, or in thinking of her love, my conscience would not sting me with the feeling of being unworthy of it.

In the place where we had paused, a house belonging to a Domenico was being built. Those beams and bricks piled along the road offered me excellent cover. I told Giustiniano that I would wait for him at that spot. He was to bring me back the weapon. If he discovered any news, he was to inform me of it. My intentions? He would know them on his return.

I did not wait long. Mamma and Giustiniano had reached home without running into a living soul. My sister Luisa had prepared her a good cup of coffee; the poor woman seemed, if not resigned, at least calm.

"And now for us!" Giustiniano added. "One does not tread on the toes of the di Rudios with impunity... that is your maxim; but it is mine as well! Let us sum it up: poor Achille has left his bones in Venezia; you are undoubtedly wanted by the Police, pursued like a malefactor through the streets in the night." This insinuated itself deeply into my thoughts; my soul came to mirror the picturesque majesty of the mountain.

I recognized Domenico’s little hovel, pressed against the cliff, the door blackening under the pergola, now bare of grapes and leaves... the pergola that stretched to the road and divided the clearing of the small vegetable garden into equal parts. From the foot of the old walnut tree that, with its trembling fronds, tickled the roof of the shack, two hounds rushed out barking; but they soon checked their impetus upon hearing themselves called by name. They followed my footsteps, stretching out their muzzles and sniffing anxiously at my calves.

One, two, three blows, at a discreet interval... sleep clings tenaciously to the mountain laborers. A fourth, a fifth resounded in the doorway, and finally from the cracks of the old door, light seeped through; two great clogs advanced noisily.

"Open up, Domenico. It's Carletto."

The door creaked and I was as if dazzled by the lantern which, with a leap, rose from the ground and cast a spray of light in my face.

"Carletto!.. taller, prouder, more sunburnt, with the goatee... but Carletto! And I thought him at the ends of the earth! Our old folks were right: only the mountains stay in their place. But how did you get here at this hour! Did you lose your way on the climb?"

I did not answer, but gesturing to the good old man's right hand, which clasped a billhook: "You meant to cut my head off!"

In the kitchen, its walls caked with smoke, a fire large enough to roast a kid goat was soon crackling under the wide hearth. We sat opposite each other in the side niches carved into the thick wall, and while I made the situation plain to him, he tormented the embers with the horns of the pipe-bellows. When he heard that my father was in chains, he dealt the log a formidable blow with that iron tool, and it sparked. From the clenching of his jaw and his anger, I would have said that the blow was intentionally aimed at the head of some Croat. He told me:

"You did well to come up here. From our height, one can survey the valley for over five miles. They barely set foot out of Belluno and we already know where they mean to cast their net. With a five-mile head start, there's no *mangiacandele* (a derogatory term for Austrians) who can outrun an Italian's legs; not to mention that the mountain is scattered all over with our people. And if the devil helps them and they stumble upon us unawares... there is a remedy for that too! Do you remember the old cave, where I sent you for punishment when you ruined my almond trees? A leap from the house, the entrance narrow and so covered that you wouldn't notice it even if you bumped your nose on it. In a desperate case, a single man with an old rifle, a rusty contraption, can ruin the liver of half of Bohemia. There's no denying it, you've shown good sense, taking refuge up here."

He was suspended for a moment, then added: "I am dawdling and you need your rest. I will run and wake the old woman. Half an hour more or less, what does it matter? She has to prepare the mash for the cow that is about to calf anyway. For this once, resign yourself to sleeping in our humble bed... we will see about preparing a bed a bit more fit for a Christian later."

And as I protested that I was inured to far greater hardships, that I preferred to keep watch by the fire, he put on the air of one who was deeply stung and said: "And forgive me: in our humble bed... but do not doubt!... We will put on clean-laundered sheets for you!"

That very day, in the afternoon, Giustiniano turned up. He had left the city on the pretext of going to hunt for finches and goldfinches; he had with him the decoy owl, the stake, and the birdlime. Nothing suspicious in his long tour; but the news he brought was very sad. An invasion by the police, a search in due form; Mamma in a state of terrible agitation... all the more so because Luisa was keeping a very compromising letter. Fortunato Calvi had a sincere admiration for Luisa; a friendship that led him to confide in her, as in a sister, his intimate thoughts. In those days he had sent his instructions to Papa, addressing them, the better to deceive the Police, precisely to Luisa. They were written in veiled language, but not so much that they would not arouse the suspicions of one who already scented our plot. Furthermore, yielding to the impulses of his heart, Calvi, in a brief postscript, expressed the hope that, "once the Fatherland was liberated, nothing more would stand in the way of his union with Teresa Duodo," a gentle young lady from Padova who, adored by him, adored him in return.

Fortunately my sister, seizing the opportunity, quickly threw the letter inside Papa's guitar... a great plucker of the strings, was Papa! And the searchers, who had nevertheless rummaged in every nook of the house and every corner, had not bothered with that humble instrument, hung upon the wall.

They were already preparing to leave, the two women were already beginning to breathe freely, when the inspector, who undoubtedly had peremptory orders, placed Luisa under arrest... A long embrace... then Mamma had fallen back into the armchair, in the grip of a terrible nervous crisis.

Upon hearing this news, it seemed to me that the heavy air of the plain had risen to the mountain and was suffocating me. To what end should I linger any longer in Cadore? I was restrained by a chivalrous sentiment that made me count it cowardice to seek my own safety while my dear ones were persecuted and exposed to every danger; I was also restrained by the hope, however vague and ephemeral, that Fortunato Calvi and Giuseppe Mazzini, upon learning of the bad turn their plans had taken, would strive to set them right and would have need of me. But events, hurtling forward, were soon to blow away even those scruples.

One morning I was walking a short distance from the cottage and, by instinct, was studying the terrain with a tactical eye, when, rounding a bluff, I found myself face to face with an Austrian officer. I do not think he noticed my agitation, because he greeted me in an irreproachably courteous manner and told me that he had left Belluno with other officers before sunrise. He had strayed from his group to pursue a flight of partridges. He asked me, in conclusion, if I had seen his hunting companions pass through those parts.

Receiving a negative reply, he offered me a cigar then, with a certain wonder: "You live in these solitary places? It takes a certain genius not to be bored here!" Before I could answer, several rifle shots echoed from crag to crag. He gestured with his thumb and said: "Those are my friends!" He bid me farewell and walked away with great strides.

A few hours later, Giustiniano arrived. I understood, from the mere sight of him, that some other piece of bad news weighed on his heart. He told me that, early in the morning, a clerk from the Police, in whom the profession had not paralyzed every honestly sensitive fiber, had knocked at our door. He confided to Mamma that his office had received positive information that I was in Belluno, or in its environs, and for purposes that could not be pleasing to the imperial royal authorities. They had issued a peremptory order to seize me, dead or alive. We were to act accordingly, and with haste. He had done his duty as a good Italian... he trusted that, once he was gone from there, we would know how to do ours, maintaining, even under oath, that we had never even seen him.

In my mind, the chance encounter with the Austrian officer and what Giustiniano had told me, connected spontaneously. If the Police want me dead or alive, they will have communicated my description to their direct and indirect branches. And if the officer, explaining his absence to his comrades-in-arms, spoke of me...

The thread was tenuous; but it would have been enough to weave a noose... mine!

I turned to Giustiniano: "Assure Mamma that tonight I will not sleep in Domenico's house. Tomorrow, at dawn, I will be far away."

The sun had barely set and I was already seeking refuge in the ancient cavern, past which, as a boy, I never went without a sense of profound apprehension. I entered it prudently, without a light; then I put back the ivy that concealed its mouth. From that hiding place, spying through the foliage, I found myself facing the window, without grilles and without shutters, of Domenico's kitchen; which was revealed to my eyes as distinctly as if I were there. Domenico, at that moment, was slicing cheese... he was preparing my viaticum for the morrow's pilgrimage. Caterina sat in a niche of the fireplace, telling the beads of her rosary.

Suddenly the dogs barked. This was not unusual. In the evening, foxes wander the mountain and the hounds, which scent them from afar, with incessant circling and turning, keep them in check. Except that, this time, their barking, having grown by degrees more furious, was succeeded by yelps and, an extremely suspicious circumstance, instead of coursing through the fields, they were falling back towards the house. They were therefore facing an adversary capable of commanding respect.

I strained my ear and was not long in noting a cadenced footfall, a dry clash of metals; then... between my eyes and the luminous window, several phantoms filed past on tiptoe. Plumes and carbines erased from my mind even the last traces of doubt... Austria's ruffians had surrounded the house.

Inside, the two honest creatures seemed utterly unaware of the storm that had gathered around their old nest. All at once, the scoundrels brought the butts of their rifles down on the fragile door, which burst open with a great crash. I saw Domenico leap backwards and instinctively his right hand, armed with the great knife, drew back to gain the momentum necessary to strike. But four leveled rifles drove from him, with the terror of reality, that which imagination had put into his body.

A lanky fellow, with a mustache twisted to a point and the face of a *mangiacandele*, made a gesture, and two gendarmes threw themselves on the bewildered man, manacled him, and tied him to a leg of the table.

No one had spoken. The lanky fellow snatched the dark lantern from a soldier, cast its light all around, and noticing Caterina who, having let the rosary fall at her feet, had remained as if paralyzed, he said to her: "Get up and come with me!" She did not stir from her rigidity and seemed not to understand, until the lanky fellow, grabbing her by a shoulder, shook her, made her get up, and dealt her a powerful blow between the shoulder blades. I distinctly saw Domenico grind his teeth.

The lanky fellow, a sergeant, disappeared through the opposite door, preceded by Caterina and followed by four of his men. I heard him give the order to put four ounces of lead into the stomach of anyone who tried to flee: then a noise of heavy steps on the little wooden staircase; then a dull moving of furniture, a banging of drawers on the brick floor. They rummaged in every hovel, even went down into the cellar.

When he reappeared at the head of his men, the sergeant was wiping his pointed mustache with the back of his hand... he had contrived to make his search less tedious by tapping a few barrels. But evidently the wine had not made his heart generous. He grabbed a chair and, planting it near the prisoner, seated himself resolutely astride it.

He began to speak with a hypocritical calm and so softly that, now and then, the words reached me. I heard at intervals, in an Italian larded with Venetian dialect and encrusted with Croatian salt, these all-too-significant phrases: "We did not climb all the way up here for the pleasure of letting ourselves be bamboozled. — The mouse is here and His Majesty the Emperor and King... — *el garon o un falò de la tua casa e di to straèsi*. — The dungeon, the cudgel on the... Short irons and the gallows!"

After this last phrase, he rested his fist on the back of the chair, his chin in the hollow of his fist, and waited. Domenico did not bat an eyelid.

The sergeant shouted: "Come on! We must finish this!" And he carved one of his gestures in the air that had the right to infuse a jolt of life into the impaled automatons standing behind him.

Domenico was untied and dragged, with his thumbs bound behind his back, into the middle of the kitchen. In less time than it takes to tell, a ruffian dropped a noose around his neck; another ruffian threw the other end of the rope over a beam. In the meantime the sergeant, having become extremely calm again, had contrived to light a cigar and, tormenting its ash between his fingers while puffing out words and smoke at the same time, said, almost syllabifying: "Either you sing... or I'll hang you like a sausage!"

The mountaineer turned pale. Nevertheless his face, common if not vulgar, appeared transfigured by a noble pride. He said: "You are wrong. There is no one here. But you are the stronger... do as you please."

He had barely finished when two scoundrels, seizing the end of the rope, pulled him up slowly, slowly. The poor man's arms attempted, with a supreme effort, to free themselves, his chest swelled horribly, his face became livid. When his tongue was already protruding from his twisted mouth, they suddenly let him drop. His clogs struck the ground violently and his knees buckled under him, as if he were a puppet made of rags. The noose was immediately loosened.

A brief silence followed, during which Domenico's face became a cadaverous pale.

"What do you think of my medicine? Has it brought you to your senses? Are you now disposed to reveal the truth to me?"

Domenico nodded his head in affirmation.

"About time!" the sergeant continued. "Give him a sip of water." And he blew out a long, thin puff of smoke — it was a brisk way of expressing his complete satisfaction.

But Domenico straightened up and, stepping forward as far as the ruffians holding the rope would permit, he snarled through clenched teeth: "To you, and to all the carrion that have befouled my Country!" And he launched a great gob of spit onto the chest of his slaughterer.

The latter gave a spasmodic start and a torrent of exotic blasphemies. But with a supreme effort, he contained his intense anger and dissimulated it with the slowness of his gesture and his glacial tone. He said: "Hang me that toad!"

The end of the rope was pulled slowly, slowly...

From my cavern I observed and meditated. Several times I had tormented the butt of my pistol, but an imperious inner voice had cried out to me: "Do not be rash!" One corpse, two, a resounding revenge... to what end? I decided to play the last desperate card only when the game was irretrievably lost. My sorrow for Domenico's torture was filled with apprehension. I knew the fiber of the proud old man and sensed that he would not betray me. But Caterina?.. the horrible spectacle, the certainty that a single word could ward off the atrocious agony... How would her woman's heart, her companion's heart, resist?

Caterina ran frantically from one wall to the other; she invoked, waving her arms, the Virgin and the saints. And when, overcome by a sudden fury, she made to launch herself, with her nails, upon the impassive tormentors and the iron arm of a gendarme had thrown her back, almost felling her, she, shrieking, tore out her hair in clumps. But the secret guarded in the depths of her breast, the more the storm of the soul rages, the more it clings to the flesh, and in the depths it remained.

Domenico was lowered for the second time. He was so stunned that the renewed questions probably reached his brain too indistinctly for him to be able to answer. The sergeant called his ruffians to a consultation. They must have concluded that the mountaineer knew nothing, that the imperial and royal Police had made a blunder. In the tranquil air, orders rang out; in the kitchen, around the cottage, could be heard the cadenced steps of the "armed force" preparing for departure. A few minutes and all was silence...

Domenico cast his searching eyes about and his chest swelled with sighs. Then he stood up, looked at his companion with a long, intense gaze, and caressed her head. A short while later, picking up the knife again, he returned to slicing his cheese, as if nothing extraordinary had happened. Caterina gathered up the rosary from beside the hearth and began to tell its beads again... this time on her knees.

I let enough time pass as might instill in me the certainty that the accursed pestilence was truly down the slope; then I emerged from the ivy and appeared on the threshold. Caterina did not even turn. Domenico contented himself with opening the front of his shirt and pointing out to me the circle incised upon him by the noose. I clasped him in my arms and, seeking that livid furrow with my lips, I repeatedly kissed it.

What I am about to narrate I gathered from the letters written to me by Giustiniano. I have impressed them upon my memory in indelible characters.

To reassure Mamma, Giustiniano sketched the situation for her, tempering it with a good dose of optimism. Luisa would undergo some interrogation, but would soon be released. Papa was known as an old patriot, an irreducible man, and would not be able to get off so easily. But how to condemn him without proof? The judicial authorities were rigorous beyond measure; but they were keen to appear most upright and would not dare to commit a patent villainy. As for me, he himself had accompanied me as far as the mines of Agordo. From that point, I had chosen paths that only I and the chamois knew how to traverse. I must have already arrived beyond the borders, while the Police still believed me to be in Belluno and were searching for me in Belluno. Soon, they would receive letters from me from Switzerland, or from some other country where the yellow and black did not fly. Everything therefore depended on not losing heart, in order to preserve one's health, so necessary in that tempest.

But the confidence that he strove to instill in Mamma's soul was far from being in his own. The newspapers, subject to rigorous censorship, seemed to agree on treating the latest conspiracy with the conspiracy of silence. But news knows how to travel a great distance even without the help of newspapers. Now from the lips of trusted friends he met along the way; now at the café, around the small table, between one phrase and another about the fine weather, or the price of agricultural products, there would slip into his ear the words that could not be openly pronounced without danger, and from them, as always in such cases, the happy assurances appeared but faintly delineated; the sinister premonitions, strongly painted.

One evening, these seemed to find a tragic resolution in the facts. "Between the gorges of the Alps, a patrol had blocked the path of the fugitive patriot... the order to surrender disregarded... the noble heart broken by Austrian lead... the series of generous impulses cut short forever. That very night, the corpse would arrive in Belluno for the legal examinations."

Giustiniano hurried home, to prevent the sad news from reaching Mamma from an incautious mouth. He found her in bed. Wake her? To what end? She would have more than enough time for tears. He paced the room back and forth until a very late hour; then, overcome by exhaustion, he let himself fall into an armchair and fell asleep.

Through the half-closed shutters, the first light of dawn was barely peeking, and already Mamma, impatient for information, was bringing him his customary cup of coffee. She saw him and, guessing the sleepless and anguished night, instead of waking him, she went to the untouched bed, took a coverlet from it and spread it over his knees and chest. Just then, the doorbell rang very loudly.

Giustiniano woke with a start, saw, remembered, understood. He threw his arms around the poor woman's neck and said to her: "My poor Mamma, be strong and prepare for the worst."

They left in a carriage, a gendarme on the box, an inspector at their side. No hysterical fits, no sobs, no silent tears. Giustiniano held tight to the hand of the grieving woman, who paid him no mind, who remained motionless, her eye fixed on some distant point, perceptible only to her.

She was led into a large, cold room. From the opposite wall a table projected, its end resting on a trestle; on the table was spread a sheet; under the sheet were outlined, with sinister uncertainty, the forms of a man in the rigidity of death. A soldier, with his rifle at rest, stood erect, chest out, beside him. Another, in a simple vest, his sleeves rolled up, his left hand plunged into a large boot, upon which he kept his eyes fixed, furiously worked the brush and picked off the loose threads. Four officers, arranged in a circle, conferred in low voices. A burning brazier adulterated, with its glow, the limpidity of the morning.

At Mamma's entrance, the officers interrupted their conversation and turned their faces toward that severe apparition. She seemed not to hear them; her soul was concentrated in her eyes, which glittered beneath the black veil.

The soldier in the vest blew his warm breath on the boot and down came the brush strokes, more furious than ever... perhaps he wished, at that moment, that the barracks and the Empire were concentrated on that muddy upper. The soldier standing stiffly beside the corpse did not dare to breathe.

One of the officers stepped forward, removed the sheet, perhaps with a sudden gesture. Then a sob resounded in the sepulchral silence, just one!.. but it was enough to reveal the depth of a heart.

I had to endeavor to calm that youthful fever, and with a voice in which vibrated the dull threat of an entire people, I said: "No, this is no small matter. I do not intend to leave the environs of Belluno until I have seen deep into these embroilments. And where could I find a more trusted person than you, to serve as my informant and, if need be, as an intermediary?"

"Come now!" I added to pronounce her triumph. She started towards the exit. The soldier in his vest blew his warm breath on the boot and down came the brush strokes, more furious than ever... perhaps he wished, at that moment, that the barracks and the Empire were concentrated on that muddy upper. The soldier standing stiffly beside the corpse did not dare to breathe. The situation was static, awaiting a command that brooked no delay. "I choose Domenico's house as my refuge. That peasant is made of the right stuff and will not betray us. I expect you tomorrow. Come by a long route and make sure no one follows you. I will judge by your cunning if you are worthy of greater enterprises."

And shaking his hand, I turned my back and left the gloomy plain. In the fields, in the crisp air, I rediscovered that purity of the scintillating sky that gives strength to ferocious emotions. I had not laid down any burden along the climb; and yet my weariness had suddenly vanished. I ascended with great strides, and with every step the serenity of the night insinuated itself deeply into my thoughts; my soul came to mirror the picturesque majesty of the mountain.

I recognized Domenico’s little hovel, pressed against the cliff, the door blackening under the pergola, now bare of grapes and leaves... the pergola that stretched to the road and divided the clearing of the small vegetable garden into equal parts.
