Proprio in quei giorni, approda all'isola del bagnato baciato la fregata "Amazzone" e ci porta un'ondata di notizie dalla vecchia Europa.

Gli uomini dalle idee retrograde e gli uomini nuovi vi avevano continuato la strenua lotta per la conquista dell'avvenire e grandi avvenimenti si erano incalzati e fissati nella Storia, benché l'Orsini e il Pieri ed io e tanti e tanti altri si fosse totalmente eliminati dalla scena politica bella lezione di umiltà! Magenta vi aveva inciso il suo nome a caratteri scintillanti e sanguigni. Ed io me ne rattristai. Perchè, perchè, che cosa vi scorgevo lì? Il Re del Piemonte aveva seguito la tattica dei suoi predecessori: valersi dei Francesi per battere gli Austriaci; salvo ad allearsi, più tardi, cogli Austriaci per mettere un freno ai Francesi e, nell'uno e nell'altro caso, quali risultati?... un ingrandimento gretto del suo Piemonte; un piccolo maggior cespite d'entrata per la vecchia dinastia... ecco che cosa vi scorgevo! Napoleone non avrebbe mai acconsentito ad avere ai confini dell'Impero, un'Italia forte, capace di sottrarsi alla sua influenza, al suo predominio; Napoleone esaltante la Chiesa e fatto a sua volta, nelle mani della Chiesa, strumento di reazione e d'oppressione, non avrebbe mai detto all'Italia: "Va, procedi trionfalmente sulla via maestra e ritrova la tua Roma!"

Vittorio Emanuele II aveva insinuato, fra il Popolo italiano e la sua Unità, quell'elemento dissolvente che metterebbe fine — pensavo — ai tempi eroici, rigeneranti il carattere, per iniziare quelli del volgare opportunismo, delle codarde tergiversazioni... insinuato, presentivo il profondo scetticismo che avrebbe invaso l'anima latina, così facile agli sconforti esagerati; l'anima latina che non rifulge senza entusiasmi, come il sangue non arde e non s'imporpora senz'ossigeno. Il segno glorioso, preconizzato da tante intelligenze elette, anelito e tortura di tante coscienze virili, mi parve fatalmente allontanato. Nella migliore delle ipotesi, tra il presente e quel segno... una landa insidiosa, ove la libertà non avrebbe potuto attecchire se non rachitica e imbastardita dalle ineluttabili esigenze di una Monarchia. Mai, quanto in quell'ora e con quell'abito dei reietti attaccato alle carni, io deplorai di non aver saputo colpire se.

Un ufficiale dell'Amazzone, impressionato dalla mia tristezza, me ne domandò gentilmente la ragione.

"Se l'Italia — gli risposi — per assurgere, o tanto o quanto a libertà, aveva assolutamente bisogno di ricorrere all'armi straniere, avrei preferito veder scendere dall'Alpi uomini che non avessero, come noi, i polsi gravi di catene. Magenta segnerà indubbiamente una data memoranda negli annali d'Italia; se altrettanto fausta ignoro. So che fausta sarebbe se Napoleone vi fosse stato vinto e disfatto!... Oh la provvida sventura! I due Popoli si stenderebbero la mano senza sottintesi, senza secondi fini; l'uno dopo aver dichiarato l'usurpatore decaduto dal trono; l'altro dopo essersi liberato, colla terza disfatta della Monarchia, dall'equivoco, dall'incubo che lo possiede e quasi lo snatura. Chi poteva resistere al loro impeto? Avrebbero fatto ben più che battere gli Austriaci e spazzar via i tirannelli che ingombrano la Penisola; avrebbero praticamente eliminato le Alpi!"

— Sogno generoso! — disse a testa bassa l'ufficiale. Poi si chinò e mi mormorò all'orecchio parole che mi fecero trasalire: "A bordo, abbiamo un Decreto d'amnistia... ed è pei condannati politici!"

Un'ora d'ansietà ed eravamo allineati di fronte alle caserme. Il Capitano della fregata pronunciò quattro frasi, accennando alla nuova gloria di cui "si erano coperte" le armi francesi e, dopo, gli elogi d'obbligo all'alto senno ed al valore dell'augusto Sovrano. Levatone a cielo la clemenza, passò alla lettura del Decreto di amnistia. Il Direttore del penitenziario pronunciò ad uno ad uno, con accorante lentezza, il nome degli individui su cui pioveva l'inaspettata rugiada. Quello del Galli, quello del Rossini, quello del Tebaldi, il mio non furono pronunciati. Il Decreto concerneva soltanto i sudditi francesi!

Parecchi ufficiali ci furono d'attorno ed esprimendo il loro rammarico, insinuarono che una nostra supplica, appoggiata dal Direttore del penitenziario, sarebbe, con tutta probabilità, favorevolmente accolta dall'Imperatore che rimedierebbe così a quella dimenticanza, senz'alcun dubbio involontaria. Perchè non stenderla immediatamente? L'Amazzone stessa l'avrebbe portata in Francia.

Galli, Rossini, Tebaldi acconsentirono e, lì per lì, mi annunciarono che fidavano più nella loro supplica che nel tentativo di fuga a cui, naturalmente, rinunciavano. E questa non fu, dal mio punto di vista tutto personale, la minima tra le tristi conseguenze dell'alleanza franco-piemontese e della vittoria di Magenta.

Scabrosa e non da me, che non conoscevo a sufficienza gli ospiti dell'albergo doloroso, era il rimediare a quella defezione. Ne affidai l'incarico al Cousin, l'ex ufficiale di Luigi Filippo. Il quale vi si adoperò con tanta circospezione, da impiegarci un paio di mesi. Al 1 Dicembre egli mi avvertì che tutto era pronto. La mattina del 9, io e il Morbioli assistevamo, dal molo, alla partenza dell'Abeille, in rotta per Cayenna. La sua scia biancheggiava ancora, quand'io, voltandomi al fiero transteverino:

"Morbioli, oggi si giuoca risolutamente l'ultima carta. Avvisane, colla debita prudenza, i compagni. Chi siano gli ultimi aggiunti non so precisamente; ma so che non sono della stessa pasta di quelli che abbiamo perduto. Debbo farti, a questo proposito una raccomandazione che reputo importante."

E lo esortai a lasciar da parte l'arciprete. Vero, egli aveva contribuito alle spese; ma il carico che portava nella Bibbia, non troppo segretamente ormai, avrebbe potuto suscitare, in altri, se non in noi, istinti perversi. Abbandonarlo al suo destino era, con tutta probabilità, un salvargli la pelle. D'altronde noi non dovevamo correre il rischio di disonorare, con azioni disoneste, un'impresa che i nostri stessi nemici si sarebbero ben guardati, in cuor loro, dal censurare.

Morbioli si fregò le mani ed osservò che l'arciprete rimaneva uccel di gabbia, appunto per quel tesoretto sul quale aveva tanto calcolato per poter battere l'ali. "Si vede che il sacro unto non gli ha infuso briciolo di spirito profetico." E soggiunse: "L'idea mi va; perchè, a dirti il vero, quel prete, in un tentativo tanto arrischiato, mi sapeva di mal augurio. Pareva se lo fossimo accaparrato per cantarci il De profundis!"

Gli diedi qualche altra istruzione e ci separammo. Alle cinque, uscendo dall'Officina, lo rividi affaccendato d'attorno alle cannoniere. Un'ora dopo, mentre la moltitudine dei disgraziati sedeva nel refettorio, d'innanzi ai piatti di stagno, io me ne stavo in luogo solitario, ritto sull'arena, coi piedi in guazzo, ansiosamente aspettando, in quel luogo basso ed incassato tra gli scogli, nessuno mi poteva scorgere che non fosse sul mare.

E dal mare mi videro i rematori. Il desiderio della cena aggiungeva vigoria ai loro muscoli e la navicella guizzava leggera sull'onde, come se le lambisse appena. Diedi una voce e alzai, alto nell'aria il pugno, mostrando, stretto tra il pollice e l'indice, un pezzo da cinque franchi.

Bestemmiarono; ma, indotti dal Cousin, che era al comando della piccola squadra, cambiarono rotta e, in un baleno, la chiglia raschia e si ferma. "Presto... Presto... quanti spiccioli!"

Per tutta risposta, fischio e dagli scogli, sbucano cinque faccie arcigne, capaci di incutere rispetto ad un branco di selvaggi. Prima che i pescatori si rimettessero dalla sorpresa, se li trovano al fianco, a lama alzata. Ma la minaccia fu una precauzione quasi superflua. Interrogati ad uno ad uno i rematori avevano, già da tempo, manifestato il loro ardente desiderio di fuggire. Paventavano i compagni e nessuno si era attentato a pronunciare un sì esplicito. Il nocciolo della mia impresa consisteva dunque nel legare in un fascio, coll'ardita iniziativa, tutti i pallidi sì individuali; nel trasformarli in una volontà forte, collettiva e dinamica.

Quand'io ebbi tratto dal nascondiglio le provvigioni e l'albero e la vela, i titubanti si guardarono reciprocamente in faccia e sorrisero. Si accontentarono di dire: "Resta inteso che noi cediamo alla forza!"

Succede precisamente così anche nelle grandi cose politiche e sociali. Quante verità sono già ben radicate nel cuore dell'uomo, quante aspirazioni già vi germogliano rigogliose! Ma niente si realizza sinché non sorgano gli audaci che osino essere i precursori. Ed è questa deplorevole inerzia, questa fatale codardia che presta tanta forza ai meno di tiranneggiare i più.

Quattro colpi di remo e siamo lontani dalla spiaggia quanto un tiro di fucile. Eravamo in undici. L'arciprete mancava; ma non era il solo. Morelli, il sarto romano, che pure ci aveva prestato opera tanto efficace, preso da febbre improvvisa, delirava all'ospedale. Io e il Morbioli, unici italiani.

Ora gli scogli non intercettano più la nostra e l'altrui visuale e lo spettacolo insolito di una barca a vela palesa, in un attimo, il tentativo tanto gelosamente occultato. L'intero refettorio è sottosopra e, nella sinfonia confusa di grida, di risate, di bestemmie, portataci dal vento, risalta il terribile comando di un ufficiale: "Aux canonnières!" Una striscia di uomini esce dal refettorio e si allunga, con sinistri serpeggiamenti, verso il molo.

La nostra navicella filava; ma con manovra, che non riuscivo a comprendere, il Cousin, invece di mantenere la prua rivolta al Continente, descrive un largo circolo, come se intendesse di girare l'isola. I forzati, che sugli scogli percorrevano una breve corda di quel circolo, arrivano prestamente al molo e calano una cannoniera a tempo per poterci intercettare la via. In altre parole, noi correvamo a tutta forza, e quasi di proposito, a dar di cozzo nel pericolo.

Guardo, con occhi inquieti, la cannoniera che galleggia, apparentemente a meraviglia; guardo, con inquietudine anche maggiore, il Morbioli. Questi comprende e dice: "Sangue transteverino non falla! Vedi? Nessuno scende a bordo perchè i calmi sono in fondo al mare... ora sgambettano per rifornirsene all'officina! E la chiglia, sul fondo... quattro occhielli mortali!" E detto questo, stringe le mascelle e preme, colle braccia tese nel vuoto, quasi si sforzasse di rendere più pesa la cannoniera e di usare violenza al mare.

D'improvviso dà un pugno nell'aria e scoppia in una risata!... I quattro occhielli avevano fatto il dover loro; la cannoniera, grave d'onda, aveva raggiunto i calmi colà dove non giungeva la luce del Sole. E lo sciame umano nereggiante sulla riva, ci acclama con un subisso di evviva, con un febbrile agitare di cenci e di cappelli.

I tormentati esultavano per la disfatta dei tormentatori!

Adesso il Cousin completa la sua manovra misteriosa e volge risolutamente la prua verso il Nord. Io gli grido nervosamente: "Che fai?"

— Sta zitto! L'Abeille ci darà presto la caccia ed è sulle coste della Guiana che si aspetterà d'arrivarci. Al nord, al nord! La libertà è frammezzo alle tempeste; la nostra sicurezza è nel pericolo!

Navigammo strenuamente la notte intera, sempre rivolti al Polo. Gran vento, grandi ondate! L'Oceano ci copriva di salsedine, rumoreggiando con gran voce nelle tenebre.

Ci ammoniva? Ci reclamava? I nostri sensi tesi, terribilmente tesi, non riescivano a comprenderlo... Ora ci spingeva in alto come se ci volesse gettar fuori del suo dominio; ora eravamo attratti precipitosamente verso l'abisso, come da un poderoso, irresistibile succhiamento. Unico affidamento, la marmorea impassibilità del capitano. Nessuna voce umana, tra quella furia rumorosa, fuorché la sua, misurata, calma, fredda, metallica...

L'alba ci trovò molli e raggrinziti. Ma avevamo lasciato alle spalle l'acque intorbidate dai grandi fiumi rovesciatevi dal Continente e già solcavamo l'azzurro profondo. Il Sole e la rinata speranza ci trasformarono in poco d'ora. L'Oceano poteva ben agitarsi, ruggire l'incessante minaccia. Ogni sua ondata poteva inghiottirci!... che importava? Ogni palpito di vela poteva avvicinarci sempre più ad una lotta finale, tragica... poiché, su ogni volto appariva la ferma risoluzione di sgozzare, o di farsi sgozzare, quando il frutto del nostro ardimento ci venisse conteso. Che importava?... Ci sentivamo liberi come il vento che correva per l'infinito!

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-FIFTH

It was precisely in those days that the frigate *Amazzone* made land at the island of the damned and blessed, bringing with her a wave of news from the old Europe.

Men of retrograde ideas and men of the new order had continued their fierce struggle for the conquest of the future, and great events had pressed upon one another and fixed themselves in History — though Orsini and Pieri and I and so many, so many others had been wholly eliminated from the political stage. What a fine lesson in humility! Magenta had cut its name there in letters glittering and blood-red. And I was saddened by it. Why, why — what did I discern in all this? The King of Piedmont had followed the tactics of his predecessors: to avail himself of the French to beat the Austrians; reserving the option, at a later hour, of allying himself with the Austrians so as to put a brake upon the French — and in either case, what results? A paltry aggrandisement of his Piedmont; a trifling increase in revenue for the old dynasty — that is what I discerned there! Napoleon would never have consented to have, upon the borders of the Empire, a strong Italy capable of withdrawing herself from his influence, from his predominance; Napoleon, who exalted the Church and was made in turn, in the hands of the Church, an instrument of reaction and oppression, would never have said to Italy: "Go, advance triumphantly upon the high road and reclaim thy Rome!"

Vittorio Emanuele II had insinuated, between the Italian People and its Unity, that dissolvent element which would put an end — so I thought — to the heroic times that regenerate character, and usher in those of vulgar opportunism, of craven equivocation. He had insinuated — I felt it in my deepest presentiment — that profound scepticism which would come to invade the Latin soul, so prone to exaggerated despondency; the Latin soul that cannot shine without enthusiasm, as blood neither blazes nor turns crimson without oxygen. The glorious sign, foretold by so many elect intelligences, the longing and the torment of so many virile consciences, seemed to me fatally receded. In the best of hypotheses, between the present and that sign lay a treacherous waste, wherein liberty could take root only stunted and debased by the ineluctable demands of a Monarchy. Never, as much as in that hour, and with the garb of the outcast clinging to my flesh, did I lament that I had not known how to strike home.

An officer of the *Amazzone*, struck by my melancholy, enquired of me its reason with a kindly air.

"If Italy," I answered him, "in order to rise, however little, to liberty, had absolute need to have recourse to foreign arms, I should have preferred to see descend from the Alps men whose wrists were not, as ours, heavy with chains. Magenta will doubtless mark a memorable date in the annals of Italy; whether equally auspicious I do not know. I know that it would be auspicious if Napoleon had been beaten and destroyed there! Oh, what a provident disaster that would have been! The two Peoples would have extended their hands to one another without hidden meanings, without ulterior aims; the one, after having declared the usurper stripped of his throne; the other, after having freed itself — by the third overthrow of the Monarchy — from the equivocation, from the nightmare that possesses it and well nigh unsettles its very nature. Who could have resisted their impetus? They would have done far more than beat the Austrians and sweep away the petty tyrants who encumber the Peninsula; they would have practically abolished the Alps!"

"A generous dream!" said the officer, bowing his head. Then he leaned close and murmured in my ear words that made me start: "On board, we carry a Decree of amnesty — and it is for the political prisoners!"

An hour of suspense, and we were drawn up in line before the barracks. The Captain of the frigate delivered four sentences, alluding to the new glory with which the French arms had "covered themselves," and following these with the obligatory encomiums upon the lofty wisdom and valour of the august Sovereign. Having extolled his clemency to the skies, he proceeded to the reading of the Decree of amnesty. The Director of the penitentiary pronounced one by one, with a heartrending deliberateness, the names of those upon whom this unexpected dew descended. That of Galli, that of Rossini, that of Tebaldi, my own — were not pronounced. The Decree concerned French subjects only.

Several officers gathered about us and, expressing their regret, suggested that a petition from us, supported by the Director of the penitentiary, would in all probability be favourably received by the Emperor, who would thus remedy what was beyond all doubt an involuntary omission. Why not draw it up at once? The *Amazzone* herself would carry it to France.

Galli, Rossini, and Tebaldi consented and, then and there, informed me that they placed greater faith in their petition than in the escape attempt, which they were naturally renouncing. And this was not, from my own entirely personal point of view, the least grievous of the sorry consequences of the Franco-Piedmontese alliance and the victory of Magenta.

To remedy that defection was a delicate matter, and not one for me, who did not know sufficiently well the other inmates of the house of sorrow. I entrusted the task to the Cousin, the former officer of Louis-Philippe. He set about it with such circumspection that it required a full two months. On the first of December he informed me that all was ready. On the morning of the ninth, I and Morbioli stood at the quay watching the departure of the *Abeille*, bound for Cayenne. Her white wake was still gleaming when I turned to the proud *Trasteverino*:

"Morbioli, today we play the last card resolutely. Warn our companions, with due caution. Who exactly the latest additions are I cannot say with precision; but I know they are not of the same mettle as those we have lost. On this point I must make you a recommendation I consider important."

And I urged him to leave the archpriest aside. True, he had contributed to the expenses; but the cargo he carried in his Bible — no longer so very secretly by now — might have been capable of arousing, in others if not in ourselves, perverse instincts. To abandon him to his fate was, in all probability, to save his skin. Besides, we must not run the risk of dishonouring, by dishonest actions, an enterprise which our very enemies would have been careful, in their hearts, never to censure.

Morbioli rubbed his hands and observed that the archpriest remained a caged bird, precisely on account of that little hoard upon which he had so greatly counted to take wing. "It seems the holy unction has not infused a scrap of prophetic spirit into him." And he added: "The idea suits me well; for, to tell you the truth, that priest, in so hazardous an attempt, struck me as an ill omen. It was as though we had secured him expressly to sing us the *De profundis!*"

I gave him a few further instructions and we parted. At five o'clock, leaving the Workshop, I saw him again, busy about the gunboats. An hour later, while the multitude of the wretched sat in the refectory before their pewter plates, I stood alone in a secluded spot, upright upon the sand, with my feet in the shallows, waiting anxiously; in that low place, hemmed in between the rocks, no one could see me save from the sea.

And from the sea the oarsmen saw me. The desire for their supper lent vigour to their muscles and the little boat darted lightly over the waves, as though barely grazing them. I gave a shout and raised my fist high in the air, displaying, pinched between thumb and forefinger, a five-franc piece.

They cursed; but, induced by the Cousin, who was in command of the little crew, they changed course, and in an instant the keel scraped and stopped. "Quick — quick — what pay is there in it!"

For all answer, a whistle — and from the rocks five surly faces burst forth, such as might have struck awe into a pack of savages. Before the fishermen recovered from their surprise, they found them at their sides, blades raised. But the threat was an almost superfluous precaution. Questioned one by one, the oarsmen had for some time already made clear their ardent desire to flee. They feared their comrades, and not one had dared to utter an explicit yes. The very kernel of my enterprise consisted, therefore, in binding into a single sheaf, by bold initiative, all those pale individual assents; in transforming them into one strong, collective, and dynamic will.

When I had drawn from their hiding-place the provisions and the mast and the sail, the waverers looked one another in the face and smiled. They contented themselves with saying: "Let it be understood that we yield to force!"

It falls out precisely thus in great political and social affairs as well. How many truths are already firmly rooted in the heart of man, how many aspirations already germinating there in luxuriant growth! Yet nothing is realised until the bold arise who dare to be the forerunners. And it is this deplorable inertia, this fatal cowardice, that lends such power to the few to tyrannise over the many.

Four strokes of the oar and we were from the shore as far as a musket-shot. We were eleven. The archpriest was absent; but he was not the only one. Morelli, the Roman tailor, who had likewise rendered us such effective service, seized by a sudden fever, lay delirious in the hospital. I and Morbioli — the only Italians.

Now the rocks no longer intercepted the view between us and the shore, and the unwonted spectacle of a sailing boat revealed in an instant the attempt so jealously concealed. The entire refectory was in uproar, and in the confused symphony of cries, of laughter, of curses, borne to us upon the wind, there rang out the terrible command of an officer: "*Aux canonnières!*" A file of men issued from the refectory and stretched out, with sinister windings, towards the quay.

Our little boat flew on; yet by a manoeuvre I could not comprehend, the Cousin, instead of keeping the prow directed towards the Continent, described a wide circle, as though he intended to round the island. The convicts, who on the rocks were traversing a short chord of that circle, arrived swiftly at the quay and launched a gunboat in time to intercept our way. In other words, we were running at full force, and almost as if by design, straight into the danger.

I cast anxious eyes at the gunboat, which rode the water with an apparently perfect ease; with even greater anxiety I looked at Morbioli. He understood and said: "Trasteverino blood never fails! You see? No one goes on board because the plugs are at the bottom of the sea — they are scrambling now to find new ones at the workshop! And the keel, on the bottom — four deadly bung-holes!" And having said this, he clenched his jaws and pressed with arms outstretched into the void, as though striving to make the gunboat heavier still and to do violence to the sea.

All of a sudden he drove a fist into the air and burst into a great laugh! The four holes had done their duty; the gunboat, swamped, had found those plugs where the light of the Sun does not reach. And the dark swarming mass of men on the shore acclaimed us with a tempest of *evviva*, with a feverish waving of rags and caps.

The tormented were exulting at the defeat of the tormentors!

Now the Cousin completed his mysterious manoeuvre and turned the prow resolutely towards the North. I cried out to him nervously: "What are you doing?"

"Hold your tongue! The *Abeille* will give us chase before long, and it is off the coasts of Guiana that they will expect to intercept us. To the north, to the north! Liberty lies amidst the tempests; our safety is in danger itself!"

We sailed strenuously the whole night, always keeping towards the Pole. Great winds, great waves! The Ocean drenched us with salt spray, roaring in great voice through the darkness.

Was it warning us? Was it claiming us? Our senses, strung to a terrible tension, could not decipher it. Now it flung us upward as though wishing to hurl us beyond its dominion; now we were drawn headlong towards the abyss, as by a powerful, irresistible suction. Our sole assurance lay in the marble impassivity of the captain. No human voice, amidst that roaring fury, save his — measured, calm, cold, metallic.

The dawn found us drenched and shrivelled. But we had left behind the waters muddied by the great rivers that pour into them from the Continent, and we were already cleaving the deep blue. The Sun and reviving hope transformed us within a short hour. The Ocean might well agitate itself, roar its unceasing menace — every wave of it might engulf us! What did it matter? Every pulse of the sail might draw us ever nearer to a final, tragic struggle — for upon every face appeared the steadfast resolution to cut throats, or to have one's own cut, should the fruit of our daring be wrested from us. What did it matter? We felt ourselves free as the wind that ran through the infinite!

Napoleon would never have consented to having at the borders of the Empire a strong Italy, capable of escaping his influence, his predominance; Napoleon, exalting the Church and made in his turn, in the hands of the Church, an instrument of reaction and oppression, would never have said to Italy: "Go, proceed triumphantly on the high road and find your Rome again!"

Vittorio Emanuele II had insinuated, between the Italian People and its Unity, that dissolving element that would put an end — I thought — to the heroic times, which regenerate character, in order to begin those of vulgar opportunism, of cowardly tergiversations... insinuated, I foresaw the profound skepticism that would invade the Latin soul, so prone to exaggerated despair; the Latin soul that does not shine without enthusiasms, as blood does not burn and does not redden without oxygen. The glorious sign, foretold by so many chosen intellects, the yearning and torture of so many virile consciences, seemed to me fatally postponed. In the best of hypotheses, between the present and that sign... an insidious wasteland, where liberty could not have taken root except rickety and bastardized by the ineluctable demands of a Monarchy. Never, so much as in that hour and with that garb of the outcasts clinging to my flesh, did I deplore not having known how to strike him.

An officer of the Amazzone, impressed by my sadness, kindly asked me the reason for it.

"If Italy," I answered him, "in order to rise, whether much or little, to liberty, had absolute need to resort to foreign arms, I should have preferred to see men descend from the Alps who did not have, like us, wrists heavy with chains. Magenta will undoubtedly mark a memorable date in the annals of Italy; whether an equally auspicious one, I do not know. I know that it would have been auspicious had Napoleon been vanquished and defeated there!... Oh, the providential misfortune! The two Peoples would extend their hands to one another without subtexts, without ulterior motives; the one after having declared the usurper fallen from the throne; the other after having freed itself, with the third defeat of the Monarchy, from the equivocation, from the nightmare that possesses and almost denatures it. Who could have resisted their impetus? They would have done far more than beat the Austrians and sweep away the petty tyrants who encumber the Peninsula; they would have practically eliminated the Alps!"

"A generous dream!" said the officer with his head lowered. Then he leaned over and murmured in my ear words that made me start: "On board, we have a Decree of amnesty... and it is for the political convicts!"

An hour of anxiety, and we were lined up in front of the barracks. The Captain of the frigate uttered a few sentences, alluding to the new glory with which the French arms "had covered themselves," and, after, the obligatory eulogies to the high wisdom and valour of the august Sovereign. Having praised his clemency to the heavens, he proceeded to the reading of the Decree of amnesty. The Director of the penitentiary pronounced one by one, with heart-breaking slowness, the names of the individuals upon whom this unexpected dew was falling. That of Galli, that of Rossini, that of Tebaldi, mine were not pronounced. The Decree concerned only French subjects!

Several officers gathered around us and, expressing their regret, insinuated that a petition from us, supported by the Director of the penitentiary, would, in all probability, be favourably received by the Emperor, who would thus remedy that oversight, which was without a doubt unintentional. Why not draw it up immediately? The Amazzone itself would carry it to France.

Galli, Rossini, and Tebaldi consented and, there and then, announced to me that they trusted more in their petition than in the escape attempt, which, naturally, they were renouncing. And this was not, from my own personal point of view, the least among the sad consequences of the Franco-Piedmontese alliance and the victory of Magenta.

To remedy that defection was a thorny task, and not for me, who did not know the guests of that sorrowful hotel sufficiently well. I entrusted the task to Cousin, the former officer of Louis Philippe. Who applied himself to it with such circumspection that he took a couple of months. On December 1st he informed me that all was ready. On the morning of the 9th, Morbioli and I watched from the pier the departure of the *Abeille*, en route for Cayenne. Its wake was still whitening the water when I, turning to the proud Trasteverino:

"Morbioli, today we resolutely play the last card. Advise the comrades, with due prudence. Who the latest additions are I do not know precisely; but I know they are not of the same stuff as those we have lost. I must, in this regard, make a recommendation that I deem important."

And I exhorted him to leave the archpriest aside. True, he had contributed to the expenses; but the load he carried in his Bible, no longer too secretly, could have aroused perverse instincts in others, if not in us. To abandon him to his fate was, in all probability, to save his skin. Besides, we were not to run the risk of dishonouring, with dishonest actions, an undertaking that our very enemies would have been careful, in their hearts, not to censure.

Morbioli rubbed his hands and observed that the archpriest would remain a caged bird, precisely because of that little treasure on which he had counted so much to be able to beat his wings. "It seems the holy unction has not infused in him a crumb of prophetic spirit." And he added: "The idea suits me; because, to tell you the truth, that priest, in such a risky attempt, felt like a bad omen to me. It was as if we had brought him along to sing the *De profundis* over us!"

I gave him a few more instructions and we separated. At five o'clock, leaving the Workshop, I saw him again, busy around the gunboats. An hour later, while the multitude of wretches sat in the refectory, before their tin plates, I stood in a solitary place, upright on the sand, with my feet in the wash, anxiously waiting; in that low place, set amongst the rocks, no one could see me who was not on the sea.

And from the sea the rowers saw me. The desire for supper added vigour to their muscles and the little boat darted lightly over the waves, as if barely lapping at them. I gave a shout and raised my fist high in the air, showing, clenched between my thumb and forefinger, a five-franc piece.

They swore; but, induced by Cousin, who was in command of the small crew, they changed course and, in a flash, the keel scraped and stopped. "Quick... Quick... how much small change!"

For all reply, a whistle, and from the rocks, five grim faces emerged, capable of commanding the respect of a pack of savages. Before the fishermen could recover from their surprise, they found them at their side, with blades raised. But the threat was an almost superfluous precaution. Questioned one by one, the rowers had, for some time now, manifested their ardent desire to flee. They feared their comrades and no one had dared to pronounce an explicit yes. The core of my undertaking consisted, therefore, in binding together in a single bundle, with a bold initiative, all the pale individual yeses; in transforming them into a strong, collective, and dynamic will.

When I had brought out the provisions and the mast and the sail from their hiding place, the hesitant ones looked each other in the face and smiled. They contented themselves with saying: "Let it be understood that we are yielding to force!"

The same thing happens in great political and social matters. How many truths are already well rooted in the heart of man, how many aspirations already sprout there vigorously! But nothing is realized until the audacious arise who dare to be the precursors. And it is this deplorable inertia, this fatal cowardice that lends so much strength to the few to tyrannize the many.

Four strokes of the oar and we are a rifle-shot away from the shore. There were eleven of us. The archpriest was missing; but he was not the only one. Morelli, the Roman tailor, who had also lent us such effective aid, seized by a sudden fever, was delirious in the hospital. Morbioli and I, the only Italians.

Now the rocks no longer block our view or that of others, and the unusual spectacle of a sailboat reveals, in an instant, the attempt so jealously concealed. The entire refectory is in an uproar and, in the confused symphony of shouts, of laughter, of curses, carried to us by the wind, the terrible command of an officer stands out: "*Aux canonnières!*" A line of men exits the refectory and stretches, with sinister undulations, toward the pier.

Our little boat was flying; but with a manoeuvre that I could not understand, Cousin, instead of keeping the bow pointed toward the Continent, described a wide circle, as if he intended to go around the island. The convicts, who on the rocks were traversing a short chord of that circle, quickly reached the pier and lowered a gunboat in time to be able to intercept our path. In other words, we were running at full speed, and almost on purpose, to collide with danger.

I watch, with anxious eyes, the gunboat that is floating, apparently marvellously; I watch, with even greater anxiety, Morbioli. He understands and says: "Trasteverino blood does not fail! See? No one is going on board because the plugs are at the bottom of the sea... now they are scurrying to get more from the workshop! And the keel, on the bottom... four mortal eyelets!" And having said this, he clenches his jaw and presses with his arms outstretched into the void, as if he were striving to make the gunboat heavier and to do violence to the sea.

Suddenly he punches the air and bursts into laughter!... The four eyelets had done their duty; the gunboat, heavy with waves, had reached the plugs there where the light of the Sun did not reach. And the blackening human swarm on the shore acclaims us with a deluge of cheers, with a feverish waving of rags and hats.

The tormented were exulting at the defeat of the tormentors!

Now Cousin completes his mysterious manoeuvre and resolutely turns the bow toward the North. I shout at him nervously: "What are you doing?"

"Be quiet! The *Abeille* will soon give us chase and it is on the coasts of Guiana that they will expect to catch us. To the north, to the north! Freedom is amidst the tempests; our safety is in danger!"

We sailed strenuously the entire night, always turned toward the Pole. Great wind, great waves! The Ocean covered us with salt spray, rumbling with a great voice in the darkness.

Was it admonishing us? Was it reclaiming us? Our senses, terribly tense, could not understand it... Now it thrust us upward as if to cast us out of its domain; now we were drawn precipitously toward the abyss, as by a powerful, irresistible suction. Our only reliance was the marble impassivity of the captain. No human voice, amidst that noisy fury, but his, measured, calm, cold, metallic...

The dawn found us soaked and shrivelled. But we had left behind the waters muddied by the great rivers poured into them from the Continent, and we were already ploughing the deep azure. CHAPTER THE TWENTY-FOURTH

DURING my hours of rest, I neglected nothing in order to obtain full intelligence of the men who lived and suffered about me. Antonio Gomez, having been assigned for some time to the service of the Officers, had recovered, though bronzed in colour, his good-natured priest’s face. But an unexpected order removed him from any influence I might have over him. He was dispatched, with others, to the penitentiary at Corù, on the Continent, and I had barely time to press his hand. From that day forth, though I believe the political upheavals of France may have procured him better days, I never saw him again nor heard any tidings of him.

Yet on the island there were other Italians, a dozen or so; Romans for the most part, all condemned for having redoubled their political hatred by superimposing upon it a private one; for having avenged, with our native tyranny, the outrage inflicted upon them by a foreign tyranny. Christ forgave much, it is said, to those who loved much; He ought to forgive far more to those who hated much and who, in their hatred, knew how to find the virtue of not being sheep. Among these Romans there was one Morelli, a tailor, condemned to life imprisonment. Who lends a readier ear to hope than he who has lost all hope?

I found moreover Italians condemned, or simply relegated, for political motives. They dwelt in part on the Île du Diable; but they crossed over frequently at low tide, on the pretext, more often than not, of availing themselves of the dispensary attached to the Hospital. There was Galli, there was Rossini, there was Tebaldi, and we quickly struck up a fraternal intimacy. Their history was known to me, and I could be certain that even were they not to take part in the escape, they would have aided it with every means at their disposal.

Among the men I had met during my first crossing from Toulon, and who had been landed on the island from the very beginning, was a French archpriest, condemned to life for the murder of a child of whom he had taken criminal advantage. Throughout the entire voyage, he had jealously kept under his arm a great Bible of his own. In a moment of expansiveness, he confided to me in a trembling voice that he had concealed, within the binding of that volume, something in the nature of twenty thousand francs, and that the tormenting thought of being strangled by hands covetous of what was his gnawed at him more than remorse. He intended to employ that money to procure the means of escaping. I kept it in mind and reckoned upon it. To save up the indispensable provisions from our limited daily ration required a sacrifice too grievous and too prolonged for our hunger and for my impatience. They would need to be purchased from the penitentiary stores gradually, so as not to arouse suspicion, paying into the bargain usurious prices. Why should not the archpriest furnish us with the *conquibus* — the wherewithal?

But my good fortune willed it that on the island there was another individual who might prove far more useful to me still. He was a former officer of the French navy, a rabid Orleanist, a man all daring and all nerve; he was serving a life sentence, one further circumstance which I reckoned favourable and would almost call propitious. That man had already battled storms; the Ocean held no terrors for him; it was not for him a boundless plain without pathways.

All these thoughts, spokes of a broken wheel, remained for a long while motionless and as if crystallised in my mind, until all at once they received an extraordinary impulse through the almost spontaneous formation of the very piece I had lacked; which in vain, during my long meditations, I had endeavoured to devise.

One evening, having left the workshop, I was making my way towards my barracks, which lay a good mile distant, when, having come close to the jetty, I caught sight of Morbioli bent over a gunboat, polishing its brasswork with perfect composure. The gunboats were in reality modest longboats carrying, fore and aft, a toy cannon apiece. They were lowered into the water early in the morning and hauled back up dry at five in the evening.

Morbioli, a boatman in Rome, was assigned to their cleaning. How many times I had watched him busy about those trinkets that were the bane of my existence! But only now, by some singular disposition of my spirit, do those soft rubbings of his, which had the feeling of a caress, stir up a great vexation in me, and I cry inwardly: “Stave it in, you great blockhead; sink it, if you can!”

That simple word — “sink it!” — flashed through my mind like a bolt of lightning illuminating a pitch-dark night. It was a revelation entire, and I shuddered at it.

I approached Morbioli, and I believe my face betrayed the secret start within me, for he looked at me with evident unease and said: “What on earth is the matter!” — Morbioli, when I was down there at the Montagne d’Argent, work had begun on erecting a hospital and we had already reached the whitewashing. A convict who held me in great affection was one day perched atop his ladder, brushing the façade, when from a nearby window there crept the murmur of voices. It was the Commandant, the Head Warder, and a condemned man in conference together. The whitewasher distinctly heard my name pronounced and stood stock-still to listen. I shall tell you briefly: the Commandant was guaranteeing the convict his immediate freedom provided he could succeed in implicating me in some escape attempt, in such a manner as to give the sentinels occasion to shoot me dead. — Why do you tell me this? — To persuade you that in the registers, beside my name, a cross is marked, and that even if I were a man contemptible enough to draw you into a conspiracy with the intention of denouncing you... what good would it do me? For me there would be no reprieve in any case. Now listen...

I confided to him the secret workings of my mind, the circumstances and the persons upon which I founded my hopes; the tactics which would undoubtedly make us masters of the boat and of the fishermen; the stratagem which he himself, unwittingly, putting an end to my long torments, had inspired in me. This was precisely the part I meant to entrust to his loyalty and his intelligence.

He scratched his head, as was his habit in moments of anxiety; then he raised his face, and I read his wholehearted assent in eyes that were ablaze. There and then, I received a compliment of a flavour as purely *trasteverino* as could be; the strangest that has ever been addressed to me in all my long existence: “You’re a devil! Blessed be the woman who bore you — and may you die... die murdered!”

With the assent of Morbioli, the first link of the chain was forged. The others were added one by one, slowly, but admirably.

On a cloudy night, troubled by the din of a fierce storm at sea, I slipped silently from my barracks and, armed with a chisel, crept along the rocks as far as the warehouses, a large timber structure founded in part upon piles. I tried and tried again, and managed at last to prise up one of the planking boards. I crawled inside, groping, nosing out the way like a rat, and when I emerged I had upon my back six great hammocks of stout canvas, which Morelli, the Roman tailor, found the means to sew up with perfect workmanship. We had our sail!
