## Chapter Nine

THANKS to our connections with the Circolo del Paradiso, we were, from the very moment of our arrival in Paris, fraternally welcomed by the Club of Batignolles. We were put to work making cartridges.

The rooms of the Club were permanently occupied by some forty revolutionaries... not a corner in which to lodge ourselves. But we ended by finding somewhere to lay our heads in the house of a Turinese hatter, residing in the faubourg Saint-Antoine, at the opposite extremity of the metropolis.

To rise before the Sun, to go about our rounds cheerfully, revisiting the vicissitudes of the past; to nourish ourselves with Spartan frugality; to submit ourselves to the life of the exploited, to comprehend their sorrows and their hopes, to share in them; to return love for love with a frank and open heart; to refine fraternally the capable but benighted intellect; to lend a hand in preparing its vindication... such was our life until the 2nd of December.

Quickening our step on the second of December 1851, as we made our way to our usual meeting-place, we had occasion to find ourselves greatly surprised. The Place de la Bastille was under military occupation; we halted. Infantry and cannon. The gunners with their match-cords lit, as though a battle were imminent. More soldiers we found drawn up along the boulevard. In the faubourg, beyond the barrière, a sudden coming and going of columns moving with grave deliberation..., the silence that presages a storm.

Hastening our step and working upon our conjectures, we soon arrived at the mouth of the Rue de la Paix. Here we stopped short.

Opposite the seat of the Club stood a hedge of Guards. We watched the workers employed in the manufacture of cartridges emerge from the gateway two by two, four by four, brutally shoved along by the gendarmes.

The affiliated Clubs had to be warned. In retracing our route, we were compelled to satisfy ourselves that the soldiers were not posted merely for a show of force. Singly, but with an ill-omened frequency, shots rang out. We were making for the Club of Saint-Denis. Before reaching it, we very nearly stumbled over several corpses... workers in their blouses, unarmed, who had gone out without suspicion and been treacherously launched into eternity!

We found at the Club a number of citizens, nerved tight with uncertainty, pallid, incapable of resolution. I recounted what I had seen... and exhorted them not to let themselves be slaughtered like sheep, but to act. Immediately the furniture is piled up into the street; the paving-stones are prised up, men labour feverishly to raise a barricade. A red kerchief, tied to a stick, flutters above that improvised bulwark and is transformed into a banner of Liberty. But before our work was even finished, a battalion of chasseurs from Vincennes, wheeling round the corner at the end of the street, advanced upon us at a run.

We were sixteen Frenchmen and two Italians. Not a finger was moved until the assailants were within certain range... then we opened fire simultaneously. No sooner had the smoke thinned than we opened fire again, with a ragged fusillade.

The assault was attempted twice and twice repulsed. At the third attempt, the battalion, closing its ranks, hurled itself forward with a temper manifestly enraged and a resolution equal to the pitch of its fury. A third volley of lead was awaited... which never came, and they scaled the barricade with shouting, striking blindly with their bayonets. They found not a living creature before them!

The few had held out against the many because they were sustained by the hope that the Parisian people would come rushing up to reinforce the resistance, with that marvellous ardour which is so characteristic of them. The shops, the windows, the doors had remained closed; the street desperately deserted. Once the last cartridge was spent, we took counsel together. What would our sacrifice have availed? We decided to disperse. The red kerchief, still fluttering, had baffled the assassins. The officer commanding them, deeming himself mocked by it, avenged himself by seizing it with a kick... not the least, perhaps, of the superhuman merits which afterwards earned him the ribbon of the Legion of Honour!

But what precisely had occurred; what was being prepared in the other quarters of the city? By what arts was Louis Napoleon consummating the Coup d'État?

It was in the dead of night that he had begun his dark work. The "suspect" generals — those whom he had been unable or unwilling to corrupt; those who, enjoying some measure of popularity, might conceivably have placed themselves at the head of an insurrection — were arrested in their beds. Simultaneously, every printing-house in the metropolis was seized and placed under seal; a trusted guard was stationed at the foot of every bell-tower. The Parisian people had not yet awakened, and already they had lost every means of publishing any protest whatsoever, of sounding the call to arms; of sending forth, from on high, a resonant stroke of alarm. Nor was even this to suffice. Louis Napoleon set the compositors of the Imprimerie Nationale to work with a pistol at their throats, and compelled Paris, compelled France, to speak on his behalf, to lend a semblance of legality to his brigandage. From the earliest hours of the morning, the still-drowsy population of the city was reading on every wall three documents that have since remained celebrated: the Proclamation to the People, the Proclamation to the Army, the Decree by which the President of the Republic dissolved the National Assembly, restored universal suffrage, convoked the electoral assemblies, declared a state of siege, and dissolved the Council of State.

This last document bore, alongside the name of "Louis Napoleon Bonaparte," that of "de Morny," Minister of the Interior. Those two men, strangers before the Law, bound by blood through the mysterious embraces of adultery, had strengthened the ties of blood by making themselves brothers in crime as well.

That same day the militia cleared the chamber of the National Assembly at bayonet-point, and the Police undertook to disperse the Supreme Court, which had assembled to protest, in homage to the Constitution, against the new parricide. The prisons of Mazas were crammed with representatives of the People.

But those Deputies of the Left had remained at large who, through some misunderstanding, had attended the Assembly only in the smallest numbers. To them fell the arduous task of effacing the outrage and saving the Republic. Not a printing-press, not arms, not powder; a people stupefied, paralysed by a fatal bewilderment; a forest of bayonets on every side, an entire Army Corps master of the strategic points, a Police force sold to the malefactor. To escape being shot, they were obliged to disguise themselves and to change their meeting-place every day. They acted with manly courage, they knew also how to face martyrdom with serenity... a blaze of heroism! but they could not — they were unable to — overcome the greatest obstacle of all: their own prejudices!

The nightmare of legality dominated them; the spectre of the Constitution held their arms fast, and they deemed it greatness not to cast it off — in such moments as these!

They were seen confronting the soldiers there with speeches, soldiers drunk with brandy and athirst for blood; opposing to the lead... dissertations upon the duties of the good citizen. They put their faith in the insignia of their own dignity, like devotees who await portents from a relic; they advanced, with great conviction, the inviolability of their persons... Language fitting enough in the mouth of a despot; ridiculous in that of men who lacked the force to sustain their own pretension.

In brief: indomitable courage, sublime self-abnegation... senile childishness!

The arid political questions left the masses cold, for they felt instinctively, if not with full consciousness, their emptiness. People who toiled from morning to night for a meagre subsistence could not have been moved by anything short of immediate improvements in their material existence. Certainly universal suffrage interested, more or less, a good number of the non-intellectual as well, and the Assembly, or rather its remnants, hastened to cancel the error they had committed and to restore it. But Louis Napoleon had forestalled them... their act seemed a forced concession and was derided.

Victor Hugo, in whose vast mind the truth flashed for an instant, proposed to his colleagues the immediate abolition of the octroi and the tax on spirits. For some time the People had been clamouring against those fiscal measures. The great lyric poet obtained that a decree to that effect be drawn up. But it availed nothing. Once again the practical men proved themselves shorter-sighted than the poets. They chose to defer its publication until the morrow of a problematical victory, thereby spitting, virtually, upon the most efficacious means of securing it. "If the People do not understand," said they, "that we are risking our necks in defence of their rights; if they do not fight, they are unworthy of any concession, unworthy of liberty." Folly from an attitude lofty and disdainful, but folly none the less. Perhaps those men, who faced death serenely every day, every hour, were afraid of giving the appearance of evoking the spectre of the red republic.

Another great mind, reputed fanciful, who had emerged from prison by chance on temporary leave — he was languishing there for an offence against Louis Napoleon — found the means of injecting among those irresolute men a note of cold-blooded positiveness. I allude to Proudhon.

"You are the victims of your own illusions. The People have let themselves be hoodwinked. They will not move, or they will move to help Bonaparte. A piece of foolery — the restoration of universal suffrage — has hooked the fools, who are always the majority. The usurper passes for a socialist. He means to be Emperor and he will be. It is a crime of impudence, but impudence triumphs when backed by bayonets. He has on his side the cannon, popular prejudices, the errors and unpopularity of the Assembly. Your attempts are doomed to fail. You, Deputies of the Left, are honest men; he is a rogue, and that too is a great advantage for him. You have scruples; he has none. Desist from the struggle. Given the circumstances, much may perhaps be attempted; but nothing obtained. And you will do nothing save drag a thousand, two thousand, ten thousand generous souls down into your own ruin."

Prophetic words! But was it in human nature to receive them; to smooth thus the highway for the enemy?

The misfortune is that the most logical, the most certain, the most expeditious, the least cruel means of paralysing the parricide occurred to no one, or at the very least no one dared to speak of it. Louis Napoleon had meditated the Coup d'État; he was the pivot around which the foul conspiracy revolved. The pivot had to be struck; Louis Napoleon had to be removed. By what strange aberration was the blood of one man, who, by perjury, was enslaving the Fatherland, to weigh in the minds of those representatives of the People more heavily than the lives of thousands upon thousands of innocents?

Victor Hugo wrote, from exile, masterly pages upon the terrible power of the "octopus." He knew that the blade of the knife slides over the tentacles of the monster, which releases its prey and falls like a rag the moment the iron is turned upon the one truly vulnerable point — the mouth that creates the vacuum and lends strength to the tentacles. But had a voice arisen within the Assembly calling up a hero Gilliatt against the baleful "octopus" of the Empire, he — Victor Hugo himself — would have been the first to hurl the thunderbolts of his indignant eloquence to execrate "the shame of a common assassination." A great and ingenuous dreamer, having stirred the Parisian People to raise barricades, to fight and to kill, he carried his daring so far as to confront the enemy's lead unarmed; he carried his sentimentalism so far as to rebuke the friend who held out to him the weapon with which to defend himself and to strike. A puerile contradiction in a sublime intellect!

But the horror of blood was not to restrain "the man without scruples." The regiments and batteries posted in the faubourgs were, by his order, concentrated on the Boulevard Montmartre. To whet the martial enthusiasm, ten thousand francs' worth of brandy had been distributed to each brigade. The hordes of passive obedience do not attain to the higher reaches of heroism without the fumes of alcohol. As for those at the top, lavish assurances of more imposing shoulder-straps and bulging envelopes "to meet any contingency" had been circulated. And for fear this should not prove sufficient, the influence of several high-class prostitutes was also skilfully brought to bear... the prostitutes who afterwards became one of the most salient characteristics of the Second Empire. The soldiers remained for several hours, weapon in hand, in silence, forcibly immobile, like hounds straining at the leash. Suddenly, shot from no one knew where, fired by no one knew precisely whom, the report of a rifle rang out. It was a signal.

All, simultaneously, began to kill. It was not arms levelled against arms; it was the merciless hunting of the inoffensive citizen attending quietly to his own affairs; it was premeditated slaughter carried out to terrorise, for the lust of slaughter. Soon, added to the intoxication of the alcohol, came that of blood, and disorder and death spread outward from the boulevard to an entire district. Foot-soldiers, lancers, cuirassiers advance in close order, cutting down all those guilty of being found in the street. Nor is not being there a sufficient defence... they fire into the courtyards; through the gratings, down into the cellars; they pour volleys of lead through the windows. As the killing runs wild it becomes by degrees a monotonous business. How to enliven it? The human beasts make a game of it; wagers are laid, laughingly, on the certainty of the aim; the target for this infernal contest — the women and the children. Soldiers were seen inflaming the frenzy of assassination by torturing the dying; widening their wounds with the steel.

After the killing with small arms, the cannon. The slaughter lasted three hours... a holocaust of several thousand persons: Napoleon announced to Europe that he had... saved "the social order." Blessed, with cowardly gratitude, by Pius IX, he set forth from so glorious a beginning, dragging France after him, towards the dark abyss of Sedan!

But Louis Napoleon never dared to publish the revealing figures. A cowardly malefactor, having struck his victims to death, he turns livid at the sight of the enormous number of corpses and takes every care to spirit them away from his own eyes, no less than from those of others.

Night descended... The centre of Paris bristles with barricades; but the People are nearly annihilated by terror and do not stir. Defending them stand, weaponless and despairing of victory, a few manly consciences... an ephemeral obstacle to the tyrant's artillery.

Nor, with victory achieved, does the carnage-lust of the executioner abate. The prisons are bursting with the wretched. How to make room for the crowds that keep arriving? The answer that came from above is brief and terrible: "Kill!" In the night they are abruptly awakened and chosen at random. Neither their name is asked, nor whence they come, nor what station they occupy in the social order... what matters is to reach the fatally established number. Three hundred and thirty-seven are thus dragged at one time from the vaults of the Tuileries, marched between a triple line of soldiers to the Champ de Mars, struck down, buried that same night in the cemetery of Montmartre, with their heads protruding above the ground... a lugubrious expedient of identification!

The horrible tragedy is repeated for twelve consecutive nights, at the Champ de Mars, in the courtyard of the Prefecture, in the adjacent Rue de Jérusalem. The slaughter is perpetrated at the mouth of the sewer, so that the human blood, flowing into it, may leave scant traces and not reveal the enormity of the hecatomb.

Then came the persecutions sanctioned by Law. What Law? Louis Napoleon creates the Military Commissions and the Mixed Commissions. It will suffice to indicate the manner in which the former conduct their proceedings. They sit with closed doors. They receive from above a list of names, they run through it — the accused being absent — and against each name they place, without further inquiry, a mark which, according to the case, has the power to acquit, or to send a wretch to Lambesa or, worse yet, to the dry guillotine of Cayenne.

In a brief space of time, the Military Commissions and the Mixed Commissions plunge forty thousand families into mourning! By such deeds does one acquire the sceptre "by divine right!"
