CHAPTER THE TENTH

THE barricade having been abandoned to its fate, Trolli and I made our way towards the boulevard. But scarcely had we crossed the Pont Saint Denis when we were forced to acknowledge that martial law was being rigorously enforced and that the surest way to court death was to walk in company. We found bodies scattered here and there. I recall still, with great vividness, that of a water-seller, lying face downward, his vessel upon his back, his arms outstretched, his head literally shattered to fragments... Blood, brains and slices of lemon mingled in a horrible confusion!

We parted, with the understanding that we should not lose sight of one another, and that we would quit the city by the Barrière du Trône. But to pass it, guarded as it was by a sound body of customs officers and soldiers, was rather a ticklish undertaking. Before reaching it, I bought a large loaf and tucked it under my arm. Trolli divined my purpose and did likewise. As workmen returning from their labours we were looked up and down from head to foot, but suffered no molestation.

And when we reached the open country, the bare and wintry fields, we came together again. Trolli asked me where we should go to lay our heads.

"Where shall we go? To the devil, but free! Where shall we lay our heads? And what does it matter to you where, so long as it be not within the lunette of the guillotine? Switzerland is far away. But we have known how to cross Spain — we shall cross France as well!"

We had covered half a dozen miles when we were overtaken by a traveller in a little carriage. Upon seeing us, he drew in the reins and invited us to climb up. I cast a searching glance at him. He was decently dressed and had the look of a good-natured, timid countryman. His nose and his eyes were exceedingly red, and not from the cold alone... he could not be a spy. And besides... he was alone, while we were two, and possessed of resolution. We accepted.

As soon as the horse had resumed his trot, the good man asked us where we were bound.

— To Troy.

— A long walk! I am not going so far; but I will shorten the road for you.

And he began to give an account of himself and his affairs. He had gone to Paris to sell vegetables, the produce of his own little kitchen-garden and his own labours. And he showed us his calloused hands. The years had been rather lean; but with a little thrift and keeping his eyes open he had never gone without his rabbit and his small wine. And he launched forth, spreading out the long litany of his amours with the gossips of the neighbourhood. Of Paris, of the coup d'état, of the horrors of that day — not a phrase, not a word; as though he had left behind him a city perfectly at peace and still. The wine he had swallowed made him talkative, but was not enough to draw him out of a prudent reserve.

At a certain point there escaped him some remark about certain speculations of his, and he concluded that if they prospered according to his hopes he would have enough to live comfortably upon until the "Lady without a nose" came to cut him down with her scythe. And with that allusion to Death he fell silent, as if absorbed in profound meditation. Ten minutes later he was snoring.

I thereupon took possession of the reins, and laid on the whip — trot, horse, trot. We passed broad stretches of land, we passed through coppices, we passed through villages... twilight came, night fell... the peasant was snoring still...

All of a sudden, in the still air, there rang out slowly the brazen toll of a bell. It was eleven o'clock. A little later the wheels of the carriage were grating over cobblestones. And then the horse planted his feet and, for all my whipping, would not budge a step forward or a step backward.

The strange thing was that this abrupt halt had the power of breaking the peasant's heavy slumber. "Sacré cœur!" he exclaimed, after peering into the darkness. "Where have you brought me? Twelve miles from home!"

He falls to blaming — mark the justice of the world! — the horse, who might well have remembered where his manger was. But he ends by consoling himself with the thought that we were not in a desert. In the village he had good friends. "Do you see that strip of light? The inn is still open and the host is an honest man. I shall stand you a drink!"

Half an hour later he was lying down in order to be off betimes. Trolli and I went out into the open air. Warmed within and without, we once more confronted the darkness of the countryside and those yet denser darknesses of the unknown.

Our subversive Society spread its net over the whole of France, and the threads of that net were knotted again at Troy, so that it was not difficult for us to make our contacts there and to obtain a place on the stage-coach as far as Langres, the locality where the meshes, drawing together, formed the next knot.

The horses were still stamping under the archway of the inn at Langres when I was already making my way in haste to the house of the Postal Officer. A letter of introduction, a few agreed signs, and he would procure us the means to continue our journey. I pulled the bell and there appeared at once upon the threshold a lady of uncertain years, but not without a certain charm.

— My husband is not at home, nor do I know when he will return.

— I am the bearer of a letter which I should like to deliver to him in person...

The lady's mouth contracted, as though she were holding a slice of lemon between tongue and palate. She became inquisitive: A letter? From whom? Give it here, give it! I will undertake to deliver it... into his own hands.

— I beg your pardon! I have my orders and...

Her face had grown pale. She insisted and stretched forth her right hand... her fingers trembled. So much the more did I consider prudence a duty. She went back indoors, slamming the door behind her.

That evening I was seated in the common-room of the inn, before the fireplace, conversing cheerfully with other guests, when, to interrupt the pleasant process of digestion, there appeared unannounced the shrewish creature with her countenance at once sharp and melancholy. She had two gendarmes at her back.

The purpose of the visit was made plain at once. The lady demanded the surrender of the mysterious letter. The gendarmes had come to impound it.

I rose, drew out my pocket-book calmly, and, removing the letter of introduction, called the attention of those present to the fact that it lay in an open envelope. Then, with a bound towards the fireplace, I cast it into the flames.

There followed some little confusion. The gendarmes, who considered "the dignity of the uniform" to have been affronted, wished to proceed forthwith; but the order they had received was to seize the document and not to arrest its bearer. "We shall go for more precise instructions." They left, without arresting me, the more readily since the bystanders had been unable to refrain from bursting into unrestrained laughter. I was compelled to protest with all my energy. The very Laws of the Republic specified that only sealed letters were liable to seizure; I had therefore, and with perfect right, resisted an abuse of authority.

And indeed the two gentlemen had placed themselves on false ground. The Law was against those who were supposed to uphold it; and, followed by the lady, discomfited and well-nigh in tears, they departed, and I saw them no more.

Late that night, however, the Postal Officer appeared, and I had the key to the riddle. His wife, an honest woman and of great merit, had the weakness of being jealous to the point of madness. This passion of hers had of late been inflamed by the mysterious coming and going inherent in our political Society, of whose existence she was unaware. Having fixed in her head the notion that I was bringing her husband the secret missive of some rival, she had made this ill-judged attempt to intercept evidence of guilt which existed only in her imagination.

Once the Swiss frontier was crossed, all our clandestine network failed us, and from that point there began the truly arduous portion of our pilgrimage. Trolli had written to his relatives to send him money to Berne. We had to trudge all the way there: our purses desperately empty, in the heart of winter, with snow lying deep, barely clothed.

We arrived. But the frost of free Helvetia had nearly crystallised itself, like fringe, about our hearts. As at Barcelona, so now at Berne — there was no money!

Night fell inexorable. We lay down in the market-place, beneath the stall of a greengrocer. Despite the fast and the bitter cold, we fell into a profound sleep. Blessed is youth!

But at some point in the night I was startled awake by a peremptory voice cursing in German. A very simple thing had occurred. Trolli was endowed with legs that called to mind a trebuchet; as he slept, he had stretched them out as far as the pavement and was, quite involuntarily, tripping a constable who was passing by. He was dragged out of the hiding-place like a sack of rags and pelted with questions. But he did not understand a word of German and, by way of reply, merely stretched and yawned. The policeman ended by losing patience, and seizing him by the collar shoved him forward with curses and buffets. I heard the heavy double-step fade into the distance; then I drew my jacket closer about my chest and went back to sleep. What good would it have done to intervene?

At daybreak I strolled about the solid prison building, whistling a little tune well known to Trolli. I was answered by a brief whistle of recognition, and immediately, framed between the bars of a small window, appeared the face of my Cardinal.

Farewell discretion! I waved my cap furiously and called out my greeting with the liveliness of a boy escaping from the stale air of a schoolroom. Nor did Trolli respond with less enthusiasm. And there and then he let me know that, in deference to the Law against vagabondage, the Police would have him escorted to the Italian frontier. "Do you promise to follow after me?"

I had barely time to extend my hand in solemn pledge.

A warder, hearing the commotion, had seized Trolli by his long dangling legs, and the cardinal face vanished.

That day I wandered long about the market, and, snatching up a turnip here and there, found a way of quelling the cramps in my stomach. When night fell again I took shelter, like Diogenes, in a barrel. My sufferings were such that, by dawn, I had almost made up my mind to give myself up to the Police.

But from that very extremity chance offered me, in a most singular fashion, the thread that gave me the means of revival. I had sunk upon a bench in the Public Gardens and was gnawing at the stem of my pipe, to deceive myself... the supply of tobacco had long since been exhausted. The paths were deserted; snow covered the green expanses, and upon it a flock of sparrows was chirping and hopping. Despite the rigours of winter, they had breakfasted.

Vexed, I seized a small stone, and among those happy creatures there passed, like a thunderbolt, the characteristic protest of empty stomachs. One sparrow gave a cry, turned its little legs in the air, and lay stiff.

This was no moment for hesitation. In like circumstances, man eats man... I could well bring myself to devour a raw sparrow. I was plucking it when I felt a hand laid upon my shoulder.

— What are you doing, young man?

— Taking a snack.

— You must have the hunger of a hawk. In any case, praise Divine Providence, which has known how to provide for your fast.

Beside me sat a neat, respectable-looking elderly man; a Protestant minister, in all probability. I pointed out to him that the Lord God had provided for my needs through the slaughter of the innocent, and in a manner too derisory for me to feel my heart overflowing with gratitude.

He jingled some silver coins in his pocket. After a brief silence: "Will you sell me your pipe for six francs?"

I pocketed the money; the stranger pocketed the pipe. Then he began: "You see that our Heavenly Father..."

As if I meant to waste my time in philosophising. I sprang up — four long strides — and gave myself no rest until I found myself seated where the sweet fragrance of roasting meat was drifting through the air.

With what remained, I was able to make my way to Lucerne, where the double problem of feeding myself and pressing on was to be solved for me, of all people, by a man against whom I had fought — Pope Mastai Ferretti.

Switzerland is a marvellous country. United by its beauties, upon those slopes, among those valleys, peoples of diverse origin, tongue and character live together in peace... a miracle of Liberty, a glorious anticipation of a holy Utopia.

And yet those peoples have failed to show the elevation of spirit needed to assume the full weight of their historic mission. In centuries gone by the heart of Europe beat mightily among the Alps; but in vain! As from the pale mountain ridges the waters rush down to furrow the fertile plains and water and fecundate so great a part of the Continent, so from crags inaccessible to the conqueror should those fair and terrible phalanxes have descended, to accomplish a work of fraternity, to revive the hopes of the oppressed, to extend the dominion of Liberty.

They did descend; but as a devastating scourge... blind instruments of the wicked, flesh and souls put up for sale!

Selfishness is fatal to nations because, among other things, the poisoned air of your neighbour, sooner or later, corrupts your own as well, imperils and diminishes your glory. Despotism, defended by Swiss mercenaries, confined within the narrow circle of the mountains the very men who had nurtured it; the Republic remained a puny thing and, in the universal estimation, became a synonym for venality. At the time when I was passing through it, the evil habit was not greatly in force, and already the Federal Government was branding as a criminal both the citizen who was not ashamed to prostitute himself to a foreign Government and the agent who hired him out and made himself his accomplice. Nevertheless Pius IX, who distrusted his own Catholics, maintained secret agents in the Lutheran Cantons, to scrape together, at the price of a few baiocchi, the handful of insects he intended, in his heart of hearts, to use to alter the course of the stars, to annihilate the hopes of the new Italy.

One such agent espied me on the shore of the lake, absorbed in my double problem. Attracted perhaps by my soldierly bearing, he opened conversation in the holy name of God and concluded it by formally proposing that I should shoulder a musket in defence of the pontifical throne and the altar.

My first impulse was to hurl him headfirst into the lake. But a sudden thought restrained me. The Garibaldian tactic!... when you have no ammunition, take it from the enemy. The funds of the Holy See employed to repatriate an adversary of the old order!... my double problem more than resolved! And, incidentally, I might do good service to my country's cause as well, by unmasking the intrigue and clipping the nails of the unctuous agent!

I smiled and, passing myself off as a Swiss from the Canton of Ticino, accepted.

I was conducted through winding lanes to a house of mean appearance; I fixed its number well in my memory, mounted a dirty little staircase, entered a room of equivocal character — something between a study and a bedchamber. The contract was duly drawn up and signed, and a few hours later, from the stern of the steamboat as it put rapidly out from shore, I was bidding farewell with broad gestures to my recruiter.

Arrived at Flüelen, I presented myself at the Commissioner's office and narrated in full what had befallen me. I was brought back to Lucerne for the identification of the individual. I retraced the winding lanes, found the house of the less than modest appearance, remounted the dirty staircase. The bird was in its cage — indeed in bed; the evidence palpable, the Law precise. He was arrested.

While he was dressing, the Federal Delegate drew out a purse and attempted to count out for me some sum of money or other. A flush of colour rose to my face and I burst out: "Italians are ever ready to confront with a tranquil mind any sacrifice, even that of assuming the appearance of a villain. For when the good of the Fatherland is at stake and the lion's leap is not possible, then let the cunning of the fox be welcomed and blessed. But learn this, and let it be your guide: if ever you find one who, having made the sacrifice, stretches out his hand and demands payment, say freely that he was not born beyond your Alps... or, if he was born there, hold it for certain that he is of bastard race, unworthy of the name he bears!" I was escorted back to Flüelen, and then, alone, set out for Göschenen, whence, facing the cold and the storm, I reached the summit of the Saint Gotthard. From up there, something passed across my face like a foretaste of southern warmth; an air saturate with trembling and with sobbing, with hatred and with love.

And I pressed forward almost at a run, with a heart full to bursting — whether with joy or with pity I could not myself have said. At Varese I was received by Trolli as a beloved dead man miraculously returned to life. And in the hospitality of that family I found ample recompense for my hardships. But in Lombardy the Austrian reaction was raging; and the sight of that hateful uniform rekindled the fever in me... and at that age, with my temperament, it is too arduous a task to put a curb upon one's impetuosity. Friends, privy to what was afoot, opportunely breathed a word of counsel. I must not compromise my hosts, and so I had myself escorted to the frontier.

Having taken leave of my escort and remaining alone, I turned to contemplate the beautiful and unhappy Land which, for the second time, I was compelled to leave. In the majesty of the place and the silence, I passed back in memory over all that had been... the desperate charge of the few, armed with nothing but their right, against the serried masses hideous with bayonets; the gaze of the innocent turned heavenward in vain; the silent tears, the streams of blood. And in the exaltation of that moment my thoughts flew from the foot of the Alps all the way south to the farthest tip of Sicily. Everywhere hearts that throbbed, hands that clasped one another in the dark, that in the dark sharpened their blades. Then the great soul of that gentle, vigorous, and unhappy race seemed to enter into me and make my own soul greater. I stretched wide my arms as though I expected to draw into them, lovingly, every one — every one of my companions in bondage. I renewed the inspired oath of Giuseppe Mazzini, and with tears upon my face turned once more upon the road of exile.
