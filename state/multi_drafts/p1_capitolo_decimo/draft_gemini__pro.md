THE barricade abandoned to its fate, Trolli and I made our way toward the "boulevard." But having scarcely crossed the Saint Denis bridge, we had to agree that martial law was being rigorously applied and that the best way to invite death was to walk in pairs. We found corpses almost everywhere. I still vividly recall that of a water-seller, stretched out on his stomach, the tank on his back, his arms outstretched, his head literally smashed to pieces… There appeared, in a horrible mixture, blood, brains, and lemon slices!

We separated, with the understanding that we would not lose sight of each other and would leave the city by the barriera del Trono. But to pass through it, guarded as it was by a good contingent of Finance guards and soldiers, proved a rather thorny undertaking. Before reaching it, I bought a large loaf of bread and tucked it under my arm. Trolli divined my purpose and did the same. As workers returning from their labour, we were scrutinized from head to toe, but without any trouble.

And when we arrived in the open, in the barren countryside, we reunited. Trolli asked me where we would lay our heads.

"Where are we going? To hell, but free! Where will we lay our heads? And what does it matter where, so long as it is not between the lunette of the guillotine? Switzerland is far. But we managed to cross Spain, we shall cross France as well!"

We had covered half a dozen miles when we were overtaken by a traveller in a small carriage. Upon seeing us, he pulled on the reins and invited us to climb aboard. I cast an inquiring glance at him. He was decently dressed and had the air of a decent, spineless countryman. His nose and eyes were extremely red, and not only from the cold… he could not be a spy. And besides… he was alone, there were two of us, and we were not lacking in nerve. We accepted.

As soon as the horse had resumed its trot, the good man asked us where we were headed.

— To Troy.

— A long walk! I am not going so far; but I will shorten the road for you.

And he began to give an account of himself and his affairs. He had gone to Paris to sell vegetables, the produce of his own small garden and his own labours. And he showed us his calloused hands. The years were rather lean; but with a little thrift and with his eyes open, rabbit and a bit of wine had never been lacking for him. And on he went, unreeling the long litany of his amours with the women of the neighbourhood. Of Paris, of the coup d'état, of the horrors of that day, not a phrase, not a word; as if he had left behind a city perfectly at peace and silent. The wine he had drunk made him loquacious, but was not enough to draw him from a prudent reserve.

At a certain point, he let slip something about certain speculations of his and concluded that if they proceeded according to his hopes, he would have enough to live on comfortably until the "Noseless Lady" arrived to strike him with her scythe. And with that allusion to Death he fell silent, as if absorbed in deep meditation. Ten minutes later he was snoring.

Then I took possession of the reins, and on with the whip and on trotted the horse. We passed great expanses, we passed thickets, we passed villages… twilight came, night fell… the peasant was still snoring…

Suddenly, in the still air, the bronze tolling of a bell rang out slowly. It was eleven o'clock. Shortly thereafter the wheels of the carriage screeched over cobblestones. And then the horse balked and, however much I lashed it, would not take a step forward or a step back.

The strange thing is that this abrupt halt had the virtue of breaking the peasant's deep sleep. "*Sacre coeur!*" he exclaimed after having peered into the darkness. "Where have you brought me? Twelve miles from home!"

He takes it out—see the justice of the world!—on the horse, which should have remembered where its manger was. But he ends up consoling himself with the thought that we were not in a desert. In the village he had good friends. "See that sliver of light? The tavern is still open and the innkeeper is an honest man. I will buy you a drink!"

Half an hour later, he was turning in so that he could set off again in good time. Trolli and I went outside. Warmed inside and out, we once again faced the darkness of the countryside and the even denser darkness of the unknown.

Our subversive Society enveloped all of France in a network whose threads were re-knotted even in Troy, so it was not difficult for us to make acquaintances there and obtain passage on the stagecoach to Langres, the locality where the links, rejoining, formed the next knot.

The horses were still stamping under the portico of the hotel in Langres as I was already hurrying to the home of the Postal Officer. A letter of recommendation, a few conventional signs, and he would have procured for us the means to continue our journey. I pulled the bell-cord and a lady of uncertain age, but not without beauty, immediately appeared on the threshold.

— My husband is not at home, nor do I know when he will return.

— I am the bearer of a letter which I would like to deliver to him personally…

The lady's mouth contracted, as if she were squeezing a lemon between her tongue and her palate. She became inquisitive: A letter? from whom? Give it, give it! I myself will undertake to deliver it to him… into his own hands.

— Forgive me! I have my orders and…

Her face had grown pale. She insisted and held out her right hand… her fingers were trembling. All the more did I deem prudence necessary. She went back inside, slamming the door.

In the evening, I was seated in the hotel drawing-room, before the fireplace, and conversing cheerfully with other patrons when, to delay the sweet preparation of the chyle, the shrewish creature appeared unannounced, her face at once harsh and melancholic. She had two gendarmes behind her.

The purpose of the visit was immediately made clear. The lady demanded the surrender of the mysterious letter. The gendarmes had come to seize it.

I stood up, calmly drew out my wallet and, taking out the letter of recommendation, pointed out to the bystanders that it was in an open envelope. Then, with a leap toward the fireplace, I threw it on the fire.

A bit of a commotion ensued. The gendarmes, who considered "the dignity of the uniform" offended, wanted to proceed, without further ado; but the order received was to seize the document and not to arrest its bearer. "We shall go for more precise instructions." They went out, and I immediately protested my arrest; all the more so since the patrons had been unable to keep from laughing uproariously. I had to protest with all my energy. The very Laws of the Republic specified that only sealed letters were subject to seizure; I had therefore, with perfect right, extricated myself from an abuse of power.

And, in truth, the two gentlemen had put themselves on false ground. The Law was contrary to its defenders; and, followed by the lady, humiliated and almost in tears, they left, and I saw them no more.

Late in the hour, the Postal Officer himself appeared, and I had the key to the enigma. His wife, an honest woman of great merit, had the weakness of being jealous to the point of madness. This passion of hers had recently been exacerbated by the mysterious coming and going inherent to our political Society, of whose existence she was unaware. Having got it into her head that I was carrying a secret missive to her husband from some rival, she had attempted this rash step to intercept the proof of transgressions that existed only in her imagination.

Once we had passed the Swiss border, all secret influence failed us, and from that point began the truly arduous part of our pilgrimage. Trolli had written to his relatives to send him money in Bern. We had to leg it all the way there: our purses desperately empty, in the heart of winter, the snow so deep, ourselves barely covered.

We arrived. But the frost of free Helvetia very nearly crystallized, in fringes, around our hearts. As in Barcelona, so too in Bern, the money was not there!

Night fell, inexorable. We lay down in the Market square, under a greengrocer's stall. Despite our hunger and the intense cold, we fell soundly asleep. Blessed youth!

But at a certain point in the night, I was woken with a start by an imperious voice swearing in German. A very simple thing was happening. Trolli was endowed with legs that recalled a trebuchet; while he slept, he had stretched them out to the pavement and, involuntarily, tripped a guard who was passing by. He was dragged, like a sack of rags, from his hiding place and peppered with questions. But he did not understand a word of German and, for all reply, he stretched and yawned. The policeman finally lost his patience and, grabbing him by the collar, shoved him onward with curses and shoves. I heard the heavy tread of the two fade into the distance; then I pulled my blouse tight around my chest and went back to sleep. What good would it have done to intervene?

When day broke, I circled the solid building of the prison, whistling a cabaletta well known to Trolli. A brief whistle answered in reply, and immediately, framed between the bars of a small window, appeared the face of my Cardinal.

Farewell, circumspection! I furiously waved my cap and shouted my greeting with the vivacity of a boy slipping out from the mephitic air of a schoolhouse. Nor did Trolli reply with less enthusiasm. And right then and there, he informed me that, in homage to the Law on vagrancy, the Police would have him escorted to the borders of Italy. "Do you promise to follow me?"

I barely had time to extend my hand solemnly.

A turnkey, hearing the din, had seized Trolli by his long, dangling legs, and the cardinal's face disappeared.

That day, I wandered about the Market for a long time and, snatching a turnip here and there, found a way to calm the cramps in my stomach. When night fell again, I took shelter, like Diogenes, in a barrel. My sufferings were such that, at dawn, I very nearly decided to give myself up to the Police.

But precisely from that extremity, chance offered me, in the strangest way, the thread that gave me the opportunity to rise again. I had let myself fall onto a bench in the Public Gardens and was chewing, to delude myself, on the stem of my pipe… the supply of tobacco had been exhausted long ago. The avenues were deserted; snow covered the green expanses and on it a flock of sparrows was chirping and hopping. Despite the rigors of winter, they had had their breakfast.

Annoyed, I grabbed a small stone and among those happy beings there passed, like a thunderbolt, the characteristic protest of empty stomachs. One sparrow gave a cry, turned its little legs to the air, and lay stiff.

This was no time for hesitation. In similar circumstances, man eats man… I could well adapt myself to devouring a raw sparrow. I was plucking it when I felt a hand placed on my shoulder.

— What are you doing, young man?

— Having a snack.

— You must be as hungry as a hawk. In any case, praise divine Providence that has known how to remedy your fasting.

Beside me was a little old man with the neat air of the bourgeoisie; a Protestant minister, probably. I pointed out to him that the Lord God had provided for my needs with the slaughter of the innocent and in a manner too derisory for me to feel my heart overflowing with gratitude.

He jingled a few silver coins in his pocket. After a brief silence: "Will you sell me your pipe for six francs?"

I pocketed the money, the stranger pocketed the pipe. Then he began: "You see that our Heavenly Father…"

Imagine if I wanted to waste my time philosophizing. I gave one leap, four leaps; nor did I allow myself any rest until I found myself seated where the sweet scent of roasting meat spread through the air.

With what I had left, I was able to get to Lucerne, where the twofold problem of feeding myself and continuing on was to be solved, for me, by a man I had fought: Pope Mastai Ferretti.

Switzerland is a marvellous country. United by its beauties, on those slopes, among those valleys, live in peace peoples diverse in origin, in language, in character… a miracle of Liberty, a glorious anticipation of a holy Utopia.

And yet those peoples have not shown the height of spirit necessary to assume their full historical mission. The heart of Europe, in centuries past, beat vigorously among the Alps; but in vain! As from the white peaks the waters rush down which, furrowing the fertile plains, irrigate and fertilize so much of the Continent, so from the crags inaccessible to the conqueror, the beautiful and terrible phalanxes should have descended, to perform a work of brotherhood, to revive the hopes of the oppressed, to extend the dominion of Liberty.

They did descend; but as a devastating scourge… the blind instrument of villains, flesh and souls for sale!

Egoism is fatal to nations because, among other things, the tainted environment of your neighbour, sooner or later, corrupts your own as well, undermining and diminishing your glory. Despotism, defended by Swiss mercenaries, confined the unwary who had nourished it within the narrow circle of the mountains; the Republic remained a minuscule entity and, in the universal conception, became a synonym for venality. At the time when I was crossing it, this evil custom was not very intense, and already the Federal Government was branding as a criminal both the citizen who was not ashamed to prostitute himself to a foreign Government, and the middleman who recruited him for it and made himself an accomplice. Nevertheless Pius IX, who distrusted his Catholics, maintained secret agents in the Lutheran Cantons, to scrape together, for a goodly sum of *baiocchi*, the fistful of insects destined, in his heart, to change the course of the stars, to annihilate the hopes of the new Italy.

One such agent saw me on the shore of the lake, absorbed in my twofold problem. Attracted perhaps by my military bearing, he began the conversation in the holy name of God and concluded by formally proposing that I take up a musket in defense of the pontifical throne and the altar.

My first impulse was to throw him, headlong, into the lake. But a sudden thought restrained me. Garibaldian tactics!... when you have no munitions you take them from the enemy. The money of the Holy See to repatriate an adversary of the old order!.. my twofold problem more than solved! And, incidentally, I could also do some good for the cause of my Country, by exposing the intrigue, by clipping the nails of the unctuous middleman!

I smiled and, passing myself off as a Swiss from the Canton of Ticino, I accepted.

I was led, through winding alleys, to a house of mean appearance; I impressed its number well in my memory, climbed a filthy little staircase, and entered a hovel of an amphibious character, a *quid* between a study and a bedroom. The contract was duly drawn up and signed and, a few hours later, from the stern of the steamboat that was rapidly pulling away, I saluted my recruiter with great gestures.

Having reached Fluelen, I presented myself at the Commissariat and recounted, from beginning to end, what had happened to me. I was taken back to Lucerne for the identification of the gentleman. I retraced the winding alleys, I found the house of less than modest appearance, I went back up the filthy little staircase. The blackbird was in his cage, or rather in his bed; the proof palpable, the Law precise. He was arrested.

While he was dressing, the Federal Delegate took out a purse and attempted to shell out to me I know not how much money. I felt a flush of red rise to my face and I burst out: "Italians are always prepared to face, with a serene spirit, any sacrifice, even that of taking on the appearance of a scoundrel. Because, when it is a matter of serving the Fatherland and the lion's leap is not possible, then welcome and blessed be the subtlety of the fox. But learn this, and let it be a rule for you: If you find one who, having made the sacrifice, holds out his hand and demands compensation for it, you can say that he was not born beyond your Alps… or, if he was born there, be certain that he is of a bastard race, unworthy of the name he flaunts!"

I was taken back to Fluelen, then all alone, I set out for Goeschenen, whence, facing the frost and the blizzard, I reached the summit of the St. Gotthard. From up there, a premonition of southern warmth passed over my face; an air saturated with tremors and sobs, with hatred and with love.

And I advanced almost at a run, with my heart swollen, I knew not myself whether with joy or with pity. In Varese I was welcomed by Trolli like a dear departed soul miraculously returned to life. And in the hospitality of that family I found ample compensation for my labours. But in Lombardy the Austrian reaction was rampant; the sight of the hated uniform rekindled my fever… and, at that age, with my temperament, it is too arduous a task to put a brake on impetuosity. Friends, privy to secret matters, opportunely insinuated some advice. I must not compromise my hosts, and I had myself accompanied to the border.

Having dismissed my escort and remaining alone, I turned to contemplate the beautiful and unfortunate Land that, for the second time, I was forced to leave. In the majesty of the place and the silence, I went over past events… the desperate charge of the few, armed with their right, against the serried masses, horrid with bayonets; the gazes of the innocent turned in vain to heaven; the silent tears, the rivers of blood. And in the exaltation of the moment, my thoughts ran from the foot of the Alps, on and on, to the extremity of Sicily. Everywhere hearts that trembled, hands that clasped in the shadows, that in the shadows sharpened their blades. Then the great soul of the noble, valiant, and unhappy race seemed to permeate me and magnify my own. I stretched out my arms as if I expected to embrace in them lovingly all, all my fellow slaves. I renewed the inspired oath of Giuseppe Mazzini and, weeping, I took up again the path of exile.
