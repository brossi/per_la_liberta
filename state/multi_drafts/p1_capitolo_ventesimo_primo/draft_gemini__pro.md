From the heights overlooking the mines of Agordo, I cast a final glance at my brother, who was shrinking into the plain below. Then I resolutely took the path up into the mountains, beginning a flight that was to prove full of thrills and truly singular. Upon reaching the zone of perpetual snows, I slowed my pace, both from exhaustion and from the growing difficulties, but also because I felt hidden and made safe by the space and the solitude. I peered into the depths... a great many of what, on the ascent, had seemed to be heights, now appeared level with the plain. The hatred of the powerful, I thought, could not reach so far as to disturb that virgin silence.

But having escaped the snares of men, I nearly fell victim to those of nature. It had been forty-eight hours since I had closed my eyes, and I felt terribly predisposed to that morbid slumber which the frost and the altitude can render fatal. At a certain point, I was seized as if by a vertigo of the sublime; my legs would no longer hold me, and I fell prostrate upon the snow.

Now see to what extent dream and reality can intertwine and become confused! I was asleep and incapable of moving a muscle... of that there was no doubt. And yet my mind, vigilant within my inert body, was conscious of that boundless whiteness, of that fearful solitude; I could feel my fragile heart beating, the icy wind blowing about me, and, amidst the grim images of life that crowded into my brain, the idea of my peril predominated, rigid, motionless as a boulder.

Suddenly, I saw myself distinctly, with the constables at my heels. I run, I run, I run: or rather, it is the mountain that comes rushing towards me. But the constables are just as fast and faster... I can already hear the crunching of the snow beneath their boots; already their foul mouths thunder 'Halt!' and I run, run... In my head, several shots rang out.

A second later I was on my feet, awake, perfectly awake! The shots were still echoing from crag to crag. I scanned the valley and spied a line of soldiers running along the forest, rifle in hand. They stop and form a circle around a human figure, prostrate and dark upon the snow. I note their agitated gestures; then I saw, or so it seemed to me, a soldier prod the flesh of the fallen man with his bayonet, as if searching for a remnant of life. A smuggler.

A smuggler, I thought, mistaken in these days of fearful suspicion for game of greater importance. It was he who, stiff and unconscious, would later cause my poor mother so much anguish.

I crossed the summit as quickly as I could. The sky remained splendid; the snow, very hard on the northern slope, no longer gave way beneath my feet. I received a more than honest welcome, and bread and milk, from a mountaineer; then, at Neumark, I handsomely tempered my cheese with an internal ablution of excellent beer. As dusk fell, I set foot in Bolzano.

If the crowd helps to conceal, I could not have arrived at a more opportune moment. For several days the life of the entire district had been flowing back to Bolzano, attracted by the annual fair. In those times and in those places of difficult communication, the fairs took on the boisterous character of a festival. Haggling brokers and frowning farmers on the hunt for a florin; mountain women, beautiful with that rustic beauty which is born of the open air and the sun, having acquired their plenary indulgence, departed in large bands, singing in chorus. There was a stench of barrels issuing in puffs from the taverns; there were herds of heifers and oxen that solemnly made way for themselves through the human herds; buffoons who attracted the public with the grimaces of their painted faces, to the sound of a great drum; sacks piled high and hardware laid out on the pavement and baskets brimming with fruit and tables of sweets, protected by enormous umbrellas, and a great crush and a humming of voices and a riot of colours... and high above, conciliating every base human dissonance, a mighty wave of bells ringing for the festival.

Not without working my elbows, I reached the main square, all cluttered with stalls and vendors. At one corner of the square stood the inn of the "Queen of England." With one of its sides, it flanked the main street, and it was upon this side that the great door opened.

I entered, went up to the first floor, and found myself face to face with a *chellerina* with a rosy little face and a snow-white apron... only to be told that the rooms were all occupied, that I should have to content myself with sharing a bed with some stranger—and good luck finding a willing fellow!

"Very well! In any case, I can begin with a good supper."

I was shown into the dining hall. Instinctively, I sat at the first small table, to the left, near the entrance. I was alone. The ample room was dimly lit by a single lamp. By its reflection I recognized, on one wall, the effigy of Ferdinando, the emperor who last remembered he was a despot in order to demand, on his deathbed, a plate of gnocchi with butter. I looked, but in vain, for that of Francesco Giuseppe... perhaps it had not yet found its way hither.

But I found something worse. Near the door was pasted a large notice upon which the double-headed eagle appeared conspicuously. Amidst the dark and jumbled lines, one name stood out in larger letters, a name that was well known to me, that of... Carlo Camillo di Rudio. The devil, what is this? I jumped up and read. Austria was putting a price of six thousand florins on my head. To be taken alive or dead... to be denounced!... a slight effort, the duty of an honest citizen for some great saint of order and the Law. By what a slender thread my salvation hung!

I should lie if I said I supped with a tranquil spirit. If being in a crowded centre had seemed my salvation a moment before, now I glimpsed in it the greatest of dangers. How to emerge from that vortex unobserved? Was it possible that among so many people, there was not a living soul who knew me? who might suspect, by intuition, the truth?

As if in answer to me, on the stairs, with their varied tread, voices rang out speaking the Bellunese dialect; voices that, I would have sworn, were not unknown to me. I hid my face in my right hand and spied anxiously between my fingers. The door swung open; several strangers entered, and behind them, one after the other, four stout fellows... the smugglers of the valle del Trevis, the Burrasca brothers.

They looked at me, they recognized me; but they feigned ignorance. They sat at the nearby table, and the eldest, who was beside me, touched my foot with his; then, without turning his face, pretending to speak to his brothers: "I am happy to see you. With the rumours going about..."

At that moment, the *chellerina* with the rosy little face and the snow-white apron entered. She lit another lamp... the letters on the notice with the double-headed eagle now loomed energetically, almost doubling their eloquence.

I said: "Saved!... only to fall out of the frying pan and into the fire." And pointing to the notice: "What a fine stroke of luck for you!"

Four faces darkened. The eldest of the Burrascas, striking a fist in the air: "Morsels seasoned with shame will not pass down this throat!"

And another of the brothers, in a deep bass voice: "The blood of traitors does not flow in the veins of the Burrascas!"

They had the chance to prove it to me. A few minutes more, and the stench of the gendarmerie spread through the wide hall. One ruffian remained on the threshold and, leaning his back against the jamb, barred the door. Another ruffian came forward and asked me, in a brusque tone, for my passport. I drew it from my wallet and was unfolding it when, growing impatient, he snatched it from my hand. He glanced over it, drew nearer the lamp to examine it better, and ended by saying: "I do not understand Italian. You must come with me to the station."

"Hold fast!" cried one of the Burrascas, who well understood the dangers of the fine proposition being made to me.

An instant... a massacre! I snatch the passport back from the importunate fellow and, before he can react, the smuggler's fist strikes him under the chin like a sledgehammer. He fell, struck the nape of his neck on the tiled floor, and moved no more.

Meanwhile, another smuggler, who had approached the threshold on the pretext of reading the terms of the bounty, lands his blow on the teeth of the ruffian blocking it... blood spurts from his mouth, from his nose; he staggers and goes down in a heap.

"Wings on your feet!" came the cry. But I needed no exhortations... I was already leaping down the steps, four at a time, clutching a pistol—a providential inspiration! A third gendarme was on guard at the foot of the stairs. He turns, sees me, sees the weapon, and understands its tacit threat, reinforced by a forceful imprecation. He lets me pass without lifting a finger, and I back away, with my weapon aimed.

But in that constable's compliance was a good dose of strategy... he had averted his own danger and had pushed me into a greater trap. A fourth gendarme, having heard the commotion upstairs, had run to fetch the patrol. Both were advancing at a run as I turned the corner into the square, and it was a miracle I did not fall into their arms. Before me the patrol, behind me the gendarme from the landing, who had followed me and was now pointing me out, gesticulating furiously.

Night had fallen. The square, crowded with stalls and people, was lit by multicoloured lanterns, Venetian-style. With a sideways movement, I leap into that hornet's nest. "He's getting away, he's getting away! Catch him, catch him!" But in the confusion, no one thinks to act. As for the soldiers, compact by virtue of discipline, but hampered by their weapons, they content themselves with pursuing me by running along the sides of the square... I slip through and cross it. Then, having been advised that I had ducked into a certain alleyway, they divided into two squads; one rushes at my back, the other goes around the block and runs to the other end of the alley, to block my path and catch me between two fires.

They ended up face to face with each other.

From under the entryway of a house, with my eye to the keyhole, I observed their movements, noted their faces, heard their words. "Der Teufel! Has the earth swallowed him up?" They discussed for a moment and concluded that I must be—the goodness of them!—a pickpocket. The Police had cast good nets throughout the town and, sooner or later, I would be caught in them. They re-formed their ranks and departed.

Without a special warrant, the constables could not, at that hour, violate a private domicile. I felt temporarily safe.

I climbed the stairs to the third landing... the top one. There were four doors; two opposite, one to the right, the other to the left. I listened... absolute silence! So I sat on the steps and, resting my elbows on my knees, my face in my palms, I began to ponder. Imperceptibly, my thoughts darkened and I fell asleep.

When I returned to the reality of things, the first light of dawn was showing through the skylight and already, beyond the door on the left, one could hear voices alternating in brief, indistinct dialogues and the clash of plates and crockery.

I get up, dust off my trousers and am about to descend, when the door opens and a woman, of uncertain age, in scanty dress, sees me and lets out a cry.

"What is it?" a gruff, masculine voice asks from within. Before I could open my mouth, I found myself before a stocky man in his shirtsleeves, an apron at his waist, an awl in his hand.

I urged them not to be alarmed. I was a cattle dealer who had arrived from Switzerland the previous evening, at a late hour. No matter how many taverns I had tried, I had not managed to find a bed, even offering to pay an arm and a leg for one. And rather than spend the night in the open...

"By Christ!" said the man, "from the pavement to a landing... what's the difference? Come in, you can warm yourself by drinking a cup of coffee in our company."

I offered to add a little brandy to it and drew out my purse which, to the eye, seemed well-furnished.

What followed was a three-way contest... the shoemaker feigning offence that I should think him capable of demanding payment for a kindness; the woman drawing back, with modest gestures, so as not to let me touch her forearm and hand with my gold coin; I rebuffing the man's protests with a string of "But... But really... for shame!" while pressing the woman, using courteous force upon her. I ended by triumphing, and then I added: "In fact, to tell the truth, the cold has given me an appetite, such an appetite!... and in this town, these days, one eats... one eats like a dog! If you would be so kind as to buy a few morsels... to your taste. A little snack does no harm to friendship!"

When the woman had gone down a pair of stairs, I raised my voice and called after her to recommend the prodigious virtue of keeping her mouth shut. I was not without friends in Bolzano, and if it reached their ears that I had preferred sleeping on a landing to disturbing them...

It was an imprudence... ninety-nine times out of a hundred, the most effective means of making a woman chatter is to recommend she keep silent. But my good fortune would have it that, precisely from that crack, my salvation should spring forth.

As soon as my hostess was on the street and the door was heard to slam shut, the shoemaker took me by the sleeve and, dragging me inside, into the kitchen, stared at me for a long time without saying a word. Then, abruptly: "You've been telling me stories. A cattle merchant, you! You are a deserter!"

— Hush! How did you know?

For his entire reply, he twisted his lips and pointed a finger at his own right eye. Which, among all known peoples, means "I have too much sense to be taken for a fool!"

The conversation having been placed on this footing, I found myself, as they say, on home ground, nor did I lack for arguments and precise details to entangle my host ever more deeply in his own error. And as he listened to me, he showed his growing trust by winking his eye and giving me certain little pats on the shoulder that were meant to take on all the air of a demonstration of brotherly affection; for... since the deserter, in my time, among those peoples, usurped the fame that belongs to heroes, and not to lend him a hand seemed to them a form of cowardice.

If my spirit had not been terribly preoccupied, that would have been a day of rest. My host showed a rude but thoroughly proven cordiality. Except that too much of a good thing can be bad, and when, solicitous of what was to come, he began to devise plans for my escape, he was unconsciously the cause of no small anxieties for me. In his enthusiasm, he did not cease from contriving projects, each more fantastic than the last, and, on the pretext of being more familiar with the places and more expert in judging the humour of men, he sought to force my hand, I who, in that game, was risking my skin.

I ended by persuading him to hire a carriage and to take me out of the town in time to coincide with the changing of the stagecoach bound for Marano, which took place several miles away, in the open countryside, late at night.

"The police have cast good nets throughout the town," the corporal of the patrol had said the evening before. But as soon as Bolzano and its fair fell quiet, I passed between the meshes, almost triumphantly, stretched out on the cushions of my carriage.

An incident, not worth the telling, was, at most, the cause of a little apprehension for me in Marano. But in Salzburg, in the German Tyrol, I ran a danger so great that to have escaped it, without excessive effort, seems almost a dream to me.

The postilion had stopped at the Posta inn, thus indicating to me the place to spend the night, which was already far advanced. I went up at once to wash my hands and face in the room assigned to me and, when I was called for supper, I took the key from the lock but, distractedly, did not close the door. Having reached the foot of the stairs—the telling of these trifles is necessary—I deposited my hat on a small table and hung the key on its little hook.

In the dining hall I found myself alone; but the inevitable two carabiniers soon arrived... unarmed this time. They ordered a tankard, a deck of cards, and struck up a game of tarot. When supper was finished, I approached them, as if interested in the game, and, to make a long story short, we ended up starting a three-handed game. We thus passed an hour or so, drinking to each other's health and teasing one another, until those tender feelings between the devil and the holy water were cut short by the hostess, who came to remind us that the Law is the Law and that the prescribed hour for closing had already struck some minutes past.

I asked her: "At what time does the stagecoach depart?"

— At seven precisely! — In that case, be so good as to wake me at four. I must go to the mountain to bargain for some heifers and I should like to be back in time to continue my journey by stagecoach. I can sleep peacefully!

— Between two pillows!

I parted from the gendarmes with great handshakes; but, having just reached the corridor, my sad thoughts returned, and I started up the stairs without retrieving the key. Go back down? The door was ajar; I could have bolted it from the inside. I entered, threw myself on the bed, and blew out the candle.

It had been many nights since I had lain down at full length upon a soft mattress, with a good blanket over my legs. When I awoke, the sun, already high on the horizon, was beating full upon my face.

A curse on the hostess! I went downstairs with my spirit in turmoil and my ear attentive... the inn seemed deserted. Whereupon I, concentrating my rage in my knuckles, knocked on the little table so forcefully that the candlesticks trembled.

And lo, the old proprietress emerged from the kitchen, her face framed in its bonnet. She was neat, dressed in her Sunday best. On seeing me, she stopped in her tracks, her mouth agape. She allowed herself to be assailed by a tempest of reproaches; then, with perfect calm: "Good heavens! Whose fault is it? The key to your room was there on the hook, and I concluded that you were already on your way to the mountain. If you do not believe me, ask your two friends from last night. They happened by right around four o'clock. They wanted to speak with you." — "Forgive me, I am sorry. I told them you were already far away... on the mountain." — "They came to speak with me? Prepare a good breakfast for three. I shall go and find them."

And I departed with all the appearance of a man who had been pacified. But the hostess was out her labour and expense.

It was a Sunday in September. The population of the village had poured into the little church, which must have been packed, because a human swarm was crowded outside, on the small churchyard. Crossing the square, I saw the plume of a gendarme's hat emerge above those dark heads, and I rejoiced. "There's one who will not arrest me!" And I added: "So it's true that even Mass is good for something."

Having reached the outskirts of the town, I left the main road and went into the fields, along hedged lanes; then I took to the pine wood, which served me as a marvellous screen.

The nearest land was Muntzen, a cluster of houses situated astride the border. It had, like Janus of ancient memory, two faces; one gloomy, the other smiling: that one Austrian, this one Swiss and republican. I knew this, and I knew, moreover, that the border line divided, politically speaking, the square and the village inn, which thus found itself under two jurisdictions. Hanging on a wall of the common room was a cuckoo clock. The gendarmes of the Empire could not pass beyond that clock without provoking an international incident. On this side, the regime of the cudgel, the handcuffs, the gallows; on the other, the regime of a free people, a breath of full lungs, salvation!

I crossed the frontier at a solitary point; I entered the village and the inn from the Swiss side. Setting foot in the hall, I was looking for the cuckoo clock when my attention was drawn to a group of Jaeger, the soldiers that Austria assigned to guard the borders. Among them, a sweet sight! the uniform and the well-known face of one of the guardian angels who, early that morning, had come to impress the holy stigmata upon my wrists. I could not help but laugh out loud.

"Ho, friend! A bad hunt this morning. And to think the blackbird was in its cage. If you had come up the stairs, the six thousand florins of the bounty would have, sooner or later, been jingling in your pocket!"

He admitted that I had been the cleverer one and extended his kindness so far as to offer congratulations. "In fact, to show you," he said, "my sincerity, come and I'll buy you a bottle!"

— And I a cask, if you wish. But you must be pleased to come to this side!" And I rubbed my hands together.

But that cheerful effervescence, so natural in one who leaves the high seas behind, was to be the last for many long months; for many long months, thinking back on it, I felt a kind of remorse.

The failure, which was already manifest to me, was accentuated by the news I gathered as I advanced further into free land, until it assumed the proportions of a true disaster, and my spirit was left as if overwhelmed.
