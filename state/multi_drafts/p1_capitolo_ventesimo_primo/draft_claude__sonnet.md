# Chapter the Twenty-First

From the heights commanding the mines of Agordo, I cast a last glance at my brother, who was dwindling away in the plain below. Then I set my face resolutely to the mountain path, beginning a flight that was to prove full of stirring incident and truly singular in character. Having reached the zone of perpetual snows, I slackened my pace, partly from weariness and the growing difficulties of the way, partly because I felt hidden and made secure by the vastness and the solitude. I plunged my face deep into it all... and a great part of what, on the ascent, had appeared to me as heights now seemed levelled to the plain. The hatred of tyrants, I thought, would not reach so far as to disturb that virgin silence.

Yet, having escaped the snares of men, I was all but undone by those of nature. It was eight-and-forty hours since I had closed my eyes, and I felt myself terribly disposed to that morbid slumber which frost and altitude can render mortal. At a certain point, I was seized as by a vertiginous ecstasy of the sublime; my legs would bear me no more and I fell stretched out upon the snow.

Now let it be seen to what degree dream and reality can interweave and confound themselves! I slept, and was incapable of moving a single muscle — of that there was no doubt. And yet the mind, wakeful within the inert body, retained consciousness of that boundless whiteness, of that fearful solitude; I could feel my slight heart beating, the icy wind blowing about me; and amidst the dark images of life that crowded in upon the brain, the idea of my danger predominated, rigid and immovable as a boulder.

All at once, I saw myself clearly — I and the *sbirri* hard upon my heels. I run, I run, I run; or rather it is the mountain that comes swiftly rushing toward me. But the constables are equally swift, and swifter — already I hear the crunch of snow beneath their boots; already their foul mouths thunder the *Alt!*, and still I run, I run... A volley of shots rang out within my head.

An instant, and I was on my feet — awake, perfectly awake! The shots still echoed from crag to crag. I peered into the valley and made out a line of soldiers running along the edge of the forest, rifles in hand. They halted and formed a ring about a human figure lying prone and dark against the snow. I noted their agitated gestures; then I saw, or seemed to see, a soldier prod with his bayonet the flesh of the fallen man, as though searching for some remaining spark of life. A smuggler.

A smuggler — I thought — mistaken, in these days of fearful suspicion, for game of rather greater importance. It was the very same who, stiff and insensible, was later to cause such anguish to my poor mother.

I crossed the summit as quickly as I might. The sky remained magnificent; the snow, hardened considerably on the northern face, no longer gave way beneath my feet. I received more than honest hospitality, and bread and milk, from a mountain peasant; then, at Neumark, I dissolved my cheese most liberally in a copious inward lavage of excellent beer. At dusk I set foot in Bolzano.

If a crowd is serviceable in concealment, I could not have arrived at a more opportune moment. For some days past, the entire life of the district had been flowing back toward Bolzano, drawn there by the annual fair. In those times and in those parts of difficult communication, fairs took on the raucous character of a village feast-day. Brokers and frowning farmers in pursuit of the florin; mountain women beautiful with that rustic beauty which is born of open air and sunshine, having obtained their plenary indulgence, were setting off in great groups, singing in chorus. There was a reek of wine-casks issuing in gusts from the taverns; there were herds of heifers and oxen that solemnly forced their passage through the human herds; mountebanks who drew the crowd with the grimaces of their painted faces, to the beat of the bass drum; sacks piled in heaps and ironmongery spread out upon the paving-stones, and baskets heaped with fruit, and tables of confectionery sheltered beneath enormous umbrellas, and a jostling and a buzzing of voices and a clamour of colours... and above all, reconciling every base human dissonance, a mighty wave of bells ringing out in festivity.

I arrived, not without working my elbows, as far as the main square, entirely obstructed by market stalls and hawkers. At one corner of the square stood the inn of the "Queen of England." One of its sides flanked the principal street, and it was upon this side that the main entrance opened.

I went in, climbed to the first floor, and found myself face to face with a little waitress with a rosy face and a white apron... only to be told that every room was taken, that I must content myself with sharing a bed with some other stranger — and good luck to me in finding an obliging one!

"Very well! In any case I may begin with a good supper."

I was shown to the dining-room. By instinct I sat down at the first little table, on the left, near the entrance. I was alone. The spacious room was dimly lit by a single lamp. By its light I recognised, on one wall, the portrait of Ferdinand — the Emperor who recalled for the last time that he was a despot by demanding, on his deathbed, a dish of gnocchi with butter. I searched, but in vain, for that of Franz Joseph — perhaps he had not yet found means to penetrate so far.

But I found something worse. Close by the door, a large placard had been pasted to the wall, upon which appeared, prominently displayed, the double-headed eagle. Amid the dark and confused lines of print there stood out, in larger characters, a name well known to me — that of... Carlo Camillo di Rudio. Good heavens, what is this? I sprang to my feet and read. Austria had placed a price of six thousand florins upon my head. To take me alive or dead... to denounce me! — a trifling labour, the bounden duty of an honest citizen for any staunch votary of Order and the Law. By how frail a thread hung my salvation!

I should be lying were I to say that I supped with a tranquil mind. If being in a crowded centre had seemed to me, but a little while before, my very salvation, I now perceived in it the utmost of dangers. How was I to escape unobserved from that vortex? Was it possible that amongst so many people there was not a living soul who knew me? who might suspect the truth by intuition?

As though in answer, on the staircase, amidst the varied tread of feet, there sounded voices speaking in the Bellunese dialect — voices which, I would have sworn, were not unknown to me. I hid my face in my right hand and spied anxiously through my fingers. The door was flung open; several strangers entered, and behind them, one after another, four stout fellows... the smugglers of the Trevis valley, the Burrasca brothers.

They looked at me, recognised me; but gave no sign. They sat down at the neighbouring table, and the eldest, who was at my side, touched my foot with his foot; then, without turning his face, feigning to speak to his brothers: "I am glad to see you. Given the rumours that are going about..."

At that moment the little waitress entered, with her rosy face and her white apron. She lit another lamp... the characters of the placard with the double-headed eagle grew blacker and bolder, as though doubling their eloquence.

I said: "Saved!... only to leap from the frying-pan into the fire." And pointing to the placard: "What a fine stroke of fortune for you!"

Four faces darkened. The eldest Burrasca, striking a fist into the air: "Morsels seasoned with shame do not pass down this throat!"

And another of the brothers, in a deep bass voice: "In the veins of the Burrasca there flows no blood of traitors!"

They had occasion to prove it to me. A few minutes more, and through the broad room there spread the reek of the gendarmerie. One ugly face remained upon the threshold and, leaning his back against the door-jamb, blocked the exit. Another such face advanced and demanded of me, in a curt tone, my passport. I drew it from my wallet and was unfolding it when he, impatiently, snatched it from my hand. He scanned it, stepped closer to examine it better by the lamp, and at last declared: "I do not understand Italian. You must come with me to the gendarmerie."

"Stand firm!" cried one of the Burrasca, who well understood the dangers of the pretty business being proposed to me.

An instant — and it was done! I snatched back the passport from the intruder, and before he could resist, the smuggler's fist struck him like a mallet beneath the chin. He fell, struck the back of his head upon the tiles, and lay motionless.

Meanwhile, another of the smugglers, who had drawn close to the threshold on the pretext of reading the terms of the reward, dealt his blow fair upon the teeth of the fellow blocking the way... blood spurted from his mouth and nose; he staggered and went down in a heap.

"Wings to your feet!" was the cry. But I needed no urging... I was already taking the stairs four at a time, with a pistol in my hand — a providential inspiration! A third gendarme stood on guard at the foot of the stairs. He turned, saw me, saw the weapon, and understood its silent menace, reinforced by an energetic imprecation. He let me pass without raising a finger, and I moved away, retreating with my pistol at the ready.

But in the compliance of that constable there lurked a goodly measure of strategy — he had conjured away his own danger and had driven me into a greater trap. A fourth gendarme, having heard the commotion above, had run to summon the assistance of the patrol. Both were now advancing at the double, just as I turned the corner into the square, and it was a miracle that I did not fall straight into their arms. The patrol in front of me, the sentry who had let me pass behind, gesticulating furiously and pointing me out.

Night had fallen. The square, obstructed with market stalls and thronged with people, was illuminated by variegated lanterns, *alla veneziana*. With a sidelong movement I plunged into that thicket of a crowd. "Stop him, stop him! Catch him, catch him!" But in the confusion, no one thinks to act. As for the soldiers, compact by virtue of their discipline, encumbered by their arms, they content themselves with pursuing me at a run along the sides of the square... I slip through and cut across it. Then, whether put on the alert that I had darted down a certain alleyway, they divided into two detachments; one flung itself at my heels, the other rounded the block and ran to the other end of the same alleyway, to bar my passage and take me between two fires.

They ended by finding themselves face to face.

Beneath the archway of a house, with my eye to the keyhole, I watched their movements, noted their faces, heard their words. "*Der Teufel!* — has the earth swallowed him?" They debated for a moment, and concluded that I must be — in their charitable opinion! — a pickpocket. The Police had spread good nets throughout the town, and sooner or later I should be caught in them. They reformed their ranks and moved off.

Without a special warrant, the constables could not, at that hour, violate a private dwelling. I felt myself temporarily safe.

I mounted the stairs to the third landing — the last. There were four doors; two facing each other, one to the right, one to the left. I listened... absolute silence! Then I sat down on the steps, and with my elbows on my knees and my face in my palms, I began to ponder. Insensibly my thoughts grew dark, and I fell asleep.

When I returned to the reality of things, the first light of dawn was filtering through the skylight, and already, beyond the left-hand door, voices could be heard alternating in brief and indistinct snatches of conversation, with the clatter of plates and crockery.

I rose, brushed off my trousers, and was about to descend when the door opened and a woman of uncertain age, in a short skirt, caught sight of me and gave a cry.

"What is it?" demanded a gruff male voice from within. Before I could open my mouth I found before me a thickset man in his shirt-sleeves, an apron at his sides, an awl in his hand.

I urged them not to be alarmed. I was a cattle-dealer arrived from Switzerland the previous evening, at a late hour. For all the inns I had tried, I had not succeeded in finding a bed, even offering to pay any price imaginable. And rather than spend the night in the open air...

"For the love of God!" said the man. "From the pavement to a landing... what difference does it make? Come in, you'll warm yourself with a cup of coffee in our company."

I offered to add a drop of spirits, and drew out my purse, which, to the eye, seemed well furnished.

There ensued a three-cornered contest: the cobbler affecting to take offence that I should think him capable of demanding payment for a kindness; the woman drawing back, with the gestures of false modesty, lest my gold coin should touch her forearm and hand; and I thrusting aside the man's protests with a succession of "But... surely... come now!" and pressing the woman and doing her courteous violence. I ended by triumphing, and then added: "Indeed, to tell the truth, the cold has given me an appetite — such an appetite!... and in the town, these days, one eats so poorly. If you would be so kind as to procure a few mouthfuls... to your own taste. A light repast does no harm to a friendship!"

When the woman had descended a couple of flights, I raised my voice and called her back to commend to her the wondrous virtue of holding water in one's mouth. I was not without friends in Bolzano, and should it reach their ears that I had preferred sleeping on a landing to disturbing them...

It was a piece of rashness — nine times out of ten the most effective means of making a woman talk is to beg her to keep silent. But my good fortune was to be such that from that very crack my salvation would spring.

No sooner had my hostess reached the street and the outer door was heard to bang, than the cobbler took me by the sleeve, dragged me inside to the kitchen, and fixed me with a long, wordless stare. Then, abruptly: "You have been spinning me tales. A cattle-dealer, you! You are a deserter!"

— Quiet! How did you come to know?

For all reply, he twisted his lips and tapped his right eye with his forefinger — the gesture which, among all known peoples, signifies: "I have too much wit to be taken in!"

With the conversation once placed on that footing, I found myself, as the saying goes, on home ground, nor did I lack arguments and precise particulars with which to enmesh my host ever more tightly in his own misapprehension. And he, as he listened, demonstrated his growing confidence by winking at me and giving me certain little claps on the shoulder that were meant to convey all the warmth of a demonstration of brotherly affection — for the deserter enjoyed, in my day among those populations, the fame that rightly belongs to heroes, and not to lend him a hand savoured to them of cowardice.

Had my spirit not been terribly preoccupied, this would have been a day of rest. My host showed a rough but thoroughly proved cordiality. Only, too much of a good thing does harm, and when, solicitous for the future, he began to devise plans for my escape, he became, unconsciously, the cause of no small anxiety to me. In his enthusiasm, he ceased not from building scheme upon scheme, each more fantastic than the last, and on the pretext of knowing the country better and of being a shrewder judge of men's dispositions, he persisted in attempting to force my hand — I who, in that particular game, was risking my skin.

In the end I persuaded him to hire a carriage and drive me out of the town in time to connect with the change of the stage-coach bound for Marano, which took place several miles off, in open country, and in the depth of night.

"The Police have spread good nets throughout the town," the corporal of the patrol had said on the previous evening. But no sooner had Bolzano and its fair fallen into slumber than I slipped through mesh and mesh, almost triumphantly, reclining upon the cushions of my carriage.

An incident not worth the telling caused me, at most, a moment of unease at Marano. But at Salzburg, in the German Tyrol, I ran a danger so great that to have escaped it without excessive effort seems to me almost like a dream.

The postilion had pulled up at the Post inn, indicating thereby the place where I was to pass the night, which was already well advanced. I went up at once, to wash my hands and face, to the room assigned to me, and when I was called down for supper, I took the key from the lock but, inadvertently, did not shut the door. Having reached the foot of the stairs — the recounting of these trifles is necessary — I set my hat upon a side table and hung the key on the little hook.

In the dining-room I found myself alone; but there soon arrived the inevitable pair of carabineers — unarmed this time. They ordered a jug, a pack of cards, and struck up a game of tarocco. When supper was done, I drew near to them as though interested in the play, and, to be brief, we ended by starting a game in three. We passed in this manner a good hour, drinking to each other's health and exchanging banter, until these endearments between the devil and holy water were cut short by the hostess, who came to remind us that the Law is the Law, and that the prescribed hour of closing had already struck some minutes since.

I asked her: "At what hour does the stage depart?"

— At seven precisely! — In that case, be so good as to wake me at four. I must go up to the mountain to bargain for certain heifers, and should like to be back in time to continue my journey by the stage. I can sleep in peace!

— Between two pillows!

I parted from the gendarmes with a great shaking of hands; but, scarcely arrived in the corridor, I took up again my dark thoughts and made my way upstairs without reclaiming the key. Go back down? The door stood ajar; I could fasten it from within with the bolt. I entered, threw myself upon the bed, and snuffed the candle.

It had been many nights since I had laid myself down at full length, stretched out, upon a soft mattress, with a good blanket over my legs. When I awoke, the sun, already high on the horizon, was beating full upon my face.

Confound the hostess! I came downstairs in a tumult of spirits, ears alert... the inn seemed deserted. Whereupon, concentrating all my rage into the knuckle of my fingers, I knocked upon the side table so energetically that the candlesticks rattled.

And out from the kitchen bustled the old landlady, her face framed in her cap. She was neat and dressed in her Sunday best. On seeing me she stopped short, with her mouth wide open. She allowed herself to be assailed by a tempest of reproaches; then, with perfect composure: "Good heavens! Whose fault is it, pray? Your room key was there on its hook, and from that I concluded you were already on your way to the mountain. If you don't believe me, ask your two friends of last evening. They came by at about four o'clock precisely. They wished to speak with you." — "I told them you were already far away... on the mountain." — "They came to speak to me? Prepare a good breakfast for three. I shall go and find them."

And I departed with every appearance of a man appeased. But the hostess lost both her labour and her expense.

It was a September Sunday. The village population had streamed into the little church, which must have been packed to the doors, for a human swarm was pressing outside on the small *sagrato* before it. Crossing the square, I noticed the plume of a gendarme's hat rising above those dark heads, and rejoiced at the sight. "Here is one who will not arrest me!" And I added: "So true is it that even the Mass serves some purpose."

Once clear of the village, I left the high road and struck off through the fields along hedged lanes; then I entered a pine-wood that served me as an admirable screen.

The nearest territory was Muntzen, a cluster of houses straddling the frontier. It had, like Janus of ancient memory, two faces: one clouded, the other smiling — the former Austrian, the latter Swiss and republican. I knew this, and knew moreover that the frontier line divided, politically speaking, the village square and the inn, which thus found itself under two jurisdictions. Hanging on the wall of the common room was a cuckoo-clock. The gendarmes of the Empire could not pass beyond that clock without provoking an international incident. On this side, the regime of the truncheon, the manacles, the gallows; on that side, the regime of free men, breath drawn in full lungs, salvation!

I crossed the frontier at a solitary point, entered the village, and came into the inn from the Swiss side. Having set foot in the common room, I was looking about with my eye for the cuckoo-clock, when my attention was drawn to a group of *Jäger* — the militia which Austria posted to guard the frontiers. Among them, welcome sight! the uniform and well-known face of one of those guardian angels who, early that very morning, had come to imprint upon my wrists their holy stigmata. I could not restrain a burst of loud laughter.

"Holla, friend! Bad hunting this morning. And to think of it — the blackbird was in his cage. Had you but climbed the stairs, the six thousand florins of the reward would have clinked in your pocket sooner or later!"

He admitted that I had proved the cleverer, and carried his graciousness so far as to offer congratulations. "Indeed, to show you my sincerity," said he, "come, and I'll stand you a bottle!"

— And I a whole cask, if it please you. But you must be so good as to come over to this side! — And I rubbed my hands together.

But that gleeful exuberance, so natural in one who has left the open sea behind him, was for many long months my last; for many long months, when I thought of it again, I felt as it were a pang of remorse.

The failure which had already been made manifest to me grew more pronounced with every piece of news gathered as I pressed forward into free territory, until it assumed the proportions of a true disaster, and my spirit was left as though engulfed by it.
