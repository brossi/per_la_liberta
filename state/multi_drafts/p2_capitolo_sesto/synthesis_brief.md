PER parecchi giorni, mi sforzai a comprimere i pensieri che mi tumultuavano pel capo, riannodando vecchie conoscenze. Cercavo, nel tempo stesso, un punto d'appoggio che valesse a metter fine alla mia situazione finanziaria estremamente spinosa, ad assicurarmi lavoro e pane per l'avvenire.

Una sera, poco prima del tramonto, me ne ritornavo, insolitamente stanco, quasi avvilito, dalla solita escursione, quando, scantonando sulla piazza dello Shoah, urtai, letteralmente, in Simon Bernard.

"Bravo! Bravo! Vengo appunto da casa vostra. Il signor Orsini ha scritto. Sapete che cosa ha scritto? Che vi conosce per prova. Che vuole un uomo di sua piena fiducia. O voi, o manda tutto a monte. Che cosa intendete di fare?"

Questa volta risposi: "Ho dato la mia parola a Felice Orsini, la manterrò".

Invece di rincasare direttamente, facemmo tre o quattro volte il giro della piazza. La lettera dell'Orsini aveva dissipata la freddezza di Simon Bernard e sciolto il suo scilinguagnolo. Seppi che Felice Orsini già riannodava, a Parigi, i fili della sua trama; che il mezzo definitivamente scelto per l'attentato era quello delle bombe. Che il barone Egassy di Torocfalda ne aveva ideate di un genere affatto nuovo, foggiate a riccio, ogni punta armata di capsula, così che sarebbero scoppiate in qualunque modo venissero lanciate. La fonderia Taylor da Birmingham le aveva fuse a pezzo a pezzo, come apparecchi pel gas. Il preparato destinato a farle esplodere era il fulminato di mercurio. Lo stesso barone di Torrocfalda aveva insegnato all'Orsini a fabbricarlo e questi seppe tanto bene approfittare della lezione, che si era tratto d'impaccio scottandosi appena qualche dito.

Nel congedarsi, Simon Bernard mi raccomandò di tenermi pronto, perchè, da un giorno all'altro, poteva capitarmi l'ordine della partenza. Ed io ne dedussi, lì per lì, che il tempo era giunto di sollecitare la cooperazione di Giuseppe Mazzini.

L'abboccassi con lui, non era tanto facile come potrebbe sembrare; sia che il Maestro, spiato da tutte le Polizie del mondo, fosse diventato diffidente; sia, come molti volevano, che certe persone, di cui si era attorniato, si adoperassero ad allontanarlo da quanti potessero coadiuarlo efficacemente in qualche sua arrischiata intrapresa. Conversando tra amici si diceva, sorridendo talvolta, ma più spesso non senza qualche risentimento, essere più difficile avere un colloquio con Giuseppe Mazzini, che non colla Regina d'Inghilterra.

In quei giorni, a dir vero, mi erano venuti d'attorno, attorno, col Massarenti, parecchi altri individui che sapevo in stretti rapporti col Maestro. Ma la mia legittima diffidenza mi aveva indotto a tenere, al loro confronto, la parte dell'uomo che ascolta e che non parla.

Inaspettatamente, il caso parve mi favorisse. Passeggiavo in Cheapside, con certo Gori, già capobanda della legione Zambeccari, quando mi vidi d'innanzi, a breve distanza, il Mazzini sotto braccio ad uno sconosciuto. Affrettai il passo e lo raggiunsi. Naturalmente, non potevo parlare di un soggetto tanto importante in presenza d'estranei e, dopo i necessari saluti, dovetti limitarmi a dire al Mazzini che desideravo avere un abboccamento e lo pregai di indicarmene l'ora ed il luogo. Giuseppe Mazzini guardò per aria, come se vi cercasse un'ispirazione; poi, chiesto il mio indirizzo, disse che Massarenti mi avrebbe saputo dire qualcosa in proposito. Massarenti... proprio il pesce che ci ripugnava!

Ci separammo. Gli avvenimenti precipitarono e mi trascinarono nel loro vortice prima che il Mazzini si facesse vivo. Che sarebbe avvenuto se avessi potuto parlargli? Mi avrebbe egli dissuaso dall'impresa o vi avrebbe portato il contributo della sua influenza e dell'ardente anima sua? Sono domande che mi vennero rivolte ripetutamente. Certo, il rispondervi nulla rileva. Tuttavia osservo: l'uomo che scrisse non doversi, certe azioni eccezionali, intese alla rivendicazione dei diritti umani, giudicare alla stregua della morale comune; l'uomo che additò al Gallenga il pugnale che avrebbe dovuto uccidere Carlo Alberto; che ebbe parte non ben definita, ma certa, nell'attentato del Tebaldi contro Luigi Bonaparte, non poteva logicamente opporsi a quello di Felice Orsini. E il Maestro ben sapeva dove la logica stia di casa!

L'attentato del Tebaldi fu appunto organizzato in quell'anno, il 1857. Napoleone III frequentava la casa della contessa Sommariva per motivi, dicevasi, più noti a lui che ai congiunti della contessa. Il Tebaldi, e i due suoi complici, dovevano penetrare, con chiave falsa, in quella casa e impossessarsi della persona dell'Imperatore. Pare si volesse forzarlo all'abdicazione. Ma la cattura di un uomo tanto vigilato, non poteva compiersi senza spargimento di sangue ed agli occhi del Maestro, la vita di un semplice poliziotto non valeva meno di quella di un Imperatore. Comunque sia, i due complici del Tebaldi non seppero mantenere il segreto e i tre vennero arrestati. Il Tebaldi fu relegato all'isola del Diavolo; i suoi due complici in Algeria.

La reazione suscitata dalle male opere di Luigi Napoleone era troppo intensa perchè non si manifestasse in modo violento e personale. Il regicidio era nell'aria e Napoleone lo sapeva tanto bene che, sin dal 1852, dopo il colpo di Stato e prima della proclamazione dell'Impero, egli faceva deportare a Cayenna i romagnoli Galli e Rossini, di nulla rei se non che d'aver incappato nell'infame Legge dei sospetti.

L'attentato di quell'altro ardito romagnolo, Giovanni Pianori, fallì perché Napoleone, non potendo ripararsi

"Sotto l'usbergo del sentirsi puro" indossava, segretamente, una maglia di acciaio. Il Pianori venne atterrato prima di poter sparare il suo secondo colpo. Egli aumentò la schiera dei martiri ghigliottinati. Il fratello suo, già corrucciato dall'odio politico, a quella tragica morte, sentì raddoppiare in petto le sue furie. Lasciò l'Italia, meditando una clamorosa vendetta. Ma toccato appena il suolo francese, venne arrestato e deportato all'inferno delle isole tropicali, come il Galli ed il Redini, senz'ombra di processo.

Fiere tempre, queste dei Pianori! Essi non conoscevano dovere se non che la rivendicazione dei propri e dell'altrui diritto e ad essa correvano risoluti, senza preoccuparsi delle Leggi, che reputavano uno spauracchio architettato dai furbi, a scopo di dominazione e di rapina; senza riguardo alla propria vita, che dicevano bene spesa, se spesa a cancellare un'ingiustizia.

Giovanni Pianori, il ghigliottinato, risalta nella sua torva interezza, da un altro incidente tragico. Egli amava perdutamente sua moglie. Costretto, dalle vicende fortunose della sua esistenza, ad allontanarsene, l'aveva affidata alle cure d'un intimo suo che, insinuatosi nel cuore dell'avvenente protetta, si mostrò troppo tenero amante, per conservarsi amico fedele. Qualche zelante soffiò la dolorosa verità nell'orecchio del tradito che, affrontando mille pericoli, fece improvvisamente ritorno. Facile gli riuscì lo strappare, alla spaventata, l'improvvida confessione. Ridivenne calmo e non le torse un capello; ma impose l'assoluto silenzio, con quella risolutezza che domina e non ammette replica.

L'intimo sopraggiunse. Il Pianori, dissimulando, lo invitò, dopo breve conversazione, all'aperto. Avrebbero potuto trattare, con maggior libertà, di affari politici segreti. D'improvviso nelle tenebre, l'intimo si sentì rivolgere questa domanda: "Che faresti tu ad un amico al quale affidasti l'onor tuo e quanto hai di più caro al mondo, se ti disonorasse, se ti tradisse?"

— Hai pronunciato la tua condanna!

E senz'altro aggiungere, lo stese boccheggiante nel proprio sangue.

Delitto? Io non dico di no. Dico solo che i tempi ferrei rendono ferreo l'uomo e che è follia l'aspettarsi costumi miti e sentimenti pecorini da gente avvezza allo spettacolo giornaliero della virtù penzolante da una forca. E dico anche che le idee astratte sono fissate nella vita pratica, incarnate nella Storia, non dai timidi belati, ma dall'azione impetuosa di tempre siffatte.

La prossima lettera inviatami da Felice Orsini era stata scritta da Parigi, ma la sua busta non portava altro timbro fuorché quello di Londra. Evidentemente una terza persona fidata, l'aveva ricevuta e impostata in quest'ultima città. Mi pregava l'Orsini di recarmi dalla signorina Elisa Cheney, dimorante a Cambdentown, la quale mi avrebbe consegnato una valigia ed un paio d'occhiali d'oro. Che cosa realmente si intendesse per "occhiali d'oro" seppi poco dopo da Simon Bernard. Probabilmente, per non attrarre l'attenzione dei doganieri con un involto soverchiamente voluminoso. Felice Orsini aveva portato con sé, nel suo viaggio a Parigi, solo una parte del fulminato di mercurio che gli abbisognava. Egli mi incaricava di trasportare il rimanente.

Fui a Cambdentown la sera stessa. La signorina Elisa Cheney donna apparentemente sulla trentina, sottile, piccoletta, bruna, non bella, nel senso classico, ma molto attraente, mi accolse con squisita gentilezza e col sorriso sulle labbra. La valigia che mi consegnò era, più propriamente, una di quelle borse che gli Inglesi chiamano "carpet bag"... di stoffa le pareti, di cuoio il fondo. Sulle pareti era trapunta una tigre. Il recipiente del fulminato di mercurio era di mogano ed aveva tutta l'apparenza di una scatola da oggetti d'abbigliamento. Era sul camino tra qualche gingillo ed una pendola. Prima di stendervi la mano, la signorina gettò sul marmo la lettera dell'Orsini. Era perfettamente calma; tanto che non attenuò il suo persistente sorriso neppure quando ebbe la scatola tra le dita. O era dotata di nervi a tutta prova o ignorava la terribile potenza dell'elegante arnese che mi porgeva. Stette ad osservarmi, in silenzio, mentre lo richiudevo nella borsa; poi d'improvviso, sorridendo sempre: "Va a Parigi? Buon viaggio e i miei complimenti al signor Orsini!"

Quando rincasai ed Elisa venne, col bimbo in braccio, a porgermi la guancia, pel bacio consueto, dovetti fare uno sforzo per portare la valigia con disinvoltura. "Hai fatto spesa?" mi domandò. Ed allungò il braccio. Ma io facetamente: "Sta in guardia! la tigre è viva e potrebbe graffiarti!" Poi, ridiventando serio: "Sono scartafacci che non ti possono interessare". E serravo in un armadio la tigre capace di squarciare, dissimulata dall'altra trapunta ed innocua.

Il giorno dopo, l'8 Gennaio 1858, comparve nuovamente sulla soglia Simon Bernard. Aveva un'espressione grave, quasi solenne ed io che mi aspettavo ormai comunicazioni definitive, compresi subito che quattro passi sulla piazza riuscivano indicatissimi. Né mi ero ingannato.

"Bisogna partire, mi disse il Bernard. L'Orsini calcola, anzi, che domattina vi mettiate in viaggio. In tal caso domani sera, tra le sei e le otto, arrivereste a Parigi. Egli sarà alla stazione... il resto andrà da sé. Ma dato il caso che non possiate partire a tempo, o che l'Orsini, per accidenti imprevisti, non venga all'appuntamento, pigliate nota del suo indirizzo!" E me lo dettava...

Mi lasciò con grandi strette di mano e coll'affidamento: "Arrivederci, fra qualche giorno, a Parigi".

Quella sera, rientrando, non feci all'Elisa il solito bacio, quasi per prepararla al distacco. Mentre ella apparecchiava per la cena, io, seduto in disparte, mi tormentavo nervosamente il pizzo. Ella mi sorprese cogli occhi fissi su lei e, non potendo trattenersi, mi gettò le braccia al collo, mormorandomi dolcemente all'orecchio: "Carlo, che hai?"

Allora mi alzai, le accarezzai i capelli, le baciai la fronte e le dissi parole che, altra volta, in ben altra circostanza, avevo impresso nel suo tenero cuore, quasi suggello di un patto solenne: "Il cospiratore deve essere solitario anche in seno alla famiglia. Egli deve, talvolta, dimenticare d'avere una madre e moglie e figli. Se la sua ora predestinata arriva, chiude le orecchie alle preghiere, non vede le lagrime e parte... parte senza confidare i suoi segreti neppure all'aria che respira. Elisa... quell'ora è venuta!"

Impallidì e, a tutta prima, non trovò una parola, non emise un sospiro. Ma fu come toccare un dato tasto; esso provoca necessariamente una data nota. Sorrise tristemente e, con un filo di voce, che pur rivelava il prepotente impero dell'animo: "Che cosa posso dire, Carlo? So che combatti per una causa giusta; che nulla puoi compiere di cui io debba arrossire. Va e fa di ritornare presto, onesto qual sei, più glorioso!"

Mi inginocchiai e le coperti le mani di baci, come il giorno che ci eravamo fidanzati.

Per non prolungare le ore penose, avevo deciso di partire quella sera stessa da casa, se non da Londra; sarei stato più solerte e più sereno la mattina, all'imbarco. Si cenò in silenzio; la piena degli affetti scambiavamo colle occhiate, colle lunghe carezze. Ad un certo punto trassi l'orologio... Ella comprese e, alzatasi, corse nell'altra nostra cameretta. Ne fece ritorno portando in braccio il nostro primogenito, grasso, roseo tra i lini candidi.

Dormiva e sorrideva... forse, in sogno, al seno turgido di mamma sua. Abbracciai con effusione la testa d'Elisa, le sussurrai qualche consiglio... Un altro bacio con labbra frementi... poi fuggii!

FOR several days, I forced myself to suppress the thoughts that surged tumultuously through my head, renewing old acquaintances. At the same time, I sought some foothold that might put an end to my extremely thorny financial situation, and secure for me work and bread for the future.

One evening, a little before sunset, I was returning, unusually tired, almost dispirited, from my customary excursion, when, turning the corner into the square of Shoah, I literally ran into Simon Bernard.

"Bravo! Bravo! I have just come from your lodgings. Signor Orsini has written. Do you know what he has written? That he knows you by proof. That he wants a man of his fullest confidence. Either you, or he sends the whole business to ruin. What do you intend to do?"

This time I answered: "I have given my word to Felice Orsini; I shall keep it."

Instead of going straight home, we walked three or four times round the square. Orsini's letter had dissipated Simon Bernard's coldness and loosened his tongue. I learned that Felice Orsini was already in Paris, gathering again the threads of his conspiracy; that the means definitively chosen for the attempt was that of bombs. That the Baron Egassy di Torocfalda had devised a wholly new type, fashioned in the form of a hedgehog, each spike armed with a percussion cap, so that they would explode in whatever manner they were thrown. The Taylor foundry in Birmingham had cast them piece by piece, in the guise of gas-fittings. The substance destined to make them explode was mercury fulminate. The same Baron di Torrocfalda had taught Orsini how to prepare it, and Orsini had so thoroughly profited from the lesson that he had managed without mishap, scorching but a few fingers.

On taking his leave, Simon Bernard recommended that I hold myself in readiness, since from one day to the next the order of departure might reach me. And I concluded at once that the time had come to seek the cooperation of Giuseppe Mazzini.

To obtain an interview with him was not so easy as it might appear; whether the Maestro, spied upon by every Police in the world, had grown distrustful; or whether, as many maintained, certain persons with whom he had surrounded himself were at pains to keep at a distance all those who might have aided him effectively in some daring enterprise of his. In conversation among friends it was said, sometimes with a smile, but more often not without a touch of resentment, that it was more difficult to obtain an audience with Giuseppe Mazzini than with the Queen of England.

In those days, truth to tell, several individuals had been drawing near me, together with Massarenti, men whom I knew to be in close relations with the Maestro. But my well-founded distrust had led me to play, in their company, the part of the man who listens and does not speak.

Unexpectedly, chance seemed to favour me. I was walking in Cheapside with a certain Gori, formerly a *capobanda* (band-leader) of the Zambeccari Legion, when I caught sight, at a short distance before me, of Mazzini arm in arm with an unknown gentleman. I quickened my pace and caught up with him. Naturally, I could not speak of so important a matter in the presence of strangers, and after the necessary greetings I was obliged to confine myself to telling Mazzini that I desired an interview, and to ask him to name the hour and place. Giuseppe Mazzini gazed into the air, as though seeking inspiration there; then, having asked for my address, said that Massarenti would be able to tell me something in that regard. Massarenti... precisely the fish that stuck in our throat!

We parted. Events came headlong and swept me into their vortex before Mazzini made any sign of life. What would have happened had I been able to speak with him? Would he have dissuaded me from the enterprise, or would he have brought to it the contribution of his influence and his ardent soul? These are questions that were put to me repeatedly. Certainly, to answer them is of no consequence. Yet I observe: the man who wrote that certain exceptional actions, undertaken in vindication of human rights, ought not to be judged by the standard of common morality; the man who pointed out to Gallenga the dagger that should have killed Carlo Alberto; who had a part — not clearly defined, yet certain — in the Tebaldi attempt against Luigi Bonaparte, could not logically have opposed that of Felice Orsini. And the Maestro knew full well where logic has its dwelling!

The Tebaldi attempt had been organised precisely in that year, 1857. Napoleon III frequented the house of the Countess Sommariva for reasons, it was said, better known to him than to the Countess's family. Tebaldi and his two accomplices were to gain entry, by means of a false key, into that house, and take possession of the Emperor's person. The intention appears to have been to force him to abdicate. But the capture of a man so vigilantly guarded could not be accomplished without bloodshed, and in the Maestro's eyes the life of a simple policeman was worth no less than that of an Emperor. However that may be, Tebaldi's two accomplices could not keep the secret, and all three were arrested. Tebaldi was exiled to the Île du Diable; his two companions to Algeria.

The reaction aroused by the misdeeds of Luigi Napoleon was too intense not to manifest itself in violent and personal fashion. Regicide was in the air, and Napoleon knew it so well that, as early as 1852, after the coup d'état and before the proclamation of the Empire, he had the Romagnoles Galli and Rossini deported to Cayenne — guilty of nothing save that of having fallen foul of the infamous Law of Suspects.

The attempt of that other daring Romagnole, Giovanni Pianori, failed because Napoleon, unable to shelter himself

"Beneath the shield of a clear conscience,"

wore, in secret, a coat of chain-mail. Pianori was struck down before he could fire his second shot. He swelled the company of those martyred by the guillotine. His brother, already embittered by political hatred, felt his fury redoubled in his breast at that tragic death. He left Italy, meditating a resounding vengeance. But no sooner had he set foot on French soil than he was arrested and deported to the hell of the tropical islands, like Galli and Redini, without a shadow of a trial.

Fierce tempers, these Pianori brothers! They recognised no duty save the vindication of their own rights and those of others, and toward it they ran resolved, heedless of the Laws, which they held to be a scarecrow contrived by the cunning, for the purpose of domination and plunder; regardless of their own lives, which they said were well spent, if spent in the erasure of an injustice.

Giovanni Pianori, the guillotined, stands forth in all his grim wholeness from yet another tragic incident. He loved his wife to distraction. Compelled by the turbulent vicissitudes of his existence to part from her, he had entrusted her to the care of a close friend who, having insinuated himself into the heart of the comely *protégée*, proved too tender a lover to remain a faithful friend. Some zealous informer breathed the painful truth into the ear of the betrayed man who, braving a thousand dangers, made a sudden return. It was no great difficulty to wring from the terrified woman her imprudent confession. He grew calm again and did not lay a hand upon her; but he imposed absolute silence, with that resoluteness which commands and admits no reply.

The intimate friend arrived. Pianori, dissembling, invited him, after a brief conversation, out of doors. They might discuss certain secret political affairs with greater freedom. All at once, in the darkness, the friend heard himself addressed with this question: "What would you do to a friend to whom you had entrusted your honour, and all that is most precious in the world, if he were to dishonour you, if he were to betray you?"

— You have pronounced your own sentence!

And without another word, he laid him gasping in his own blood.

A crime? I do not say otherwise. I say only that iron times make iron men, and that it is folly to expect mild manners and sheep-like sentiments from people accustomed to the daily spectacle of virtue swinging from a gibbet. And I say further that abstract ideas are fixed in practical life, embodied in History, not by timid bleatings, but by the impetuous action of tempers such as these.

The next letter sent me by Felice Orsini had been written from Paris, but its envelope bore no other postmark than that of London. Evidently some trusted third party had received it and posted it in the latter city. Orsini asked me to call upon Miss Elisa Cheney, residing at Camden Town, who would hand me a travelling-bag and a pair of gold spectacles. What was actually meant by "gold spectacles" I learned shortly afterwards from Simon Bernard. Probably so as not to attract the attention of the customs officials with an excessively bulky parcel, Felice Orsini had carried with him, on his journey to Paris, only a portion of the mercury fulminate he required. He was now entrusting me with the transport of the remainder.

I went to Camden Town that very evening. Miss Elisa Cheney — a woman who appeared to be somewhere in her thirties, slender, slight, dark, not beautiful in the classical sense, but most attractive — received me with exquisite graciousness and a smile upon her lips. The bag she handed me was, more precisely, one of those receptacles the English call a "carpet-bag" — its sides of fabric, its base of leather. Upon the sides a tiger was embroidered. The container of the mercury fulminate was of mahogany and had every appearance of a dressing-case. It stood on the mantelpiece among a few trinkets and a clock. Before stretching out her hand to it, the young lady cast Orsini's letter down upon the marble. She was perfectly calm; so much so that not even the faintest tremor disturbed her persistent smile when she held the box between her fingers. Either she possessed nerves of the most proven temper, or she was ignorant of the terrible power of the elegant article she was handing me. She stood and watched me in silence while I closed it again inside the bag; then all at once, smiling still: "You are going to Paris? A safe journey, and my compliments to Signor Orsini!"

When I returned home and Elisa came, with the child in her arms, to offer me her cheek for the customary kiss, I was obliged to make an effort to carry the bag with an air of unconcern. "Have you been shopping?" she asked. And she reached out her arm. But I said playfully: "Have a care! The tiger is alive and might scratch you!" Then, turning serious again: "These are papers of no interest to you." And I locked away in a cupboard the tiger capable of rending asunder, disguised beneath the other, embroidered and harmless one.

The day after, the 8th of January 1858, Simon Bernard appeared once more upon the threshold. He wore a grave, almost solemn expression, and I, who was now expecting definitive communications, understood at once that a few turns about the square would be most advisable. Nor had I been mistaken.

"You must depart," Bernard told me. "Orsini calculates — indeed he insists — that you set out tomorrow morning. In that case, tomorrow evening, between six and eight o'clock, you would arrive in Paris. He will be at the station... the rest will take care of itself. But in the event that you cannot depart in time, or that Orsini, by some unforeseen mishap, should fail to keep the appointment, take note of his address!" And he dictated it to me.

He left with a vigorous handshake and the assurance: "Au revoir, in a few days, in Paris."

That evening, on returning, I did not give Elisa the usual kiss, as though to prepare her for the parting. While she laid the table for supper, I sat to one side, nervously tormenting my beard. She caught me with my eyes fixed upon her and, unable to restrain herself, threw her arms about my neck, murmuring softly in my ear: "Carlo, what ails you?"

Then I rose, stroked her hair, kissed her brow, and spoke to her the words that, on another occasion, in a very different moment, I had pressed upon her tender heart as a seal upon a solemn bond: "The conspirator must be solitary even within his family. He must sometimes forget that he has a mother and a wife and children. If his predestined hour arrives, he closes his ears to entreaties, he does not see the tears, and he departs... he departs without confiding his secrets even to the air he breathes. Elisa... that hour has come!"

She grew pale and, at first, found not a word, breathed not a sigh. But it was as though a certain key had been struck; it necessarily calls forth a certain note. She smiled sadly and, with a thread of voice that nonetheless revealed the imperious sovereignty of her spirit: "What can I say, Carlo? I know that you fight for a just cause; that there is nothing you can do of which I need be ashamed. Go, and come back soon — as honest as you are, and more glorious!"

I knelt down and covered her hands with kisses, as on the day we had pledged ourselves to each other.

So as not to prolong the painful hours, I had resolved to depart that very evening from the house, if not yet from London; I should be more alert and more composed in the morning, at the embarkation. We supped in silence; the fullness of our affections we exchanged through glances, through long caresses. At a certain moment I drew out my watch... She understood, and rising, hurried into our other small room. She returned carrying in her arms our firstborn — plump and rosy among his white linens.

He slept, and smiled... dreaming, perhaps, at his mother's full breast. I clasped Elisa's head to me with deep tenderness, whispered to her some counsel... One more kiss with trembling lips... then I fled!

## Chapter the Fifth

IN September of 1857, while I was making shift to live by giving language lessons in Nottingham, an Italian exile passing through that city came to call on me. He found himself in circumstances far more critical than my own. I came to his aid, and that same day he continued on his way. Arriving in Birmingham, he applied to Felice Orsini for assistance — Orsini being, if I am not mistaken, his fellow-countryman. In the course of recounting his misfortunes, he made mention of the visit he had paid me. “By God!” exclaimed Felice Orsini. “The very man I am looking for!” And he wrote to me, giving me an appointment at the Great Northern station in London.

I had not seen Felice Orsini for a long while. But no sooner had the train puffed in under the station roof (Charing Cross) than I caught sight of him standing erect amid the crowd, with his little cases and his very black moustaches. A few seconds and I was face to face with him. “Where the devil have you been all this time?” he said to me. And I told him. When I touched upon my marriage, his face clouded over. “Then, farewell to patriotic resolutions!” I disabused him. Indeed, I now felt all the greater obligation to conspire and to fight — my son was not to have a servile and unhappy fatherland.

He asked me whether I knew the reason he had given me the appointment, and upon my replying that I did not, he invited me to step outside the station. Once in the open air, he reminded me without preamble of the letter I had written to Giuseppe Mazzini and of my proposal to rid France of her troubles by suppressing Napoleon. He had it in mind to translate that old idea of mine into action. The times seemed to him propitious. Was I still of the same opinion?

— What does Mazzini think of it?

At this he made a gesture as if to say: Why do you speak to me of archaeology? And he strengthened his point by declaring that he no longer spoke with Mazzini. Moreover, Massarenti, the *alter ego* of the “prophet,” was a discredit to the Bolognese breed, and would, the moment he caught wind of the matter, undoubtedly denounce him. For this reason too, it was necessary to leave Mazzini out of it.

In the end I told him that I was not averse to taking part in the enterprise. But I wished to see things clearly. Napoleon once killed, what would happen? What were his plans for the aftermath?

I shall repeat, in summary, his explanations. They reveal, if not the plan, the aims of a conspiracy about which so much has been written, yet without ever fully laying bare the truth of it. They serve, at the same time, as a posthumous refutation of the charges which certain historians, lacking the necessary grounds for judgement, chose to bring against Orsini.

Felice Orsini had come to a full understanding with the French Republicans. The coup d’état had surprised and paralysed them — but had not tamed them. They were conspiring to prepare a revolution. A successful bold stroke in Paris would mean the liberation of France, and France restored to liberty would mean all Europe in ferment. Ledru Rollin — Orsini assured me — despairs of success so long as he finds the sinister figure of Napoleon before him. There is more force than one might suppose in a name. If France stirs, Kossuth vouches for Hungary. As regards our Italy — Napoleon suppressed, Paris throws up barricades. The enemy, caught unawares, without an authoritative leader, with the cohesion of the army already undermined by subtle intrigue, falls ignominiously. The Republic is proclaimed, and immediately I am appointed, with full powers, Commissioner with the French army stationed at Rome. Those are thirty thousand seasoned and well-equipped men that I win, at a single stroke, to the Italian cause. What do I do? In a single day I cancel the work and the shame of Napoleon’s deed of 1849. The Pope and the Cardinals become my prisoners and I lock them up in Castel Sant’Angelo — their fate will depend upon the attitude of Europe. Meanwhile I sever the temporal power from the spiritual, and proclaim from the Campidoglio the resurrection of the Roman Republic. The French garrison quits Rome, and I drive it swiftly forward in support of the Italian revolution. Do we win? So much the better! Do we suffer a reverse? Republican France, stung to the quick, rises like a fury and lays down her arms no more until the prestige of her own name has been fully vindicated. Austria, assailed on every side, without friends in Europe, with rebellion blazing within her very borders, is swept away and never again sets foot in Italy...

Certainly, it would remain to establish the definitive political order of the Peninsula. But there are things which, in despite of their vastness, arrange themselves almost of their own accord. The blind, obstinate dualism between republicans and monarchists has already proved fatal to the Italian People; the lesson has instructed it. The People desires independence and unity, and now places everything else in the second rank. If the Savoyard Monarchy showed itself capable of giving the People the strength it lacks and of leading it to victory, the People would throw itself, as one man, at the feet of Vittorio Emanuele. If victory should come from republican Rome and from republican allies, what sovereign would feel himself great enough to oppose the proclamation of the Republic?

In the present moment the noble contest is open. Should we republicans spontaneously renounce the glorious initiative?

Everything depends upon audacity! One must know how to cast the first spark. Without it there is no conflagration, independence is impossible, the Republic is impossible. You know that I am opposed to the shedding of blood. Nature has endowed me with a humane temperament: I aim high, and the means imposed by these dark times repel me. But I say that the blood of Louis Bonaparte — of the man who has knowingly broken faith with solemnly sworn obligations, who has betrayed us, who has put our Fatherland back in chains, who tramples upon every noble aspiration of his own — must not weigh in the balance against the liberty of seventy millions of human creatures. To shed it averts the sacrifice of thousands upon thousands of honest lives, and the cruel act becomes a work of mercy. Decide!

And I decided. The man’s rectitude was known to me; the revolutionary undercurrent in France was known to me, more or less; the conditions of Italy were known. To probe further I deemed superfluous, and I contented myself with asking the names of those who were to execute the plan materially. But Orsini fended off the question. The great danger in great conspiracies — he pointed out to me — is precisely their magnitude. The more numerous those who carry the secret in their hearts, the greater become the chances that some lip will let it escape. To guard, in part, against this unavoidable peril, he had decided that the participants in the attempt should not know one another.

To shelter ourselves from the cold, which that day was rather sharp, we entered a public house. The conversation continued by fits and starts. Orsini had to be in Liverpool on the morrow, and he begged me to return to Nottingham. “Stay quietly at home and work, until you hear from me.” If he could not come in person, he would send a trusted agent with a letter from him and with his instructions. And as a further precaution, we established a password. I asked him whether, with regard to the attempt, he had any well-defined plan already. He knitted his brows and appeared vexed. Orsini was unable to dissemble his emotions. After a brief silence, he answered me that he could communicate nothing precise. “My intention,” he added, “would be to have ourselves invited to Court. There is someone in a position to procure us as many invitations as we wish. To approach the usurper during a ball is a matter of little moment, and it might be possible, with cold steel, to achieve the object without spilling a single drop of blood more than necessary. But it is feared that interested parties would close the doors of the palace and prevent news of what had occurred from spreading, until all the orders necessary to avert the consequences had been given; until they had proclaimed the Regency. Perhaps it will be necessary to resort to a noisier means. However that may be — your word is given. Can I count upon it with certainty?”

I replied that Carlo di Rudio had never failed in his undertakings, and since, by simple intuition, I believed I had understood, more or less, what the “noisier means” might consist in, I observed to him that the retinue of the usurper must necessarily be composed of supporters of the empire and of soldiers complicit in the coup of the 2nd of December. The more of them one killed, the fewer we should find before us on the morrow’s barricades.

We parted that same evening. The following day I returned to Nottingham, and turning over in my mind, to the rhythm of the train, what had been confided to me and the prospects of drawing from it a broad movement that might prove salutary to the popular cause throughout the whole of Europe, I understood at once what an immense advantage Mazzini’s awareness of it would prove. I knew no influence more profound or more efficacious than his. To leave such a man aside was to run the risk of losing the fruit of our daring. The figure of the “Bolognese” presented itself to my mind, but did not strike me as a matter of consequence. I knew well enough that it sufficed to narrate the affair with discretion and to exact a promise of absolute silence for the Maestro to face and endure any torture whatsoever without letting fall a single compromising syllable. The “coward” of the Savoy expedition, as certain moderate newspapers called him, was incontestably among the most humanly courageous individuals of the century.

The ill-feeling between the Maestro and Orsini? I felt certain that it would vanish in the face of the supreme interests of the fatherland. And from that moment I resolved to open my heart to Mazzini, to confide to him as much as would suffice that events should not catch him unawares.

My wife was pleasantly surprised at my return, but did not venture a single indiscreet question, and for several days our life passed in its usual calm, as though nothing were in the air. Towards the middle of November, a letter was delivered to me bearing the Birmingham postmark. It contained these simple words: “Go to London and send me your address.”

I understood that the knot was drawing tight, and reflecting that, in all likelihood, once I had quitted English soil, it would be given me no more to see again either my wife or my child, I said curtly to Elisa: “Pack your trunk; I intend to settle in London!” She set about complying at once. I watched her. From her every movement there shone the loving conviction that she ought not to seek beyond; that everything I asked of her was for a good end and for a just cause. She brushed, folded, and put away — gentle and silent.

We departed and took up lodgings in Bedman Buildings, on Shoah Square. Two days of waiting. On the morning of the third, I was tickling the child under the chin — Elisa had just lowered him into a basin of warm water — when a ring at the bell made me start. On the threshold I found myself face to face with a well-dressed gentleman, evidently a foreigner; who, having asked whether I was such and such a one, without adding anything further, handed me an open letter. It was from Felice Orsini, begging me to place “full confidence in Simon Bernard, a French physician from Carcassonne.” By way of greater surety, I asked for and received the password. I then invited my visitor to enter.

The child was kicking in the basin and beating the water with his little hands, spattering the table. At that sight, Simon Bernard fixed his eyes upon my face as if putting to me a mute question; then he looked at the child again, and ended by casting at my wife a troubled and perplexed glance.

I believed I could divine the cause of his embarrassment, and observed to him that he might speak freely in French; my wife would not understand a syllable.

“Oh, that is not precisely the point!” And he became thoughtful again. Suddenly he appeared to make up his mind, and having recovered all the gallantry natural to his race, he lavished compliments upon the mother for the thriving appearance of her little one, and upon the husband for his good taste in the choice of a wife. He spoke of London and its accursed climate; of France and the matchless gaiety of Paris; he touched upon a hundred subjects without going deep into any of them, with an elegance of phrase and a volatility of thought more suited to a drawing-room of idle gentlefolk than to the humble lodging of a conspirator and among conspirators.

When he took his leave, I accompanied him to the door, and he, touching my arm, said to me in an undertone: “Let us take a few turns about the square; we shall have more freedom to talk.”

Scarcely had we descended than he placed himself before me and, at last giving rein to the thought which had preoccupied him: “*Parbleu!* I did not know you were a family man!”

— Felice Orsini knows it.

— But the Secret Committee here in London does not. In any case, this visit was necessary for us to make each other’s acquaintance. This evening the Committee assembles, and I shall report. Tomorrow we shall meet again.

And the following day he came. From the moment I opened the door to him, I noted that his expression was that of a cheerful man. This time he ventured to speak in Elisa’s presence — in French, naturally. He had nothing new to communicate regarding the conspiracy. But he was pleased to announce to me that the Secret Committee had decided to pay my wife, during my absence, fourteen shillings a week. In the event of misfortune, the allowance would be continued until my child had reached the age of fourteen years.

Rightly or wrongly, the overriding concern of being able to serve my Country had almost entirely displaced from my mind the other concern of the future. Elisa’s mother belonged to that order of women whose name cannot be separated from the idea of veneration. The certainty that she would never leave either her daughter or the little creature destitute had sufficed to keep me, in that respect, perfectly tranquil. And, to tell the truth, the news communicated to me by Simon Bernard, at first, nettled me. But had I the right to yield to the impulse of pride; to impose upon weak and unknowing creatures the whole weight of my actions? Would it have been honest to refuse?

I gave thanks, and Simon Bernard, citing urgent business as his pretext — business which, moreover, would prevent him from showing himself for some days — pressed my hand and departed. No small was my wonder, therefore, when on the following morning I found him once more upon my threshold. He set before me, without even crossing it, the reason for that unexpected visit. The Secret Committee had reconsidered its decision and had concluded that it could not place at risk the life of a man who had a wife and was a father. I was therefore relieved, with a thousand thanks, from taking any part in the affair. I contented myself with replying: “Very well. Let us say no more about it.”
