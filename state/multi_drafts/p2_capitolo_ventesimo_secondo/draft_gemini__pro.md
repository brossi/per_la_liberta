THE DURR sailed on, carrying in her spacious belly, divided into three decks, some three hundred convicts. Gomez and I were locked in the deepest one. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and must not have harboured an excessive antipathy for us, as soon as he learned who we were, had us transferred to the upper deck. Indeed, he carried his benevolent partiality so far as to permit me to go up on deck from time to time, to smoke freely... the Regulations prescribed the trifle of fifty lashes of the rope for anyone who did so in the compartments.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz (Carmarie) to take on water and remained there for two days, with windows closed, to remove any possibility of our escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale of the Salvation Islands, where about two hundred convicts were disembarked.

I, Gomez, and another hundred derelicts were moved aboard a small steamer, and on the 11th of December 1858, after an additional day and a half of navigation, we were anchored before the Montagne d'Argent, which rises at the confluence of the Saint-Georges and the Oyapock, a relatively short distance from the border of Brazil. A free colony once flourished in that place, and free hands there cultivated coffee and every tropical fruit that can tempt the human palate. The French Government had purchased it. Its officers occupied the planter’s house; numerous reed-thatched sheds, supported by poles, were erected there as shelter for the convicts; the earth was pierced here and there, to bury therein, in slow agony, those refractory to discipline; a few substantial buildings also arose for the storehouses, for defence, and for offence: the vanguard of civilisation had been transformed into a penal colony. But the natural beauties marvellously concealed the hell that men had accumulated there.

The commander, a Major of the marine infantry whom the Republic had retired and the Emperor had reinstated and decorated with honorific insignia, hastened to receive us "into his charge" and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it and, sizing me up from head to foot: “Ah, so you are that— (here a filthy oath!) I am pleased to see you here, and I swear you will leave your skin here!”...

That vulgarity was so out of place that even the guards made a grimace of disgust. But I did not lose my composure and, as if flattered by a compliment: “Thank you for the good wish, and I return it with all my heart!” He clenched his jaw; but he had Gallic ferocity, not French wit, and remained silent.

That small incident persuaded me that the air at Montagne d'Argent was foul for me, and from that moment on, I resolved to repress my every impulse. The relative liberty that was granted to the disciplined—I thought—could offer me the opportunity to gain a greater one... the liberty of civilised men. But it was fated that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the shed assigned to me there were some fifty convicts who formed my squad. I spent the first night sleeping in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise, our bones aching, we set off towards the forest. I wore a large straw hat, a pair of wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, and a brown blouse, on the back of which was marked in large characters my number—the baptism of the penal colony!

In crossing a swamp, I had the misfortune of losing one of my clogs, and the stumps of the reeds, cut at water level to facilitate the ford, sliced up the sole of my foot. I had to contrive to cover three miles, limping and marking my every footprint with blood, until I arrived, the last of my squad, in the thick of the forest... a splendid forest that, for a moment, almost made me forget my being a convict and my agony.

A pile of logs. The task of the day was to carry them, on our shoulders, to the penitentiary, making five consecutive trips. I eyed a log that, by its look, seemed light; but it was *boia fer* (ironwood), and when I had it at the height of my knees, it fell noisily back onto the pile. Then, instead of reattempting the trial, I sat down, resolved to rest just enough to regain my strength. The convict-guard stood observing me and when he was certain of my thought: “Get up, you filthy pig! If you don’t hurry, I’ll kick you all the way there. Damn you and the whole Italian seed!”

Had he contented himself with goading me with a simple threat I would have, probably, taken the matter with all philosophy; but that contempt for my nationality stirred in my breast the accumulated wrath against the oppressors who were savaging it. I brandished my *sabre d'abattis* (clearing blade) and made a leap of which, considering my condition, I would not have thought myself capable. “Take care! Italians do not suffer insults. The first... I let pass! At the second, your pumpkin head will no longer see the sun from atop your shoulders!”

My companions, having left one by one, were already far away. Alone, perfectly alone in a deserted place! He swallowed the pill, turning pale. However, to make a show of bravado, he asked my name. “Be content with the number!” and I turned my back on him.

It was a bad start! But as chance would have it, my guard, though able to spell more or less correctly, could not write. To make his report, he had to resort to the clerk of the supervising Engineer, who, with the help of the number, verified in the registers my name and who I was. What sentiments animated him with regard to me, I do not know; but I learned that he persuaded my guard not to be cruel. And in his exhortations he deftly insinuated fear: “That man,” he said, “fears neither Laws, nor punishments, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later, you’re a dead man!”

Upon his return to the forest, the guard called me aside. “We have certain types in the penitentiary!... But I know how to make my distinctions.” And to prove it to me, he asked pardon for his harsh words and granted me leave not to work, neither then nor later. I was to hide myself daily in the thicket... I would reappear at the last departure of my squad. And he extended his right hand, as if to seal a pact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours destined for work stretched out amidst a wondrous vegetation, smoking a cigarette, listening to the complicated whisper of the forest. In my eyes, in those hours, there was a fantastic intertwining of embroideries never seen before: enormous leaves cut from velvet, trunks tall and straight as minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their own colours enslaved strange insects to their ill-concealed loves; birds with metallic sheens, emerging from the rustling shadows, passed over my head with wings spread, like lightning. There were grottoes of a deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults, dripping with dew and aromas, from which the Sun gazed placidly, lordly, as if watching that every blade of grass had its due.

The Sun, which, consciously or unconsciously adored in all ages by all Peoples, had, with secular energy, raised to itself that temple, worthy of a God. Oh, how beautiful the Earth is when it is not disfigured by the greed of man!... A temple worthy also of Elisa!... Her image, intensely recalled, dominated me as if she were present!

It was in that forest and in those hours that I meditated my second escape attempt. The plan I finally settled on came from the primitive art of the Indians who roam the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a large tree, to hollow it out so as to fashion a capable pirogue. With it, I would ascend the Oyapok by night for a distance of fifty miles and then cross the Saint-Georges, to land on the shores of Brazil. Having escaped the pursuit of the French Authorities, I would have had to face, probably, the ferocity of the savages and, undoubtedly, the almost insurmountable obstacles of the unexplored regions and the climate. But the prize was worth the risk!

All this could not even be initiated without the help of several accomplices. I sounded out the soul of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, satisfied with his position as assistant canteen-keeper and probably fearing the worst, pretended not to understand me. It was necessary to resort to less noble material. By what criteria to choose it? How to acquire over it the necessary influence to bind it to my intentions?

In this case too, my lucky star, more than my wisdom, came to my aid. The penitentiary received its provisions of fresh meat from Brazil. At more or less fixed periods, a sailing ship would arrive with a cargo of cattle. The shore, which sloped gradually into the Ocean, did not afford it sufficient depth to moor near the mainland, so the anchor was dropped offshore, at a distance of about three miles. The guard would then choose one convict from each decuria and form a special squad that would make its way painfully, with the water above their waists, to the sailing ship. The wild, enraged oxen were thrown into the sea, and the men drove them, with great shouts and great lashes, into a stockade within the penitentiary.

Once that work began, it proceeded without interruption until the unloading was complete, skipping, if need be, even a few meals. At Montagne d'Argent, one ate by decurias. The leader received the victuals, thinned by the sordid speculation of the officers, and arranged them, on a sort of table, into the ten mess tins. Each convict ran to his own and devoured it, seated on some step, or on a tree trunk, or on the bare earth. On unloading days, the leader of the decuria was obliged to keep the ration of the absent convict and deliver it to him on his return.

Chosen to lend a hand in that devilish work, one day I made my final return at eight in the evening, when my decuria was already snoring in their hammocks. My hunger, sharpened by the incessant gymnastics, the long bath, and the harsh sea breeze, had become ferocious. I shook the leader of the decuria, raised a minor commotion, and he, showing me his fist instead of the mess tin: "*Tais toi, Italien de m...*" (The word of Cambronne).

I chewed on nothing and remained silent. The evening of the following day, I got in line with the decuria and waited around the table. When the leader arrived, holding the pot overflowing with soup, I lunged and gave it a mighty kick. The leader remained stupefied, his arms outstretched, his hands empty. “It takes,” I said, “nine Frenchmen to make an Italian go hungry, but a single Italian is enough to make nine Frenchmen go hungry!”

Now it was the others who raised a commotion. The guard, one Cosson, came running and, informed of the matter, asked me brusquely: “Why, instead of taking revenge, did you not submit your report?”

—Because Italians are not informers!

Cosson, playing Solomon, condemned both the leader of the decuria and Count Carlo di Rudio to fifty lashes of the rope. But my resolve and, above all, my proud reply, had suddenly elevated me in the estimation of those wretches. They surrounded the guard and said so much that he consented to swallow his sentence. From that day, the convicts condescended, bless their hearts, to treat me fraternally as one worthy of their congregation; from that day, it became a relatively easy enterprise for me to study the various humours, to choose eight individuals so fond of liberty that they would cheerfully face any danger in the attempt to regain it.

We would leave our shed at night, crawling like beasts emerging from their lair, to deceive any eyes that might still be awake. We proceeded by different routes to a pre-established spot on the edge of the forest; then, united, we advanced into its labyrinths. From the very first night, we had marked our path with signs almost invisible to other mortals, thus warding off the danger of getting lost.

A tree of gigantic proportions was cut at its base with relatively little effort; but, in falling, it landed on the dense growth and remained leaning there. We had to climb it and hollow it out while clinging on: a complication that required precious time. We finally decided to resort to fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary and the smoke was rendered invisible by the night; but amidst so much resinous vegetation, so many dead shrubs, so many splinters, so much refuse that had accumulated for centuries, the operation required great prudence, or we would have fed a fire so rapid and so vast that the very deluges of the tropics would have struggled to tame it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constitutes, for me, one of those incidents whose impression only death can erase. We were not in that land as pioneers of civilisation, and some might even ironically observe that we were not of missionary stock. But the surroundings... oh, the grandeur of the surroundings, rendered ineffable by the silent night, how it penetrated, with its own essence, into ours, and how it transformed us! Around us, there was nothing that was not born and grown in the most boundless liberty, and liberty became almost tangible in every atom of air we breathed. It seemed to me that my muscles, as they tired, exhaled from their every fibre something luminous, as if they were returning to things the uncontaminated life that things lent to us. There were moments when I would have sworn I was as vigorous and whole as the virgin forest. And we became such a homogeneous part of the environment that, after the first few nights, innumerable troops of monkeys, attracted by the glow of the resin, came to contemplate and almost to encourage our work, cheering it.

After a couple of months, or little more, we managed to drag into a bend of the river, and to hide among the lianas, a canoe seven feet wide and twenty-five long. It floated marvellously. “And now,” I exclaimed, when we separated full of enthusiasm, “to the oars and the sail!” Unexpectedly, an event to which, at its first appearance, no one paid serious attention, threw the entire penal Colony into turmoil, and with it all my hopes.

In broad daylight, with a clear sky that was a lyric poem in itself, with a sun capable of hardening ostrich eggs in its direct rays, a sentry was seized by violent tremors. Relieved as quickly as possible, he was seen to walk with slow steps, trembling, into the antediluvian shack that bore the pompous name of “Cabinet of decency.” He emerged ashen, distraught. He affirmed that, however much he strained, he had not managed to pass a single drop of water. The convicts laughed loudly... the thing seemed so droll to them! By evening, the poor man was vomiting clotted blood, black as coal... two days later he was a corpse!

But even before he had drawn his last breaths, five, seven, ten convicts, though showing a high temperature on the thermometer, were trembling like leaves. The penitentiary was overrun by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was swift beyond all telling. In those days, it was believed that the seat of the disease was in the intestines; the remedies, very scarce and wholly inadequate to the vastness of the plague, were, to make matters worse, applied with erroneous criteria. The Commander gave the order to open the storehouses and the cellar and to leave them at the full disposal of the healthy. From that cellar, yawning and black, I felt the very sense of doom issue forth. If the yellow fever was not crouching in there, it had some mysterious ally within. The convicts were inexorably drawn there by vice and by fear. There they became like beasts and passed from drunkenness to delirium; from delirium to nothingness!... with a death all the more fulminating, the more robust they were.

The arrangements for the victims were very simple: to throw them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Every evening, the chief guard went to report at the Commander’s house.

—How many today?

—Twenty, thirty, forty!...

—And the convict di Rudio?

—Fit as a fiddle!

One night the Commander’s wife and two children died almost simultaneously, and he ceased to take an interest in my health. Not long after, it was the chief guard who ceased to go to the usual report. He had too easily yielded to the temptations of the cellar and, when he was found stretched out on the beaten earth, it was suspected at first that he was simply drunk. But his gums were covered with bloody froth... he died without having regained either speech or a glimmer of consciousness, and he was one of the many I had to grab by the feet. “One, two, three...” Before throwing him, I would have liked to say a “Requiescat!” to him—he had spared me fifty lashes of the rope. But down there in that abyss it would have seemed...

I lent a hand to bury, one by one, in that manner, my eight accomplices in the escape attempt, and others, and others... I counted to over a hundred; then I became confused and counted no more. Some unfortunate, who already seemed to have passed the boundary beyond which one no longer suffers, as soon as he was thrown into that horrible common grave, was seen to revive and writhe in agony!

When the fever, finding no more flesh to gnaw, disappeared, the French Deputies raised, in Paris, the usual humanitarian uproar. But the Minister replied to them, with an air of triumph, that he had already given the opportune orders to transport the entire Colony to the Île du Salut. And in fact one evening, we saw silhouetted against the twilight, the black streak of smoke from a ship that was proceeding at full steam toward our shore. It was the "Abeille," bringing us the orders of His Excellency. The following day the entire Colony was aboard... Of over six hundred, between convicts, soldiers, civil employees, and servants, we were reduced to sixty-three! Most of the survivors had already suffered from yellow fever in other times and on other shores, which had, it seems, made them resistant, if not absolutely invulnerable; several, shadows of their former selves, showed that they had been touched by it then and there, if not cut down: among these, Antonio Gomez... Oh, how his face was transformed—that of a good-natured priest! Only three had not had their blood infected by it, neither then nor before. Among the three was the convict di Rudio! From the deck of the "Abeille," around which the Ocean already foamed and broke, I cast a final glance at the Montagne d'Argent and thought, with profound regret, of my canoe that floated, solitary and motionless, under the thick lianas. The landscape shrank rapidly. The hell that men had accumulated there was no more; what remained was one of its most fatal consequences, the vast charnel house from which we had miraculously escaped.
