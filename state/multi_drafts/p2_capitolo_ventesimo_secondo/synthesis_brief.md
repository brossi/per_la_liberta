LA DURR filava portando nel suo ampio ventre, diviso in tre ponti, circa trecento condannati. Io e il Gomez eravamo rinchiusi nel più profondo. Ma il Comandante, che era un orleanista e non doveva nutrire soverchio antipatie per noi, non appena seppe chi eravamo, ci fece trasportare sul ponte superiore. Anzi egli spinse la sua benevole parzialità sino a permettermi di salire, di quando in quando, sopra coperta per fumare liberamente... i Regolamenti prescrivevano la bagatella di cinquanta colpi di corda a chi l'avesse fatto nei compartimenti.

Poggiammo all'isola di Santa Cruz (Carmarie) per approvvigionarci d'acqua e vi rimanemmo due giorni, a finestre chiuse, per toglierci ogni possibilità di fuga; poi si procedette direttamente all'Isola reale del Saluto, ove vennero sbarcati circa duecento forzati.

Io, il Gomez ed un altro centinaio di derelitti passammo a bordo di un vaporino e l'11 Dicembre del 1858, dopo un giorno e mezzo di navigazione addizionale, eravamo ancorati di fronte alla Montagne d'Argent, che sorge sul confluente del San Giorgio e dell'Ojapok, a distanza relativamente breve dal confine del Brasile. Fioriva un tempo, in quella località, una colonia libera e libere braccia vi coltivavano il caffè ed ogni frutto tropicale che possa tentare il palato umano. Il Governo francese ne aveva fatto acquisto. I suoi ufficiali occuparono la casa del piantatore; numerose tettoie di giunchi, sorrette da pali, vi vennero erette a riparo dei condannati; la terra venne forata qua e là, per sepellirvi, a lenta agonìa i refrattari alla disciplina; sorse anche qualche edificio sostanziale pei magazzini, per la difesa e per l'offesa: l'avanguardia della civiltà si era trasformata in un ergastolo. Ma le bellezze naturali dissimúlavano meravigliosamente l'inferno che gli uomini vi avevano accumulato.

Il comandante, un Maggiore d'infanteria marina, che la Repubblica aveva messo a riposo e l'Imperatore reintegrato e fregiato d'insegne onorifiche, si affrettò a riceverci "in consegna" e a fare il nostro appello. Quando pronunciò il mio nome, l'ufficiale che ci aveva accompagnato dall'isola del Saluto, gli porse una lettera. Evidentemente mi riguardava. Egli la lesse e, squadrandomi dal capo ai piedi: "Ah siete voi quel — (giù una parolaccia da porcile!) Sono contento di vedervi qui e vi giuro che qui lascerete la pelle!"...

Quella villania era tanto fuor di proposito, che persino le guardie fecero una smorfia di disgusto. Ma io non mi scomposi e, quasi lusingato da un complimento: "Grazie dell'augurio e lo ricambio di cuore!" Egli strinse le mascelle; ma aveva la ferocia gallica, non lo spirito francese e rimase a bocca chiusa.

Quel piccolo incidente mi persuase che alla Montagne d'Argent tirava, per me, aria cattiva e, sin d'allora, mi proposi di reprimere ogni mio impeto. La libertà relativa che veniva concessa ai disciplinati — pensai — mi può offrire il destro di guadagnarmene una maggiore... la libertà degli uomini inciviliti. Ma era destino che i miei saggi proponimenti svanissero non più tardi del giorno successivo.

Nel capannone che mi venne assegnato era una cinquantina di galeotti che formavano la mia squadra. Passai la prima notte dormendo, nella mia amaca, un sonno tormentato da uno sciame d'insetti. Al sorgere del sole ci avviammo, le ossa rotte, verso la foresta. Portavo un gran cappello di paglia, un paio di zoccoloni di legno, un paio di calzoni di tela, una blusa bruna, sul cui dosso era segnato, a grandi caratteri, il mio numero — il battesimo dell'ergastolo!

Nel traversare una palude, ebbi la mala sorte di perdere uno dei miei zoccoloni e i mozziconi dei giunchi, recisi a pelo d'acqua per facilitare il guado, mi tagliuzzarono la pianta del piede. Dovetti industriarmi a percorrere tre miglia zoppicando e segnando, a sangue, ogni mia orma; finché giunsi, ultimo della mia squadra, nel folto della foresta... splendida foresta che, per poco, non mi faceva dimenticare l'essere galeotto e lo spasimo.

Una catasta di tronchi. Il compito della giornata era di trasportarli, a spalla, sino al penitenziario, compiendo cinque viaggi consecutivi. Adocchiai un tronco che, alla vista, sembrava leggero; ma era "boia fer" e quando l'ebbi all'altezza de' miei ginocchi, ricadde rumorosamente sulla catasta. Allora, invece di ritentare la prova, sedetti, risoluto a riposare quel tanto che bastasse a ripigliar lena. Il galeotto-guardiano stette ad osservarmi e quando fu certo del mio pensiero: "Alzati sporcaccione! Se non ti sbrighi, ti fo correre a calci nel sedere. Maledetto te e tutto il seme italiano!"

Se si fosse accontentato di aizzarmi colla semplice minaccia avrei, probabilmente, presa la cosa con tutta filosofia; ma quello sprezzo alla mia nazionalità, suscitò nel mio petto l'ira accumulata contro i prepotenti che ne facevano strazio. Mulinai la mia 'sabre d'abattis' e diedi un salto del quale, considerate le mie condizioni, non mi sarei creduto capace. "Bada! gli Italiani non soffrono insulti. Il primo... passi! Al secondo, la tua zucca non vede più il sole dall'alto delle tue spalle!"

I miei compagni, partiti ad uno ad uno, eran già lontani. Soli, perfettamente soli in località deserta! Inghiottì, impallidendo, la pillola. Tuttavia, a ostentazione di bravura, mi chiese il nome. "Accontentati del numero!" e gli voltai le spalle.

Si incominciava male! Ma il caso volle che il mio guardiano, pur compitando più o meno correttamente, non sapesse scrivere. Per fare il suo rapporto, dovette ricorrere allo scritturare dell'Ingegnere soprintendente: il quale, colla scorta del numero, verificò sui registri, il mio nome e chi mi fossi. Quali sentimenti lo animassero a mio riguardo, ignoro; ma seppi che persuase il mio guardiano a non incrudelire. E nelle sue esortazioni insinuò destramente la paura: "Quell'uomo — disse — non teme né Leggi, né castighi, né Dio, né il diavolo. Se giura vendetta, o tosto o tardi, sei spacciato!"

Al suo ritorno nella foresta, il guardiano mi chiama in disparte. "Noi abbiamo nel penitenziario certe stoffe!... Ma so fare le mie distinzioni". E per dimostrarmelo, domandava scusa delle sue male parole e mi accordava licenza di non lavorare, né allora né poi. Mi celassi, giornalmente, nel folto... sarei ricomparso all'ultima partenza della mia squadra. Ed allungò la destra, come per suggellare un patto.

Così, per un buon mese, passai le ore destinate al lavoro, disteso frammezzo ad una vegetazione portentosa, fumando la sigaretta, ascoltando il complicato sussurrìo della foresta. Nei miei occhi era, in quell'ore, un intreccio fantastico di ricami non più veduti: foglie enormi tagliate nel velluto, fusti alti e diritti come minareti; fiori che parevano orgogliosi dei propri colori, asservivano, ai mal celati amori, insetti strani; uccelli dai riflessi metallici, sbucati dall'ombre tutte un fruscio, mi passavano ad ali spiegate, e come folgore, sul capo. Erano antri di un verde cupo, pieni di mistero; eran volte maestose, gocciolanti rugiada ed aromi, dalle quali il Sole traguardava placidamente, signorilmente, quasi vegliando che ogni fil d'erba avesse il fatto suo.

Il Sole che adorato, coscientemente o incoscientemente, in tutte le età, da tutti i Popoli, aveva, con secolare energia, innalzato a sé stesso quel tempio, degno di un Dio. Oh quanto è bella la Terra quando non è deturpata dalla cupidigia dell'uomo!... Tempio degno anche d'Elisa!... La sua immagine, intensamente rievocata, mi dominava come se ella fosse presente!

Fu in quella foresta ed in quell'ore che io meditai il mio secondo tentativo di fuga. Il piano a cui mi appigliai in modo definitivo, mi venne dall'arte primitiva degli Indiani scorrenti nelle regioni meridionali della Guiana: abbattere un grosso albero, scavarlo in modo da foggiare una piroga capace. Con essa avrei risalito, di notte tempo, l'Ojapok per un tratto di cinquanta miglia ed attraversato poi il San Giorgio, per approdare sulle terre del Brasile. Sfuggito alla caccia delle Autorità francesi, avrei dovuto affrontare, probabilmente, la ferocia dei selvaggi e, indubbiamente gli ostacoli, quasi insuperabili, delle regioni inesplorate e del clima. Ma il premio valeva il rischio!

Tutto questo non si poteva neppure iniziare senza l'aiuto di parecchi complici. Scandagliai l'animo di Antonio Gomez. Ma l'ardente cospiratore politico, soddisfatto della sua posizione di assistente cantiniere e paventando, probabilmente, il peggio, finse di non comprendermi. Bisognava ricorrere a materiale meno nobile. Con quali criteri sceglierlo? Come acquistare su esso l'ascendente necessario a legarlo ai miei intenti?

Anche in questo caso, la mia buona stella, più che la mia saggezza, mi venne in aiuto. Il penitenziario riceveva le sue provvigioni di carne fresca dal Brasile. A periodi, più o meno fissi, arrivava un veliero con un carico di bestie bovine. La spiaggia, che moriva gradatamente nell'Oceano, non gli concedeva profondità sufficiente ad ormeggiare presso la terra ferma, così che l'ancora veniva gettata al largo, ad una distanza di circa tre miglia. Il guardiano sceglieva allora un galeotto ogni decuria e ne formava una squadra speciale che recavasi penosamente, coll'acqua oltre la cintola, sin presso il veliero. I buoi selvatici, inferociti, venivano gettati a mare e gli uomini li sospingevano, con grandi urli e con grandi sferzate, sin entro uno stecconato, nel penitenziario.

Incominciato quel lavoro, si procedeva, senz'interruzione, sino a scarico compiuto, saltando, all'uopo, anche qualche pasto. Alla Montagne d'Argent, si mangiava per decurie. Il capo riceveva in consegna le vettovaglie, assottigliate dalla sordida speculazione degli ufficiali, e le compariva, su una specie di tavola, nelle dieci gamelle. Ogni galeotto correva alla sua e si divorava, seduti su qualche gradino, o su un tronco d'albero, o sulla nuda terra. Nei giorni di scarico, al capo della decuria correva l'obbligo di custodire la razione del galeotto assente e di consegnargliela al suo ritorno.

Scelto a prestar mano a quel lavoro indiavolato, un giorno feci il mio ritorno definitivo alle otto della sera, quando la mia decuria già russava nelle amache. La fame, inasprita dalla incessante ginnastica, dal lungo bagno, dall'aspra brezza del mare, era diventata feroce. Scuoto il capo della decuria, sollevo un mezzo subbuglio, ed egli mostrandomi, invece della mella, il pugno: "Tais toi, Italien de m..." (La parola di Cambronne).

Mastico a vuoto e sto zitto. La sera del giorno successivo, mi metto nel rango colla decuria e attendo, d'attorno al tavolo. Quando il capo arriva, sorreggendo la pentola traboccante di minestra, mi lancio e le applico un calcio poderoso. Il capo resta inebetito, a braccia tese, a mani vuote. "Ci vogliono — dico — nove Francesi per far digiunare un Italiano, ma un Italiano solo basta per far digiunare nove Francesi!"

Adesso sono gli altri che sollevano il subbuglio. Il guardiano, certo Cosson, accorre e informato della faccenda, mi domanda bruscamente: "Perchè, invece di vendicarti, non hai inoltrato il tuo rapporto?"

— Perchè gli Italiani non fanno la spia!

Cosson, rubando il mestiere a Salomone, condannò a cinquanta colpi di corda tanto il capo della decuria, quanto il Conte Carlo di Rudio. Ma la mia risolutezza e, sopratutto, la mia fiera risposta, mi avevano improvvisamente innalzato nel concetto di quei disgraziati. Essi attorniarono il guardiano e tanto dissero, che egli si prestò a ringoiare la sua sentenza. Da quel giorno, i galeotti accondiscesero, bontà loro, a trattarmi fraternamente come degno della loro congrega; da quel giorno mi riuscì impresa relativamente facile lo studiare i vari umori, lo scegliere otto individui tanto amanti della libertà da affrontare allegramente, nel tentativo di riacquistarla, qualsiasi pericolo.

Lasciavamo il nostro capannone di notte, strisciando, come belve che escano dalla tana, per deludere gli occhi eventualmente ancor desti. Si procedeva per diverse vie, sino ad una località prestabilita, sull'orlo della foresta; poi, uniti, ci inoltravamo ne' suoi labirinti. Sin dalla prima notte, avevamo tracciato il nostro cammino con segni pressochè invisibili agli altri mortali, scongiurando, per tal modo, il pericolo di smarrirci.

Un albero di gigantesche proporzioni fu tagliato al piede, con fatica relativamente lieve; ma, cadendo, esso diede sulla fitta e vi rimase appoggiato. Bisognava arrampicarsi e scavarlo stando aggrappati: complicazione che richiese un tempo prezioso. Finimmo per deciderci a ricorrere al fuoco. La luce non poteva essere scorta dal penitenziario e il fumo era reso invisibile dalla notte; ma fra tanta vegetazione resinosa, fra tanti arbusti morti, fra tante lische, fra tanto cascame che si accumulava da secoli, l'operazione richiedeva grande oculatezza, o si sarebbe alimentato un incendio così rapido e così vasto che difficilmente gli stessi diluvi dei tropici lo avrebbero domato.

Quella nostra notturna intrapresa costituisce, per me, uno di quegli incidenti la cui impressione solo la morte può cancellare. Non si era in quella terra quali pionieri di civiltà e taluno potrebbe anche, ironicamente, osservare che non eravamo farina da missionari. Ma l'ambiente... oh la grandezza dell'ambiente, reso ineffabile dalla tacita notte, come penetrava, colla sua, nella nostra essenza e quanto ci trasformava! Attorno a noi, non era cosa che non fosse nata e cresciuta nella più sconfinata libertà e la libertà diventava quasi tangibile in ogni atomo d'aria da noi respirato. A me pareva che i muscoli, affaticandosi, esalassero da ogni loro fibra, qualcosa di luminoso, come se ritornassero alle cose la vita incontaminata che le cose ci prestavano. V'eran momenti in cui avrei giurato d'essere vigoroso ed intero come la vergine foresta. E diventammo siffattamente parte omogenea dell'ambiente che, dopo le prime notti, branchi innumerevoli di scimmie, attratti dal chiarore della resina, venivano a contemplare e quasi ad incoraggiare, rallegrandola, l'opera nostra.

Dopo un paio di mesi, o poco più, riuscimmo a trascinare in una sinuosità del fiume, e a nascondere tra le liane, un canotto largo sette piedi, lungo venticinque. Galleggiava a meraviglia. "Ed ora — esclamai, quando ci separammo pieni di entusiasmo — ai remi ed alla vela!" Inaspettatamente, un avvenimento, a cui sul primo suo manifestarsi, nessuno prestò seria attenzione, sconvolse l'intera Colonia penale e con essa ogni mia speranza.

Di pieno giorno, con un cielo terso che è tutta una lirica, con un sole capace d'indurire, ai suoi raggi diretti, uova di struzzo: una sentinella vien presa da forti tremiti. Sostituita al più presto, fu vista recarsi a passi lenti, tremando, nella baracca antidiluviana che portava il nome pomposo di "Gabinetto di decenza". Ne uscì terrea, sconvolta. Affermò che, per quanto si sforzasse, non era riuscita a scander una sola goccia d'acqua. I galeotti risero sonoramente... la cosa sembrava loro tanto buffa! A sera, il poverino aveva sbocchi di sangue coagulato, nero come il carbone... due giorni dopo era cadavere!

Ma prima ancora che egli avesse dato gli ultimi tratti, cinque, sette, dieci forzati, pur dimostrando al termometro, un'alta temperatura, tremavano come foglie. Il penitenziario era invaso dalla febbre gialla.

La catastrofe fu rapida oltre ogni dire. A quei tempi, si riteneva che la sede del male fosse negli intestini; i rimedi scarsissimi e affatto inadeguati alla vastità della moria, venivano, per maggiore disdetta, applicati con criteri erronei. Il Comandante diede l'ordine che si aprissero i magazzini e la cantina e si lasciassero a piena disposizione dei sani. Da quella cantina, spalancata e nereggiante, mi pareva uscisse la sensazione della fatalità. Se la febbre gialla non era accovacciata là dentro, essa vi aveva qualche misterioso alleato. I galeotti vi erano inesorabilmente sospinti dal vizio e dalla paura. Vi si imbestialivano e passavano dall'ubriachezza al delirio; dal delirio al nulla!... con morte tanto più fulminea, quanto più erano robusti.

Le disposizioni per le vittime, semplicissime: gettarli, senza cerimonie, nella calce viva.

Ogni sera, il capo guardiano si recava a rapporto nella casa del Comandante.

— Quanti, oggi?

— Venti, trenta, quaranta!...

— E il galeotto di Rudio?

— Sano come un pesce!

Una notte morirono al Comandante, quasi contemporaneamente, la moglie e due figli ed egli cessò d'interessarsi della mia salute. Non molto dopo, è il capo guardiano che cessa di recarsi al solito rapporto. Aveva troppo facilmente ceduto alle tentazioni della cantina e, quando fu trovato disteso sulla terra battuta, si sospettò, a tutta prima, che fosse semplicemente ubriaco. Ma aveva le gengive coperte da bava sanguigna... morì senz'aver riacquistato né la parola, né un barlume di coscienza e fu uno dei tanti che dovetti afferrare pei piedi. "Uno, due, tre..." Prima di lanciarlo, avrei voluto dirgli un "Requiescat!" — m'aveva risparmiato cinquanta colpi di corda. Ma laggiù in quel baratro sarebbe parsa...

Prestai mano a sepellire, ad uno ad uno, in quel modo, i miei otto complici nel tentativo di fuga ed altri, ed altri... Contai sin oltre il centinaio; poi mi confusi e non contai più. Qualche disgraziato, che già pareva avesse passato il confine al di là del quale più non si soffre, gettato appena in quell'orribile sepoltura comune, fu visto rinvenire e contorcersi per lo spasimo!

Quando la febbre, non trovando più carne da rodere, scomparve, i Deputati francesi sollevarono, a Parigi, il solito chiasso umanitario. Ma il Ministro rispose loro, in aria di trionfo, che già aveva dato gli ordini opportuni per trasportare l'intera Colonia all'isola del Saluto. E in realtà una sera, vediamo disegnarsi sulla luce crepuscolare, la nera striscia di fumo di una nave che procedeva, a tutto vapore, verso la nostra spiaggia. Era l'"Abeille" che ci portava gli ordini di Sua Eccellenza. Il giorno seguente l'intera Colonia era a bordo... Di oltre seicento, tra condannati, militari, impiegati civili ed inservienti, eravamo ridotti a sessantatre! La maggior parte dei superstiti aveva già sofferto di febbre gialla in altri tempi e su altri lidi: il che li aveva, pare, resi refrattari, se non assolutamente invulnerabili; parecchi, ombre di sé stessi, mostravano d'esserne stati allora allora tocchi, se non recisi: tra questi, Antonio Gomez... Oh quanto era trasformata la sua faccia — d'un prete bonaccione! Tre soli non ne avevano riportato, né allora, né prima, il sangue infetto. Fra i tre il forzato di Rudio! Dall'alto dell'"Abeille", a cui già spumeggiava rotto d'attorno, l'Oceano, volgo un ultimo sguardo alla Montagne d'Argent e penso, con profondo rammarico, al mio canotto che galleggiava, solitario ed immobile, sotto le folte liane. Il paesaggio rimpicciolisce rapidamente. L'inferno che gli uomini vi avevano accumulato non era più; restava una delle sue più funeste conseguenze, il vasto carnale a cui eravamo miracolosamente sfuggiti. Natura, indifferente, stendeva su tanto orrore, tinte incantevoli e nell'aria scintillante vibravano i suoi misteriosi amoreggiamenti!

CHAPTER TWENTY-TWO

THE *DURR* ploughed the waters, bearing in her ample belly, divided into three decks, some three hundred condemned men. Gomez and I were confined in the deepest. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and could hardly be expected to harbour excessive antipathy towards us, no sooner learned who we were than he had us transferred to the upper deck. Nay, he carried his benevolent partiality so far as to permit me, from time to time, to come up on deck and smoke freely — the Regulations prescribed the trifle of fifty strokes of the rope for any man who should have done so in the compartments.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz (Carmarie) to take on water and remained there two days, with the portholes sealed, to deprive us of any possibility of escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale du Salut, where some two hundred convicts were put ashore.

Gomez, myself, and another hundred wretches passed aboard a small steamer, and on the 11th of December 1858, after a day and a half of additional sailing, we lay at anchor off the Montagne d'Argent, which rises at the confluence of the Saint-Georges and the Oyapock, at a relatively short distance from the Brazilian frontier. A free colony had once flourished in that place, and free hands had cultivated its coffee and every tropical fruit that might tempt the human palate. The French Government had purchased it. Its officers occupied the planter's house; numerous reed-thatched shelters, supported by poles, were erected to house the condemned; the earth was pierced here and there, that refractory souls might be buried alive in slow agony; some substantial buildings arose for the storehouses, for offence and defence alike — the vanguard of civilisation had been transformed into a penal settlement. Yet the natural beauty of the place dissembled, marvellously, the hell that men had heaped up within it.

The commandant, a Major of the marine infantry, whom the Republic had put on half-pay and the Emperor reinstated and adorned with honorary distinctions, hastened to receive us "in custody" and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it, and surveying me from head to foot: "Ah, so you are that — " (down came an obscenity fit for a pigsty!) "I am glad to see you here, and I swear to you that here you will leave your skin!" ...

That villainy was so entirely out of place that even the guards grimaced with disgust. But I did not lose my composure, and, as though flattered by a compliment: "My thanks for the good wish — and I return it with all my heart!" He clenched his jaws; but he possessed the Gallic ferocity, not the French wit, and remained with his mouth shut.

That small incident persuaded me that at the Montagne d'Argent the air blew ill for me, and from that very hour I resolved to suppress every impulse. The relative freedom granted to those who kept the rules — I thought — may afford me the occasion to earn a greater one ... the freedom of civilised men. But it was fated that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the hut assigned to me was some fifty convicts who formed my gang. I passed the first night sleeping, in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise we set off, our bones broken, towards the forest. I wore a great straw hat, a pair of heavy wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, and a brown blouse, upon the back of which was marked, in large characters, my number — the baptism of the penal settlement!

In crossing a swamp, I had the ill fortune to lose one of my clogs, and the stumps of the reeds, cut level with the water to ease the passage, lacerated the sole of my foot. I was obliged to contrive a way of covering three miles hobbling, and marking every footprint with blood; until at last I arrived, last of my gang, in the depths of the forest — a splendid forest that, for a moment, almost made me forget that I was a convict, and the anguish with it.

A pile of tree-trunks. The task of the day was to carry them upon our shoulders to the penitentiary, making five consecutive journeys. I set my eye upon a trunk that looked light; but it was *boia fer* — tough as iron — and when I had raised it to the height of my knees, it fell back noisily upon the pile. Then, instead of making another attempt, I sat down, resolved to rest just long enough to recover my breath. The convict-overseer stood watching me, and when he was certain of my intention: "Get up, you filthy wretch! If you don't stir yourself, I'll send you running with kicks to your backside. A curse on you and all the Italian breed!"

Had he contented himself with goading me with the mere threat, I should in all probability have taken the matter with full philosophy; but that contempt for my nationality stirred in my breast the anger accumulated against all the bullies who made sport of it. I swung my *sabre d'abattis* and sprang forward with a leap of which, given my condition, I should not have believed myself capable. "Take care! Italians do not suffer insults. The first — let it pass! At the second, your skull will no longer see the sun from the top of your shoulders!"

My companions, who had set off one by one, were already far away. Alone, perfectly alone in a deserted place! He swallowed the pill, turning pale. Nevertheless, by way of a show of bravado, he asked my name. "Be content with the number!" and I turned my back on him.

We were beginning badly! But chance would have it that my overseer, though he could spell more or less correctly, could not write. To make out his report, he was obliged to apply to the clerk of the superintending Engineer, who, with the number as a guide, verified in the registers my name and who I was. What sentiments animated him towards me I cannot say; but I learned that he persuaded my overseer not to press his cruelty. And into his exhortations he deftly insinuated the element of fear: "That man," he said, "fears neither laws, nor punishments, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later you are done for!"

On his return to the forest, the overseer drew me aside. "We have certain ways of settling matters in this penitentiary! ... But I know how to make my distinctions." And to prove it, he begged pardon for his harsh words and granted me leave neither to work then nor thereafter. Each day I was to conceal myself in the thicket ... I should reappear at the last departure of my gang. And he stretched out his right hand, as if to seal a pact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours assigned to labour lying stretched amid a wondrous vegetation, smoking my cigarette, listening to the intricate murmuring of the forest. In those hours my eyes drank in a fantastic interweaving of embroideries never seen before: enormous leaves cut as from velvet, trunks tall and straight as minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their own colours pressed strange insects into the service of their ill-concealed loves; birds with metallic lustre, bursting from shadows all a-rustle, would sweep past overhead on outstretched wings, swift as lightning. There were caverns of deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults dripping with dew and fragrances, through which the Sun gazed peacefully, lordly, as if keeping watch that every blade of grass had its due.

The Sun which, consciously or unconsciously, in every age and by every People has been adored, had with secular energy raised up to itself that temple, worthy of a God. Oh, how beautiful is the Earth when it is not defaced by the cupidity of man! ... A temple worthy, too, of Elisa! ... Her image, intensely evoked, dominated me as if she were present!

It was in that forest and in those hours that I meditated my second attempt at escape. The plan to which I definitively committed myself came to me from the primitive art of the Indians ranging the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a great tree, hollow it out so as to fashion a serviceable pirogue. With it I would have ascended, by night, the Oyapock for some fifty miles and then crossed the Saint-Georges, to land on Brazilian soil. Having eluded the pursuit of the French authorities, I should then have had to face, in all probability, the ferocity of the savages, and assuredly the well-nigh insuperable obstacles of unexplored regions and of the climate. But the prize was worth the risk!

None of this could even be begun without the help of several accomplices. I sounded the spirit of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, satisfied with his position as assistant canteen-keeper and probably fearing the worst, feigned not to understand me. I was obliged to have recourse to less noble material. By what criteria was I to choose it? How was I to acquire over it the ascendancy necessary to bind it to my purposes?

In this case also, my good star, more than my own sagacity, came to my aid. The penitentiary received its provisions of fresh meat from Brazil. At intervals more or less regular, a sailing-vessel arrived with a cargo of cattle. The shore, which shelved gradually into the Ocean, afforded it no sufficient depth to moor near the mainland, so that the anchor was cast offshore at a distance of some three miles. The overseer would then choose one convict from each squad of ten and form a special detail which made its laborious way, with the water above the waist, up to the vessel. The wild bullocks, maddened, were flung into the sea, and the men drove them, with great shouts and great lashings, into a stockade within the penitentiary.

Once that work was begun, it proceeded without interruption until the unloading was complete, even skipping a meal if need be. At the Montagne d'Argent, meals were taken by squads of ten. The squad-leader received the rations in charge — already whittled thin by the sordid speculation of the officers — and set them out, on a kind of table, in ten mess-bowls. Each convict ran to his own and ate, seated on some step, or on a tree-trunk, or on the bare earth. On unloading days, it fell to the squad-leader to keep the absent man's ration and hand it to him on his return.

Chosen to lend a hand at that infernal labour, one day I made my definitive return at eight in the evening, when my squad was already snoring in their hammocks. Hunger, sharpened by the incessant exertion, the long soaking, the keen sea-breeze, had become fierce. I shook the squad-leader, raised a minor commotion, and he, showing me his fist instead of the mess-bowl: "Tais toi, Italien de m..." (the word of Cambronne).

I chew on emptiness and hold my tongue. The evening of the following day I fall into line with the squad and wait about the table. When the leader arrives, carrying the pot brimming with soup, I fling myself upon it and deliver it a mighty kick. The leader stands stupefied, arms extended, hands empty. "It takes nine Frenchmen," I say, "to make one Italian go hungry, but one Italian alone is enough to make nine Frenchmen go hungry!"

Now it is the others who raise the commotion. The overseer, a certain Cosson, comes running, and being informed of the affair, demands of me sharply: "Why, instead of taking your revenge, did you not submit your complaint?"

— Because Italians do not turn informer!

Cosson, pirating the trade of Solomon, sentenced to fifty strokes of the rope both the squad-leader and Count Carlo di Rudio. But my resoluteness, and above all my proud reply, had in an instant raised me in the esteem of those poor wretches. They gathered round the overseer and pleaded with such effect that he was prevailed upon to swallow his sentence back. From that day, the convicts condescended, by their good grace, to treat me fraternally as one worthy of their fellowship; from that day it proved a relatively easy undertaking for me to study the various temperaments, and to choose eight individuals sufficiently in love with freedom to face cheerfully, in the attempt to regain it, whatever danger might arise.

We left our hut by night, crawling like beasts emerging from their lair, to deceive any eyes that might still be open. We proceeded by separate ways to a pre-arranged place on the edge of the forest; then, reunited, we made our way into its labyrinths. From the very first night we had marked out our path with signs almost invisible to other mortals, thus warding off the danger of losing ourselves.

A tree of gigantic proportions was felled at the root with relatively little difficulty; but in falling it struck the dense canopy and remained suspended upon it. We had to climb up and hollow it out while clinging to its sides — a complication that consumed precious time. In the end we resolved to have recourse to fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary and the smoke was made invisible by the night; but amid such resinous vegetation, amid so many dead shrubs, so much dry brush, so much fallen matter accumulated over centuries, the operation demanded the greatest caution, lest one feed a conflagration so rapid and so vast that even the tropical deluges themselves would scarcely have mastered it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constitutes, for me, one of those incidents whose impression only death can efface. We were not in that land as pioneers of civilisation, and someone might with irony observe that we were not made of the stuff of missionaries. But the setting — oh the grandeur of the setting, rendered ineffable by the silent night — how it entered, together with its own essence, into ours, and how it transformed us! Around us there was nothing that had not been born and grown in the most boundless freedom, and freedom became almost tangible in every atom of air we breathed. It seemed to me that my muscles, in their exertions, exhaled from every fibre something luminous, as though restoring to the things around us the uncontaminated life those things were lending to us. There were moments when I could have sworn I was as vigorous and whole as the virgin forest. And we became so homogeneous a part of our surroundings that, after the first few nights, numberless troops of monkeys, attracted by the glow of the resin, came to contemplate, and as if to encourage with their gambols, our work.

After some two months, or a little more, we contrived to drag into a bend of the river, and to conceal among the lianas, a canoe seven feet broad and twenty-five feet long. It floated admirably. "And now," I exclaimed, when we parted full of enthusiasm, "to the oars and the sail!" Unexpectedly, an event to which, at its first appearance, no one paid serious attention, threw the entire penal colony into confusion — and with it all my hopes.

In broad daylight, under a clear sky that is itself a lyric poem, with a sun capable of hard-boiling ostrich eggs in its direct rays, a sentry was seized with violent shivering. Relieved as quickly as might be, he was seen making his way at slow steps, trembling, to the antediluvian shed that bore the pompous name of "Cabinet of Decency." He emerged from it ashen, undone. He declared that, try as he might, he had been unable to pass a single drop of water. The convicts laughed loudly — the thing seemed to them so droll! By evening the poor wretch was vomiting clotted blood, black as coal ... two days later he was a corpse!

But before he had even drawn his last breath, five, seven, ten convicts, while showing the thermometer a high temperature, were trembling like leaves. The penitentiary was invaded by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was rapid beyond all telling. In those days it was believed that the seat of the disease lay in the intestines; the remedies, exceedingly scarce and wholly inadequate to the vast extent of the dying, were applied, to make matters worse, on mistaken principles. The Commandant gave orders that the storehouses and the canteen be thrown open and placed at the full disposal of the able-bodied. From that canteen, flung wide and black as a cavern's mouth, there seemed to issue the very sensation of doom. If yellow fever was not crouching inside it, it had there some mysterious ally. The convicts were inexorably driven thither by vice and by fear. They brutalised themselves within it and passed from drunkenness to delirium, from delirium to nothingness — their death the more sudden the more robust they had been.

The arrangements for the victims were of the simplest: to fling them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Every evening the chief overseer presented himself for his report at the Commandant's house.

— How many, today?

— Twenty, thirty, forty! ...

— And the convict di Rudio?

— Sound as a fish!

One night the Commandant lost his wife and two children almost simultaneously, and he ceased to take any interest in my health. Not long afterwards, it is the chief overseer himself who ceases to attend his accustomed report. He had too readily yielded to the temptations of the canteen, and when he was found stretched upon the beaten earth, it was at first suspected that he was simply drunk. But his gums were covered with bloody froth ... he died without having recovered either speech or the faintest glimmer of consciousness, and he was one of the many whom I was obliged to seize by the feet. "One, two, three ..." Before casting him in, I should have liked to say a *Requiescat* over him — he had spared me fifty strokes of the rope. But down there in that abyss it would have seemed ...

I lent my hands to bury, one by one, in that manner, my eight accomplices in the attempt at escape, and others, and others still ... I counted past a hundred; then I lost count and counted no more. Some poor wretch who already seemed to have passed beyond the frontier where suffering ends, flung scarce into that horrible common sepulchre, was seen to revive and writhe in agony!

When the fever, finding no more flesh to gnaw, disappeared, the French Deputies raised in Paris the usual humanitarian clamour. But the Minister replied to them, in a tone of triumph, that he had already given the necessary orders to transfer the entire Colony to the Île du Salut. And indeed one evening we saw, traced against the twilight, the black streak of smoke from a vessel proceeding at full steam towards our shore. It was the *Abeille*, bringing us the orders of His Excellency. The following day the entire Colony was on board ... Of more than six hundred souls — convicts, military, civilian employees and servants — we had been reduced to sixty-three! The greater part of the survivors had already suffered from yellow fever at other times and in other places, which had rendered them, it appears, resistant if not absolutely invulnerable; a number, shadows of their former selves, showed signs of having been but lately touched, if not utterly cut down — among them, Antonio Gomez ... Oh how transformed was his face — that face of a good-natured priest! Three alone had not carried away from it, then or before, any taint of infected blood. Among the three, the convict di Rudio! From the deck of the *Abeille*, already churning the Ocean into broken foam about her sides, I cast a last glance at the Montagne d'Argent and thought, with profound regret, of my canoe floating alone and motionless beneath the thick lianas. The landscape dwindled rapidly. The hell that men had accumulated there was no more; what remained was one of its most sinister consequences, the vast common grave from which we had so miraculously escaped. Nature, indifferent, spread over all that horror enchanting hues, and in the glittering air her mysterious amorous stirrings vibrated on.

CHAPTER TWENTY-FIRST

"FREE... in a year!" I had not said it out of selfishness alone, nor out of pity alone, still less out of vainglory. My first escape attempt had been pondered, precisely in those days at La Roquette. There I had made the acquaintance of an Englishman who was serving six months' imprisonment for fraudulent bankruptcy. He was a man endowed with a certain culture, of few words; he possessed, from what I could conclude, means more than sufficient. Like the great majority of his countrymen, he took an interest in politics and hated Napoleon. He himself came, so to speak, to meet my own inclination, and offered to help me with all his resources. *"In chiesa coi santi, in taverna coi ghiottoni!"* — with saints in church, with gluttons at the tavern! And, on the other hand, if hypocritical society finds it convenient to take account only of a man's faults in order to depress him, despite his virtues; I find it more logical, more fraternal, to fix one's mind upon his virtues and to exalt him, despite his faults. Had my Englishman truly acquired his wealth by reprehensible means? To spend it in restoring a husband to his widowed consort, a father to his son, was to turn ill-gotten gains to good use. I accepted.

We had calculated that he would return to freedom before I was taken to Toulon. We knew that the condemned, before being transported to the tropical territories, make a halt, of greater or lesser duration, at that penal station. We had no plan of the locality, and contriving a well-defined scheme proved a disjointed affair; but it was not unknown that if it is possible to induce, for money, a human creature to commit base acts, it is equally possible to induce him, for money, to attempt acts of generous daring.

Once out of La Roquette, my Englishman would have made his way to Toulon. It would be easy enough, after my arrival, to ascertain the place where I was confined and the persons to whose custody I was entrusted. Simultaneously, he would have organised a means of rapid transport, by sea or by land. Money, lavished with an open hand, would have broken my chains, offering me thereby the opportunity to profit from that means... Italy and Spain are not, from the French coast, the ends of the earth. The more I studied the character of my Englishman, the more I became convinced that he possessed qualities rendering him capable of bringing the enterprise to a good conclusion.

The day of his release came quickly enough, and I, embracing him, slipped clandestinely into his hands a letter for Elisa... the only one, as I later learned, that she ever received from me.

A week afterwards, I was transported by railway, in a cellular compartment, as far as Marseilles, and from there by horse-drawn coach, as far as Toulon. It was the month of April (1858), and nature was in a wondrous state of flowering when I was locked in a casemate below sea-level and chained, together with other wretches, to an iron bar fixed into the damp wall.

One wishes to know what occupation Count Carlo di Rudio was put to during a period of approximately seven months? I wore a red jacket and a pair of yellow trousers; I wore a green Phrygian cap, the distinctive mark of those condemned in perpetuity (those condemned to a term wore it red); I was without moustaches and had my hair cut in strips. I slept, when I could sleep, on a wide beam. The hours in which I lay wakeful, I passed in waging a furious hunt against the insects that tormented me, and in rubbing away at the ring that bound me. This last labour of mine was so persistent and tenacious that, on no fewer than four occasions, I managed to be taken to the blacksmith and thus to walk, holding my chain between my hands, a quarter of a mile in the open air... on four separate occasions seeing the Sun once more.

As at La Roquette, morbid curiosity exposed me to the humiliating visits of this or that personage. At that period, the Czarina was attempting to restore her ailing health by breathing the balmy air of the Riviera. The officers of a Russian squadron, en route for Nice, where they were to take the pallid sovereign on board, put in at Toulon and wished to see the man who had attempted to kill Napoleon... the man who had inflicted upon their Fatherland the humiliation of Sebastopol. They offered to bestow upon me a sum of money to provide for my most pressing needs. But the Regulations allowed me nothing beyond bread and water, and the jailers opposed it. So that I derived from it nothing but the sterile satisfaction of being able to say that even the steppes of eastern Europe contributed their contingent to the swarm of the importunate.

But among my visitors there was one, of an altogether different kind, who deserves a mention entirely apart.

Sister Teresa had not contented herself with her first letter and my first reply. She had sent me at least a dozen more. They all exhorted me to trust in God, to repent of my transgressions; to turn myself, with fervour, towards the wide-open arms of divine mercy, which would surely not reject me. Wearied to death, I had written to her, a few days before leaving La Roquette, that I had been educated to place faith in facts, and facts alone. Words slid inexorably off my soul, and hers were nothing but words. I thought I had put an end to the comedy... but one does not become worthy of an apparition of Christ for nothing. Unexpectedly, I beheld again, even at Toulon, the fine and elegant handwriting of my sister in God.

Nothing less than the visit of a bishop was announced to me — a bishop travelling, as a missionary, to China. I was to open every thought of mine to him. He was a holy man, brimful of celestial grace, and he would not fail to "touch my heart."

One day, at a wholly unusual hour, the gate of the casemate swings open and in comes an old man of about sixty, wrapped in a violet-coloured cloth. The jailer points me out to him and departs. I found myself face to face with the announced missionary bishop... who evidently proposed to inaugurate his apostolic triumphs by rendering, through my conversion, a service to the Empire.

He gave vent to the conversation by declaring that he was in that place at the behest of Sister Teresa, and straightway added that, in compliance with the exhortations of that pious soul, he had provided, with sufficient generosity, for the earthly needs of my wife and my child.

At this point I scrutinised his face intently, and perceived in it nothing but an expression of candid good-nature. And yet those saintly episcopal lips had lied like those of a common rogue.

However that may be, presuming that he had thus well disposed my spirit by means of the supposed beneficence, that most pure vessel of celestial grace proceeded to spread the balm he had deposited upon my breast and, in a tranquil voice, announced to me how Sister Teresa had thought of me as well. He was the bearer of a gift from her. Without further ado, he drew from beneath his violet vestment a coral rosary mounted in silver, from which I saw suspended a crucifix and a medal.

I observed it without extending my hand, and in a voice no less tranquil: "A most beautiful gift... if only I could pawn it and exchange it for tobacco. But many things are denied in here that are permitted to simple mortals. Please thank Sister Teresa on my behalf, and write to tell her that I have refused."

The bishop's eyes flashed with an Inquisitor's gleam. "My son," he said, "you will not refuse at least this further pledge of brotherly love. It is blessed by the Pope and will bring you good fortune!" And here he set swinging before my eyes a pair of small scapulars of satin, with the features of Christ embroidered in gold.

— "Oh, father! In this wretched place, with so much filth... of what use could they be save as a comfortable nest for my lice?"

He did not consider himself beaten, and without uttering a word, drew out and spread before me an elegant prayer-book. But I observed to him that for some time I had no longer prayed, and that in that jumble of words my mind would find no nourishment whatsoever.

The conversation came to an abrupt end. The bishop gathered up his gifts and, turning his back on me, departed without bidding me farewell, with a step that sought to appear dignified and solemn. I believe he was thinking: "If the Chinese are all like this dog, I am in for a fine time!"

Then, shrugging my shoulders, I looked at my fellow captives in the chain. Some watched me with an expression of stupefaction, as though I were the devil himself; others with sparkling eyes, as if my hair cut in strips could not quite conceal from them the features of a hero. But I was neither a hero nor a devil. I was simply a reasonable man who, guided by his own deep convictions, had evaded the subtle arts of imposture.

I find it fitting to relate here, by analogy, an incident that befell my wife while I was already serving out my sentence at Cayenne.

Nottingham, where Elisa had withdrawn to spend, with her mother, her days of widowhood, was, and is certainly still, an episcopal see. Catholics regard that bishopric as an advanced bastion of truth against heresy and, giving free play to fancy, surround it with the serene air that inspires chaste thoughts... a kind of oasis where, by day, the doves flutter; at dark of night, the luminous angels.

Unexpectedly, Elisa received an invitation to present herself at the episcopal palace on a matter of the utmost importance. Mindful of my counsel, she did not venture to go alone. But here I think the words are better suited to the lips of my Elisa...

He pressed the button of a bell, and the lady came at once. He begged her to recount, simply, the conversation she had had with the Bishop of Nottingham.

"There is not a great deal to tell. I went to the episcopal palace with my mother. She was asked to wait in the antechamber; I was immediately conducted into Monsignor's study... a tall, lean man, all skin and bones. He received me with a grave face and, unfolding a letter that he took good care not to hand to me, told me that he was exceedingly grieved to have sorrowful news to communicate. A colleague of his, stationed at the penitentiary of Cayenne, had written to notify him of my husband's death; which had moreover already been confirmed by the press and by the French Authorities. Although the misfortune was irreparable and he sympathised with my inevitable grief, it was not without a deep and serene satisfaction of his priestly soul that he could assure me that you had sincerely repented of your transgressions, that you had passed away acknowledging the sanctity of the religion of your fathers. On this point you had, moreover, expressed the ardent desire that I, for my eternal salvation, should embrace the Catholic faith and become, if at all possible, a nun. You further enjoined upon me, as the expression of your last, absolute will, to entrust our son to the care of some Catholic, apostolic, Roman Institution, so that he might be raised in the principles which had sustained you in your misfortune and comforted you on your deathbed.

— For a moment I found neither tears nor words. Then a flash crossed my mind and, looking him steadily in the eye, I said: 'Count Carlo di Rudio cannot have died in such a fashion. And since this is a lie, it is equally a lie that he is no more. I feel it within me — my husband lives!'

— The priest crumpled the letter, flung it on the writing-table, and with a terrible face (which betrayed the desire to burn me alive) loosening and clenching his fingers as though they were talons, said through gritted teeth: 'Ah, if only we were in France!'

I was seized with great fear and, uttering a cry, I fled. I was so shaken that I could say nothing to my mother until we were out in the street, far away, very far away!"

Thus — Count di Rudio continued — ended the Catholic clergy's attempt to act as spy on behalf of the Second Empire.

While I, amid the horrors of the casemate, laboured at gnawing through the link of my chain in order to catch a fleeting glimpse of the Sun, my Englishman was engaged, for his part, in bringing our agreement to fruition, with a cunning and pertinacity wholly Anglo-Saxon. He had gathered every possible item of information about the place where I was confined and the routines of the prison; he had procured disguise clothing; he had prepared places of temporary refuge; means of transport as far as the Pyrenees. With the help of some woman or other, he had managed to establish direct communication with my immediate jailer. It had even been settled that the day of my escape should coincide with that of my next excursion to the blacksmith. Unexpectedly, I was brought before the Commander of the Arsenal.

I heard myself informed that on the morrow I would be embarked aboard the frigate *La Dure*. "You are going to Cayenne," added the Commander in a dry tone, though not entirely devoid of an underlying affability. "Vile climate! Europeans compelled to live in your conditions cannot survive there more than two or three years. I am authorised to tell you that if you reveal your accomplices, you will be immediately set at liberty and furnished with the means necessary to leave the frontiers of France. It is within your power to die in the penitentiary, or to live in the bosom of your family. Choose!"

I replied that I knew nothing. But had I possessed revelations to make, the opinion in which I held the servants of the Empire was far too low for me to place the least trust in their promises. I chose the penitentiary!

— As you wish! retorted the Commander bluntly, and gave orders for me to be taken back to the casemate.

On the morrow I was on the high seas. My first escape attempt had failed!

CHAPTER TWENTY-SECOND

*La Dure* sailed on, bearing in her ample hull, divided into three decks, some three hundred condemned men. Gomez and I were confined in the deepest. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and could not have harboured excessive antipathies towards us, no sooner learned who we were than he had us transferred to the upper deck. Indeed he pressed his benevolent partiality to the point of permitting me to go up on deck from time to time to smoke freely... the Regulations prescribed the trifling penalty of fifty lashes of the rope for anyone who had done so in the compartments below.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz in the Canaries to take on water and remained there two days, with hatches closed to deprive us of any possibility of escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale of the Salut archipelago, where some two hundred convicts were disembarked.

Gomez, myself, and another hundred or so wretches transferred to a small steam-vessel, and on the 11th of December 1858, after a day and a half of additional navigation, we were anchored off the Montagne d'Argent, which rises at the confluence of the São Jorge and the Oyapock, at a relatively short distance from the border of Brazil. There had flourished, in former times in that place, a free colony where free hands cultivated coffee and every tropical fruit that might tempt the human palate. The French Government had purchased it. Its officers occupied the planter's house; numerous reed-thatched shelters, supported on stakes, were erected there for the shelter of the condemned; the earth was pierced here and there in order to bury, in a slow agony, those refractory to discipline; a few substantial buildings arose too, for the storehouses, for defence and for offence... the vanguard of civilisation had been transformed into a penal establishment. But the natural beauties concealed admirably the hell that men had accumulated there.

The commandant, a Major of the marine infantry, whom the Republic had placed on the retired list and the Emperor had reinstated and adorned with badges of honour, hastened to receive us "in consignment" and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it and, looking me up and down from head to foot: "Ah, so you are that —" (here came an expression fit for a pigsty!) "I am glad to see you here, and I swear to you that you will leave your skin here!"

That piece of coarseness was so entirely uncalled for that even the guards pulled a grimace of disgust. But I did not lose my composure, and, almost as though flattered by a compliment: "I thank you for the good wish, and return it with all my heart!" He clenched his jaw; but he possessed Gallic ferocity, not the French spirit, and remained tight-lipped.

That small incident persuaded me that at the Montagne d'Argent the air was blowing ill for me, and from that very moment I resolved to repress every impulse. The relative freedom granted to the well-disciplined — I thought — may afford me the occasion to earn myself a greater one... the freedom of civilised men. But it was fated that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the hut assigned to me were some fifty convicts... they formed my squad. I passed the first night sleeping, in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise we set out, our bones aching, towards the forest. I wore a large straw hat, a pair of wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, a brown blouse, upon whose back was marked, in large characters, my number... the baptism of the penitentiary!

In crossing a swamp, I had the misfortune to lose one of my clogs, and the stumps of reeds, cut flush with the water to facilitate the ford, cut into the sole of my foot. I was obliged to contrive a way to cover three miles limping and marking, in blood, each footprint; until I arrived, the last of my squad, in the depths of the forest... a splendid forest which, for a little, almost made me forget that I was a convict and that I was in pain.

A pile of logs. The day's task was to carry them, on one's shoulder, to the penitentiary, making five consecutive trips. I singled out a log that seemed, to the eye, light; but it was *bois de fer* — ironwood — and when I had raised it to the height of my knees, it fell back noisily upon the pile. Then, instead of making another attempt, I sat down, resolved to rest for as long as it might take to recover my breath. The convict-overseer stood watching me, and when he was certain of my intention: "Get up, you filthy creature! If you don't get a move on, I'll send you packing with kicks to your backside. A curse on you and all Italian stock!"

Had he contented himself with goading me with a mere threat, I should in all probability have taken the matter with complete philosophical calm; but that contempt for my nationality stirred in my breast the anger accumulated against the bullies who made sport of it. I swung my *sabre d'abattis* and leapt forward with a bound of which, given my condition, I should not have thought myself capable. "Take care! Italians do not suffer insults! The first — let it pass! At the second, your pumpkin will see the sun no more from atop your shoulders!"

My companions, who had set out one by one, were already far off. Alone, perfectly alone in a deserted place! He swallowed, turning pale, the pill. Nevertheless, in a show of bravado, he asked me my name. "Content yourself with the number!" and I turned my back on him.

We were making a bad beginning! But as chance would have it, my overseer, though he could puzzle it out more or less, did not know how to write. To make his report, he had to apply to the clerk of the supervising Engineer; who, with the help of the number, verified, in the registers, my name and who I was. What sentiments animated him towards me, I cannot say; but I learned that he persuaded my overseer not to press his cruelty too hard. And into his exhortations he skillfully insinuated the element of fear: "That man," he said, "fears neither Laws, nor punishments, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later, you are a dead man!"

On his return to the forest, the overseer drew me to one side. "We have a certain sort of stuff here at the penitentiary!... But I know how to make my distinctions." And to prove it, he begged my pardon for his ill words and granted me licence not to work, either then or thereafter. I was to conceal myself daily in the depths of the forest... I was to reappear at the last departure of my squad. And he extended his right hand, as though to seal a pact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours allotted to labour stretched out amid a wondrous vegetation, smoking a cigarette, listening to the intricate murmuring of the forest. In my eyes there was, in those hours, a fantastical interlacing of embroidery I had never seen before... enormous leaves cut from velvet, tall straight trunks like minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their colours, and subjected strange insects to their not quite concealed amours; birds with metallic iridescence, darting out of shadows all a rustle, passed above my head on spread wings, swift as lightning. There were caverns of a deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults, dripping dew and fragrance, through which the Sun gazed placidly, lordly, as though keeping watch that every blade of grass had what it needed... the Sun which, worshipped consciously or unconsciously in all ages by all peoples, had with secular energy drawn into itself that temple, worthy of a God. Oh how beautiful is the Earth when it is not defaced by the cupidity of man!... A temple worthy of Elisa, too!... Her image, intensely evoked, held dominion over me as though she were present.

It was in that forest, and in those hours, that I pondered my second escape attempt. The plan to which I finally resolved was suggested to me by the primitive art of the Indians ranging the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a great tree, to hollow it out so as to fashion a serviceable dug-out canoe. With it I would have ascended, by night, the Oyapock for a stretch of fifty miles, and then crossed the São Jorge, to make landfall on Brazilian soil. Having escaped the pursuit of the French Authorities, I should then have been obliged to face, in all probability, the ferocity of savages, and beyond any doubt the nearly insuperable obstacles of unexplored regions and of the climate. But the prize was worth the risk.

None of this could even be begun without the help of several accomplices. I sounded the spirit of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, satisfied with his position as assistant canteen-keeper and probably dreading the worst, feigned not to understand me. I was obliged to have recourse to less noble material. By what criteria was I to choose it? How was I to acquire over it the ascendancy necessary to bind it to my purposes?

In this case too, my good star, more than my own wisdom, came to my aid.

The penitentiary received its supplies of fresh meat from Brazil. At intervals more or less regular, a sailing vessel would arrive with a cargo of cattle. The shore, which shelved gradually into the ocean, did not afford sufficient depth to permit it to moor close to land, so the anchor was cast offshore, at a distance of some three miles. The overseer would then choose one convict from each squad of ten and form them into a special party which made its laborious way, wading with water above the waist, up to the side of the vessel. The wild bullocks, maddened with rage, were thrown into the sea, and the men drove them, with great shouts and great blows, into a stockade within the penitentiary.

Once that work had begun, it proceeded without interruption until the unloading was complete, skipping, as needs required, even a meal or two. At the Montagne d'Argent, meals were taken by squads of ten. The squad-leader received in charge the provisions, diminished by the sordid speculation of the officers, and set them out, upon a sort of table, in the ten mess-bowls. Each convict ran to his own and devoured it, seated on some step, or on a tree-trunk, or on the bare ground. On days of unloading, it fell to the squad-leader to keep the absent convict's ration and hand it to him on his return.

Having been chosen to lend a hand at that frenzied work, one day I made my way back at eight o'clock in the evening, when my squad was already snoring in its hammocks. Hunger, sharpened by incessant exertion, by the prolonged bath, by the keen sea breeze, had become ferocious. I shake the squad-leader, and cause a half-commotion, and he, showing me his fist instead of the mess-bowl: "*Tais-toi, Italien de m...*" (the word of Cambronne).

I chew on nothing and hold my tongue. On the evening of the following day, I take my place in the rank with the squad and wait around the table. When the squad-leader arrives, holding up the pot brimming with soup, I lunge at it and give it a mighty kick. The leader stands there dumbfounded, arms outstretched, hands empty. "It takes," I say, "nine Frenchmen to make an Italian go hungry, but a single Italian is enough to make nine Frenchmen go hungry!"

Now it is the others who raise the commotion. The overseer, a certain Cosson, comes running, and having been informed of the matter, asks me curtly: "Why, instead of taking your revenge, did you not file your report?"

— "Because Italians do not inform."

Cosson, stealing Salomon's trade, condemned both the squad-leader and Count Carlo di Rudio to fifty lashes of the rope. But my resoluteness and, above all, my fierce reply, had instantly raised me in the estimation of those wretches. They surrounded the overseer and spoke to such effect that he consented to swallow his sentence. From that day, the convicts condescended, in their goodness, to treat me fraternally as one worthy of their fellowship; from that day I found it a relatively easy undertaking to study the various temperaments, to select eight individuals sufficiently enamoured of freedom to face cheerfully, in the attempt to regain it, whatever danger might arise.

We left our hut by night, crawling out like beasts emerging from their lair, in order to deceive any eyes that might still be awake. We proceeded by separate paths to a pre-arranged spot on the edge of the forest; then, united, we pressed on into its labyrinths. From the very first night, we had marked our route with signs nearly invisible to other mortals, averting in that way the danger of losing ourselves.

A tree of gigantic proportions was felled at its base with comparatively little effort; but in falling it caught in the dense undergrowth and remained propped upon it. We had to climb up and hollow it out while clinging to it... a complication that consumed precious time. We finally resolved to have recourse to fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary, and the smoke was rendered invisible by the night; but amid so much resinous vegetation, amid so many dead shrubs, amid so much kindling, amid the debris accumulated over centuries, the operation demanded great circumspection, lest one were to feed a conflagration so swift and so vast that even the tropical deluges would have found difficulty in mastering it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constitutes, for me, one of those incidents whose impression only death can efface. We were not in that land as pioneers of civilisation, and someone might even remark, not without irony, that we were not of the stuff of missionaries. But the setting — oh the grandeur of that setting, rendered ineffable by the silent night — how it penetrated, together with its own, into our very being, and how it transformed us! All about us, there was nothing that had not been born and grown in the most boundless freedom, and freedom became almost palpable in every atom of air we breathed. It seemed to me that my labouring muscles, in their very exertion, exhaled from every fibre something luminous, as though giving back to things the unstained life that things lent to us. There were moments in which I could have sworn I was as vigorous and eternal as the virgin forest. And we became so thoroughly a homogeneous part of the setting that, after the first few nights, numberless troops of monkeys, drawn by the glow of the resin, came to contemplate and almost to encourage, brightening it with their presence, our work.

After a couple of months, or a little more, we succeeded in dragging into a bend in the river, and in concealing among the lianas, a canoe seven feet wide and twenty-five feet long. It floated admirably. "And now," I exclaimed, when we parted full of enthusiasm, "to the oars and the sail!"

Unexpectedly, an event to which, at its first manifestation, no one had paid serious attention, threw the entire penal colony into disorder, and with it every hope of mine.

In broad daylight, beneath a cloudless sky that is a whole lyric poem in itself, with a sun capable of hard-boiling ostrich eggs in its direct rays... a sentry is seized with violent trembling. Relieved as quickly as possible, he was seen to make his way with slow steps, still trembling, to the antediluvian hut that bore the pompous name of "cabinet of decency." He emerged from it ashen, undone. He declared that try as he might, he had been unable to pass a single drop of water. The convicts laughed loudly... the thing seemed to them so comical. By evening, the poor wretch was vomiting clotted blood, black as coal... two days later he was a corpse.

But before he had drawn his last breath, five, seven, ten convicts, while showing at the thermometer a high temperature, were trembling like leaves. The penitentiary had been invaded by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was swift beyond all telling. In those days, it was held that the seat of the disease lay in the intestines; the remedies, scarce in the extreme and wholly inadequate to the vastness of the mortality, were applied, for greater misfortune, on erroneous principles. The Commander gave orders to throw open the warehouses and the cantina and to leave them at the full disposal of the able-bodied. From that cantina, flung wide and black with shadow, there seemed to issue the sensation of fatality. If yellow fever was not crouching in there, it had there some mysterious ally. The convicts were inexorably drawn into it by vice and by fear. They brutified themselves within it and passed from drunkenness to delirium; from delirium to nothingness!... with a death all the more sudden the more robust they were.

The arrangements made for the victims were of the simplest: to cast them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Each evening, the chief overseer went to make his report at the Commander's house.

One night the Commander lost his wife and two children almost simultaneously, and he ceased to concern himself with my welfare. Not long afterwards, it was the chief overseer who ceased to make his customary report. He had too easily yielded to the temptations of the cantina, and when he was found stretched out on the beaten earth, it was at first suspected that he was simply drunk. But his gums were coated with bloody froth... he died without having recovered either speech or the faintest glimmer of consciousness, and he was one of the many whom I was obliged to seize by the feet. "One, two, three..." Before casting him in, I should have wished to say a *Requiescat* over him!... he had spared me fifty lashes of the rope. But down there in that pit... it would have sounded like irony.

I lent my hands to the burying, one by one, in that fashion, of my eight fellow-conspirators in the escape attempt, and of others, and still others... I counted past a hundred; then I lost count and counted no more. Some wretch who already seemed to have crossed the boundary beyond which one suffers no more, hardly thrown into that horrible common grave, was seen to revive and writhe in agony.

When the fever, finding no more flesh to gnaw upon, at last disappeared, the French Deputies raised in Paris the customary humanitarian clamour. But the Minister answered them, in a tone of triumph, that he had already given the appropriate orders to transfer the entire colony to the Île du Salut. And indeed one evening, standing out against the crepuscular light, we saw the dark trail of smoke from a vessel proceeding at full steam towards our shore. It was the *Abeille*, bringing us His Excellency's orders. On the following day the entire colony was on board... Of more than six hundred — convicts, military men, civil servants, and attendants alike — we were reduced to sixty-three! The greater part of the survivors had already suffered from yellow fever at other times and on other shores; which had, it seems, rendered them resistant, if not absolutely invulnerable; several, shadows of their former selves, showed signs of having been but recently touched, if not cut down, by it... among these, Antonio Gomez... Oh, how transformed was that good-natured priestly face of his! Three alone had not contracted the infected blood, either then or before. Among the three — the convict di Rudio!

From the deck of the *Abeille*, already churning the broken ocean about her bows, I cast a last glance towards the Montagne d'Argent and think, with profound regret, of my canoe floating solitary and motionless beneath the dense lianas. The landscape dwindles rapidly. The hell that men had accumulated there was no more; what remained was one of its most baleful consequences, the vast charnel pit from which we had miraculously escaped. Nature, indifferent, spread over so much horror her enchanting hues, and in the scintillating air vibrated her mysterious amourings.
