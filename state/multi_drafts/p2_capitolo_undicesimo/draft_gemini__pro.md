ELEVENTH CHAPTER


NO sooner did I see myself out of danger, than my first thought was for Elisa. I had made my way toward the Boulevard des Italiens... I entered a café. I must recount things that will seem trifles; but they had their weight. The café was empty. The proprietress, behind the counter, was speaking in a frenzied manner with a customer who stood upright before her. She did not see me enter and did not notice my presence until I asked her, if you please, for paper and an inkwell. I wrote briefly: "Elisa, all is well. I will depart tomorrow for Oporto." Having sealed the letter, I returned the inkwell to the counter and asked for a stamp of eight *soldi*. The lady rummaged in a drawer and asked me, handing them over, if four of two *soldi* each would not serve me.

I set off for the Piazza della Concorde. It was deserted. I stood watch on the bridge for a long time. The thousand noises of the city reached me confusedly, the buzzing of an immense beehive. The Senna, made gloomier by the restless reflection of the streetlamps, ran toward the sea mysteriously, like the looming hour. None of the conspirators appeared. When to wait any longer would have been folly, I rid myself of the revolver by throwing it into the river and set out toward the Boulevard, determined to reconnoiter.

During that journey, every light whisper of a voice, caught on the wing, struck my breast like a tempest's blow. The noble ardors, the boundless hopes, the pertinacity to glimpse a sliver of blue sky in the gloomiest of situations; the instinctive faith in the slow but sure justice of things—all were as if uprooted, turned to dust, and scattered into nothingness. Napoleon III was alive!...

Protected by the iron plates of his carriage, he had remained as if paralyzed until the explosion of the third bomb... Suddenly he appeared in the theatre, erect in the imperial box, ashen like a corpse. At his side was Eugenia Montijo, deathly pale but ever beautiful, and a few drops of blood streaked her forehead with vermilion. At the frenetic applause that erupted from all around, she had let herself fall into the armchair and her breast heaved, as if she had suddenly found again the one atmosphere that could infuse her with intensity of life. But Napoleon had remained on his feet. The feeling of being Emperor once more had returned color to that pulsating corpse who now saluted, bowing complacently, to the right and to the left.

Outside, meanwhile, the Police and his most trusted battalions were making his way to the Palace secure.

Thus fell the planned revolution; thus the Empire remained, in all its integrity, perhaps even stronger. Napoleon III clung, more than ever, like a vampire, to France; and France stood, more than ever, in my mind, between Italy and her independence and her greatness. And I asked myself, trembling, if it were an inexorable law of Nature that the vulgarity and betrayal and corruption and greed of the few must hold eternal dominion in Europe; if derided noble aspirations, courage, persecution, misery, and the hangman's noose must, for all eternity, be the sole inheritance of the honest.

And I despaired. But not like the man who, paralyzed by destiny, merely bites his own hands. Not a single fiber within me, in those moments, was not swollen with a turbulent spirit, was not a complete impulse of rebellion. Had I the opportunity, I feel I would have killed even without the highest of aims; simply as a human protest; to die, perhaps, but avenging the humble and the oppressed...

I decided to return to the Hotel France et Champagne. Along the way I became convinced that the situation was growing turbid... public consciousness, as revealed by the scattered groups of people, was already attributing the attack to foreigners. It was no longer a matter solely of beginning the unequal struggle anew; one also had to think of saving one's own neck. And, perhaps from that very moment, I would have provided for my own safety, if departing without at least ascertaining the fate of my roommate had not struck me as cowardice.

Nothing out of the ordinary at the hotel. Pieri had not returned... the room was in precisely the order in which we had left it. I packed my valise, the one with the embroidered tiger; I went down to the office and asked for my bill. From that moment, events precipitated.

While the proprietress was confirming the date of my arrival in the register, I settled myself at a small table and began to leaf through an illustrated journal. Just then, several individuals entered noisily... plainclothes policemen; the uniform cut of their hair was enough to reveal them to me. They asked for permission to inspect the register. One of them, probably the chief, leaning toward the lady, whispered: "Who is the *Monsieur* who is reading?" The lady indicated a point on the register with the tip of her pen. A dozen eyes fixed upon me, so that I felt their gazes piercing my skin, and the *Maitre d'Hotel* came and, all obsequiousness, asked for my passport. I took it out, unfolded it... the policemen took possession of it, scanned it, looked at one another. "*Pardon, monsieur*!" And they left.

They left, but from my seat, I saw out of the corner of my eye two hounds remain on guard at the main door. Was I to consider myself a prisoner in that hotel? The proprietress came with the bill and I was forced to maintain the flirtatious conversation she was pleased to initiate. Suddenly I sighed; but not for the reason perhaps imagined by my beautiful temptress. Peeking out the window, I had seen the policemen move away. The way was clear!

And I took advantage of it. At first slowly, so as not to arouse suspicion; then, when I was quite certain I was not being watched, away almost at a run.

I was now persuaded that Pieri had thought to get away from Paris. I came upon a public carriage and commandeered it: "Stazione d'Orleans... a good tip if you arrive in time!" The coachman plied his whip so, it seemed he meant to flay the hide from his nag. We arrive... it is eleven o'clock... four minutes late!... no train for Bordeaux until four in the morning!

What was I to do? Doze at the station? Seek hospitality at the Zappa house, or with some other trusted friend? I decided to return to the hotel. The Police had already nosed about the place... a circumstance that ought to suffice, it seemed to me, to render it an invulnerable sanctuary.

And so I set off on foot, with my valise hanging from my arm. I had gone five hundred paces, or thereabouts, when a squadron of municipal guards arrived at a full gallop. It passed me like a whirlwind, slowed at the station, surrounded it, and enclosed the passengers within a ring of iron. I thought: "If the bird has been slow to take flight, the hunters have been equally slow in laying their trap."

At midnight I was in my room. I had left orders to be woken early; I had turned off the gas; I had stretched out, as was my habit in such circumstances, without disrobing in the least. And I was already promising myself I would take good counsel from the night, when I heard the bell at the main door ring. The window of my room looked onto the courtyard. I leaped to it and saw, far below, a group of people looming in the darkness, about whom I could harbor no illusions; but, even if I could have, it would not have lasted long. In the silence of the night, these words reached my ear: "We want to see the foreigner in number 53!" The number of my room.

In a great hurry I undressed and flung myself under the covers. For a greater show of composure, I had left the door ajar. The guards arrived, accompanied by the *Maitre d'Hotel*; they enter by the dim light of a candle, they light the gas... I felt a gentle touch on my shoulder. Then I stopped snoring. "Excuse us! we must do our duty. Your name? Could you favor us with your passport?" They look at it minutely, then look at the *Maitre d'Hotel*: "But is this not the gentleman with whom we spoke an hour ago, in your office? Truly, a night of mistakes! *Pardon, monsieur*!" I heard their footsteps recede along the corridor and lose themselves down the stairs.

But it was my destiny not to rest in my bed. Half an hour later they were back, and this time they dispensed with ceremony. "A certain *monsieur* Pierey," they said, "who was lodging in this very room, affirms that he is your friend. He is at the Prefecture, in safe hands. You must resign yourself to keeping him company. We have orders to arrest you!"

While I dress, two policemen stand at my sides, at attention; others set about forcing open our chest of drawers. From it they drew the revolver case, which Pieri had thrown in there, and a few bullets. "What is this?"

"I do not know! The wardrobe is for the exclusive use of the gentleman who lodged in this room. All that is mine is this bag."

They felt it, they rummaged through it, they drew out, piece by piece, the little it contained; but the dagger, hidden between the small plank and the bottom lining, escaped their search.

Once we reached the Prefecture, another blow awaited me. They had transported me in a cab, with two guardian angels at my side, a third seated on the box with the coachman, a fourth behind the carriage. I was about to descend and did not yet have my foot on the step, when another loaded carriage entered the courtyard at a trot. I started up the grand staircase and, seized by a sad premonition, turned my head. A few paces away, preceded and followed by a guard, came a man who surely held within his breast at that moment all the sorrows of his Italy. He had a pale face, black sideburns, his head bandaged... Felice Orsini
