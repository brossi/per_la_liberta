NON appena mi vidi fuori di pericolo, il mio primo pensiero fu per l'Elisa. Mi ero diretto verso il Boulevard des Italiens... entrai in un caffè. Debbo narrare cose che sembreranno inezie; ma esse ebbero il loro peso. Il caffè era vuoto. La padrona, dietro il banco, parlava in modo spiritato con un avventore che le stava ritto di fronte. Non mi vide entrare e non notò la mia presenza sinché non le domandai, per favore, carta e calamaio. Scrissi brevemente: "Elisa, tutto va bene. Partirò domani per Oporto." Suggellata la lettera, riportai il calamaio al banco e chiesi un francobollo da otto soldi. La signora frugò in un cassetto e mi domandò, porgendomeli, se non mi servirebbero quattro da due.

Mi avviai alla Piazza della Concorde. Era deserta. Rimasi di piantone sul ponte a lungo. I mille rumori della città mi giungevano confusamente, ronzìo di un immenso alveare. La Senna, fatta più cupa dal riflesso irrequieto dei fanali, correva verso il mare misteriosamente, come l'ora che incombeva. Nessuno dei congiurati comparve. Quando l'aspettare più a lungo sarebbe stata follia, mi sbarazzai della rivoltella gettandola nel fiume e mi incamminai verso il Boulevard, deciso a scoprire terreno.

Durante quel tragitto, ogni lieve soffio di voce sorpreso al volo, giunse entro al mio petto come un colpo di bufera. I nobili ardimenti, le sconfinate speranze, la pertinacia a scorgere un filo d'azzurro nelle più tetre situazioni; la fede istintiva nella giustizia lenta, ma sicura delle cose, ne rimasero come sradicate e fatte polvere e disperse nel nulla. Napoleone III viveva!...

Protetto dalle ferree piastre della sua carrozza, vi era rimasto come paralizzato sino allo scoppio della terza bomba... D'improvviso comparve in teatro, ritto nel palco imperiale, terreo come un defunto. Gli era al fianco Eugenia Montijo, pallidissima ma sempre bella e poche goccia di sangue le rigavano di vermiglio la fronte. Agli applausi frenetici che scoppiarono d'ogni intorno, ella si era lasciata cadere sulla poltrona e il petto le ansava, come se avesse d'un subito ritrovata l'unica atmosfera capace di infonderle intensità di vita. Ma Napoleone era rimasto in piedi. Il sentirsi nuovamente Imperatore, aveva ridato colore a quel cadavere pulsante che ora salutava, chinandosi compiacentemente, a destra e a sinistra.

Fuori, la Polizia e i battaglioni più fidati gli facevano, intanto, sicura la via della Reggia.

Così cadeva la progettata rivoluzione; così restava, in tutta la sua integrità, più forte forse, l'Impero. Napoleone III attaccato, più che mai, come un vampiro, alla Francia; la Francia frapposta più che mai, nel mio concetto, tra l'Italia e la sua indipendenza e la sua grandezza. Ed io mi domandavo, fremendo, se fosse legge inesorabile di Natura che la volgarità e il tradimento e la corruzione e la cupidigia dei pochi debbano avere predominio eterno in Europa; se le nobili aspirazioni derise, il coraggio, la persecuzione, la miseria, il capestro vi debbano, in eterno, essere l'unico retaggio degli onesti.

E disperai. Ma non come l'uomo che, paralizzato dal destino, si limiti a mordere le proprie mani. Non una sola fibra era, in quei momenti, in me, che non fosse gonfia di spirito turbolento, che non fosse tutta un impeto di ribellione. Se ne avessi avuto il destro, sento che avrei ucciso anche senza un fine altissimo; semplicemente come umana protesta; per morire, forse, ma vendicando gli umili e gli oppressi...

Mi decisi a ritornare all'albergo France et Champagne. Strada facendo ebbi a convincermi che la situazione si intorbidava,... la coscienza pubblica, quale si palesava dai crocchi sparsi qua e là, già attribuiva l'attentato a stranieri. Non si trattava più unicamente di ricominciare la lotta diseguale; bisognava anche pensare a mettere la testa in salvo. E, forse sin da quel momento, avrei provveduto alla mia sicurezza, se il partire senz'accertare almeno la sorte del mio compagno di camera, non mi fosse parsa codardia.

Nulla di eccezionale all'albergo. Il Pieri non vi aveva fatto ritorno..., la camera perfettamente nell'ordine in cui l'avevamo lasciata. Preparai la mia valigia, quella dalla tigre trapunta; scesi nell'ufficio e domandai il mio conto. Da quel momento gli avvenimenti precipitarono.

Mentre la padrona accertava, sul registro, la data del mio arrivo, io mi accomodai presso un tavolino e principiai a sfogliare un giornale illustrato. Proprio in quella, entrarono rumorosamente parecchi individui... poliziotti in borghese; bastò a rivelarmelo il taglio uniforme dei capelli. Chiesero il permesso di ispezionare il registro. L'un d'essi, probabilmente il capo, chinandosi verso la signora, sussurrò: "Chi è il Monsieur che sta leggendo?" La signora indicò colla punta della penna, un punto sul registro. Una dozzina d'occhi mi fissò così, da sentirmene gli sguardi conficcati nella pelle e il Maitre d'Hotel venne e, tutto ossequioso, mi chiese il passaporto. Lo trassi, lo spiegai... i poliziotti se ne impossessarono, lo scorsero, si guardarono in faccia. "Pardon, monsieur!" E se ne andarono.

Se ne andarono ma dal mio posto, vidi colla coda dell'occhio due segugi rimanere di guardia al portone. Dovevo considerarmi come prigioniero in quell'albergo? La padrona venne col conto e fui costretto a sostenere la conversazione a tinte galanti che ella si compiacque di intavolare. D'improvviso sospirai; ma non pel motivo immaginato forse dalla mia bella tentatrice. Sbirciando oltre la finestra, avevo visto i poliziotti allontanarsi. Il passo era libero!

E ne approfittai. Dapprima lentamente, per non destare sospetti; poi, quando fui ben certo di non essere spiato, via quasi a passo di carica.

Adesso ero persuaso che il Pieri avesse pensato ad allontanarsi da Parigi. Mi imbatto in una vettura pubblica e me l'accaparro: "Stazione d'Orleans..., buona mancia se arrivi a tempo!" Il vetturino sferza così, che pare voglia levare le cuoia al ronzino. Si arriva... sono le undici... quattro minuti di ritardo!... nessun treno per Bordeaux sino alle quattro del mattino!

Che cosa risolvere? Sonnecchiare alla stazione? Cercare ospitalità in casa Zappa, o presso qualche altro amico fidato? Decisi di ritornare all'albergo. La Polizia già vi aveva ficcato il naso,... circostanza che doveva bastare, mi parve, a renderlo un asilo invulnerabile.

E mi incamminavo a piedi, colla mia valigia appesa al braccio. Avevo percorso cinquecento passi, o giù di lì, quand'ecco giungere al gran galoppo uno squadrone di guardie municipali. Mi passa come un turbine d'accanto, rallenta alla stazione, la circonda, vi rinchiude, in un cerchio di ferro, i passeggieri. Penso: "Se l'uccel di bosco è stato tardo alla fuga, tardi del pari si mostrano i cacciatori all'insidia."

A mezza notte ero nella mia camera. Avevo lasciato l'ordine di svegliarmi per tempo; avevo spento il gas; mi ero sdraiato, come sempre in simili circostanze, senza punto svestirmi. E già mi ripromettevo di trarre buon consiglio dalla notte, quando udii suonare il campanello del portone. La finestra della mia camera dava sul cortile. Vi balzo e giù in fondo, vidi nereggiare un gruppo di persone, sulle quali non potevo farmi illusione alcuna; ma, quand'anche non sarebbe durata a lungo. Nel silenzio della notte, giunsero al mio orecchio le parole: "Vogliamo vedere lo straniero del numero 53!" Il numero della mia camera.

A precipizio mi svestii e mi cacciai sotto le coltri. A maggiore ostentazione d'animo tranquillo, avevo lasciato l'uscio socchiuso. Le guardie vi giunsero accompagnate dal Maitre d'Hotel; entrano alla scarsa luce d'una candela, accendono il gas... mi sentii toccare gentilmente ad una spalla. Allora smessi di russare. "Scusate! dobbiamo fare il nostro dovere. Il vostro nome? Potete favorirci il vostro passaporto?" Lo guardano minutamente, poi guardano il Maitre d'Hotel: "Ma non è questo il signore col quale abbiamo parlato un'ora fa, nel vostro ufficio? Proprio la notte degli equivoci! Pardon, monsieur!" Udii il loro passo allontanarsi lungo il corridoio e perdersi giù per le scale.

Ma era destino che non dovessi riposare nel mio letto. Mezz'ora dopo erano di ritorno e questa volta non conobbero cerimonie. "Un certo monsieur Pierey — dissero — il quale alloggiava in questa camera stessa, afferma d'essere amico vostro. Egli è alla Prefettura, in mani sicure. Bisogna rassegnarsi a tenergli compagnia. Abbiamo ordine d'arrestarvi!"

Mentre mi vesto, due poliziotti mi stanno ai fianchi, sull'attenti; altri si industriano di scassinare il nostro canterano. Ne trassero l'astuccio della rivoltella, che il Pieri vi aveva gettato e qualche proiettile. "Che è questo?"

— Non so! L'armadio è d'uso esclusivo del signore che alloggiava in questa camera. Di mio non ho che questa borsa.

La palparono, la frugarono, ne trassero, capo per capo, il poco che conteneva; ma il pugnale nascosto tra l'assicella ed il fondo sfuggì alle loro indagini.

Giunti alla Prefettura, un altro colpo mi aspettava. Mi avevano trasportato in cittadina, con due angeli custodi al fianco, un terzo seduto a cassetta col cocchiere, un quarto dietro la vettura. Stavo per scendere e non avevo ancora il piede sul predellino, quando un'altra carrozza carica entrò al trotto nel cortile. Io infilo lo scalone e, colto da triste presentimento, giro il capo. A pochi passi, preceduto e seguito da una guardia, veniva un uomo che certo racchiudeva in petto in quel momento, tutti i dolori dell'Italia sua. Aveva la faccia pallida, le basette nere, la testa fasciata... Felice Orsini!

SCARCELY had I found myself out of danger than my first thought was for Elisa. I had made my way towards the Boulevard des Italiens... and entered a café. I must recount things that will seem trifles; yet they had their weight. The café was empty. The proprietress, behind the counter, was holding a lively conversation with a customer who stood before her. She did not see me enter and took no notice of my presence until I asked her, if she would be so kind, for paper and ink. I wrote briefly: "Elisa, all is well. I shall leave tomorrow for Oporto." Having sealed the letter, I returned the inkwell to the counter and asked for an eight-sou stamp. The lady rummaged in a drawer and, holding them out to me, asked whether four two-sou ones would not serve my purpose.

I made my way to the Place de la Concorde. It was deserted. I stood long at my post on the bridge. The thousand sounds of the city reached me in confusion, the humming of an immense hive. The Seine, made darker by the restless reflections of the gas-lamps, flowed towards the sea mysteriously, like the hour that hung above us. None of the conspirators appeared. When to wait longer would have been madness, I rid myself of the revolver by casting it into the river, and set off towards the Boulevard, resolved to reconnoitre the ground.

During that passage, every faint breath of voice caught upon the air descended into my breast like a gust of storm. The noble daring, the boundless hopes, the stubborn will to descry a thread of blue sky in the most cheerless of situations; the instinctive faith in the slow but certain justice of things — all these were as if uprooted, ground to dust, and scattered into nothingness. Napoleon III was alive!...

Sheltered by the iron plates of his carriage, he had remained therein as though paralysed until the bursting of the third bomb... Then all at once he appeared in the theatre, erect in the imperial box, ashen as a corpse. At his side was Eugenia Montijo, very pale yet beautiful still, and a few drops of blood scored her brow with crimson. At the frantic applause that broke out on all sides, she had let herself sink into her chair, her breast heaving, as though she had all at once recovered the only air capable of breathing life into her with full intensity. But Napoleon had remained standing. The sensation of being Emperor once more had restored colour to that pulsing cadaver, which now acknowledged the ovation, bowing complacently to right and left.

Outside, meanwhile, the Police and the most trusted battalions were making his road to the Palace secure.

Thus fell the intended revolution; thus the Empire remained, in all its integrity, stronger perhaps than before. Napoleon III clinging to France more than ever, like a vampire; France interposed, more than ever in my reckoning, between Italy and her independence and her greatness. And I asked myself, trembling, whether it were the inexorable law of Nature that the vulgarity and the treachery and the corruption and the cupidity of the few must hold eternal dominion in Europe; whether noble aspirations mocked, courage, persecution, misery, the halter must be, in eternity, the only heritage of the righteous.

And I despaired. But not as the man who, paralysed by destiny, does nothing but gnaw his own hands. Not a single fibre was there in me, in those moments, that was not swollen with turbulent spirit, that was not all impulse and rebellion. Had I found the occasion, I feel I could have killed even without a lofty purpose; simply as a human protest; to die, perhaps, but avenging the humble and the oppressed...

I resolved to return to the Hôtel France et Champagne. On the way I had occasion to persuade myself that the situation was growing murky... public opinion, as it revealed itself from the groups scattered here and there, was already attributing the attempt to foreigners. It was no longer merely a matter of renewing the unequal struggle; one had also to think of saving one's neck. And perhaps from that very moment I should have seen to my own safety, had it not seemed to me an act of cowardice to depart without ascertaining at least the fate of my room-companion.

Nothing out of the ordinary at the hotel. Pieri had not returned... the room in precisely the order in which we had left it. I prepared my valise — the one with the quilted tiger — went down to the office and asked for my bill. From that moment events came thick and fast.

While the proprietress was confirming, in the register, the date of my arrival, I settled myself at a small table and began to leaf through an illustrated newspaper. Just then, several individuals entered noisily... plain-clothes policemen; their uniformly cropped hair was enough to betray them. They asked leave to inspect the register. One of them, the leader in all likelihood, leaning towards the lady, murmured: "Who is the Monsieur who is reading?" The lady indicated a point on the register with the tip of her pen. A dozen eyes fixed themselves upon me with such insistence that I felt their gaze driven into my skin, and the *maître d'hôtel* came and, with every show of deference, asked for my passport. I drew it out, unfolded it... the policemen took possession of it, scanned it, looked at one another. "Pardon, monsieur!" And they departed.

They departed; but from where I sat, I caught with the corner of my eye two *limiers* — hounds — remaining on guard at the entrance. Was I to consider myself a prisoner in that hotel? The proprietress came with the bill, and I was compelled to sustain the conversation in a gallant vein which she was pleased to strike up. All at once I sighed; yet not for the reason perhaps imagined by my fair temptress. Glancing beyond the window, I had seen the policemen move away. The way was clear!

I took advantage of it. Slowly at first, so as not to arouse suspicion; then, when I was quite certain I was not being watched, off at very nearly a running pace.

I was by now persuaded that Pieri had thought to get himself out of Paris. I came upon a public cab and secured it for myself: "Gare d'Orléans... a good tip if you get there in time!" The coachman laid on his whip as though he meant to flay the hide off the nag. We arrived... it is eleven o'clock... four minutes too late!... no train for Bordeaux until four in the morning!

What was to be done? Doze at the station? Seek hospitality at the house of Zappa, or with some other trustworthy friend? I decided to return to the hotel. The Police had already poked their nose in there... a circumstance which seemed to me sufficient, in itself, to render it an inviolable refuge.

And I set off on foot, my valise hanging on my arm. I had gone some five hundred paces, or thereabouts, when there came galloping up at full tilt a squadron of municipal guards. It swept past me like a whirlwind, slackened pace at the station, surrounded it, and enclosed the passengers within a ring of iron. I thought: "If the woodland bird was slow to take flight, the hunters are no less slow in setting their snare."

At midnight I was in my room. I had left orders to be wakened in good time; I had turned off the gas; I had stretched myself out, as is my custom in such circumstances, without undressing at all. And I was already promising myself that the night would bring counsel, when I heard the bell of the street door ring. My room window gave onto the courtyard. I sprang to it, and down in the depths I made out a dark group of figures upon whom I could harbour no illusion; though, had I been disposed to, it would not have lasted long. In the silence of the night, there reached my ear the words: "We wish to see the foreigner in number 53!" The number of my room.

In haste I undressed and thrust myself under the covers. To make a greater show of untroubled composure, I had left the door ajar. The guards reached it accompanied by the *maître d'hôtel*; they entered by the scant light of a candle, turned on the gas... I felt a gentle touch upon one shoulder. I thereupon ceased to snore. "Forgive us! We must do our duty. Your name? Would you be so good as to furnish us with your passport?" They examine it minutely, then look at the *maître d'hôtel*: "But is this not the very gentleman with whom we spoke an hour ago, in your office? A night of misunderstandings indeed! Pardon, monsieur!" I heard their step recede along the corridor and fade away down the staircase.

But it was my fate not to rest in my bed that night. Half an hour later they were back, and this time they dispensed with ceremony. "A certain Monsieur Pierey," they said, "who lodged in this very room, declares himself to be a friend of yours. He is at the Prefecture, in safe hands. You must resign yourself to keeping him company. We have orders to arrest you!"

While I dress, two policemen stand at my sides, at attention; others busy themselves picking the lock of our chest of drawers. From it they drew the revolver case, which Pieri had tossed there, and some cartridges. "What is this?"

— I do not know! The wardrobe is for the exclusive use of the gentleman who lodged in this room. Of my own I have nothing but this bag.

They felt it, rummaged through it, drew out, piece by piece, the little it contained; but the dagger concealed between the wooden lining and the false bottom escaped their investigation.

On arriving at the Prefecture, another blow awaited me. I had been conveyed there in a cab, with two guardian angels at my side, a third seated on the box with the coachman, a fourth behind the carriage. I was on the point of alighting and had not yet set my foot upon the step, when another laden carriage entered the courtyard at a trot. I make for the staircase and, seized by a dark presentiment, I turn my head. A few paces behind me, preceded and followed by a guard, came a man who at that moment surely bore within his breast all the sorrows of his Italy. His face was pale, his side-whiskers black, his head bound in bandages... Felice Orsini!

## Chapter the Tenth

A FEW minutes later we were in position opposite the Opera. The light cast by the premises of a trattoria — the Broggi, if I am not mistaken — struck me from behind; the extraordinary illumination of the theatre fell full upon our faces. The entrance to the trattoria was flanked by columns. Against one of these Orsini leaned his back; I placed myself upright to his right. We were among a great many people, most of them standing still, awaiting the imperial cortège. Opposite us, just beyond the pavement, a cordon of municipal guards had drawn up in line, arms at the foot... beyond the rue Le Pelletier, along the sides of the little square, a dark crowd surged and pressed... among it, at the place assigned him, I saw Antonio Gomez, tall, erect, motionless as a statue; but though I searched as keenly as I could on the other side, I did not see Giuseppe Pieri.

And already one could hear, in the distance, the confused clamour of the multitude acclaiming the Emperor... a few seconds more, and the voices grew sonorous and distinct. To cry up to heaven the man who had mown them down with grapeshot... a deplorable degradation of Peoples who prefer a quiet life to the arduous struggles for Liberty!

The imperial cortège arrived. Its forerunner was a carriage containing several Court officers. The one in which, behind well-concealed iron cuirasses, Napoleon III sat as though in hiding, came second and was preceded and followed by half a squadron of mounted guides... the very same who had so ably seconded the heroic enterprise of the 2nd of December. After the drunkenness of blood and the proclamation of the Empire, they had been rechristened with the designation of Lancers of the Imperial Guard.

A simple and strange thing occurred. My gaze had flown to the Emperor's carriage and was attempting to penetrate beyond the opaque veil which the cold had condensed upon the glass of the door. At that moment, a white handkerchief, which betrayed the restless nervousness of the hand that clasped it, wiped away that veil, as though some sensitive soul, having fully caught my gaze and the hatred with which it was charged, were scrutinising the street with a suspicious and watchful eye.

I did not see, but I felt the presence of Eugenia Moutijo and the magical power of the eternal feminine, which knows how to work its way through cuirasses; which, by caressing and softening, bends force and triumphs over it.

But to sweep away the sentiment of pity, I had at once the intuition of the presence of a grim figure, with a nose like a vulture's beak, and with vibrations of the most repulsive kind. And I felt — oh, let it be believed as truth, even though it wear the face of falsehood! — I felt the warm odour of republican blood made to run in the sewers of Paris; blood of generous souls, slaughtered under cover of night, their hands bound behind their backs; slaughtered like herds of cattle. And the Roman epic, glorious with deeds and sacrifices, flashed through my mind... not as a pale memory drawn from the remoteness of time and space, but almost as a present tumult, full of tangible forms and of maledictions. I was seized by the fever that gunpowder gives on the field of battle. "It is just, it is just that the liberty of nations should spring from the death of him who has betrayed; it is just that this man should fall headlong even as he savours the triumph procured for him by his crimes! The Italian does not pay on Saturday — but he does pay!"

The carriage slowed. I sought Antonio Gomez with my eye and found him again. But overcome, perhaps, by a generous impatience, he could not bring himself to wait. His hand, wrapped in the fateful black handkerchief, rose threateningly before the carriage had reached the peristyle, and those few seconds of anticipation brought the trajectory of the bomb into a line perpendicular to the trattoria of the Broggi. I had barely time to fling myself to the ground and to give a tug at Orsini's overcoat so that, following my example, he might save himself. Orsini remained rigid as the column against which he had planted himself as though rooted.

A sinister flash of light, accompanied by a deafening roar... then a darkness that seemed to me infernal. I sprang to my feet as the air, but moments before ringing with cheers, trembled with shattered glass and screams. "Your turn, your turn!" Felice Orsini whispered to me, in a strangled voice. Blood was spurting from a wide wound on his brow and his face was all besmirched with it. He bent down and laid the terrible black handkerchief upon the flagstones. I hurled myself forward, crouching low,

I hurled myself forward, crouching low, jaw clenched, passing over municipal guards stretched out in the convulsions of agony, or dead. The Lancers of the Imperial Guard were at that moment taking to headlong flight, abandoning to his fate the man they had sworn to defend. A few paces... I threw!... I threw!... the second flash, the second thunderclap! The diffused light reveals to me the Emperor's carriage lurching toward the theatre, one wheel in pieces, the coachman rolling on the cobblestones, the horses down and ripped open. Every lantern is extinguished and a black and terrible night enfolds men and things alike.

I had flung myself face down among the dead and the half-living, waiting in anguished suspense for the throwing of the remaining bombs. How long did I lie there? Ten seconds, it is maintained. I was prepared to die, but I should be lying were I not to say that those seconds seemed to me a century. And a third bomb came. Who threw it? Felice Orsini said it was the Italian to whom he had handed it over, in the rue Le Pelletier, a few minutes before the attempt. He was not believed, and the question still awaits an explicit answer... for others, not for me, who saw and knew that Italian. Be that as it may, whoever threw the third bomb had a stout soul and a steady wrist. It went straight to its mark and burst directly beneath the shattered carriage. Its detonation seemed to strike my very brain... but in an instant I had recovered full possession of my senses. I heard the ground resound with the trampling of horses galloping up. The Lancers of the Guard came thundering in at the gallop and reacted against the impulse that had swept them into flight, firing point-blank upon those who fled. The flashes of their weapons leapt in the darkness and revealed to me, in fitful glimpses, the horror and confusion. And the victims — unquestionably numerous — of that heroism which bore so strong a resemblance to cowardice, were afterwards attributed one and all to the "blind and ferocious fury" of the conspirators.

From that horror, from that confusion, I extricated myself, now crawling, now in lurching bounds, escaping — I could not tell you how — the horses that seemed to pour forth in swarms from the night, and the bullets that whistled about my head.

Repeatedly, in the course of my brief but tumultuous existence, my spirit had been saddened by the spectacle of innocent victims sacrificed to the security and the placid digestions of despotism. But this was not enough to render me impervious to a deep sense of compassion for those caused by our attempt. Nor was it sufficient to suppress that sentiment, the idea that, by inexorable law, nothing is granted to the

People without grave sacrifice of tears and blood.

Nevertheless, I must also say that the depressing effect of pity was, in great part, paralysed by the conviction that France, that Italy, had now a clear road to reach their glorious destiny; that Napoleon III was no more; that my own deed had helped to lift that nightmare from the world.
