On the very day of our flight, I had my first plunge into the intense life... and it was to be a decisive one.

The Germans were filing past on the bastions that run from Porta San Celso to Porta Romana, weary, dejected and grim at once. And since we were to place ourselves at their rear, we watched, standing still, their forced withdrawal. From the bastions one dominated the city. Here and there, one could discern houses in flames; amidst the roofs, the white cathedral rose gigantic, and the Germans shook their fists at the tricolour that fluttered from its highest pinnacle. The bells continued to toll the alarm.

Between the bastions and the thick of the houses, in that era, a stretch of gardens grew green... a strange quietude reigned there, in that metropolis coursed and re-coursed by the impetuous wave of passions. Solitary, near the bastions, stood the farmers' cottage. A child, as serene as the patch of earth that had probably seen him born, came running out and, scrambling up the green ramp, stopped in mute contemplation under the horse-chestnut trees that loom over the walls. Then, as if drawn to the marvellous world of the tiniest things, he bent down and his slender fingers played among the little stones that glittered at his feet.

The troops continued to file past, each Company preceded by three Croats who acted as forerunners. One of these spied the child, lowered his rifle and, drawing it back for greater impetus, delivered a thrust, as if it were a matter of spitting a wild animal. The bayonet entered the side and exited the back of that innocent who, lifted bodily by the ferocious impetus, opened his mouth wide. But the scream, intercepted by death, never came.

The little one's mother was at a window of the solitary cottage and saw it, and she screamed for him. She came running, scrambling with difficulty up the green slope... the ascent was difficult for her, her belly again heavy and fecund. The derelict woman did not manage to clasp the tender remains in her arms. No sooner was she upon him than the tormentors of Italy delivered another bayonet thrust, and she fell backwards, a corpse with a corpse in her womb.

I was seized with fever, with delirium. On the verge of levelling my rifle, blindly, against the assassins, I felt the iron hand of my brother Achilles holding me back. He whispered a phrase in my ear that, though I did not distinguish it, I understood. Then I instinctively raised my face, as if awaiting vengeance from on high. The sky was leaden, motionless... God, if He exists, must have been absorbed in the affairs of a better world.

It was then that the conviction began to make its way into my heart that the derelict have no salvation save in the awakening of their own rebellious energies. "To the foolish," I concluded, "the feeble prayer; to the strong who strongly will, action."

Our retreat from Milano to Verona was not of such a nature as to weaken that terrible impression. Radetzky had given the order to the garrisons stationed here and there in the various parts of the Kingdom to concentrate on the main body of the army. At the same time, to avert the danger of being tormented by an enemy that would decimate his forces and slip through his fingers, as ungraspable as a swarm of mosquitos, he enjoined the evacuation of every land, large or small, situated on his path. At the slightest hint of resistance, cannonades and the horrors of pillage.

The various garrisons came down almost undisturbed, in time to replenish the forces of Radetzky, weakened by continuous desertions. The confusion, the panic of the Imperials were such that, having barely reached Lodi, all their discipline appeared ephemeral. If Carlo Alberto, instead of wasting precious time intriguing to satisfy the demands of Diplomacy, had crossed the Ticino like a thunderbolt, the retreat would have degenerated into a rout, and perhaps Austria, having lost its army of Italy, would never have risen again.

As for the populace, deprived of arms, misled by uncertain, contradictory news, they neither knew how, nor were able, to resolve to attempt great things. Nevertheless, inveterate hatred and virile spirit, here and there, burst forth. Melegnano, which had flattered itself it could block the enemy's road, was soon brought into subjection by fifty pieces of artillery. I saw the Croats who were sacking it, drunk and savage, throw dishevelled women and defenceless children from the windows.

Not a day passed that I did not have to witness ferocious and cowardly scenes, often entirely unprovoked, and the repressed anger all the more rapidly tempered my patriotism.

It had its first outburst at Desenzano. The garrison of Brescia, captained by a de Domini, my maternal uncle, had effected its conjunction with Radetzky in that locality. Having learned of the presence of his two nephews, he wished to see them. He questioned us about our political opinions. My uncle was a rabid pro-Austrian, and I was not ignorant of it. I expressed myself in vibrant and free language.

The old soldier paled and interrupted me. "These," he said, "are the cursed sentiments of your father. He has infiltrated them into your blood. Poor fool! In what do you trust? In what do you hope? Do you believe that four peasants armed with pitchforks and clubs will be enough to frighten the most battle-hardened army in the world; to jeopardise the stability of the empire?" I contented myself with coldly pointing to Radetzky's retreat; to the revolution erupting from one end to the other of the usurped dominion.

"Ah, if I were not your uncle..." he muttered between his teeth. "Thank your mother; she saves you! But so be it!... sooner or later you will leave your head in the hands of the executioner!"

And I, smiling: "I prefer to face the executioner a hundred times than to be a vile renegade, as you are!"

He departed in a huff, and I never saw him again.

But those were mere words. Another terrible incident soon came to fill the measure, to give the fatal bent to my existence.

The army had left Peschiera behind when informants arrived to announce that little Castelnuovo dared to barricade itself. The spirited men, arguing, correctly, that Radetzky could not reach the strong shelter of Verona without passing through their locality, heeded the call of Carlo Alberto, who incited them to impede the retreat, and they decided to oppose their breasts to the arms, to offer themselves as a holocaust to the Fatherland.

In those times of heroic enthusiasm, one did not debate too much on the probability of success. The greater, the more certain the sacrifice, the stronger the spur that drove one to submit to it.

The imperial artillery soon got the better of Italian patriotism. Castelnuovo, riddled, in flames, was abandoned to the lust of the soldiery. We, the cadets of the Academy, witnessed that carnage, drawn up near the gate of the old walls, at the end of an elliptical piazza. And as the Croats, in their blind fury, were not aiming precisely at the mark, we had sought shelter behind several abandoned carriages.

Suddenly, I saw a young girl emerge from a burning house with her hair loose, barefoot, ill-covered by a simple chemise... evidently a sick person surprised by the fury of the fire and the men. And behold, from the same house, burst forth, weapon in hand, the filthy Croat who was pursuing her.

I thought: "If that dog reaches her, he will slaughter her!" And following an irresistible impulse, I was already lunging to her aid, when another brutish soldier, unmindful that in his own country too there were trembling mothers and sisters, levelled his carbine and fired. The young girl fell, and a scarlet streak lined the white linen that enveloped her.

An imprecation, a leap, and I was beside her, trembling with pity and rage. At the touch of my hands, which lifted her head, she opened her eyes; but having barely seen the hated uniform, she shuddered and turned her face away... her pale face where, more prominent than the deep signs of the approaching end, an expression of extreme revulsion dominated.

"Do not fear. I am one of yours; I am Italian too!" and I touched her flesh to search for the wound.

The unfortunate girl gestured to her heart, sighed, and her young limbs were seized by the ineffable relaxation of death. I stopped to contemplate her. She was of a beauty rendered sublime to my eyes by the aureola of martyrdom. Then it was as if those lips spoke to me in a passionate and vibrant language. My emotion became overpowering. I suffered from it as if I were receiving into my soul all the sorrows of the Fatherland, and an impetus of new resolves was suddenly unleashed from the depths. Nor ever, after that day, did I invoke, in moments of danger, the sacred ideal of Liberty without the delicate features of that young girl, thrust into eternal silence by the oppressor's lead, unconsciously great, tragic and beautiful, reappearing, clear in my mind, to renew that impetus.

Night fell, and it was the last for certain tormentors who had expected to savour the juice of our grapes for a long time to come. The livid lips revealed no secrets. Nor were there any close investigations. To what end in times of inextricable confusion? But I can speak now without fear and without bravado. It was that night that my hands and those of Achilles knew the first blood. An act in defence of my resurgent race, an act of Justice!..
