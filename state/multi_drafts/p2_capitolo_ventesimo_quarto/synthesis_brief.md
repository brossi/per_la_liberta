NELLE mie ore di riposo, nulla trascuravo per avere ampie informazioni sugli uomini che vivevano e soffrivano a me d'attorno. Antonio Gomez, dopo essere stato addetto, per qualche tempo, al servizio degli Ufficiali, aveva ritrovata, benché colorata in bronzo, la sua faccia da prete bonaccione. Ma un ordine inaspettato lo sottrasse ad ogni mia influenza. Fu inviato, con altri, al penitenziario del Corù, sul Continente ed ebbi appena il tempo di stringergli la mano. D'allora, benché abbia fede che i rivolgimenti politici di Francia gli procurassero giorni migliori, io più non lo rividi e più nulla ne intesi.

Sull'isola erano però altri Italiani, una dozzina forse; Romani per lo più, tutti condannati per aver raddoppiato il loro odio politico, sovrapponendosi quello privato; per aver vendicato, colla prepotenza nostrana, l'onta loro inflitta dalla prepotenza straniera. Il Cristo ha molto perdonato — dicesi — a chi ha molto amato; egli dovrebbe perdonare molto di più a chi ha molto odiato e, nell'odio seppe trovare la virtù di non esser pecora. Fra questi Romani era un Morelli, sarto, condannato a vita. Chi presta facile orecchio alla speranza, più di colui che le ha tutte perdute?

Trovai inoltre Italiani condannati, o semplicemente relegati, per motivi politici. Dimoravano, in parte sull'isola del Diavolo; ma tragittavano di frequente, a marea bassa, col pretesto, per lo più, di ricorrere alla farmacia annessa all'Ospedale. V'era Galli, v'era Rossini, v'era Tebaldi e stringemmo presto fraterna dimestichezza. La loro storia m'era nota e potevo star certo che, quand'anche non partecipassero alla fuga, l'avrebbero aiutata con ogni loro mezzo.

Tra gli uomini conosciuti durante la mia prima traversata da Tolone e sbarcati, sin d'allora, all'isola, era un arciprete francese, condannato a vita per l'assassinio di un bimbo del quale aveva abusato. Durante l'intero viaggio, aveva custodito gelosamente, sotto il braccio, una sua grossa Bibbia. In un momento d'espansione, egli mi confidava, tremando nella voce, d'aver nascosto, nella legatura di quel volume, qualcosa come ventimila franchi e il pensiero tormentoso d'essere strozzato da mani cupide del suo, lo rodeva più dei rimorsi. Intendeva valersi di quel danaro per procurarsi i mezzi di fuggire. Me ne rammentai e vi feci assegnamento. Risparmiare le indispensabili vettovaglie sulla nostra limitata razione giornaliera, richiedeva sacrificio troppo grave e troppo continuato per la nostra fame e per la mia impazienza. Bisognava farne acquisto ai magazzini del penitenziario gradatamente, per non destare sospetti, pagando magari prezzi di usura. Perché l'arciprete non ci avrebbe fornito il conquibus?

Ma la mia buona ventura volle che, all'isola, fosse un altro individuo che mi poteva riuscire ben altrimenti utile. Era un ex ufficiale della marina francese, un orleanista arrabbiato, uomo tutto ardire e tutto nervi; scontava una condanna a vita, altra delle circostanze che lo ritenni favorevoli e direi quasi felici. Quell'uomo aveva già trattato le tempeste; l'Oceano non aveva terrori per lui; non era per lui una pianura sterminata senza sentieri.

Tutti questi pensieri, raggi di una ruota sconnessa, restarono per molto tempo immobili e come cristallizzati nella mia mente, finché d'improvviso, ricevettero un impulso straordinario per la formazione quasi spontanea del pezzo che mi mancava; che indaro, nelle mie lunghe meditazioni, io avevo tentato di ideare.

Una sera, uscito dall'officina, mi incamminavo verso la mia baracca, che ne distava un buon miglio, quando, giunto in prossimità del molo, scorsi il Morbioli chino su una cannoniera, della quale lustrava, con tutta placidezza, gli ottoni. Le cannoniere erano, in realtà, modeste scialuppe portanti, a poppa e a prua, un cannoncino da trastullo. Esse venivano calate nell'onde di buon mattino e risollevate a secco alle cinque della sera.

Il Morbioli, canottiere a Roma, era addetto alla loro pulizia. Quante volte l'avevo visto affaccendarsi d'attorno a quei gingilli che erano il mio incubo! Ma ora soltanto, per una singolare disposizione del mio animo, quei suoi molli strofinamenti, che mi sapevano di carezza, suscitano una gran stizza e grido entro me stesso: "Sfondali, pisellone; fondali, se puoi!"

Quella semplice parola "fondali!" mi guizzò nella mente a guisa di lampo che rischiari una notte tenebrosa. Fu tutta una rivelazione e n'ebbi i brividi.

Mi avvicinai al Morbioli e credo che la mia faccia rivelasse il segreto sussulto, perchè, egli mi guardò con evidente inquietudine e disse: "Che cosa c'è di guasto!" — Morbioli, quand'io ero laggiù alla

Montagne d'Argent, si pose mano ad erigere un ospedale e già eravamo giunti all'imbiancatura. Un forzato, che mi aveva in grande simpatia, era un giorno in cima alla sua scala a pioli e spennellava la facciata quando, da una finestra vicina, uscì un sussurrio di voci. Erano il Comandante, il Capo guardiano e un condannato che confabulavano. L'imbianchino udì distintamente pronunciare il mio nome e se ne stette rigido ad ascoltare. Ti dirò in breve: il Comandante garantiva al condannato l'immediata libertà purché riuscisse a coinvolgermi in qualche tentativo di fuga, in modo che le sentinelle avessero il destro di freddarmi a fucilate. — Perchè mi racconti questo? — Per persuaderti che sui registri, d'accanto al mio nome è segnata una croce e che, se pure io fossi uomo tanto spregevole da tirarti in un complotto coll'intenzione di denunciarti... a che mi gioverebbe? Tanto per me non vi sarebbe grazia. Ed ora ascolta...

Gli confidai il segreto lavorìo della mia mente, le circostanze e le persone su cui fondavo le mie speranze; la tattica che ci avrebbe indubbiamente resi padroni del canotto e dei pescatori; lo stratagemma che egli, a sua insaputa, mettendo fine ai miei lunghi tormenti, m'aveva ispirato. Era appunto questa la parte che avrei affidato alla sua lealtà ed alla sua intelligenza.

Si grattò la testa, com'era sua abitudine nei momenti di ansietà; poi alzò la faccia ed io lessi la sua piena adesione negli occhi che sfavillavano. Lì per lì, mi ebbi un elogio di sapore prettamente transteverino; il più strano che mi sia mai stato rivolto durante la mia lunga esistenza: "Sei un demonio! Benedetta la donna che ti ha partorito e che tu possa murì... muri ammazzato!"

Coll'adesione del Morbioli, il primo anello della catena era concretato. Gli altri lo furono a mano a mano, lentamente, ma mirabilmente.

Una notte nuvolosa, turbata dal frastuono di una fiera tempesta di mare, sguscio silenziosamente dalla mia baracca e, armato di scalpello, striscio lungo gli scogli, sin sotto ai magazzini, ampia costruzione in legno fondata, in parte, su palafitte. Tento, ritento, e mi vien dato di sollevare una tavola dell'assito. Penetrai là dentro brancolando, fiutando la via come un topo e, quando n'esco, ho sul groppone sei grandi amache di tela resistentissima che Morelli, il sarto romano, trova il modo di cucire a perfetta regola d'arte. Avevamo la vela!

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-FOURTH

DURING my hours of rest, I neglected nothing in order to obtain full intelligence of the men who lived and suffered about me. Antonio Gomez, having been assigned for some time to the service of the Officers, had recovered, though bronzed in colour, his good-natured priest's face. But an unexpected order removed him from any influence I might have over him. He was dispatched, with others, to the penitentiary at Corù, on the Continent, and I had barely time to press his hand. From that day forth, though I believe the political upheavals of France may have procured him better days, I never saw him again nor heard any tidings of him.

Yet on the island there were other Italians, a dozen or so; Romans for the most part, all condemned for having redoubled their political hatred by superimposing upon it a private one; for having avenged, in the manner of our native *prepotenza* — our home-grown tyranny — the outrage inflicted upon them by a foreign tyranny. Christ forgave much, it is said, to those who loved much; He ought to forgive far more to those who hated much and who, in their hatred, knew how to find the virtue of not being sheep. Among these Romans there was one Morelli, a tailor, condemned to life imprisonment. Who lends a readier ear to hope than he who has lost all hope?

I found moreover Italians condemned, or simply relegated, for political motives. They dwelt in part on the Île du Diable; but they crossed over frequently at low tide, on the pretext, more often than not, of availing themselves of the dispensary attached to the Hospital. There was Galli, there was Rossini, there was Tebaldi, and we quickly struck up a fraternal intimacy. Their history was known to me, and I could be certain that even were they not to take part in the escape, they would have aided it with every means at their disposal.

Among the men I had met during my first crossing from Toulon, and who had been landed on the island from the very beginning, was a French archpriest, condemned to life for the murder of a child of whom he had taken criminal advantage. Throughout the entire voyage, he had jealously kept under his arm a great Bible of his own. In a moment of expansiveness, he confided to me in a trembling voice that he had concealed, within the binding of that volume, something in the nature of twenty thousand francs, and that the tormenting thought of being strangled by hands covetous of what was his gnawed at him more than remorse. He intended to employ that money to procure the means of escaping. I kept it in mind and reckoned upon it. To save up the indispensable provisions from our limited daily ration required a sacrifice too grievous and too prolonged for our hunger and for my impatience. They would need to be purchased from the penitentiary stores gradually, so as not to arouse suspicion, paying into the bargain usurious prices. Why should not the archpriest furnish us with the *conquibus* — the wherewithal?

But my good fortune willed it that on the island there was another individual who might prove far more useful to me still. He was a former officer of the French navy, a rabid Orleanist, a man all daring and all nerve; he was serving a life sentence, one further circumstance which I reckoned favourable and would almost call propitious. That man had already battled storms; the Ocean held no terrors for him; it was not for him a boundless plain without pathways.

All these thoughts, spokes of a broken wheel, remained for a long while motionless and as if crystallised in my mind, until all at once they received an extraordinary impulse through the almost spontaneous formation of the very piece I had lacked; which in vain, during my long meditations, I had endeavoured to devise.

One evening, having left the workshop, I was making my way towards my barracks, which lay a good mile distant, when, having come close to the jetty, I caught sight of Morbioli bent over a gunboat, polishing its brasswork with perfect composure. The gunboats were in reality modest longboats carrying, fore and aft, a toy cannon apiece. They were lowered into the water early in the morning and hauled back up dry at five in the evening.

Morbioli, a boatman in Rome, was assigned to their cleaning. How many times I had watched him busy about those trinkets that were the bane of my existence! But only now, by some singular disposition of my spirit, do those soft rubbings of his, which had the feeling of a caress, stir up a great vexation in me, and I cry inwardly: "Stave it in, you great blockhead; sink it, if you can!"

That simple word — "sink it!" — flashed through my mind like a bolt of lightning illuminating a pitch-dark night. It was a revelation entire, and I shuddered at it.

I approached Morbioli, and I believe my face betrayed the secret start within me, for he looked at me with evident unease and said: "What on earth is the matter!" — Morbioli, when I was down there at the Montagne d'Argent, work had begun on erecting a hospital and we had already reached the whitewashing. A convict who held me in great affection was one day perched atop his ladder, brushing the façade, when from a nearby window there crept the murmur of voices. It was the Commandant, the Head Warder, and a condemned man in conference together. The whitewasher distinctly heard my name pronounced and stood stock-still to listen. I shall tell you briefly: the Commandant was guaranteeing the convict his immediate freedom provided he could succeed in implicating me in some escape attempt, in such a manner as to give the sentinels occasion to shoot me dead. — Why do you tell me this? — To persuade you that in the registers, beside my name, a cross is marked, and that even if I were a man contemptible enough to draw you into a conspiracy with the intention of denouncing you... what good would it do me? For me there would be no reprieve in any case. Now listen...

I confided to him the secret workings of my mind, the circumstances and the persons upon which I founded my hopes; the tactics which would undoubtedly make us masters of the boat and of the fishermen; the stratagem which he himself, unwittingly, putting an end to my long torments, had inspired in me. This was precisely the part I meant to entrust to his loyalty and his intelligence.

He scratched his head, as was his habit in moments of anxiety; then he raised his face, and I read his wholehearted assent in eyes that were ablaze. There and then, I received a compliment of a flavour as purely *trasteverino* as could be; the strangest that has ever been addressed to me in all my long existence: "You're a devil! Blessed be the woman who bore you — and may you die... die cut down by a blade!"

With the assent of Morbioli, the first link of the chain was forged. The others were added one by one, slowly, but admirably.

On a cloudy night, troubled by the din of a fierce storm at sea, I slipped silently from my barracks and, armed with a chisel, crept along the rocks as far as the warehouses, a large timber structure founded in part upon piles. I tried and tried again, and managed at last to prise up one of the planking boards. I crawled inside, groping, nosing out the way like a rat, and when I emerged I had upon my back six great hammocks of stout canvas, which Morelli, the Roman tailor, found the means to sew up with perfect workmanship. We had our sail!

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-THIRD

I HAD scarcely thrown my canvas sack upon the mole of the Île du Salut when hope already flooded my breast, intense, overpowering.

The island, with a little exercise of the imagination, presented the shape of a figure 8. Upon the larger loop, which was also the more elevated, stood the dwellings, the hospital, a church; upon the smaller loop were the workshops, and along its outermost curve, the barracks. From that extremity, directed more or less exactly to the north-west, one could toss a stone by hand onto the long islet of the Diable, the same that afterwards became sadly notorious for the murky drama of which Captain Dreyfus was the victim. At low tide, it was no great difficulty, leaping upon the jagged rocks left exposed, to pass from one island to the other dry-shod.

To the east, there rose from the waters another small islet, called Saint-Joseph, an uneven, nearly circular expanse of ground, scattered with a few trees, stunted and dwarfish.

The island upon which I found myself had, measured by the eye and at its widest diameter, an extent of some two miles. Its shores were rocky and all indentations.

These were circumstances I had noted in passing. What knew how to draw my attention and to fix it so as to absorb me in deep reflection was a small rowing-boat, gliding over the waves at a short distance from the shore. She had on board four or five convicts, who were rowing and singing. I thought to myself: "What prevents these men from making for the open sea? True, one does not cross the Ocean by the strength of oars. But why do they not procure themselves a sail? I left a canoe in the waters of the Oyapock, and here I find another at the Île du Salut. Courage, di Rudio — you have lost nothing!"

I was still standing motionless, my eye fixed upon that nutshell of a boat, when I felt a touch upon my shoulder. I turned with a start and found myself face to face with a man wearing the grotesque uniform of the convict.

I scrutinised him, and he broke the silence:

— I have been told you are Italian...

— And who are you? By your accent you seem to me a Roman of Rome.

— I am from Trastevere, and proud of it!

— Why are you here?

— That is quite another matter. But may I never see my Country again, and may I die with the noose about my throat, if I would not do it all over again, should the need arise: a French dog seduced my sister and left her in trouble... I put a hole through his heart. Long live the Trastevere blood, and to the devil with all who speak ill of it! For this I lost my Rome... ten little years on my back!... but they will pass. My name is Morbioli.

We exchanged, so to speak, our visiting cards; then, all at once: — How long have you been here?

— Seven years!

— Seven years!... you are Italian, Roman, Trasteveran, and you have never attempted to escape?

— Impossible! Alone, one cannot even think of it. In company, worse than going out at night! In this accursed place, to the cowards who turn informer, not bullets but a pardon! Do you follow me? To denounce a plot leads to freedom more surely than attempting flight.

— I believed there was more solidarity among those who have fallen than among the fortunate of this world.

— Even so! It is plain you are a newcomer to this place and have no exact notion of the difficulties. Ah, do you think there have been no bold fellows with the stomach to risk it? But to what end? Listen!...

And he told me how, thirteen years before, thirteen convicts from the Île Mère, another French penal establishment situated in those parts, had seized a pilot-boat and made for the open sea. The *Abeille* gave chase, but in vain. Some three months passed, and already it was thought they had reached free land, when, on one of her crossings from Cayenne to the Île du Salut, the *Abeille* sighted a boat drifting with the current. She came alongside — and whom did she find aboard?

— Three men only. The three had devoured the other ten.

An attempt at escape had also been made overland, from the Montagne d'Argent. There were twenty of them, and once they had penetrated into the forest no one troubled himself further to pursue them. After several months, one man, gone wild, made an unexpected appearance at the penal settlement. He was the sole survivor... he confessed he had kept himself alive until then because he had, more or less thoroughly, stripped the flesh from the other nineteen. Having now nothing left upon which to set his teeth, he resolved to give himself up. They ruled that he had eaten quite enough, and... cut off his head!

An escape had also been attempted from the islet of the Diable. The son of Giacomo Pianori, forerunner of Felice Orsini in the attempt and in martyrdom, had been interned there as a suspect. He had noticed that the tide cast up upon the shore numerous tree trunks. He gathered them together and succeeded in putting together a raft. The sinuosities of the shore had lent themselves to the secret labour. He set out by night, with twenty companions. On the high seas he was overtaken by a squall. The waves broke the raft in two. One half drifted for a long time with the currents, until it ran aground down there, somewhere in Dutch Guiana. The fugitives were succoured, and... handed back to the French authorities. Chained in pits, exposed to the torrid sun and to torrential rains, they soon breathed their last amid their sufferings. Twelve corpses flung to the sharks!

The other eight, Pianori among them, also reached the Continent upon the other half of the raft; but unhappily they ran into a *vase*. Do you know what a *vase* is? A bank of bluish mud that grips like cement. The air is full of *mosquitos* capable of stripping the carcass of a whale to the bone within a few days; the ground swarms with crabs... the terrible crabs of Guiana that dig long galleries underground, invade the cemeteries, and thanks to their good fortune the corpses sleep heavily. The *Abeille*, in passage through those parts, found the raft and what little remained of Pianori and the other wretches. The crabs and the insects had not even had the courtesy to wait until they were dead, and had straightway made a feast of them!

I had listened without interrupting. Meanwhile the nutshell had drawn nearer to the island and, skirting its shore, disappeared from time to time behind some rock, only to reappear always closer. The convicts were gathering on the beach and exchanging shouts with the oarsmen. I asked: What is that boat, and what are those men about?

— They fish on behalf of the penitentiary, and when fish is plentiful they sell some, more or less covertly, to whoever wishes to buy.

He scratched his head and added: — Morbioli is no empty gourd. You are thinking that one might take advantage of that boat to try something new. Count your reckoning without our four gunboats. They would merrily take aim at you as at game. They would laugh to see you go under. And then there is the *Abeille*: a mere canoe is no match for her!

I was assigned to the anchor workshop. I worked half naked... a heat that shrivelled the skin; sparks flying from the anvil speckled my body all over. But never, as in those moments, did I understand the power of the intellect in opposing itself to painful sensations and in mastering them. The obstinate meditation upon a plan of escape transported me elsewhere, among the island's rocks, out upon the open sea, and the impressions of the workshop reached me as if enfeebled by distance. My plan was taking shape from the very history of my predecessors' failures, and it seemed to me that its chain of reasoning issued from my mind more rigid and more resistant than the iron I was hammering.

Pianori had run headlong into his dreadful fate by trusting to a raft... therefore, no raft! And why, moreover, should I undertake the slow and thankless labour of building one, when I had at hand a small vessel lacking only a scrap of sail? I reckoned that seizing her by a bold stroke need not prove an excessively difficult enterprise for men of resolute temper, all the more so since the fishermen would doubtless have resigned themselves with good grace to a piece of high-handedness that would have restored them to liberty.

True, being upon a boat had profited the other fugitives not at all; but evidently they had not known how to handle one, and they lacked provisions... it was therefore necessary to secure the complicity of some man of the sea; it was necessary to accumulate a great store of victuals.

Where? If the low-lying Île du Diable had offered a safe hiding-place to a raft, the high, broken shore of the Île du Salut might well offer one equally safe for a few sacks of bread, for a few demijohns of water and of rum.

The *Abeille*! She made regular crossings from the island to Cayenne: one must put to sea during her absence.

And the gunboats? There lay the problem! Every time I set myself to solve it my brain became barren... barren as a wrung-out sponge!
