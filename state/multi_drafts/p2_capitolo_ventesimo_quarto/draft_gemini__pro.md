TWENTY-FOURTH CHAPTER

IN my hours of rest, I neglected nothing to obtain ample information about the men who lived and suffered around me. Antonio Gomez, after having been assigned for some time to the service of the Officers, had recovered, though bronzed by the sun, his good-natured priest's face. But an unexpected order removed him from all my influence. He was sent, with others, to the penitentiary of Corù, on the Continent, and I barely had time to shake his hand. From then on, although I have faith that the political upheavals in France procured better days for him, I never saw him again and heard no more of him.

On the island, however, were other Italians, a dozen perhaps; Romans for the most part, all condemned for having redoubled their political hatred by superimposing upon it their private hatred; for having avenged, with our native arrogance, the shame inflicted upon them by foreign arrogance. Christ pardoned much—it is said—in one who loved much; he ought to pardon much more in one who has hated much and, in hatred, knew how to find the virtue of not being a sheep. Among these Romans was one Morelli, a tailor, condemned for life. Who lends a readier ear to hope than he who has lost all of it?

I also found Italians condemned, or simply relegated, for political reasons. They dwelled, in part, on the Devil's Island; but they frequently crossed over, at low tide, on the pretext, for the most part, of needing the pharmacy attached to the Hospital. There was Galli, there was Rossini, there was Tebaldi, and we soon struck up a fraternal familiarity. Their story was known to me, and I could be certain that, even if they did not participate in the escape, they would have aided it with all their means.

Among the men I had met during my first crossing from Tolone and who had been disembarked, since then, on the island, was a French archpriest, condemned for life for the murder of a child whom he had abused. During the entire voyage, he had jealously guarded, under his arm, a large Bible of his. In a moment of expansiveness, he confided in me, his voice trembling, that he had hidden, in the binding of that volume, something like twenty thousand francs, and the tormenting thought of being strangled by hands greedy for his money gnawed at him more than remorse. He intended to make use of that money to procure the means to escape. I remembered this and counted upon it. To save the indispensable provisions from our limited daily ration required a sacrifice too grave and too continuous for our hunger and for my impatience. It was necessary to purchase them from the penitentiary's storehouses gradually, so as not to arouse suspicion, paying usurious prices if need be. Why would the archpriest not furnish the wherewithal?

But my good fortune willed it that, on the island, there was another individual who could prove far more useful to me. He was a former officer of the French navy, a rabid Orleanist, a man all daring and all nerves; he was serving a life sentence, another of the circumstances that I deemed favorable and I would say almost fortunate. That man had already dealt with storms; the Ocean held no terrors for him; for him it was not a boundless plain without paths.

All these thoughts, spokes of a disjointed wheel, remained for a long time motionless and as if crystallized in my mind, until suddenly, they received an extraordinary impulse from the almost spontaneous formation of the piece I was missing; which in vain, in my long meditations, I had tried to devise.

One evening, having left the workshop, I was walking towards my barracks, which was a good mile distant, when, having arrived near the pier, I caught sight of Morbioli bent over a gunboat, whose brass fittings he was polishing with complete placidity. The gunboats were, in reality, modest longboats carrying, at the stern and at the bow, a little toy cannon. They were lowered into the waves early in the morning and lifted out of the water at five in the evening.

Morbioli, a boatman in Roma, was assigned to their cleaning. How many times had I seen him bustling about those baubles that were my nightmare! But only now, through a singular disposition of my spirit, did his soft rubbing, which seemed to me like a caress, arouse a great vexation, and I cried within myself: "Smash them, you big oaf; sink them, if you can!"

That simple word "sink them!" flashed through my mind like a bolt of lightning that illuminates a dark night. It was a complete revelation, and I shuddered from it.

I approached Morbioli, and I believe my face revealed my secret start, because he looked at me with evident unease and said: "What's amiss!"
— Morbioli, when I was down there at Montagne d'Argent, they set about erecting a hospital, and we had already reached the whitewashing stage. A convict, who held me in great sympathy, was one day at the top of his ladder and was painting the facade when, from a nearby window, came a whisper of voices. It was the Commandant, the Head Guard, and a condemned man who were confabulating. The whitewasher distinctly heard my name pronounced and stood rigid to listen. I will tell you in brief: the Commandant was guaranteeing the condemned man immediate freedom, provided he succeeded in involving me in some escape attempt, so that the sentinels would have the opportunity to gun me down.
— Why do you tell me this?
— To persuade you that in the registers, next to my name, a cross is marked, and that, even if I were a man so despicable as to draw you into a plot with the intention of denouncing you... what good would it do me? For me, in any case, there would be no pardon. And now listen...

I confided to him the secret workings of my mind, the circumstances and the people on whom I founded my hopes; the tactic that would undoubtedly make us masters of the dinghy and the fishermen; the stratagem that he, unknowingly, putting an end to my long torments, had inspired in me. This was precisely the part that I would entrust to his loyalty and his intelligence.

He scratched his head, as was his habit in moments of anxiety; then he raised his face, and I read his full agreement in his sparkling eyes. Right then and there, I received a compliment of a purely Trasteverine flavor; the strangest that has ever been addressed to me during my long existence: "You are a devil! Blessed be the woman who bore you, and may you d—... die murdered!"

With Morbioli's consent, the first link in the chain was forged. The others were forged one by one, slowly, but admirably.

One cloudy night, disturbed by the roar of a fierce sea storm, I slipped silently from my barracks and, armed with a chisel, I crept along the rocks to beneath the storehouses, a large wooden construction founded, in part, on stilts. I try, I try again, and I manage to lift a plank of the floorboards. I penetrated inside, groping and sniffing my way like a rat, and when I came out, I had on my back six large hammocks of very resistant canvas, which Morelli, the Roman tailor, found a way to sew together according to the perfect rules of the art.
