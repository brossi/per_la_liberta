HISTORIANS assert that the spy Felicità Buanvecchiato, having overheard a fragment of a conversation held by Pietro Fortunato Calvi, hastened to reveal our attempt to the Authorities, who thus had full knowledge of it even before we made our move to put it into effect.

To what extent such a betrayal went, I know not; but I do know that to bring about our ruin it was almost superfluous.

How I came from Londra to Belluno without trouble and without hindrance, I have already narrated. Others, having set out from different points, but with the identical objective, did not have equal fortune.

Foremost among the merits of a conspirator is that of knowing how to judge, at a glance, which papers he must keep and which destroy... a simple art, and yet so difficult. Ignorance of it has caused the collapse of infinite enterprises that would have modified History; it has provided the executioner with innumerable victims.

Francesco Chinaglia and a certain Cerruti, both from Lonato, both of our party, crossed the Alps in all safety and arrived at a small village, on the border of Tirolo and Cadore. Here they committed the fatal blunder of raising their elbows too much and picking a fight with the innkeeper. They were arrested, taken to the gendarmerie, and searched. What is this? An itinerary marked with initials and with annotations that aroused some suspicion. The Police were put on the alert and detained them, and arrested, at brief intervals, Luigi Moratti from Castiglione delle Stiviere, a Roberto Morin, and a Fontana. They either had the identical compromising itinerary in their pockets, or they could not give a satisfactory account of themselves. The official report states that these men were arrested at the same time as Calvi, who passed through those parts on the 17th of September. When the latter was interrogated, he identified himself as a certain Meyer, a subject of the Duchy of Baden, and showed a corresponding passport; but it was of no avail. He was taken to the Police and searched, like the others... from the lining of his overcoat was drawn forth, signed by Mazzini, Kossuth, and Ledru Rollin, his commission as general-in-chief of the Italian revolution. It was Calvi himself who, out of an overly modest conception of his own influence, had insisted on obtaining that document. A formality as vain as it was fatal. Who would have contested the first place, or denied obedience to the modern Leonidas, in that Cadore which was his Thermopylae?

He was taken to Mantova. The grim walls of that fortress had already swallowed don Bastiano Barozzi, my father, and my sister Luisa. Poor Luisa!... her indomitable instinct for freedom confined within a veritable sepulchre; her delicate constitution tortured by the iron, inhuman discipline; her sensitive heart rent by the thought of her father's sufferings and by the unheard-of miseries that surrounded her; her young girl's modesty violated at every hour by the boorish searches of the turnkeys, insolent and ferocious... not a word of comfort, not love, not light!

Did she receive compensation for it? Margherita di Savoja, having become Queen of Italy, was pleased to pay a visit to the women who, for political reasons, had tasted the delights of Mantova. She haughtily forgot Luisa of the Counts of Rudio. From her point of view, she was right. Our family fought and suffered, in those dark times, so that Italy might exist; but it had not fought and suffered to round out the domains of the House of Savoy.

The castle of San Giorgio, at Mantova, was by now what the Spielberg had been in the time of Ferdinando. Other fortresses suppressed, in their dungeons, the beating of hearts that yearned for the redemption of the Fatherland; but in none, as at Mantova, did the ferocity of the oppressor manifest itself in tragedies so vast, so sinister, so persistent. And yet, fatally, from that torment of bodies the Italian soul shone forth. In their fearful caution, the Culozes, the Krauses, the Benedecks might well try to hide from the civilized world the identity of the imprisoned and the terrible barbarity of their procedural methods. There are virtues that cannot be hidden, that spread their own light beyond the walls of any Bastille; revealing, by their own excellence, the full abjection of the ephemeral superiority that has attempted to break them.

Italy saw, trembling, in the small space of those secret cells, pass by without official robes, almost in rags, but no less pure than the ancient one, the iron temper of her Attilio Regolos, of her Thrasea Paetus; the soul of more than one Socrates, no less serene than the ancient one... and it was no small impulse for her to find herself again. A period of history sweating martyrdom, it found shrewd researchers who coordinated its pages, who documented, with meticulous care, its every phase... but not its Tacitus to make it eternal in prose of granite, to draw from it an austere monument, worthy of such greatness. And let him arise!... on that day the thousands upon thousands of statues that Italy lavishes upon her crossroads will seem like the toys of pygmies.

When Calvi was admitted to the Castle of San Giorgio, don Giovanni Grioli, Pietro Frattini, Angelo Scarsellini, Count Carlo Montanari, Carlo Poma, Giovanni Zambelli, don Enrico Tazzoli, Tito Speri, the hero of the ten days of Brescia, giants of duty, a select contingent from every social class, had already submitted, under the shadow of the noose, to the extreme insult of despotism.

A designated victim, Felice Orsini was instead buried there some months later, precisely on 28 March 1855, who was later, by sawing through the double bars of a window and lowering himself with his bedsheets and crippling a foot, to regain his freedom and have so much influence on my destiny.

Felice Orsini was, for a certain period, shut in cell No. 9, where my father was with several other patriots. He would tell me, in 1857 in Londra, that the good old man, breaking prison discipline through the kind condescension of the guards, helped his fellow captives to bear the bitterness of the long imprisonment by gathering them around him and humming the romances then most in vogue.

Felice Orsini also had the opportunity to correspond, by tapping on the wall, with Pietro Fortunato Calvi. They encouraged one another, wishing they might soon go out, free among the free. But the heroic defender of Cadore held no illusions. He knew too well the systems of government dear to Austria and, from the same wretched end of the ten martyrs who had preceded him therein, he inferred his own inexorable condemnation.

In the long, tedious trial, he showed the imperturbable calm of a man accustomed, on the fields of battle, to looking death in the face, and only kindled with noble indignation when his judges, having brought him face to face with an abject informer, were not ashamed to prefer the latter's purchased word to his own as a loyal enemy, as a man of honour.

His participation in the movements intended to overthrow, by violence, a tyranny that had imposed itself by violence, he admitted without reticence; the comrades who were arrested with him, he defended to the last, asserting that they had joined him to take part in an alpine excursion, knowing nothing of his subversive projects; the secret of the other conspirators' complicity remained jealously guarded within his adamantine breast, and my father and Luisa and don Bastiano Barozzi, long detained on simple suspicion, later went free by virtue of a decree of amnesty.

And the sad prediction of Pietro Fortunato Calvi came true. By the sentence of 17 January 1855, confirmed in Vienna the following June, he was condemned to die by the noose. At thirty-eight years of age! For an enterprise that was scarcely conceived, much less initiated. So anxious was Austria to suppress the men who, to thought, would have known how to couple action and become agitators and leaders of other men!

In his last days, he desired a meeting with his companions in misfortune and, rather than asking them for comfort, he took pains to comfort them. He wrote most affectionate letters to his brother Luigi and to his father who, a faithful servant of Austria, disapproved, as madness and worse, of his impetuous patriotism, his irrepressible need for action. And this thought cost him the only tear that History records of him.

And he wrote a letter to Teresa Duodo of Padova, the young girl whose kisses he promised himself as an ineffable prize, once the Fatherland was redeemed. Which young girl, broken by the tragic parting, lived in voluntary, perpetual widowhood.

Austria had devised, in those times, a new kind of piety... to grant some grace, provided the condemned man requested it in humiliating terms and as a repentant sinner. If the pardon was not asked for, she could say: "The charge of cruelty, which is levelled at me from every part of the world, is unjust. I was disposed to forgive, but he would not have it." But if the request for pardon did reach her, the very terms in which it was drawn up stripped all prestige from the unwary patriot... to live physiologically, but at the price of one's own moral suicide.

It was asserted, in my time, that Calvi was begged to solicit that strange clemency. He is said to have scornfully replied: "My hatred for Austria is more intense than my love for life. I will ask for nothing and I want nothing from the oppressors of my Country!" If this is a legend, how well it harmonizes with that strong and upright character!

But what is certainly not legend is the dignified attitude with which he faced death. He moved towards the scaffold as towards a goal foreseen from the very beginning of his heroic struggle with the oppressor, and it seemed that, accustomed for long years to meditating on horrors, his soul had become refractory to them.

Having passed the bridge of San Giorgio, he leaned his head out of the funeral carriage and contemplated, with a sad gaze, the city and the countryside. I firmly believe that he, a lover of "the truer and the greater," lamented, at that moment, that he had not been able to realize the yearning of his life. On those lands he did not see raised the holy colours of redemption; he did not see, scattered through the cultivated fields and joyfully occupied, free men.

He drew back and returned to the profound meditations of the life beyond the grave which he, despite the residue left in his soul by sinisterly reactionary schools, had always pictured as Hellenically serene.

Having reached the foot of the scaffold, he took the cigar from his mouth and gave it, lit, to one of his guards. He repulsed with scornful roughness the arm of one who wished to help him ascend the plank laid beneath the column from which he was to hang. From up there he looked, smiling, at the Austrian officers, several of whom had taken off their hats as a sign of respect. And having repeatedly kissed Monsignor Martini, a priest in disfavour, for his honesty, where prebends are dispensed, he said in a firm voice: "I am ready!" Then the executioner set about his work.

To crown the misfortune, the gallows did not correspond to Calvi's tall stature, and his feet tickled the earth. His agony was long and extremely painful. Edoardo Winkler, commander of the military escort, was fiercely indignant at this and could not contain himself. He cursed the executioner and, when the execution was completed, he departed, almost fleeing, running his hands through his hair.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi was the last of the great men murdered for the Italian cause. He remained hanging for several hours; taken down in the evening, he was thrown into a ditch dug at the foot of the scaffold.

But his greatness moved even a hyena, the infamous warden Francesco Casati, who composed his remains in a coffin and preserved his relics for the veneration of the risen Italians.

And the virtue, which the Mantovan Bastille had not been able to conceal, seemed to shine forth from that humble grave, dug at the foot of a scaffold. His moral influence was so great, so intense the treasure of latent energies that it aroused, as to trouble the oppressors. Marshal Radetzky, already decrepit, already on the point of abandoning forever the odious contests of the world, felt no shame in urging the Special Tribunal that had condemned Calvi to draw up a defamation, to paralyze the "deleterious" influence of that shade.

He was to find in President Visentin the vile soul disposed to second him. How many official lucubrations, which yet serve as a guide to exalt or to vilify the individuals belonging to history, repeat a similar origin and have no more value than that of Visentin!

The sycophancy that blossomed after the hour of triumph, and busied itself with the jealous care of attributing all the merit for the redemption of Italy to the great and small who knew how to exploit it, passes with the lightness of a glider over the beneficent influence exercised, beyond the grave, by the martyrs who sacrificed their existence to rouse the slothful. It is an influence that can be intuited, but which escapes all calculation... so much the easier it becomes to adulterate the truth!

Nevertheless Calvi was fortunate in this: the spite of the partisan cliques, which spared no adversary, however great and unsullied, seemed to be blunted before this figure so disinterested, so calm in his audacity.
