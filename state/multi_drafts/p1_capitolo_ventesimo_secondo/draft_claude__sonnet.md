CHAPTER TWENTY-TWO

HISTORIANS affirm that the spy Felicità Buanvecchiato, having caught some fragment of a conversation held by Pietro Fortunato Calvi, hastened to reveal our attempt to the Authorities, who thus had full wind of it even before we had taken the first steps to carry it into effect.

How far that denunciation extended I do not know; but I do know that in bringing about our ruin it was well-nigh superfluous.

How I reached Belluno from London without molestation or mishap, I have already told. Others, who set out from different points but with the identical objective, were not so fortunate.

The very first among the virtues of a conspirator is that of knowing, at a glance, which papers he ought to keep and which to destroy — an art at once simple and yet so difficult to master. Ignorance of it has brought infinite enterprises crashing to ruin, enterprises that would have altered the course of History; it has furnished the executioner with countless victims.

Francesco Chinaglia and a certain Cerruti, both from Lonato, both of our number, cross the Alps in perfect safety and arrive at a small village on the border of the Tyrol and Cadore. Here they commit the fatal indiscretion of lifting their elbows excessively and picking a quarrel with the innkeeper. They are arrested, conducted to the gendarmerie, and searched. What is this? An itinerary marked with initials and annotations that arouse some suspicion. The Police take notice and detain them, and presently arrest, at brief intervals, Luigi Moratti of Castiglione delle Stiviere, one Roberto Morin, and a Fontana. Either they carry in their pockets the same compromising itinerary, or they cannot give a satisfactory account of themselves. The official report asserts that these men were arrested at the same time as Calvi, who passed through those parts on the 17th of September. When the latter was interrogated, he gave himself out as a certain Meyer, a subject of the Duchy of Baden, and produced a corresponding passport; but it availed him nothing. He was taken to the Police headquarters and searched, like the others — and from the lining of his overcoat was drawn, bearing the signatures of Mazzini, Kossuth, and Ledru Rollin, his commission as commander-in-chief of the Italian revolution. It was Calvi himself who, out of an excessively modest estimate of his own influence, had insisted upon obtaining that document. A formality as vain as it was fatal. Who would have disputed the first place, denied obedience to the modern Leonidas, in that Cadore which was to have been his Thermopylae?

He was conveyed to Mantova. The grim walls of that fortress had already swallowed up don Bastiano Barozzi, his father, and his sister Luisa. Poor Luisa! — her indomitable impulse towards life forced within a veritable tomb; her delicate constitution tortured by the iron, inhuman discipline; her sensitive heart torn by the thought of her father's sufferings and by the unheard-of miseries that surrounded her; the modesty of her girlhood violated at every hour by the coarse searches of the warders, insolent and brutal — not a word of comfort, no love, no light!

Was she rewarded for it? Margherita di Savoia, having become Queen of Italy, was pleased to pay a visit to the women who had, for political reasons, savoured the delights of Mantova. She haughtily forgot Luisa of the Counts of Rudio. From her point of view, she was not wrong. Our family had fought and suffered in the dark times so that Italy might exist; but it had not fought and suffered in order to round out the dominions of the House of Savoia.

The Castello di San Giorgio at Mantova had become, by then, what the Spielberg had been in the days of Ferdinando. Other fortresses compressed, in their dungeons, the pulsings of hearts that yearned for the redemption of the Fatherland; but in none, as at Mantova, did the ferocity of the oppressor manifest itself in tragedies so vast, so sinister, so unrelenting. Yet, fatally, from that torment of bodies the Italian spirit blazed forth. In their fearful prudence, the Culoz, the Kraus, the Benedeck did their utmost to conceal from the civilised world the identity of the imprisoned and the terrible barbarity of their methods of procedure. But there are virtues that cannot be hidden, that shed their light beyond the walls of any Bastille; revealing, by their very excellence, the full abjection of the ephemeral supremacy that has sought to crush them.

Italy saw, trembling, passing through the narrow compass of those dungeons without pomp, in rags almost, yet no less bright than of old, the iron temper of her Attilio Regolo, of her Trasea Peto; no less serene than in the ancient world, the soul of more than one Socrate — and it was no small spur towards the finding of herself again. A period of history sweating martyrdom, it found keen researchers who co-ordinated its pages, who documented, with meticulous care, its every phase — but not yet its own Tacitus to immortalise it in prose of granite, to draw from it an austere monument worthy of such greatness. Let him arise! — on that day the thousand and thousand statues that Italy lavishes upon her crossroads will seem the toys of pygmies.

When Calvi was thrust into the Castello di San Giorgio, don Giovanni Grioli, Pietro Frattini, Angelo Scarsellini, Count Carlo Montanari, Carlo Poma, Giovanni Zambelli, don Enrico Tazzoli, Tito Speri, the hero of the ten days of Brescia — giants of duty, a chosen contingent from every class of society — had already endured, beneath the shadow of the noose, the utmost insult of despotism.

A victim already marked out, there was instead immured, some months later — precisely on the 28th of March 1855 — Felice Orsini, who was afterwards, by sawing through the double bars of a window and lowering himself on knotted sheets and laming a foot, to regain his liberty and exercise so great an influence upon my own fate.

Felice Orsini was, for a certain period, confined in Cell No. 9, where my father was held together with several other patriots. He told me, in 1857 in London, that the good old man, breaking, by the kindly indulgence of the warders, the prison discipline, used to help his fellow captives to bear the bitterness of their long imprisonment by gathering them about him and humming the romances most in fashion at the time.

Felice Orsini also had the opportunity to correspond, by tapping on the wall, with Pietro Fortunato Calvi. They encouraged one another by turns, expressing the hope that they might soon go forth, free among free men. But the heroic defender of Cadore harboured no illusion whatsoever. He knew too thoroughly the methods of governance dear to Austria and, from the same wretched fate of the ten martyrs who had preceded him within those walls, he deduced his own inexorable condemnation.

Throughout the long, tedious trial, he displayed the unperturbable calm of a man accustomed, upon the field of battle, to look death in the face, and only kindled with noble indignation when his judges, confronting him with a base informer, did not blush to prefer the purchased word of that creature to his own — the word of an honourable enemy, a man of honour.

His participation in the movements aimed at overthrowing, by force, a tyranny that had imposed itself by force, he admitted without reserve; the companions who had been arrested with him he defended to the last, declaring that they had joined him to take part in an alpine excursion, with no knowledge whatever of his subversive designs; the secret of the complicity of the other conspirators remained jealously guarded within his adamantine breast, and my father and Luisa and don Bastiano Barozzi, held long under bare suspicion, eventually came out into the open by virtue of a decree of amnesty.

And the sad foreboding of Pietro Fortunato Calvi came true. By sentence of the 17th of January 1855, confirmed at Vienna in the following June, he was condemned to die by the noose. At thirty-eight years of age! for an enterprise more conceived than barely begun. So anxious did Austria show herself to suppress the men who, to the power of thought, would have been able to join action and become agitators and leaders of other men!

In his last days, he desired a meeting with his companions in misfortune, and, more than seeking comfort from them, set himself to comfort them. He wrote most affectionate letters to his brother Luigi and to his father who, a faithful servant of Austria, condemned as madness and worse that headlong patriotism of his, that irrepressible need for action. And this thought cost him the only tear that History records of him.

And he wrote a letter to Teresa Duodo of Padova, the young woman whose kisses he had promised himself as the ineffable reward, once the Fatherland was redeemed. That same young woman, broken by the tragic bereavement, lived on in voluntary and perpetual widowhood.

Austria had devised, in those times, a new species of mercy — to grant some clemency, provided the condemned man requested it in humiliating terms and as a repentant sinner. If the clemency were not sought, she could say: "The charge of cruelty flung at me from every corner of the world is unjust. I was prepared to pardon, but it was not wished." Whereas if the petition for clemency reached her, the very terms in which it was couched stripped all prestige from the imprudent patriot — to live physiologically, but at the price of one's own moral suicide.

It was affirmed, in my time, that Calvi was urged to solicit that singular clemency. He would have answered with disdain: "My hatred for Austria is more intense than my love of life. I shall ask nothing and I want nothing from the oppressors of my Country!" If this be legend, how well it harmonises with that strong and upright temper!

But legend it certainly is not that the dignified bearing with which he faced death. He moved towards the gallows as towards a destination foreseen from the very outset of his heroic struggle with the oppressor, and it seemed that, having meditated long years upon its horrors, his spirit had grown impervious to them.

Having crossed the Ponte di San Giorgio, he thrust his head from the funeral carriage and gazed, with a sorrowful eye, upon the city and the countryside. I am firmly persuaded that he, who loved what was truest and greatest, mourned at that moment his inability to have realised the longing of his life. Over those lands he saw no sacred colours of redemption raised aloft; he saw no free men scattered across the fields, cheerfully at their labours.

He drew back and returned to the deep meditations upon what lies beyond the grave — a life which he, despite the dark residue left in his spirit by schools of the most sinisterly reactionary cast, had always conceived in the Hellenic manner, serene.

Having arrived at the foot of the gallows, he took the cigar from his lips and gave it, still lit, to one of his guards. He thrust aside with scornful roughness the arm of one who sought to help him mount the plank set beneath the post from which he was to hang. From that height he looked down, smiling, upon the Austrian officers, several of whom had removed their caps in a sign of respect. And having kissed monsignor Martini repeatedly — a priest held in disfavour, by reason of his honesty, in those places where benefices are dispensed — he said in a firm voice: "I am ready!" Thereupon the executioner set about his work.

To crown the misfortune, the gallows was not proportioned to the great height of Calvi, whose feet grazed the ground. His agony was long and exceedingly painful. Edoardo Winkler, commanding the military escort, was seized with fierce revulsion and could not contain himself. He cursed the executioner and, once the execution was complete, went away, almost at a run, his hands thrust into his hair.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi was the last of the great men slain for the Italian cause. He remained hanging for several hours; lowered at evening, he was cast into a pit dug at the foot of the gallows.

But his greatness moved even a hyena — the notorious warder Francesco Casati — who composed his remains in a coffin and kept his relics for the veneration of resurgent Italians.

And the virtue that the Mantuan Bastille had been unable to conceal seemed to blaze forth from that humble grave, dug at the foot of a scaffold. His moral influence was so great, so intense the treasury of latent energies that it stirred to life, as to disquiet the oppressors. The Marshal Radetzky, already decrepit, already on the point of abandoning for ever the hateful contests of the world, did not scruple to press the Special Tribunal that had condemned Calvi to draw up a slander, in order to paralyse the "deleterious" influence of that shade.

He was to find in the President Visentin the base soul ready to second him. How many official elaborations, which nonetheless serve as a guide for exalting or vilifying the individuals who belong to history, repeat an analogous origin and have no greater worth than that of Visentin's!

The sycophancy that blossomed in the hour of triumph, busily engaged in jealously attributing all merit for the redemption of Italy to the great and the small who knew how to profit by it, glides with the lightness of a wing over the beneficent influence exercised, beyond the grave, by the martyrs who sacrificed their existence to rouse the sluggish. It is an influence that may be intuited, but that eludes all calculation — all the easier, therefore, to adulterate the truth!

Yet Calvi was fortunate in this: the spite of the cliques and factions, which spared no adversary, however great and unblemished, seemed to blunt itself before this figure so disinterested, so calm in its audacity. And it was said that Italy "would have had in the hero of Cadore a second Garibaldi." But the opportunity of becoming one was criminally wrested from him.
