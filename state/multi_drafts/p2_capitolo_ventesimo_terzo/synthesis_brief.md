AVEVO gettato appena il mio sacco di tela sul molo dell'isola del Saluto e già la speranza mi inondava il petto, intensa, prepotente.

L'isola, mettendo in giuoco un po' di imaginazione, presentava la forma di un 8. Sull'occhio maggiore, che era anche il più elevato, sorgevano le abitazioni, l'ospedale, una chiesa; sull'occhio più esiguo, erano le officine e, sulla sua curva estrema, le caserme. Da quell'estremità, rivolta più o meno esattamente a nordovest, si poteva gettar con mano un sasso sul lungo isolotto del Diavolo, lo stesso che divenne poi tristemente celebre pel losco dramma di cui fu vittima il capitano Dreyfus. A marea bassa, non riesciva difficile, saltando sugli scheggioni rimasti scoperti, il passare dall'una all'altra isola, a piedi asciutti.

Ad oriente, sorgeva dall'acque un altro isolotto, detto di San Giuseppe, pianura accidentata, quasi circolare, sparsa di pochi alberi, stentati e nani.

L'isola su cui mi trovavo aveva, misurata ad occhio e nel suo diametro maggiore, un'estensione di circa due miglia. Le sue spiaggie erano rocciose e tutte insenature.

Circostanze, queste, che io avevo rilevato alla sfuggita. Ciò che seppe attrarre la mia attenzione e fissarla così da concentrarmi in profonde riflessioni, era una piccola barca a remi, scorrente sull'onde, ad una breve distanza dalla spiaggia. Aveva a bordo quattro o cinque condannati, che vogavano cantando. Io pensavo: "Chi impedisce a costoro di pigliare il largo? Vero, non si compie la traversata dell'Oceano a forza di remi. Ma perchè non si procurano una vela? Ho lasciato un canotto nelle acque dell'Ojapok ed ecco che ne trovo un altro all'isola del Saluto. Coraggio di Rudio, tu non hai nulla perduto!"

Me ne stavo tuttora immobile, coll'occhio fisso su quel guscio di noce, quando mi sento toccare su una spalla. Mi volto di soprassalto e mi trovo di fronte un uomo che vestiva la grottesca uniforme del forzato.

Lo scrutai ed egli ruppe il silenzio:

— Mi hanno assicurato che sei Italiano...

— E tu chi sei? All'accento mi sembri Romano di Roma.

— Sono transteverino e me ne vanto!

— Perchè sei qui?

— Questo è un altro paio di maniche. Ma che io possa non veder più il mio Paese e morire col capestro alla gola se, occorrendo, non tornerei daccapo: Un cane francese mi ha sedotta la sorella e l'ha abbandonata nei pasticci... gli ho bucato il core. Viva il sangue transteverino e all'inferno chi ne dice corna! Per questo ho perduto la mia Roma... dieci annetti sul groppone!... ma passeranno. Mi chiamo Morbioli.

Gli scambiai, per così dire, la carta da visita: poi d'improvviso: Da quanto tempo sei qui?

— Sette anni!

— Sette anni!... sei Italiano, romano, transteverino e non hai tentato di fuggire?

— Impossibile! Da solo, neppure pensarci. In compagnia, peggio che andar di notte! In questo luogo maledetto, ai codardi che fanno la spia, non le schioppettate, la grazia! Mi capisci? Denunciare un complotto, conduce a libertà più certa che non il tentare la fuga.

— Credevo fosse più solidarietà tra gli entrati che non tra i felici del mondo.

— E quand'anche! Si vede che sei novellino del Paese e non ti fai un concetto esatto delle difficoltà. Ah credi che non vi siano state barbe col fegataccio sano da arrischiarvi si? Ma a qual prò? Ascolta!...

E mi narrò che, tredici anni prima, tredici forzati dell'isola Madre, altro penitenziario francese situato in quei paraggi, si erano impossessati di un battello-pilota ed avevano preso il largo. L'"Abeille" diede loro la caccia, ma inutilmente. Passarono circa tre mesi e già si pensava che avessero toccato terra libera quando, in una sua traversata da Cayenna all'isola del Saluto, l'"Abeille" avvistò un battello che andava alla deriva. Lo sopraggiunge e chi vi trovò?

— Tre soltanto. I tre avevano divorato gli altri dieci.

Si era tentata una fuga anche per la via di terra, dalla Montagne d'Argent. Erano in venti e, quando furono penetrati nella foresta, nessuno si diede più la briga di inseguirli. Dopo parecchi mesi, un uomo inselvatichito, fece inaspettata comparsa al penitenziario. Era l'unico superstite... confessò d'essere campato sino allora, perchè aveva, o tanto o quanto, spolpato gli altri diciannove. Non avendo ormai più nulla su cui mettere i denti, decise di costituirsi. Sentenziarono che aveva mangiato anche troppo e... gli tagliarono la testa!

Si era tentata una fuga anche dall'isolotto del Diavolo. Il figliuolo di Giacomo Pianori, precursore di Felice Orsini nell'attentato e nel martirio, vi era stato relegato come persona sospetta. Egli aveva notato che la marea gettava sulla spiaggia numerosi tronchi d'albero. Ne fece incetta e riescì a mettere assieme una zattera. Le sinuosità della spiaggia si erano prestate al segreto lavorio. Partì, di notte, con venti compagni. In alto mare, fu sorpreso da un turbine. I marosi spezzarono la zattera in due parti. L'una errò per lungo tempo colle correnti, finché diede in secco laggiù, in qualche parte della Guiana olandese. I fuggitivi furono soccorsi e... riconsegnati alle Autorità francesi. Incatenati nelle buche, esposti al torrido Sole e alle pioggie torrenziali, esalarono presto, tra i patimenti, l'ultimo respiro. Dodici cadaveri gettati ai pescicani!

Gli altri otto, fra cui il Pianori, raggiunsero pure, sull'altra parte della zattera, il Continente; ma disgraziatamente andarono a battere su una "vase". Sai che cos'è una "vase"? Un banco di terra azzurriccola che fa presa come il cemento. L'aria vi è piena di "mosquitos" capaci di denudare, in pochi giorni, la carcassa di una balena; la terra formicola di granchi... i terribili granchi della Guiana che scavano lunghe gallerie sottoterra, invadono i cimiteri e per loro ventura i cadaveri dormono della grossa. L'Abeille, in rotta per quelle parti, trovò la zattera e quel poco che restava del Pianori e degli altri disgraziati. I granchi e gli insetti non hanno neppure avuta la compiacenza d'aspettare che fossero morti e, certo, lì per lì, ne fecero un festino!

Avevo ascoltato senz'interrompere. Nel frattempo il guscio di noce si era avvicinato all'isola e, costeggiandola, spariva di quando in quando, dietro qualche scoglio, per ricomparire sempre più vicino. I forzati si raggruppavano sulla spiaggia e scambiavano voci coi rematori. Io domandai: Che cos'è quella barca e che cosa fanno quegli uomini?

— Pescano per conto del penitenziario e, quando il pesce è abbondante, ne vendono, più o meno velatamente, a chiunque ne voglia comprare.

Si grattò la testa e soggiunse: — Morbioli non è una zucca vuota. Tu pensi che si potrebbe approfittare di quella barca, per tentare qualche novità. Fai i conti senza le nostre quattro cannoniere. Si tirerebbe su te allegramente, come a caccia di selvaggina. Si riderebbe nel vederti andare a fondo. E poi c'è l'Abeille: un semplice canotto non ce la può fare!

Fui addetto all'officina dell'ancore. Lavoravo mezzo nudo... un calore che raggrinzava la pelle; le faville, scattando di sopra l'incudine, mi chiazzavano tutto il corpo. Ma non mai, come allora, compresi la potenza dell'intelletto nell'opporsi alle sensazioni dolorose e nel domarle. La meditazione ostinata di un piano di fuga, mi trasportava altrove, tra gli scogli dell'isola, sull'alto mare e le impressioni dell'officina mi giungevano come affievolite dalla lontananza. Il mio piano si andava svolgendo dalla storia stessa degli insuccessi de' miei predecessori e a me sembrava che la sua concatenazione mi uscisse dalla mente più rigida e più resistente del ferro che martellavo.

Pianori era corso incontro al suo orribile destino per aver fidato in una zattera... dunque, niente zattera! E perchè, d'altronde, assumerei la lenta improba fatica di costruirne una, quando avevo sottomano una navicella mancante solo di un cencio di vela? Io calcolavo che il riimossessarsene, con un colpo di mano, non dovesse riuscire impresa soverchiamente difficile ad uomini di tempra risoluta, tanto più che i pescatori si sarebbero indubbiamente rassegnati di buona grazia ad una prepotenza che li avrebbe restituiti alla libertà.

Vero è, l'essere su una nave non aveva punto giovato agli altri evasi; ma, evidentemente, non ne conoscevano il maneggio e difettavano di provvisioni... bisognava dunque accaparrarsi la complicità di qualche uomo di mare; bisognava accumulare grande quantità di vettovaglie.

Dove? Se la bassa isola del Diavolo aveva offerto un sicuro nascondiglio ad una zattera, la spiaggia alta, accidentata dell'isola del Saluto ben poteva offrirne uno sicuro a qualche sacco di pane, a qualche damigiana d'acqua e di rhum.

L'Abeille! Compiva traversate regolari dall'isola a Cayenna: bisognava pigliare il largo durante la sua assenza.

E le cannoniere? Ecco il problema! Ogni qualvolta mi proponevo di scioglierlo il mio cervello diventava arido... arido come una spugna spremuta!

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-THIRD

I HAD scarcely thrown my canvas pack upon the quay of the Île du Salut when hope already flooded my breast, intense, overpowering.

The island, with a little exercise of the imagination, presented the shape of a figure 8. Upon the larger loop, which was also the higher, stood the dwellings, the hospital, a church; upon the smaller loop were the workshops, and along its outermost curve, the barracks. From that extremity, directed more or less exactly to the north-west, one could toss a stone by hand onto the long islet of the Diable, the same that afterwards became sadly notorious as the scene of the murky drama of which Captain Dreyfus was the victim. At low tide, it was no great difficulty, leaping from one exposed crag to another, to pass from one island to the other with dry feet.

To the east, there rose from the waters another small islet, called Saint-Joseph, an uneven, nearly circular expanse of ground, scattered with a few trees, stunted and dwarfish.

The island upon which I found myself had, measured by the eye and at its widest diameter, an extent of some two miles. Its shores were rocky and all indentations.

These were circumstances I had noted in passing. What knew how to draw my attention and to fix it so as to absorb me in deep reflection was a small rowing-boat, gliding over the waves at a short distance from the shore. She had on board four or five convicts, who were rowing and singing. I thought to myself: "What prevents these men from making for the open sea? True, one does not cross the Ocean by the strength of oars. But why do they not procure themselves a sail? I left a canoe in the waters of the Oyapock, and here I find another at the Île du Salut. Courage, di Rudio — you have lost nothing!"

I was still standing motionless, my eye fixed upon that nutshell of a boat, when I felt a touch upon my shoulder. I turned with a start and found myself face to face with a man wearing the grotesque uniform of the convict.

I scrutinised him, and he broke the silence:

— I have been told you are Italian...

— And who are you? By your accent you seem to me a Roman of Rome.

— I am from Trastevere, and proud of it!

— Why are you here?

— That is quite another matter. But may I never see my Country again, and may I die with the noose about my throat, if I would not do it all over again, should the need arise: a French dog seduced my sister and left her in trouble... I put a hole through his heart. Long live the Trastevere blood, and to the devil with all who speak ill of it! For this I lost my Rome... ten little years on my back!... but they will pass. My name is Morbioli.

We exchanged, so to speak, our visiting cards; then, all at once: — How long have you been here?

— Seven years!

— Seven years!... you are Italian, Roman, Trasteveran, and you have never attempted to escape?

— Impossible! Alone, one cannot even think of it. In company, worse than going out at night! In this accursed place, to the cowards who turn informer, not bullets but a pardon! Do you follow me? To denounce a plot leads to freedom more surely than attempting flight.

— I believed there was more solidarity among those who have fallen than among the fortunate of this world.

— Even so! It is plain you are a newcomer to this place and have no exact notion of the difficulties. Ah, do you think there have been no bold fellows with the stomach to risk it? But to what end? Listen!...

And he told me how, thirteen years before, thirteen convicts from the Île Mère, another French penal establishment situated in those parts, had seized a pilot-boat and made for the open sea. The *Abeille* gave chase, but in vain. Some three months passed, and already it was thought they had reached free land, when, on one of her crossings from Cayenne to the Île du Salut, the *Abeille* sighted a boat drifting with the current. She came alongside — and whom did she find aboard?

— Three men only. The three had devoured the other ten.

An attempt at escape had also been made overland, from the Montagne d'Argent. There were twenty of them, and once they had penetrated into the forest no one troubled himself further to pursue them. After several months, one man, gone wild, made an unexpected appearance at the penal settlement. He was the sole survivor... he confessed he had kept himself alive until then because he had, more or less thoroughly, stripped the flesh from the other nineteen. Having now nothing left upon which to set his teeth, he resolved to give himself up. They ruled that he had eaten quite enough, and... cut off his head!

An escape had also been attempted from the islet of the Diable. The son of Giacomo Pianori, forerunner of Felice Orsini in the attempt and in martyrdom, had been interned there as a suspect. He had noticed that the tide cast up upon the shore numerous tree trunks. He gathered them together and succeeded in putting together a raft. The sinuosities of the shore had lent themselves to the secret labour. He set out by night, with twenty companions. On the high seas he was overtaken by a squall. The waves broke the raft in two. One half drifted for a long time with the currents, until it ran aground down there, somewhere in Dutch Guiana. The fugitives were succoured, and... handed back to the French authorities. Chained in pits, exposed to the torrid sun and to torrential rains, they soon breathed their last amid their sufferings. Twelve corpses flung to the sharks!

The other eight, Pianori among them, also reached the Continent upon the other half of the raft; but unhappily they ran into a *vase*. Do you know what a *vase* is? A bank of bluish mud that grips like cement. The air is full of *mosquitos* capable of stripping the carcass of a whale to the bone within a few days; the ground swarms with crabs... the terrible crabs of Guiana that dig long galleries underground, invade the cemeteries, and thanks to their good fortune the corpses sleep heavily. The *Abeille*, in passage through those parts, found the raft and what little remained of Pianori and the other wretches. The crabs and the insects had not even had the courtesy to wait until they were dead, and had straightway made a feast of them!

I had listened without interrupting. Meanwhile the nutshell had drawn nearer to the island and, skirting its shore, disappeared from time to time behind some rock, only to reappear always closer. The convicts were gathering on the beach and exchanging shouts with the oarsmen. I asked: What is that boat, and what are those men about?

— They fish on behalf of the penitentiary, and when fish is plentiful they sell some, more or less covertly, to whoever wishes to buy.

He scratched his head and added: — Morbioli is no empty gourd. You are thinking that one might take advantage of that boat to try something new. Count your reckoning without our four gunboats. They would merrily take aim at you as at game. They would laugh to see you go under. And then there is the *Abeille*: a mere canoe is no match for her!

I was assigned to the anchor workshop. I worked half naked... a heat that shrivelled the skin; sparks flying from the anvil speckled my body all over. But never, as in those moments, did I understand the power of the intellect in opposing itself to painful sensations and in mastering them. The obstinate meditation upon a plan of escape transported me elsewhere, among the island's rocks, out upon the open sea, and the impressions of the workshop reached me as if enfeebled by distance. My plan was taking shape from the very history of my predecessors' failures, and it seemed to me that its chain of reasoning issued from my mind more rigid and more resistant than the iron I was hammering.

Pianori had run headlong into his dreadful fate by trusting to a raft... therefore, no raft! And why, moreover, should I undertake the slow and thankless labour of building one, when I had at hand a small vessel lacking only a scrap of sail? I reckoned that seizing her by a bold stroke need not prove an excessively difficult enterprise for men of resolute temper, all the more so since the fishermen would doubtless have resigned themselves with good grace to a piece of high-handedness that would have restored them to liberty.

True, being upon a boat had profited the other fugitives not at all; but evidently they had not known how to handle one, and they lacked provisions... it was therefore necessary to secure the complicity of some man of the sea; it was necessary to accumulate a great store of victuals.

Where? If the low-lying Île du Diable had offered a safe hiding-place to a raft, the high, broken shore of the Île du Salut might well offer one equally safe for a few sacks of bread, for a few carboys of water and of rum.

The *Abeille*! She made regular crossings from the island to Cayenne: one must put to sea during her absence.

And the gunboats? There lay the problem! Every time I set myself to solve it my brain became barren... barren as a wrung-out sponge!

*LA DURE* ploughed the waters, bearing in her ample belly, divided into three decks, some three hundred condemned men. Gomez and I were confined in the deepest. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and could not have harboured excessive antipathies towards us, no sooner learned who we were than he had us transferred to the upper deck. Indeed he pressed his benevolent partiality so far as to permit me, from time to time, to come up on deck and smoke freely... the Regulations prescribed the trifling penalty of fifty lashes of the rope for anyone who had done so in the compartments below.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz (Carmarie) to take on water and remained there two days, with hatches closed, to deprive us of any possibility of escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale du Salut, where some two hundred convicts were put ashore.

Gomez, myself, and another hundred wretches passed aboard a small steamer, and on the 11th of December 1858, after a day and a half of additional navigation, we lay at anchor off the Montagne d’Argent, which rises at the confluence of the São Jorge and the Oyapock, at a relatively short distance from the frontier of Brazil. A free colony had once flourished in that place, and free hands had cultivated its coffee and every tropical fruit that might tempt the human palate. The French Government had purchased it. Its officers occupied the planter’s house; numerous reed-thatched shelters, supported on stakes, were erected there for the condemned; the earth was pierced here and there, that refractory souls might be buried alive in slow agony; a few substantial buildings arose too for the storehouses, for defence and for offence — the vanguard of civilisation had been transformed into a penal settlement. But the natural beauties dissembled marvellously the hell that men had accumulated there.

The commandant, a Major of the marine infantry, whom the Republic had placed on the retired list and the Emperor reinstated and adorned with badges of honour, hastened to receive us “in consignment” and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it and, surveying me from head to foot: “Ah, so you are that —” (here came an obscenity fit for a pigsty!) “I am glad to see you here, and I swear to you that here you will leave your skin!” ...

That piece of coarseness was so entirely uncalled for that even the guards pulled a grimace of disgust. But I did not lose my composure, and, as though flattered by a compliment: “My thanks for the good wish — and I return it with all my heart!” He clenched his jaws; but he possessed the Gallic ferocity, not the French wit, and remained tight-lipped.

That small incident persuaded me that at the Montagne d’Argent the air blew ill for me, and from that very hour I resolved to repress every impulse. The relative freedom granted to the well-disciplined — I thought — may afford me the occasion to earn myself a greater one... the freedom of civilised men. But it was fated that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the hut assigned to me were some fifty convicts who formed my squad. I passed the first night sleeping, in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise we set out, our bones aching, towards the forest. I wore a great straw hat, a pair of heavy wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, and a brown blouse, upon the back of which was marked, in large characters, my number — the baptism of the penitentiary!

In crossing a swamp, I had the ill fortune to lose one of my clogs, and the stumps of the reeds, cut flush with the water to ease the passage, lacerated the sole of my foot. I was obliged to contrive a way of covering three miles hobbling, and marking, in blood, every footprint; until at last I arrived, the last of my squad, in the depths of the forest — a splendid forest that, for a little, almost made me forget that I was a convict, and the anguish with it.

A pile of logs. The task of the day was to carry them upon our shoulders to the penitentiary, making five consecutive trips. I singled out a log that seemed, to the eye, light; but it was *bois de fer* — ironwood — and when I had raised it to the height of my knees, it fell back noisily upon the pile. Then, instead of making another attempt, I sat down, resolved to rest just long enough to recover my breath. The convict-overseer stood watching me, and when he was certain of my intention: “Get up, you filthy wretch! If you don’t stir yourself, I’ll send you running with kicks to your backside. A curse on you and all Italian stock!”

Had he contented himself with goading me with a mere threat, I should in all probability have taken the matter with complete philosophical calm; but that contempt for my nationality stirred in my breast the anger accumulated against all the bullies who made sport of it. I swung my *sabre d’abattis* and sprang forward with a leap of which, given my condition, I should not have thought myself capable. “Take care! Italians do not suffer insults. The first — let it pass! At the second, your pumpkin will see the sun no more from atop your shoulders!”

My companions, who had set out one by one, were already far away. Alone, perfectly alone in a deserted place! He swallowed, turning pale, the pill. Nevertheless, by way of a show of bravado, he asked my name. “Content yourself with the number!” and I turned my back on him.

We were making a bad beginning! But chance would have it that my overseer, though he could spell more or less correctly, could not write. To make out his report, he was obliged to apply to the clerk of the supervising Engineer; who, with the help of the number, verified in the registers my name and who I was. What sentiments animated him towards me I cannot say; but I learned that he persuaded my overseer not to press his cruelty. And into his exhortations he deftly insinuated the element of fear: “That man,” he said, “fears neither Laws, nor punishments, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later, you are a dead man!”

On his return to the forest, the overseer drew me aside. “We have a certain sort of stuff here at the penitentiary!... But I know how to make my distinctions.” And to prove it, he begged my pardon for his ill words and granted me licence not to work, either then or thereafter. I was to conceal myself daily in the depths of the forest... I was to reappear at the last departure of my squad. And he extended his right hand, as though to seal a pact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours allotted to labour stretched out amid a wondrous vegetation, smoking my cigarette, listening to the intricate murmuring of the forest. In my eyes there was, in those hours, a fantastical interweaving of embroideries never before seen: enormous leaves cut as from velvet, trunks tall and straight as minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their own colours pressed strange insects into the service of their ill-concealed loves; birds with metallic lustre, bursting from shadows all a-rustle, swept past overhead on outstretched wings, swift as lightning. There were caverns of a deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults, dripping with dew and fragrance, through which the Sun gazed placidly, lordly, as though keeping watch that every blade of grass had its due.

The Sun which, consciously or unconsciously, in every age and by every People has been adored, had with secular energy raised up to itself that temple, worthy of a God. Oh, how beautiful is the Earth when it is not defaced by the cupidity of man!... A temple worthy, too, of Elisa!... Her image, intensely evoked, held dominion over me as though she were present!

It was in that forest and in those hours that I pondered my second attempt at escape. The plan to which I definitively committed myself came to me from the primitive art of the Indians ranging the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a great tree, to hollow it out so as to fashion a serviceable pirogue. With it I would have ascended, by night, the Oyapock for a stretch of fifty miles and then crossed the São Jorge, to make landfall on Brazilian soil. Having eluded the pursuit of the French Authorities, I should then have had to face, in all probability, the ferocity of the savages, and beyond any doubt the well-nigh insuperable obstacles of unexplored regions and of the climate. But the prize was worth the risk!

None of this could even be begun without the help of several accomplices. I sounded the spirit of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, satisfied with his position as assistant canteen-keeper and probably dreading the worst, feigned not to understand me. I was obliged to have recourse to less noble material. By what criteria was I to choose it? How was I to acquire over it the ascendancy necessary to bind it to my purposes?

In this case too, my good star, more than my own wisdom, came to my aid. The penitentiary received its supplies of fresh meat from Brazil. At intervals more or less regular, a sailing vessel arrived with a cargo of cattle. The shore, which shelved gradually into the ocean, afforded it no sufficient depth to moor near the mainland, so that the anchor was cast offshore at a distance of some three miles. The overseer would then choose one convict from each squad of ten and form them into a special party which made its laborious way, with water above the waist, up to the vessel. The wild bullocks, maddened, were flung into the sea, and the men drove them, with great shouts and great lashings, into a stockade within the penitentiary.

Once that work had begun, it proceeded without interruption until the unloading was complete, skipping, as needs required, even a meal or two. At the Montagne d’Argent, meals were taken by squads of ten. The squad-leader received in charge the provisions — already whittled thin by the sordid speculation of the officers — and set them out, upon a sort of table, in the ten mess-bowls. Each convict ran to his own and devoured it, seated on some step, or on a tree-trunk, or on the bare earth. On unloading days, it fell to the squad-leader to keep the absent convict’s ration and hand it to him on his return.

Chosen to lend a hand at that infernal labour, one day I made my way back at eight in the evening, when my squad was already snoring in its hammocks. Hunger, sharpened by the incessant exertion, the prolonged bath, the keen sea-breeze, had become ferocious. I shake the squad-leader, raise a half-commotion, and he, showing me his fist instead of the mess-bowl: “*Tais-toi, Italien de m...*” (the word of Cambronne).

I chew on nothing and hold my tongue. The evening of the following day I fall into line with the squad and wait about the table. When the squad-leader arrives, holding up the pot brimming with soup, I fling myself upon it and deliver it a mighty kick. The leader stands there dumbfounded, arms outstretched, hands empty. “It takes,” I say, “nine Frenchmen to make one Italian go hungry, but a single Italian is enough to make nine Frenchmen go hungry!”

Now it is the others who raise the commotion. The overseer, a certain Cosson, comes running, and having been informed of the affair, demands of me curtly: “Why, instead of taking your revenge, did you not file your report?”

— Because Italians do not turn informer!

Cosson, stealing Solomon’s trade, condemned to fifty lashes of the rope both the squad-leader and Count Carlo di Rudio. But my resoluteness, and above all my fierce reply, had in an instant raised me in the estimation of those poor wretches. They surrounded the overseer and pleaded with such effect that he was prevailed upon to swallow his sentence. From that day, the convicts condescended, in their goodness, to treat me fraternally as one worthy of their fellowship; from that day it proved a relatively easy undertaking for me to study the various temperaments, and to select eight individuals sufficiently enamoured of freedom to face cheerfully, in the attempt to regain it, whatever danger might arise.

We left our hut by night, crawling like beasts emerging from their lair, to deceive any eyes that might still be awake. We proceeded by separate paths to a pre-arranged spot on the edge of the forest; then, united, we pressed on into its labyrinths. From the very first night we had marked our route with signs almost invisible to other mortals, thus averting the danger of losing ourselves.

A tree of gigantic proportions was felled at its base with comparatively little effort; but in falling it struck the dense canopy and remained propped upon it. We had to climb up and hollow it out while clinging to its sides — a complication that consumed precious time. In the end we resolved to have recourse to fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary and the smoke was rendered invisible by the night; but amid such resinous vegetation, amid so many dead shrubs, so much dry kindling, so much fallen matter accumulated over centuries, the operation demanded the greatest circumspection, lest we feed a conflagration so swift and so vast that even the tropical deluges themselves would scarce have mastered it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constitutes, for me, one of those incidents whose impression only death can efface. We were not in that land as pioneers of civilisation, and someone might even remark, with irony, that we were not of the stuff of missionaries. But the setting — oh the grandeur of the setting, rendered ineffable by the silent night — how it penetrated, together with its own essence, into ours, and how it transformed us! All about us there was nothing that had not been born and grown in the most boundless freedom, and freedom became almost palpable in every atom of air we breathed. It seemed to me that my muscles, in their exertions, exhaled from every fibre something luminous, as though giving back to things the unstained life that things were lending to us. There were moments when I could have sworn I was as vigorous and whole as the virgin forest. And we became so thoroughly a homogeneous part of our surroundings that, after the first few nights, numberless troops of monkeys, drawn by the glow of the resin, came to contemplate and as if to encourage, brightening it with their presence, our work.

After some two months, or a little more, we succeeded in dragging into a bend of the river, and in concealing among the lianas, a canoe seven feet broad and twenty-five feet long. It floated admirably. “And now,” I exclaimed, when we parted full of enthusiasm, “to the oars and the sail!” Unexpectedly, an event to which, at its first manifestation, no one paid serious attention, threw the entire penal colony into turmoil — and with it every hope of mine.

In broad daylight, beneath a cloudless sky that is a whole lyric poem in itself, with a sun capable of hard-boiling ostrich eggs in its direct rays — a sentry was seized with violent trembling. Relieved as quickly as might be, he was seen making his way at slow steps, still trembling, to the antediluvian hut that bore the pompous name of “Cabinet of Decency.” He emerged from it ashen, undone. He declared that, try as he might, he had not been able to pass a single drop of water. The convicts laughed loudly... the thing seemed to them so droll! By evening the poor wretch was vomiting clotted blood, black as coal... two days later he was a corpse!

But before he had even drawn his last breath, five, seven, ten convicts, while showing at the thermometer a high temperature, were trembling like leaves. The penitentiary had been invaded by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was swift beyond all telling. In those days it was held that the seat of the disease lay in the intestines; the remedies, scarce in the extreme and wholly inadequate to the vast extent of the mortality, were applied, for greater misfortune, on erroneous principles. The Commandant gave orders to throw open the storehouses and the cantina and to leave them at the full disposal of the able-bodied. From that cantina, flung wide and black with shadow, there seemed to issue the very sensation of doom. If yellow fever was not crouching within it, it had there some mysterious ally. The convicts were inexorably driven thither by vice and by fear. They brutalised themselves within it and passed from drunkenness to delirium; from delirium to nothingness! — their death the more sudden the more robust they had been.

The arrangements for the victims were of the simplest: to cast them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Every evening the chief overseer went to make his report at the Commandant’s house.

— How many, today?

— Twenty, thirty, forty! ...

— And the convict di Rudio?

— Sound as a fish!

One night the Commandant lost his wife and two children almost simultaneously, and he ceased to concern himself with my health. Not long afterwards, it was the chief overseer himself who ceased to make his customary report. He had too readily yielded to the temptations of the cantina, and when he was found stretched upon the beaten earth, it was at first suspected that he was simply drunk. But his gums were coated with bloody froth... he died without having recovered either speech or the faintest glimmer of consciousness, and he was one of the many whom I was obliged to seize by the feet. “One, two, three...” Before casting him in, I should have wished to say a *Requiescat* over him — he had spared me fifty lashes of the rope. But down there in that pit it would have seemed...

I lent my hands to the burying, one by one, in that fashion, of my eight accomplices in the attempt at escape, and of others, and still others... I counted past the hundred; then I lost count and counted no more. Some poor wretch who already seemed to have crossed the frontier beyond which one suffers no more, scarce flung into that horrible common sepulchre, was seen to revive and writhe in agony!

When the fever, finding no more flesh to gnaw upon, at last disappeared, the French Deputies raised in Paris the customary humanitarian clamour. But the Minister replied to them, in a tone of triumph, that he had already given the necessary orders to transfer the entire Colony to the Île du Salut. And indeed one evening we saw, traced against the crepuscular light, the black streak of smoke from a vessel proceeding at full steam towards our shore. It was the *Abeille*, bringing us the orders of His Excellency. The following day the entire Colony was on board... Of more than six hundred souls — convicts, military, civil servants, and attendants alike — we were reduced to sixty-three! The greater part of the survivors had already suffered from yellow fever at other times and on other shores, which had rendered them, it appears, resistant if not absolutely invulnerable; several, shadows of their former selves, showed signs of having been but lately touched, if not cut down — among these, Antonio Gomez... Oh, how transformed was that face — that good-natured priestly face of his! Three alone had not carried away from it, then or before, any taint of infected blood. Among the three, the convict di Rudio! From the deck of the *Abeille*, about which the ocean already foamed and broke, I cast a last glance towards the Montagne d’Argent and think, with profound regret, of my canoe floating solitary and motionless beneath the dense lianas. The landscape dwindles rapidly. The hell that men had accumulated there was no more; what remained was one of its most baleful consequences, the vast charnel pit from which we had miraculously escaped. Nature, indifferent, spread over so much horror her enchanting hues, and in the scintillating air vibrated her mysterious amourings!
