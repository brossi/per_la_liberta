I HAD scarcely thrown my canvas sack upon the mole of the isola del Saluto, and already hope inundated my breast, intense, overpowering.

The island, with a little play of the imagination, presented the form of a figure 8. On the larger loop, which was also the more elevated, rose the dwellings, the hospital, a church; on the more slender loop were the workshops and, on its extreme curve, the barracks. From that extremity, facing more or less exactly to the northwest, one could toss a stone onto the long islet of Diavolo, the same which later became sadly famous for the dark drama of which Captain Dreyfus was a victim. At low tide, it was not difficult, by jumping on the jagged rocks left exposed, to pass from one island to the other, dry-shod.

To the east, another islet, called San Giuseppe, rose from the waters, a rugged, almost circular plain, scattered with a few stunted and dwarfish trees.

The island on which I found myself had, measured by eye and in its greatest diameter, an extent of about two miles. Its shores were rocky and all coves.

These were circumstances I had noted only in passing. What succeeded in capturing my attention and fixing it, such that I was lost in deep reflection, was a small rowboat, gliding over the waves a short distance from the shore. It had on board four or five convicts, who were rowing and singing. I thought: "Who prevents these men from making for the open sea? True, one cannot cross the Ocean by strength of oars. But why do they not procure a sail? I left a dinghy in the waters of the Ojapok and here I find another at the isola del Saluto. Courage, di Rudio, you have lost nothing!"

I was still standing motionless, with my eye fixed on that nutshell, when I felt a touch on my shoulder. I turned with a start and found myself before a man who wore the grotesque uniform of the convict.

I scrutinized him and he broke the silence:

— They assured me you are Italian...

— And who are you? By your accent you seem a Roman from Rome.

— I am from Trastevere, and I'm proud of it!

— Why are you here?

— That's another story. But may I never see my country again and die with a noose around my neck if, should the need arise, I would not do it all over again: A French dog seduced my sister and left her in trouble... I pierced his heart. Long live Trastevere blood and to hell with anyone who speaks ill of it! For this I lost my Rome... ten little years on my back!... but they will pass. My name is Morbioli.

I exchanged, so to speak, my calling card; then suddenly: How long have you been here?

— Seven years!

— Seven years!... you are Italian, Roman, from Trastevere, and you have not tried to escape?

— Impossible! Alone, don't even think of it. In company, worse than going by night! In this cursed place, for the cowards who turn informer, there is no gunshot, but a pardon! Do you understand me? To denounce a plot leads to a more certain freedom than attempting to escape.

— I thought there was more solidarity among the fallen than among the fortunate of the world.

— And even if there were! One can see you are a newcomer to this place and you do not have a clear idea of the difficulties. Ah, do you think there have not been stout-hearted men with the guts to risk it? But to what avail? Listen!...

And he told me that, thirteen years before, thirteen convicts from the isola Madre, another French penitentiary situated in those parts, had seized a pilot-boat and had made for the open sea. The *Abeille* gave them chase, but in vain. About three months passed, and it was already thought that they had reached free land when, on one of its crossings from Cayenna to the isola del Saluto, the *Abeille* sighted a boat that was adrift. It came upon it, and whom did it find there?

— Only three. The three had devoured the other ten.

An escape had also been attempted by the land route, from Montagne d'Argent. There were twenty of them and, when they had penetrated the forest, no one bothered to pursue them anymore. After several months, a feral man made an unexpected appearance at the penitentiary. He was the only survivor... he confessed to have survived until then because he had, more or less, stripped the flesh from the other nineteen. Having now nothing left on which to set his teeth, he decided to turn himself in. They sentenced that he had eaten too much and... they cut off his head!

An escape had also been attempted from the islet of Diavolo. The son of Giacomo Pianori, a precursor to Felice Orsini in the assassination attempt and in martyrdom, had been exiled there as a suspicious person. He had noticed that the tide washed numerous tree trunks onto the beach. He made a collection of them and succeeded in putting together a raft. The sinuosities of the shore had lent themselves to the secret labor. He departed, at night, with twenty companions. On the high seas, he was caught in a squall. The great waves broke the raft in two parts. One part drifted for a long time with the currents, until it ran aground down there, in some part of Dutch Guiana. The fugitives were rescued and... handed back over to the French Authorities. Chained in the pits, exposed to the torrid sun and the torrential rains, they soon breathed their last, amidst their suffering. Twelve corpses thrown to the sharks!

The other eight, among them Pianori, also reached the Continent, on the other part of the raft; but unfortunately they went and struck a *vase*. Do you know what a *vase* is? A bank of bluish earth that grips like cement. The air there is full of "mosquitos" capable of stripping bare, in a few days, the carcass of a whale; the earth swarms with crabs... the terrible crabs of Guiana that dig long tunnels underground, invade the cemeteries and, lucky for them, the corpses are sleeping soundly. The *Abeille*, on its route through those parts, found the raft and what little remained of Pianori and the other wretches. The crabs and insects did not even have the courtesy to wait for them to be dead and, certainly, right then and there, they made a feast of them!

I had listened without interrupting. In the meantime the nutshell had drawn nearer to the island and, coasting along it, disappeared from time to time behind some rock, only to reappear ever closer. The convicts were grouping on the shore and exchanging calls with the rowers. I asked: What is that boat and what are those men doing?

— They fish on behalf of the penitentiary and, when the fish is abundant, they sell some, more or less covertly, to anyone who wants to buy.

He scratched his head and added: — Morbioli is no fool. You are thinking that one could take advantage of that boat, to try some new venture. You are reckoning without our four gunboats. They would shoot at you merrily, as if hunting game. They would laugh to see you go to the bottom. And then there is the *Abeille*: a simple dinghy cannot make it!

I was assigned to the anchor workshop. I worked half-naked... a heat that shriveled the skin; the sparks, flying up from the anvil, mottled my whole body. But never, as then, did I understand the power of the intellect in opposing painful sensations and in taming them. The stubborn meditation on a plan of escape transported me elsewhere, among the rocks of the island, upon the high seas, and the impressions of the workshop reached me as if weakened by distance. My plan was developing from the very history of my predecessors' failures, and it seemed to me that its chain of logic emerged from my mind more rigid and more resistant than the iron I was hammering.

Pianori had run to meet his horrible destiny for having trusted in a raft... therefore, no raft! And why, moreover, should I take on the slow, arduous labor of building one, when I had at hand a small vessel lacking only a rag of a sail? I calculated that to repossess it, with a surprise attack, ought not to prove an overly difficult enterprise for men of resolute temper, all the more so since the fishermen would undoubtedly have resigned themselves with good grace to an act of force that would have restored them to liberty.

It is true, being on a ship had not in the least benefited the other escapees; but, evidently, they did not know how to handle it and they lacked provisions... it was therefore necessary to secure the complicity of some man of the sea; it was necessary to accumulate a great quantity of victuals.

Where? If the low island of Diavolo had offered a secure hiding place for a raft, the high, rugged shore of the isola del Saluto could well offer a secure one for some sacks of bread, some demijohns of water and of rum.

The *Abeille*! It made regular crossings from the island to Cayenna: it was necessary to make for the open sea during its absence.
