Of my excursion, which extended twice as far as the remote Udine, I shall narrate, in brief, a few episodes.

Having eluded the police in Milano, Como, Bergamo, and Brescia, I approached Mantova with great confidence of spirit... I was bolstered by the certainty of not being known there by a living soul. But just as I was passing the custom-house, as violent as the blow of a battering ram, I felt my full name hurled directly at my chest.

For a moment, I believed myself discovered and lost. But the man who stood before me had a smiling face, and his outstretched right hand invited mine. He was the very chief of the guards. Where I had known him, I do not now recall... he was a patriot privy to secret matters.

When he learned that I was not in Mantova for a change of air, his face darkened. Just the day before, the police had carried out various arrests, and no one knew why... he saw a connection between those unexpected rigors and my arrival. He advised me to take refuge immediately at his house and to remain locked inside. He would endeavor to survey the terrain and, in the evening, over a bite to eat, we would decide on a course of action.

Provoked by the conspiracy in which the gentle figure of the priest Tazzoli was pre-eminent, the series of refined tortures that drove the handful of martyrs of Belfiore to an inhuman death was just then beginning in Mantova. But, returning late in the evening, my host could bring me nothing but vague news, and with it a breath of the terror that hung over the city. He had not met a single brother who felt sure of being able to spend the night in his own bed.

I lay down in the attic as best I could, without undressing... prepared to attempt, at the slightest alarm, an escape over the rooftops.

In the early morning, questioning my host at length, I learned that among the garrison was an officer who had been raised in the Military Academy of Milano and was a fellow student of mine: the bonds of early youth are not frayed by the passing of years. Half an hour later, I was in the Castello, giving and receiving a fraternal embrace, or something of the sort. I was under the protection of the double-headed eagle.

To explain my presence, I said I was on my way to Belluno to attend to my father, who was burdened by his years... I had made a detour to Mantova specifically to see my friend again; to recall, in his company, the age of happy thoughtlessness. As if by tacit agreement, we refrained from touching upon subjects of a political nature; but in the half-merrymaking organized to celebrate me, I did not lose sight of the purpose of my journey and I obtained, in bits and pieces, all the information I sought. Two hours before sunset, the officer himself kindly offered to accompany me in his carriage beyond the city walls. And, under his aegis, I passed the custom-house without molestation, receiving a military salute.

No sooner had I escaped a probably imaginary danger, however, than I had to face one that was all too real, in Verona.

I had taken a room at the "Scudo di Francia" and, as soon as I was shut in my room, I instinctively made a small inspection. It was situated on the third floor; the only window looked out onto a courtyard. Opposite me was the rear wall of another hotel, that of the "Regina d'Inghilterra." Peering down, I spied a wide shed roof that sloped toward the center of the courtyard. A stable stench rose even that high; I heard the stamping of a horse.

I sat at the small table, near the lit fireplace, and began to write my report on Mantova. Suddenly, cadenced steps resounded in the corridor. I blew out the candle and threw my letter into the fire; carbonized, it flew up the flue.

There was a knock at the door and an obsequious voice, that of a servant: "Signor Moretti, if you please!" After a brief silence, another gruff and imperious voice: "In the name of the Law, open up!"

I had climbed over the parapet and was clinging to it with outstretched arms, to lessen the distance that separated me from the shed roof. The gendarmes began to beat on the door with the butts of their rifles... I loosened my hands and let myself drop, close to the wall. The blow was rough, but the disaster befell the tiles. I crawled along them and, having reached the edge, I took advantage of a protruding beam and what little gymnastics my aching limbs would allow to put the gutter between myself and the gendarmes. Crouched down there, I heard the door of my room give way with a crash... then a hasty trampling, then... silence! then a sharp voice: "Sergeant, I bet he jumped out the window!" And the sergeant, who knew a thing or two: "You blockhead! No one jumps from a third floor!"

I believe they did not even lean out of the window. The courtyard was deserted. From the ajar door of the stable, a strip of light was cast. It revealed to me a pile of manure. I jumped into it...

A little later, I walked resolutely into the kitchen of the "Regina d'Inghilterra:" scullery boys and servants, busy at their tasks, did not even raise their heads. A cook, his large paunch wrapped in a white apron, a white cap on his head, was at that moment blowing on his ladle... He looked at me, astonished; but he had not recovered from his surprise before I was already slipping through the door to the dining room. The waiters probably took me for a friend of some scullery boy. In a few seconds, I found myself in the open air.

Where to spend the night? The Police, made more suspicious by my disappearance, would not have allowed me to find rest in any hotel. I wandered adrift until I happened upon the Post Office. Lined up there were several mail coaches, their doors wide open... into one of them I threw myself down and lay crouched until the break of dawn. Several times, between sleep and wakefulness, I heard the patrol pass by my feet. Had I snored, in all probability, I would not be here to tell of that adventure.

I brought upon myself a still more devilish peril, through an excess of zeal, upon my return to Milano. On the advice of a certain Valentini, a chemistry student, I was lodging, during my stay in the Lombard capital, in the house of his sweetheart; she made a living from the proceeds of her milk-seller's shop and by keeping a boarding house. Several finance guards and a Hungarian sergeant, a stubborn tormentor of the piano, attended her table. It seemed to me I was entrenched in the enemy's own camp.

The milk-seller, a stout woman, with a face like a fresh panettone, was of the most Italian of sentiments, so much so that on my previous visit I had given her, for her to destroy, a bundle of revolutionary leaflets that had become superfluous. Now she announced to me that she had kept it, intact, at the bottom of a chest of drawers. I rejoiced at this... I would be able, before returning to Piemonte, to give the final touch to my work with a good posting of them.

I spoke of it to the Revolutionary Committee which, being extremely irritated in those days by the terrible news arriving from Mantova, approved my decision. And among those patriots, I even found one who spontaneously offered to lend me a hand... a stocky workman with a large scar on his face. He did not tell me his name; he was a tapestry-maker, like the poor Antonio Sciesa.

A cold, rainy night, the streets deserted. We began at the place called the Carrobbio; I on one pavement, he on the other... we pasted up our proclamations with those little wafers that the Milanese call *obiadìtt*. We were headed toward the Duomo and I could already see the facade of the magnificent temple gleaming white, when from the other side of the piazza, precisely from under the Coperto dei Figini, the terrible warning resounded in the silence: "Run, run!"

I saw the stocky figure of the tapestry-maker flee at top speed toward piazza Mercanti, and a police patrol, which had probably emerged from some dark passageway, pursued him, shouting. If not for his fraternal solicitude, I might have gotten away without attracting notice; but by shouting that cry to me, he had also put the gendarmes on alert; a good number of whom advanced at a run toward me.

There were no alternatives. I threw down the leaflets and fled headlong through a labyrinth of narrow arteries until, breathless, I found myself facing the steep line of the city walls. I climbed the ramp on all fours; but with my pursuers at my heels and a custom-house, with its respective guard post, to my right and left, I could consider myself trapped. I found temporary shelter behind one of those large horse-chestnut trees and, for a moment, I flattered myself that, uncertain of the direction I had taken, the cursed men would turn back, crestfallen; or, having climbed onto the bastion, they would all turn right, leaving me free to slip away silently to the left. But they were not such fools.

Having reached the top, they divided into two groups that, turning their backs to each other, proceeded simultaneously on both sides, cautiously observing behind every tree. As in Verona, I found myself in the terrible dilemma of either submitting, later, to a small drop that, by virtue of the noose, would have no remedy, or facing the immediate dangers of a great leap, which still left me some percentage of escape. I decided on the great leap.

How long I remained stunned down there at the bottom, in truth, I never knew. Fortunately, I had landed on soft ground and perhaps what helped me to my feet was the rain that was falling as if it were being poured over me. I set off, painfully and soaked to the bone, toward Pavia.

In what state did I arrive? It is not difficult to imagine. Three days under the covers and I was back in fine fettle. The brother of Giovine Italia who had lovingly cared for me would have liked me to stay longer; but the land of salvation was too near for me not to feel a fever to set foot on it again. He accompanied me as far as the bridge that crosses the Ticino and connects the Lombard bank to the Piedmontese, and we parted.

And behold, two, three, a crowd of crazed men ran noisily to the entrance of the bridge. From the phrases they exchanged in loud voices, I understood that a stranger, some smuggler or some "hothead" pursued by a patrol, had thrown himself from the parapet. They had hit him in mid-air with a shotgun blast in the back and were now endeavoring to fish him out.

I gave them a wide berth and, by passing myself off as a deserter, found a way to be ferried across, arriving at Stradella around midnight. The Winkler house was still lit. I entered and found myself before a group of patriots who received me as if I had fallen from the clouds. A secret message, arrived from Pavia, had brought the news that I had been discovered, pursued, and mortally wounded while trying to escape by jumping into the Ticino. Fortunato Calvi had already been informed by telegraph.

I set off again immediately and found Calvi still in bed. As soon as he recovered from his surprise, his first thought was for don Bastiano Barozzi, with whom he had already made an appointment for nine o'clock at the Caffé del Pantheon. The good priest entered, pale and almost distraught. His myopia prevented him from seeing me, and Calvi moved toward him. Seeing him, he exclaimed: "Ah, what a calamity! What will my benefactor say when he learns that I have repaid him by pushing his son to perdition?"

— And what would you say, if Carlo were to walk in here, healthy and smiling?

Barozzi's face twitched convulsively. Suddenly, the lump that was choking him dissolved and he burst into a flood of tears.
