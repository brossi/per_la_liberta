Mi fecero attraversare tre camere occupate, malgrado l'ora, da impiegati intenti allo scrittoio. Giunto in una quarta, sedetti a lungo d'innanzi ad un caminetto, sul quale ardevano due tizzoni in croce. E poiché, a meglio concentrarmi, avevo chiuso gli occhi, udii una guardia dire sottovoce: "Falso allarme! Se fosse un complice, non dormirebbe così!"

Mi trassero alfine in una gran sala, la cui gran luce per un momento mi abbagliò. La prima impressione distintamente percepita fu quella dell'uscio che si chiuse alle mie spalle; poi vidi disegnarsi a me di fronte — è necessario che mi perda in questi particolari — una scrivania sparsa di carte, perfettamente isolata in mezzo alla sala. Un vecchio apparentemente sulla sessantina, dall'aspetto tuttora energico, dalla pelle tersa, piena di riflessi lucidi, le stava seduto da un lato e scorreva, accigliato, un suo documento. Al mio apparire se lo distese sulle ginocchia e alzò la faccia. Era il Prefetto di Polizia.

Un'altra scrivania era a quattro passi lungo la parete di destra. Aveva le staffe tanto alte da lasciarmi scorgere, dell'uomo che vi stava seduto, la sommità del cranio e nulla più... il cranio del Giudice d'Istruzione. D'accanto alla scrivania, in parte seduti, in parte ritti, stavano vari alti papaveri dell'esercito, il petto ingombro di medaglie e, tra quelle uniformi chiassose, nereggiavano tristemente alcuni scarafaggi in borghese. Tra questi ultimi, riconobbi un personaggio della famiglia imperiale... Plon-Plon! V'eran guardie di diverso grado e in diversi atteggiamenti e, mentre scorrevo con gli occhi quelle uniformi chiassose, or qua or là, coll'intensità di chi indaga l'abisso da cui potrebbe essere inghiottito, vidi risaltare dalle pareti a fondo rosso, e come se ne uscissero in quel punto, parecchi busti in marmo che mi fissarono coi loro occhi senza pupilla. E gli uomini pure mi fissavano senza batter palpebra, come se partecipassero della natura marmorea di quei busti. Il Giudice Istruttore si alzò e si avanzò verso di me, e la sua fisionomia non mi giunse nuova. Egli aveva una storia. Nei giorni in cui Luigi Napoleone era in basso, aveva calpestato le Leggi delle quali era, adesso, il paladino stipendiato. Doveva la sua posizione agli antichi servigi resi all'Imperatore. Ad un suo cenno sedetti e due guardie, che stavano presso l'uscio, vennero e si fissero ritte ai miei fianchi.

Allora il magistrato mi chiese le mie generalità ed io risposi a tenore del passaporto che mi avevano sequestrato. Ma quando mi domandò se conoscessi il mio compagno di camera ed io ebbi risposto negativamente, la sua faccia divenne ilare.

— Come può essere — chiese — Dormite con un individuo e non sapete neppure chi sia? Dove lo incontraste la prima volta?

— Durante il mio viaggio da Calais a Parigi. Era la prima volta che venivo in questa città e gli chiesi l'indirizzo di un albergo. "Scendete al mio, mi rispose. Ho una camera con due letti. Egli comprende un po' la mia lingua. Spenderete meno." Accettai. Mi disse il suo nome, gli dissi il mio. Ecco tutto! Perchè avrei dovuto chiedergli conto del suo passato?

— Ma voi che cosa venivate a fare?

— A guadagnarmi il pane, smerciando Burton Ale.

— Come passaste la giornata d'ieri? Avevo già studiata a memoria la mia risposta che, del resto, differiva di poco dalla verità. Monsieur Pierey, come persona pratica di Parigi, mi aveva condotto la mattina, alla Galleria del Louvre. Più tardi il Pierey stesso si era recato a far visita a qualche sua conoscenza ed io l'avevo aspettato in un'osteria. Poi narrai del pranzo fatto al restaurant della via Montmartre.

— A che ora avete pranzato?

— Verso le sette. Ritornammo al nostro albergo. Giunti appena, il portiere disse a Monsieur Pierey che un signore era venuto a cercarlo... l'aspettasse, sarebbe ritornato tra breve. Così appunto avvenne. Ecco tutto!

— Chi era quel signore?

— Non lo so. Era venuto per l'altro e non per me. D'altronde, m'accorsi che voleva parlare d'affari, per prudenza mi allontanai.

— Dove vi recaste?

— Al gabinetto in fondo al corridoio. Quando feci ritorno, il signore era partito.

— Ma non vi ricordate la sua fisionomia?

— La camera era al buio. Rammento però che egli portava il barbone.

— Lo riconoscereste vedendolo?

— Forse sì, forse no.

A questo punto, il giudice si rivolse ad una guardia e le ordinò di far entrare "l'altro prigioniero". Quando l'uscio si riaprì, sulla soglia comparve Felice Orsini. La benda che gli fasciava il capo era intrisa di sangue. Aveva l'aria debole e stanca. Il suo primo sguardo fu per me. Parve mi rivolgesse una muta interrogazione e sulla sua fisionomia vidi passare una fugace inquietudine.

— È questi? mi domandò il Giudice.

— L'uomo che venne all'albergo portava il barbone. Credo d'averlo detto.

Allora il giudice lasciò la scrivania e, fattosi presso l'Orsini, gli fece scorrere la punta delle dita sotto il mento. Voleva assicurarsi che il rasoio non vi fosse passato di fresco. Fortunatamente, la pelle del prigioniero deve essergli sembrata, al tatto, irta di punte.

È all'Orsini che egli rivolse la sua domanda successiva. Parlò in inglese.

— Da quale parte d'Inghilterra venite?

— Da Kent.

— Quanto dista Kent da Londra?

— Trenta leghe.

Quelle due parole bastarono. In Inghilterra si parla di miglia, non di leghe. D'altra parte l'Orsini aveva pronunciato il suo "thirty" cambiando in t il suono del th e facendo suonare energicamente l'r... il che non è nelle abitudini delle bocche inglesi.

Disse il Giudice: "Voi siete inglese come io sono turco. Anzi, per dirvi tutta la verità, non siete neppure francese."

La pallida faccia di Felice Orsini ebbe un lampo. Egli si rizzò romanamente sdegnoso. Disse, anzi gridò: "Ah no, per dio! Sono italiano e ne vado orgoglioso. Il mio nome è Felice Orsini. Non domandatemi altro, non vi risponderò".

Aveva trasfusa l'anima nelle sue parole, l'anima dell'uomo nobile e forte che comanda ai pigmei del carattere. Essi ne furono soggiogati e non ardirono opporvisi. Fu, senz'altro, condotto altrove. Ma il Prefetto della Polizia che, durante l'interrogatorio, era rimasto ritto ed immobile, presso la sua scrivania, si diede a frugare febbrilmente nei cassetti. "Ah, ah! — disse alfine avanzandosi verso i personaggi con e senza uniforme — Ecco una circolare segreta dell'Austria. Felice Orsini... il rivoluzionario pericoloso che fuggì dal castello di Mantova. Abbiamo fatto una buona presa!"

Poi ritornò alla scrivania e rivolgendosi a me: "Monsieur da Sylva, conoscete questi arnesi?"

Guardai e fu come se un raggio rischiarasse le cellule del mio cervello. Adesso comprendevo perchè Napoleone viveva e trionfava! Due degli "arnesi" che avrebbero dovuto, col loro scoppio, dare il segnale del risveglio a due Popoli, stavan inoperosi, come fermacarte, sulla scrivania del Prefetto. In qual modo vi erano giunti? Vi erano!... questo mi bastava. Le loro dimensioni... eguali; ad entrambe sulle punte della metà inferiore luccicavano ancora le capsule micidiali. Inesplicabile imprudenza!

Quella vista bastò a suscitare, nel mio petto, le furie che il pensiero predominante della propria salvezza aveva, momentaneamente, assopite. Ed ogni mia fibra ridivenne gonfia di spirito turbolento; ogni mia fibra riebbe un impeto di ribellione.

Pensai: "Forse, ciò che non si è ottenuto per via diretta, si otterrà indirettamente, portando nuova confusione nel campo nemico, indubbiamente già scompigliato... e perchè no? Ma ad ogni modo... uccidere come umana protesta, vendicando i traditi e gli oppressi!"

Io non dico, ora, che il mio pensiero fosse quello dell'uomo ponderato; dico che tale fu il mio pensiero. E dico che oggi ancora, reputo supremamente umano il grido: "Pera Sansone e con esso i Filistei!"

M'alzai e piegai in avanti la persona, socchiudendo gli occhi, come se fossi miope. Due passi, solo due passi senza destare sospetti e avrei dato un balzo... una rapida mossa, la folgore... guardie, Prefetto, Giudice, generali, me stesso... ambizioni e speranze, servilità e libidine di comando, delitti e virtù... pochi brandelli di carne... il nulla...

Una mano greve come il fato, mi afferrò alla spalla e ricaddi sulla sedia. Il cuore mi batteva convulsamente, ma feci uno sforzo e dissi in tono pacato e quasi perplesso: "Non comprendo!... di che si tratta?"

Da quel momento il mio interrogatorio procedette rapido ed a seconda dei miei desideri. Nel dare gli ultimi tocchi al rendiconto delle mie azioni, ebbi cura di trasporre, per così dire, qualche minuto e feci coincidere la mia entrata nel caffè, precisamente coll'ora dell'attentato. Allusi anche alla lettera che vi scrissi. Il giuoco era pericoloso; ma la padrona non m'aveva visto entrare ed io vi facevo assegnamento. Il Giudice sussurrò poche parole nell'orecchio ad una guardia che immantinente partì. Ne dedussi che si voleva procedere ad una verifica. Non passò gran tempo e la guardia stessa, fatto ritorno, confermò appieno la mia deposizione, narrando persino l'incidente dei quattro francobolli da due che io avevo dimenticato. Il Giudice si rivolse allora al Prefetto e gli disse: "Per conto mio non ho altra interrogazione a fare".

"Sta bene! — gli osservò quest'ultimo — Ma monsieur da Sylva potrebbe essere un testimonio prezioso. Io lo pregherei di rimanere al suo albergo a disposizione e a spese dello Stato. Gli consegneremo il passaporto più tardi. Nessuna obbiezione?"

— Nessuna affatto, e specialmente se lo Stato si sobbarca alle spese. I miei mezzi sono limitatissimi!

E per la terza volta in quella notte, udii la Polizia francese profondersi in scuse. Gli alti personaggi, con e senza uniforme, attaccarono discorso tra loro, guardandomi di quando in quando come se si comunicassero le loro impressioni sul conto mio. E il Giudice, auguratemi la buona notte, si volse alle guardie che mi stavano ai fianchi. Le sue ultime parole furono in portoghese: "Monsieur da Sylva fuori della Prefettura!"

Mentre attraversavo le varie camere il mio pensiero andava a ritroso dei passi e consideravo la straordinaria perspicacia di un Giudice che si lasciava sfuggire in quel modo il lupo dalla trappola. Bastava che egli mi avesse interrogato sul mio luogo d'origine; che m'avesse chiesto con quali clienti avessi trattato nella mia qualità di venditore di Burton Ale; che mi avesse rivolto, o fatto rivolgere, una sola parola in portoghese e la mia vera natura era svelata ed a nudo. Ne conclusi che Giudice e Polizia si trovavano in uno stato di sovr'eccitazione, fors'anche di vaga apprensione che ne annebbiava la vista, che ne dimezzava la potenza dinamica.

Tuttavia chi stanotte — pensai — potrebbe essere desto ed all'erta domani... Domani? Ch'io metta un'altra volta il piede all'aperto e furbo chi mi ritrova! La stazione d'Orleans è guardata a vista; lo stesso dev'essere delle altre stazioni... ma diverse vie mettono fuori di Parigi e Parigi è vasta, né v'entrò né vi mancò d'amici e di nascondigli.

Ero giunto all'ultima camera e vi trovai l'inaspettato... Dall'uscio, che si era aperto per concedere la libertà al lupo, entrò proprio in quella, sotto buona scorta, Antonio Gomez. Guardai subito altrove, unico mezzo alla mia portata per fargli comprendere che doveva ignorare la mia presenza. Ma egli pure aveva il comprendonio annebbiato e, passandomi d'accanto, mormorò a mezza voce, in francese: "Siamo perduti!"

Volle il mio destino che, tra la sua scorta, fosse un uomo intelligente. "Ah, ah! vi conoscete?" E il Gomez ribadendo il chiodo che mi configgeva: "Certo, che ci conosciamo!"

Mi ero affrettato a mettere il piede sulla soglia. "Ferma!" mi tuonò una voce alle spalle. Fui invitato a sedermi e l'uscio si chiuse. Gomez continuò il suo cammino sino alla gran sala; io rimasi nella trappola in grande ansietà, mordendomi le labbra. Passarono dieci minuti. Una pendola cigolò e battè quattro rintocchi. Altre guardie vennero. Mi presero pel braccio, senz'aprir bocca e mi condussero via. Percorremmo un transito sotterraneo.

Passai il resto della notte in una cella della Conciergerie!

## Chapter Twelve

They made me pass through three rooms occupied, despite the hour, by clerks intent at their writing-desks. Having arrived in a fourth, I sat for a long while before a fireplace, upon which two brands burned crosswise. And since, the better to collect my thoughts, I had closed my eyes, I heard a guard say in an undertone: "A false alarm! Were he an accomplice, he would not sleep so soundly!"

At length they drew me into a great hall, whose brilliant light for a moment dazzled me. The first impression I distinctly perceived was that of the door closing behind me; then I saw take shape before me — I must needs lose myself in these particulars — a writing-desk strewn with papers, standing perfectly isolated in the middle of the hall. An old man, apparently in his sixties, of aspect still vigorous, with smooth skin full of bright reflections, was seated at one side of it, frowning over a document. At my appearance he spread it across his knees and raised his face. It was the Prefect of Police.

Another writing-desk stood some four paces off along the right-hand wall. Its shelves were set so high as to allow me to discern, of the man seated behind it, only the crown of his skull and nothing more — the skull of the Examining Magistrate. Beside the desk, partly seated, partly standing, were various high *papaveri* — poppy-heads, men of consequence — of the military, their chests laden with medals, and among those gaudy uniforms there moved about in gloomy fashion several black beetles in civilian dress. Among the latter I recognised a personage of the imperial family — Plon-Plon! There were guards of various ranks and in various attitudes, and as I swept my eyes over those gaudy uniforms, now here, now there, with the intensity of one who peers into the abyss by which he might be swallowed, I saw stand out from the walls of red ground, and as though emerging from them at that very moment, several marble busts that fixed me with their pupilless eyes. And the men, too, fixed me without the flutter of an eyelid, as though they partook of the marmoreal nature of those busts. The Examining Magistrate rose and advanced towards me, and his countenance was not unknown to me. He had a history. In the days when Louis Napoleon was brought low, this man had trampled upon those very Laws of which he was, now, the salaried champion. He owed his position to the old services rendered to the Emperor. At a sign from him I sat down, and two guards who stood beside the door came and placed themselves upright at my sides.

The magistrate then asked me for my particulars, and I replied in accordance with the passport they had confiscated. But when he asked whether I knew my room-mate, and I had answered in the negative, his face grew merry.

"How can that be," he asked, "you sleep with an individual and do not even know who he is? Where did you first encounter him?"

"During my journey from Calais to Paris. It was the first time I had come to this city, and I asked him the address of an hotel. 'Come down to mine,' he replied. 'I have a room with two beds. He understands a little of my language. You will spend less.' I accepted. He told me his name, I told him mine. That is all! Why should I have called him to account for his past?"

"But what were you coming to do?"

"To earn my bread, selling Burton Ale."

"How did you pass yesterday?"

I had already committed my answer to memory, which, moreover, differed but little from the truth. Monsieur Pierey, as one well acquainted with Paris, had taken me in the morning to the Louvre Gallery. Later, Pierey himself had gone to pay a call on some acquaintance of his, and I had awaited him in a tavern. I then recounted the dinner taken at the restaurant in the rue Montmartre.

"At what hour did you dine?"

"Towards seven o'clock. We returned to our hotel. Scarcely arrived, the porter told Monsieur Pierey that a gentleman had come seeking him — he should wait, he would return shortly. And so indeed it fell out. That is all!"

"Who was that gentleman?"

"I do not know. He had come for the other, not for me. Moreover, perceiving that he wished to speak of business, I withdrew out of discretion."

"Where did you go?"

"To the water-closet at the end of the corridor. When I returned, the gentleman had departed."

"But do you not recall his appearance?"

"The room was in darkness. I remember, however, that he wore a full beard."

"Would you recognise him on seeing him?"

"Perhaps yes, perhaps no."

At this point, the magistrate turned to a guard and ordered him to show in "the other prisoner." When the door reopened, on the threshold appeared Felice Orsini. The bandage that swathed his head was soaked in blood. He had the air of a man weak and spent. His first glance was for me. He seemed to address me a mute question, and across his features I saw pass a fleeting unease.

"Is this the man?" the Magistrate asked me.

"The man who came to the hotel wore a full beard. I believe I said as much."

The magistrate then left his desk and, drawing close to Orsini, ran the tips of his fingers under his chin. He wished to assure himself that a razor had not recently passed there. Fortunately, the prisoner's skin must have felt, to his touch, bristling with points.

It was to Orsini that he put his next question. He spoke in English.

"From which part of England do you come?"

"From Kent."

"How far is Kent from London?"

"Thirty leagues."

Those two words sufficed. In England one speaks of miles, not of leagues. Moreover, Orsini had pronounced his "thirty" by converting the sound of the *th* into a *t*, and causing the *r* to ring out vigorously — which is not in the habit of English mouths.

Said the Magistrate: "You are as English as I am a Turk. Indeed, to tell you the full truth, you are not even French."

The pale face of Felice Orsini flashed. He drew himself up, disdainful in the Roman manner. He said — nay, he cried: "Ah no, by God! I am Italian and proud of it. My name is Felice Orsini. Ask me nothing further; I shall not answer you."

He had poured his very soul into his words — the soul of the noble and strong man who commands the pygmies of character. They were subdued by it and dared not oppose him. He was forthwith conducted elsewhere. But the Prefect of Police, who throughout the interrogation had remained standing and motionless beside his desk, now fell to rummaging feverishly in the drawers. "Ah, ah!" he said at length, advancing towards the uniformed and civilian personages alike. "Here is a secret circular from Austria. Felice Orsini — the dangerous revolutionary who escaped from the castle of Mantova. We have made a good catch!"

He then returned to his desk, and turning to me: "Monsieur da Sylva, do you know these implements?"

I looked, and it was as though a ray of light illuminated the cells of my brain. Now I understood why Napoleon lived and triumphed! Two of the "implements" which should, by their explosion, have given the signal for awakening to two Peoples, lay idle, like paperweights, upon the Prefect's desk. By what means had they arrived there? There they were — that was sufficient for me. Their dimensions were equal; on both, at the tips of the lower half, the deadly detonating caps still glittered. An inexplicable imprudence!

That sight sufficed to rouse, in my breast, the furies which the predominant thought of one's own preservation had, for a time, lulled to sleep. And my every fibre swelled again with turbulent spirit; my every fibre recovered an impulse of rebellion.

I thought: "Perhaps that which has not been obtained by direct means may be obtained indirectly, by bringing fresh confusion into the enemy's camp, undoubtedly already thrown into disorder — and why not? But in any case — to kill as a human protest, avenging the betrayed and the oppressed!"

I do not say, now, that my thought was that of a measured man; I say that such was my thought. And I say that even today I hold supremely human the cry: "Let Samson perish, and with him the Philistines!"

I rose and bent my body forward, half-closing my eyes, as though I were short-sighted. Two steps — only two steps without arousing suspicion — and I should have made a spring, a rapid movement, a thunderbolt — guards, Prefect, Magistrate, generals, myself — ambitions and hopes, servility and lust for power, crimes and virtues — a few shreds of flesh — nothingness —

A hand, heavy as fate, seized me by the shoulder, and I fell back into the chair. My heart was beating convulsively, but I made an effort and said in a composed and almost perplexed tone: "I do not understand — what is the matter?"

From that moment my interrogation proceeded rapidly and according to my wishes. In giving the final touches to the account of my actions, I took care to transpose, so to speak, a few minutes, and made my entry into the café coincide precisely with the hour of the attempt. I alluded also to the letter I had written there. The game was dangerous; but the landlady had not seen me enter, and upon that I was counting. The Magistrate whispered a few words into a guard's ear, and the latter departed forthwith. I gathered from this that a verification was to be carried out. No great time passed before the same guard returned and fully confirmed my deposition, recounting even the incident of the four two-centime stamps which I had forgotten. The Magistrate then turned to the Prefect and said to him: "For my part I have no further question to put."

"Very well!" the latter observed, "but Monsieur da Sylva might prove a valuable witness. I would request him to remain at his hotel, at the disposal and expense of the State. His passport will be returned to him later. No objection?"

"None whatsoever — particularly if the State undertakes the expenses. My means are extremely limited!"

And for the third time that night, I heard the French Police lavish apologies upon me. The high personages, uniformed and otherwise, fell to conversing among themselves, glancing at me from time to time as though exchanging their impressions on my account. And the Magistrate, having wished me good night, turned to the guards at my sides. His last words were in Portuguese: "Monsieur da Sylva outside the Prefecture!"

As I was crossing the various rooms, my thoughts retraced my steps and I considered the extraordinary perspicacity of a Magistrate who let the wolf escape from the trap in such a fashion. It would have sufficed for him to have questioned me regarding my place of origin; to have asked me with which clients I had dealt in my capacity as seller of Burton Ale; to have addressed, or caused to be addressed to me, a single word in Portuguese — and my true nature would have been laid bare and naked. From this I concluded that Magistrate and Police found themselves in a state of over-excitement, perhaps also of vague apprehension, which clouded their vision and halved their dynamic power.

Yet he who was off guard tonight, I thought, might be wide awake and watchful tomorrow — Tomorrow? Let me but set foot out of doors again and shrewd is he who finds me! The Gare d'Orléans is watched; the same must hold of the other stations — but many roads lead out of Paris, and Paris is vast, and never has it lacked for friends and hiding-places.

I had reached the last room and there I found the unexpected. Through the door, which had opened to grant liberty to the wolf, there entered at that very moment, under close escort, Antonio Gomez. I looked away at once, the only means within my power of letting him understand that he must be ignorant of my presence. But his own wits were likewise clouded, and, passing close by me, he murmured in a low voice, in French: "We are lost!"

It was my fate that among his escort there should be an intelligent man. "Ah, ah! You know each other?" And Gomez, driving home the nail that was being hammered into me: "Of course we know each other!"

I had hastened to set foot upon the threshold. "Halt!" a voice thundered behind me. I was invited to sit down and the door closed. Gomez continued on his way to the great hall; I remained in the trap in great anxiety, biting my lips. Ten minutes passed. A clock whirred and struck four strokes. Other guards came. They took me by the arm, without opening their mouths, and led me away. We traversed an underground passage.

I passed the rest of the night in a cell of the Conciergerie!

Beside the desk, some seated, some standing, were various high dignitaries of the army, their chests encumbered with medals and, among those gaudy uniforms, a few beetles in civilian dress gloomed sadly. Among these last, I recognized a personage of the imperial family... Plon-Plon! There were guards of different ranks and in different postures and, as I scanned those gaudy uniforms with my eyes, now here, now there, with the intensity of one who plumbs the abyss by which he might be swallowed, I saw standing out from the red-backed walls, as if emerging from them at that very point, several marble busts that fixed me with their pupilless eyes. And the men too fixed me without batting an eyelid, as if they partook of the marmoreal nature of those busts. The Examining Magistrate rose and advanced toward me, and his countenance was not new to me. He had a history. In the days when Louis Napoleon was low, he had trampled upon the Laws of which he was, now, the salaried champion. He owed his position to the old services rendered to the Emperor. At a nod from him I sat, and two guards, who stood near the door, came and stood erect at my sides.

Then the magistrate asked for my particulars, and I answered in accordance with the passport that they had confiscated from me. But when he asked me if I knew my roommate and I had answered in the negative, his face became mirthful.

— How can that be — he asked — you sleep with an individual and do not even know who he is? Where did you meet him for the first time?

— During my journey from Calais to Paris. It was the first time I had come to this city and I asked him for the address of a hotel. "Come down to mine," he answered me. "I have a room with two beds. He understands my language a little. You will spend less." I accepted. He told me his name, I told him mine. That is all! Why should I have asked him for an account of his past?

— But what did you come here to do?

— To earn my bread, by trading in Burton Ale.

— How did you pass yesterday? I had already studied my answer by heart which, for that matter, differed little from the truth. Monsieur Pierey, as a person familiar with Paris, had taken me in the morning to the Louvre Gallery. Later Pierey himself had gone to visit some acquaintance of his and I had waited for him in an inn. Then I recounted the dinner we had at the restaurant on the rue Montmartre.

— At what time did you dine?

— Around seven. We returned to our hotel. Scarcely had we arrived when the porter told Monsieur Pierey that a gentleman had come looking for him... he should wait for him, he would return shortly. And so it happened. That is all!

— Who was that gentleman?

— I do not know. He had come for the other and not for me. Besides, I noticed that he wanted to speak of business; out of prudence I moved away.

— Where did you go?

— To the cabinet at the end of the corridor. When I returned, the gentleman had left.

— But do you not remember his countenance?

— The room was dark. I recall, however, that he wore a thick beard.

— Would you recognize him if you saw him?

— Perhaps yes, perhaps no.

At this point, the judge turned to a guard and ordered him to let "the other prisoner" enter. When the door reopened, on the threshold appeared Felice Orsini. The bandage that swathed his head was soaked in blood. He had a weak and tired air. His first glance was for me. He seemed to address a mute question to me, and on his countenance I saw a fleeting disquiet pass.

— Is this he? the Judge asked me.

— The man who came to the hotel wore a thick beard. I believe I have said so.

Then the judge left the desk and, drawing near to Orsini, ran the tips of his fingers under his chin. He wanted to make sure that the razor had not passed there recently. Fortunately, the prisoner's skin must have seemed to him, to the touch, bristling with points.

It was to Orsini that he addressed his next question. He spoke in English.

— From what part of England do you come?

— From Kent.

— How far is Kent from London?

— Thirty leagues.

Those two words were enough. In England one speaks of miles, not of leagues. Furthermore, Orsini had pronounced his "thirty" changing the sound of the 'th' to a 't' and strongly sounding the 'r'... which is not in the habits of English mouths.

The Judge said: "You are as English as I am a Turk. Indeed, to tell you the whole truth, you are not even French."

The pale face of Felice Orsini flashed. He drew himself up with Roman disdain. He said, nay shouted: "Ah no, by God! I am Italian and I am proud of it. My name is Felice Orsini. Do not ask me more, I will not answer you."

He had transfused his soul into his words, the soul of the noble and strong man who commands the pygmies of character. They were subjugated by it and did not dare to oppose him. He was, without another word, led elsewhere. But the Prefect of Police who, during the interrogation, had remained standing and motionless near his desk, began to search feverishly in the drawers. "Ah, ha!" he said at last, advancing toward the personages with and without uniform, "Here is a secret circular from Austria. Felice Orsini... the dangerous revolutionary who escaped from the castle of Mantova. We have made a good catch!"

Then he returned to his desk and turning to me: "Monsieur da Sylva, do you know these implements?"

I looked, and it was as if a ray of light illuminated the cells of my brain. Now I understood why Napoleon lived and triumphed! Two of the "implements" that should have, with their explosion, given the signal of awakening to two Peoples, lay idle, like paperweights, on the Prefect's desk. In what way had they arrived there? They were there!... that was enough for me. Their dimensions... equal; on both, the deadly percussion caps still gleamed on the points of the lower half. Inexplicable imprudence!

That sight was enough to arouse, in my breast, the furies that the predominant thought of my own salvation had, for a moment, lulled to sleep. And every fibre of me became swollen again with a turbulent spirit; every fibre of me regained an impulse of rebellion.

I thought: "Perhaps, what has not been obtained by direct means, will be obtained indirectly, by bringing new confusion into the enemy camp, undoubtedly already in disarray... and why not? But in any case... to kill as a human protest, avenging the betrayed and the oppressed!"

I do not say, now, that my thought was that of a prudent man; I say that such was my thought. And I say that even today, I deem supremely human the cry: "Perish Samson, and with him the Philistines!"

I rose and bent my body forward, half-closing my eyes, as if I were myopic. Two steps, only two steps without arousing suspicion and I would have made a leap... a swift motion, the thunderbolt... guards, Prefect, Judge, generals, myself... ambitions and hopes, servility and lust for command, crimes and virtues... a few shreds of flesh... nothingness...

A hand, heavy as fate, seized my shoulder and I fell back upon the chair. My heart was beating convulsively, but I made an effort and said in a calm and almost perplexed tone: "I do not understand!... what is this about?"

From that moment my interrogation proceeded rapidly and according to my wishes. In giving the final touches to the account of my actions, I took care to transpose, so to speak, a few minutes, and I made my entry into the café coincide precisely with the hour of the attack. I also alluded to the letter I wrote there. The game was dangerous; but the proprietress had not seen me enter, and I was counting on this. The Judge whispered a few words in the ear of a guard who immediately departed. I deduced that they wished to proceed with a verification. Not much time passed and the same guard, having returned, fully confirmed my deposition, recounting even the incident of the four two-franc stamps that I had forgotten. The Judge then turned to the Prefect and said to him: "For my part I have no other question to ask."

"Very well!" the latter observed to him. "But Monsieur da Sylva could be a precious witness. I would ask him to remain at his hotel at the disposal and at the expense of the State. We will deliver his passport to him later. No objection?"

— None at all, and especially if the State takes on the expenses. My means are most limited!

And for the third time that night, I heard the French Police lavish their apologies. The high personages, with and without uniform, struck up a conversation among themselves, glancing at me from time to time as if communicating their impressions about me. And the Judge, having wished me good night, turned to the guards who stood at my sides. His last words were in Portuguese: "Monsieur da Sylva fuori della Prefettura!"

As I crossed the various rooms my thought went back over my steps, and I considered the extraordinary perspicacity of a Judge who thus let the wolf escape from the trap. It would have been enough for him to have questioned me on my place of origin; for him to have asked me with which clients I had dealt in my capacity as a seller of Burton Ale; for him to have addressed, or had addressed to me, a single word in Portuguese, and my true nature would have been revealed and laid bare. I concluded that the Judge and the Police were in a state of over-excitement, perhaps even of vague apprehension that clouded their vision, that halved their dynamic power.

However, he who is asleep tonight—I thought—could be awake and on the alert tomorrow... Tomorrow? Let me but set foot outside once more, and he who finds me again will be a cunning one! The Gare d'Orléans is under close watch; the same must be true of the other stations... but various roads lead out of Paris, and Paris is vast, and one neither enters it nor lacks for friends and hiding places within it.

I had reached the last room and there I found the unexpected... From the door, which had opened to grant the wolf his freedom, entered at that very moment, under heavy guard, Antonio Gomez. I immediately looked elsewhere, the only means at my disposal to make him understand that he should ignore my presence. But his comprehension too was clouded and, passing beside me, he murmured in a low voice, in French: "We are lost!"

My destiny willed that, among his escort, there should be an intelligent man. "Ah, ha! you know each other?" And Gomez, driving home the nail that was pinning me: "Of course, we know each other!"

I had hastened to set my foot on the threshold. "Halt!" a voice thundered behind me. I was invited to sit down and the door closed. Gomez continued his way to the great hall; I remained in the trap in great anxiety, biting my lips. Ten minutes passed. A pendulum clock creaked and struck four chimes. Other guards came. They took me by the arm, without opening their mouths, and led me away. We traversed a subterranean passage.

## Chapter the Eleventh

SCARCELY had I found myself out of danger than my first thought was for Elisa. I had made my way towards the Boulevard des Italiens… and entered a café. I must recount things that will seem trifles; yet they had their weight. The café was empty. The proprietress, behind the counter, was speaking in a frenzied manner with a customer who stood before her. She did not see me enter and took no notice of my presence until I asked her, if she would be so kind, for paper and inkwell. I wrote briefly: “Elisa, all is well. I shall leave tomorrow for Oporto.” Having sealed the letter, I returned the inkwell to the counter and asked for an eight-sou stamp. The lady rummaged in a drawer and, holding them out to me, asked whether four two-sou ones would not serve my purpose.

I made my way to the Place de la Concorde. It was deserted. I stood long at my post on the bridge. The thousand sounds of the city reached me confusedly, the humming of an immense hive. The Seine, made darker by the restless reflections of the gas-lamps, flowed towards the sea mysteriously, like the hour that hung above us. None of the conspirators appeared. When to wait longer would have been madness, I rid myself of the revolver by casting it into the river, and set off towards the Boulevard, resolved to reconnoitre the ground.

During that passage, every faint breath of voice caught upon the air descended into my breast like a gust of storm. The noble daring, the boundless hopes, the pertinacity to descry a thread of blue sky in the most cheerless of situations; the instinctive faith in the slow but certain justice of things — all these were as if uprooted, ground to dust, and scattered into nothingness. Napoleon III was alive!…

Sheltered by the iron plates of his carriage, he had remained therein as though paralysed until the bursting of the third bomb… Then all at once he appeared in the theatre, erect in the imperial box, ashen as a corpse. At his side was Eugenia Montijo, deathly pale yet beautiful still, and a few drops of blood scored her brow with crimson. At the frantic applause that broke out on all sides, she had let herself sink into her chair, her breast heaving, as though she had all at once recovered the only atmosphere capable of breathing life into her with full intensity. But Napoleon had remained standing. The sensation of being Emperor once more had restored colour to that pulsing cadaver, which now acknowledged the ovation, bowing complacently to right and left.

Outside, meanwhile, the Police and the most trusted battalions were making his road to the Palace secure.

Thus fell the intended revolution; thus the Empire remained, in all its integrity, stronger perhaps than before. Napoleon III clinging to France more than ever, like a vampire; France interposed, more than ever in my reckoning, between Italy and her independence and her greatness. And I asked myself, trembling, whether it were the inexorable law of Nature that the vulgarity and the treachery and the corruption and the cupidity of the few must hold eternal dominion in Europe; whether noble aspirations mocked, courage, persecution, misery, the halter must be, in eternity, the only heritage of the righteous.

And I despaired. But not as the man who, paralysed by destiny, does nothing but gnaw his own hands. Not a single fibre was there in me, in those moments, that was not swollen with turbulent spirit, that was not all impulse and rebellion. Had I found the occasion, I feel I could have killed even without a lofty purpose; simply as a human protest; to die, perhaps, but avenging the humble and the oppressed…

I resolved to return to the Hôtel France et Champagne. On the way I had occasion to persuade myself that the situation was growing murky… public opinion, as it revealed itself from the groups scattered here and there, was already attributing the attempt to foreigners. It was no longer merely a matter of renewing the unequal struggle; one had also to think of saving one’s neck. And perhaps from that very moment I should have seen to my own safety, had it not seemed to me an act of cowardice to depart without ascertaining at least the fate of my room-companion.

Nothing out of the ordinary at the hotel. Pieri had not returned… the room in precisely the order in which we had left it. I prepared my valise — the one with the quilted tiger — went down to the office and asked for my bill. From that moment events came thick and fast.

While the proprietress was confirming, in the register, the date of my arrival, I settled myself at a small table and began to leaf through an illustrated newspaper. Just then, several individuals entered noisily… plain-clothes policemen; their uniformly cropped hair was enough to betray them. They asked leave to inspect the register. One of them, the leader in all likelihood, leaning towards the lady, murmured: “Who is the Monsieur who is reading?” The lady indicated a point on the register with the tip of her pen. A dozen eyes fixed themselves upon me with such insistence that I felt their gaze driven into my skin, and the maître d’hôtel came and, with every show of deference, asked for my passport. I drew it out, unfolded it… the policemen took possession of it, scanned it, looked at one another. “Pardon, monsieur!” And they departed.

They departed; but from where I sat, I caught with the corner of my eye two hounds remaining on guard at the entrance. Was I to consider myself a prisoner in that hotel? The proprietress came with the bill, and I was compelled to sustain the conversation in a gallant vein which she was pleased to strike up. All at once I sighed; yet not for the reason perhaps imagined by my fair temptress. Glancing beyond the window, I had seen the policemen move away. The way was clear!

And I took advantage of it. Slowly at first, so as not to arouse suspicion; then, when I was quite certain I was not being watched, off at very nearly a running pace.

I was by now persuaded that Pieri had thought to get himself out of Paris. I came upon a public cab and secured it for myself: “Gare d’Orléans… a good tip if you get there in time!” The coachman laid on his whip as though he meant to flay the hide off the nag. We arrived… it was eleven o’clock… four minutes too late!… no train for Bordeaux until four in the morning!

What was to be done? Doze at the station? Seek hospitality at the house of Zappa, or with some other trustworthy friend? I decided to return to the hotel. The Police had already poked their nose in there… a circumstance which seemed to me sufficient, in itself, to render it an inviolable refuge.

And I set off on foot, my valise hanging on my arm. I had gone some five hundred paces, or thereabouts, when there came galloping up at full tilt a squadron of municipal guards. It swept past me like a whirlwind, slackened pace at the station, surrounded it, and enclosed the passengers within a ring of iron. I thought: “If the woodland bird was slow to take flight, the hunters are no less slow in setting their snare.”

At midnight I was in my room. I had left orders to be wakened in good time; I had turned off the gas; I had stretched myself out, as is my custom in such circumstances, without disrobing in the least. And I was already promising myself that I would take good counsel from the night, when I heard the bell of the street door ring. My room window gave onto the courtyard. I sprang to it, and down in the depths I made out a dark group of figures upon whom I could harbour no illusion; though, had I been disposed to, it would not have lasted long. In the silence of the night, there reached my ear the words: “We wish to see the foreigner in number 53!” The number of my room.

In haste I undressed and thrust myself under the covers. To make a greater show of untroubled composure, I had left the door ajar. The guards reached it accompanied by the maître d’hôtel; they entered by the scant light of a candle, turned on the gas… I felt a gentle touch upon one shoulder. I thereupon ceased to snore. “Forgive us! We must do our duty. Your name? Would you be so good as to furnish us with your passport?” They examine it minutely, then look at the maître d’hôtel: “But is this not the very gentleman with whom we spoke an hour ago, in your office? A night of misunderstandings indeed! Pardon, monsieur!” I heard their step recede along the corridor and fade away down the staircase.

But it was my fate not to rest in my bed that night. Half an hour later they were back, and this time they dispensed with ceremony. “A certain Monsieur Pierey,” they said, “who lodged in this very room, declares himself to be a friend of yours. He is at the Prefecture, in safe hands. You must resign yourself to keeping him company. We have orders to arrest you!”

While I dress, two policemen stand at my sides, at attention; others busy themselves picking the lock of our chest of drawers. From it they drew the revolver case, which Pieri had tossed there, and some cartridges. “What is this?”

— I do not know! The wardrobe is for the exclusive use of the gentleman who lodged in this room. Of my own I have nothing but this bag.

They felt it, rummaged through it, drew out, piece by piece, the little it contained; but the dagger concealed between the small board and the bottom escaped their investigation.

On arriving at the Prefecture, another blow awaited me. I had been conveyed there in a cab, with two guardian angels at my side, a third seated on the box with the coachman, a fourth behind the carriage. I was on the point of alighting and had not yet set my foot upon the step, when another laden carriage entered the courtyard at a trot. I make for the grand staircase and, seized by a dark presentiment, turn my head. A few paces behind me, preceded and followed by a guard, came a man who at that moment surely bore within his breast all the sorrows of his Italy. His face was pale, his side-whiskers black, his head bound in bandages… Felice Orsini!
