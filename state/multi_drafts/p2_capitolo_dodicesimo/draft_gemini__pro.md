They had me cross three rooms occupied, despite the hour, by clerks intent at their desks. Having reached a fourth, I sat for a long while before a fireplace, upon which two embers burned in a cross. And as, to better concentrate, I had closed my eyes, I heard a guard say in a low voice: "False alarm! If he were an accomplice, he would not sleep so!"

At last they drew me into a great hall, whose great light for a moment dazzled me. The first distinctly perceived impression was that of the door closing at my back; then I saw outlined before me—it is necessary that I lose myself in these particulars—a writing-desk strewn with papers, perfectly isolated in the middle of the hall. An old man apparently in his sixties, with a still energetic appearance, with smooth skin, full of gleaming reflections, was seated to one side of it and was scanning, with a frown, a document of his. At my appearance he spread it upon his knees and raised his face. It was the Prefect of Police.

Another writing-desk stood four paces away along the right-hand wall. Its supports were so high as to let me perceive, of the man who sat there, the top of his skull and nothing more... the skull of the Examining Magistrate. Beside the desk, some seated, some standing, were various high dignitaries of the army, their chests encumbered with medals and, among those gaudy uniforms, a few beetles in civilian dress gloomed sadly. Among these last, I recognized a personage of the imperial family... Plon-Plon! There were guards of different ranks and in different postures and, as I scanned those gaudy uniforms with my eyes, now here, now there, with the intensity of one who plumbs the abyss by which he might be swallowed, I saw standing out from the red-backed walls, as if emerging from them at that very point, several marble busts that fixed me with their pupilless eyes. And the men too fixed me without batting an eyelid, as if they partook of the marmoreal nature of those busts. The Examining Magistrate rose and advanced toward me, and his countenance was not new to me. He had a history. In the days when Louis Napoleon was low, he had trampled upon the Laws of which he was, now, the salaried champion. He owed his position to the old services rendered to the Emperor. At a nod from him I sat, and two guards, who stood near the door, came and stood erect at my sides.

Then the magistrate asked for my particulars, and I answered in accordance with the passport that they had confiscated from me. But when he asked me if I knew my roommate and I had answered in the negative, his face became mirthful.

— How can that be — he asked — you sleep with an individual and do not even know who he is? Where did you meet him for the first time?

— During my journey from Calais to Paris. It was the first time I had come to this city and I asked him for the address of a hotel. "Come down to mine," he answered me. "I have a room with two beds. He understands my language a little. You will spend less." I accepted. He told me his name, I told him mine. That is all! Why should I have asked him for an account of his past?

— But what did you come here to do?

— To earn my bread, by trading in Burton Ale.

— How did you pass yesterday? I had already studied my answer by heart which, for that matter, differed little from the truth. Monsieur Pierey, as a person familiar with Paris, had taken me in the morning to the Louvre Gallery. Later Pierey himself had gone to visit some acquaintance of his and I had waited for him in an inn. Then I recounted the dinner we had at the restaurant on the rue Montmartre.

— At what time did you dine?

— Around seven. We returned to our hotel. Scarcely had we arrived when the porter told Monsieur Pierey that a gentleman had come looking for him... he should wait for him, he would return shortly. And so it happened. That is all!

— Who was that gentleman?

— I do not know. He had come for the other and not for me. Besides, I noticed that he wanted to speak of business; out of prudence I moved away.

— Where did you go?

— To the cabinet at the end of the corridor. When I returned, the gentleman had left.

— But do you not remember his countenance?

— The room was dark. I recall, however, that he wore a thick beard.

— Would you recognize him if you saw him?

— Perhaps yes, perhaps no.

At this point, the judge turned to a guard and ordered him to let "the other prisoner" enter. When the door reopened, on the threshold appeared Felice Orsini. The bandage that swathed his head was soaked in blood. He had a weak and tired air. His first glance was for me. He seemed to address a mute question to me, and on his countenance I saw a fleeting disquiet pass.

— Is this he? the Judge asked me.

— The man who came to the hotel wore a thick beard. I believe I have said so.

Then the judge left the desk and, drawing near to Orsini, ran the tips of his fingers under his chin. He wanted to make sure that the razor had not passed there recently. Fortunately, the prisoner's skin must have seemed to him, to the touch, bristling with points.

It was to Orsini that he addressed his next question. He spoke in English.

— From what part of England do you come?

— From Kent.

— How far is Kent from London?

— Thirty leagues.

Those two words were enough. In England one speaks of miles, not of leagues. Furthermore, Orsini had pronounced his "thirty" changing the sound of the 'th' to a 't' and strongly sounding the 'r'... which is not in the habits of English mouths.

The Judge said: "You are as English as I am a Turk. Indeed, to tell you the whole truth, you are not even French."

The pale face of Felice Orsini flashed. He drew himself up with Roman disdain. He said, nay shouted: "Ah no, by God! I am Italian and I am proud of it. My name is Felice Orsini. Do not ask me more, I will not answer you."

He had transfused his soul into his words, the soul of the noble and strong man who commands the pygmies of character. They were subjugated by it and did not dare to oppose him. He was, without another word, led elsewhere. But the Prefect of Police who, during the interrogation, had remained standing and motionless near his desk, began to search feverishly in the drawers. "Ah, ha!" he said at last, advancing toward the personages with and without uniform, "Here is a secret circular from Austria. Felice Orsini... the dangerous revolutionary who escaped from the castle of Mantova. We have made a good catch!"

Then he returned to his desk and turning to me: "Monsieur da Sylva, do you know these implements?"

I looked, and it was as if a ray of light illuminated the cells of my brain. Now I understood why Napoleon lived and triumphed! Two of the "implements" that should have, with their explosion, given the signal of awakening to two Peoples, lay idle, like paperweights, on the Prefect's desk. In what way had they arrived there? They were there!... that was enough for me. Their dimensions... equal; on both, the deadly percussion caps still gleamed on the points of the lower half. Inexplicable imprudence!

That sight was enough to arouse, in my breast, the furies that the predominant thought of my own salvation had, for a moment, lulled to sleep. And every fibre of me became swollen again with a turbulent spirit; every fibre of me regained an impulse of rebellion.

I thought: "Perhaps, what has not been obtained by direct means, will be obtained indirectly, by bringing new confusion into the enemy camp, undoubtedly already in disarray... and why not? But in any case... to kill as a human protest, avenging the betrayed and the oppressed!"

I do not say, now, that my thought was that of a prudent man; I say that such was my thought. And I say that even today, I deem supremely human the cry: "Perish Samson, and with him the Philistines!"

I rose and bent my body forward, half-closing my eyes, as if I were myopic. Two steps, only two steps without arousing suspicion and I would have made a leap... a swift motion, the thunderbolt... guards, Prefect, Judge, generals, myself... ambitions and hopes, servility and lust for command, crimes and virtues... a few shreds of flesh... nothingness...

A hand, heavy as fate, seized my shoulder and I fell back upon the chair. My heart was beating convulsively, but I made an effort and said in a calm and almost perplexed tone: "I do not understand!... what is this about?"

From that moment my interrogation proceeded rapidly and according to my wishes. In giving the final touches to the account of my actions, I took care to transpose, so to speak, a few minutes, and I made my entry into the café coincide precisely with the hour of the attack. I also alluded to the letter I wrote there. The game was dangerous; but the proprietress had not seen me enter, and I was counting on this. The Judge whispered a few words in the ear of a guard who immediately departed. I deduced that they wished to proceed with a verification. Not much time passed and the same guard, having returned, fully confirmed my deposition, recounting even the incident of the four two-franc stamps that I had forgotten. The Judge then turned to the Prefect and said to him: "For my part I have no other question to ask."

"Very well!" the latter observed to him. "But Monsieur da Sylva could be a precious witness. I would ask him to remain at his hotel at the disposal and at the expense of the State. We will deliver his passport to him later. No objection?"

— None at all, and especially if the State takes on the expenses. My means are most limited!

And for the third time that night, I heard the French Police lavish their apologies. The high personages, with and without uniform, struck up a conversation among themselves, glancing at me from time to time as if communicating their impressions about me. And the Judge, having wished me good night, turned to the guards who stood at my sides. His last words were in Portuguese: "Monsieur da Sylva fuori della Prefettura!"

As I crossed the various rooms my thought went back over my steps, and I considered the extraordinary perspicacity of a Judge who thus let the wolf escape from the trap. It would have been enough for him to have questioned me on my place of origin; for him to have asked me with which clients I had dealt in my capacity as a seller of Burton Ale; for him to have addressed, or had addressed to me, a single word in Portuguese, and my true nature would have been revealed and laid bare. I concluded that the Judge and the Police were in a state of over-excitement, perhaps even of vague apprehension that clouded their vision, that halved their dynamic power.

However, he who is asleep tonight—I thought—could be awake and on the alert tomorrow... Tomorrow? Let me but set foot outside once more, and he who finds me again will be a cunning one! The Gare d'Orléans is under close watch; the same must be true of the other stations... but various roads lead out of Paris, and Paris is vast, and one neither enters it nor lacks for friends and hiding places within it.

I had reached the last room and there I found the unexpected... From the door, which had opened to grant the wolf his freedom, entered at that very moment, under heavy guard, Antonio Gomez. I immediately looked elsewhere, the only means at my disposal to make him understand that he should ignore my presence. But his comprehension too was clouded and, passing beside me, he murmured in a low voice, in French: "We are lost!"

My destiny willed that, among his escort, there should be an intelligent man. "Ah, ha! you know each other?" And Gomez, driving home the nail that was pinning me: "Of course, we know each other!"

I had hastened to set my foot on the threshold. "Halt!" a voice thundered behind me. I was invited to sit down and the door closed. Gomez continued his way to the great hall; I remained in the trap in great anxiety, biting my lips. Ten minutes passed. A pendulum clock creaked and struck four chimes. Other guards came. They took me by the arm, without opening their mouths, and led me away. We traversed a subterranean passage.
