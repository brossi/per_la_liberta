LA sera del dodici, il cappellano Hughon venne a farmi la solita visita. Era grave, grave, grave. Gli parlai del nostro appello respinto ed egli divenne impacciato. Allora mi balenò il sospetto della triste realtà e ricorsi, per accertarmene, a parecchi sotterfugi. Ma l'Hughon assunse prontamente l'aria dell'ingenuo e, al riparo di quel semplice artificio, divenne inattaccabile. Forse ogni mio sospetto svaporava se, nel congedarsi, il mio visitatore non mi avesse stesa la mano. A me parve che quelle dita segnassero nell'aria segni misteriosi, tanto visibilmente tremavano.

Aspettai il ritorno di Saturno che, delle due guardie, mi sembrava la più schietta e, senza dargli neppure il tempo di levarsi il berretto: "Saturno — dissi — so che domattina verrò tratto al supplizio. Dà retta e fa, tu che sei buono, di eseguire appuntino la mia ultima volontà. Alle cinque mi sveglierai."

Qui egli incominciò a protestare. Perchè mi ero ficcata nella mente simile balordaggine? Respinto l'appello, restava ancora, grazie alle nostre protezioni, molto filo da torcere e prima che... eh, eh!... ce ne sarebbe voluto!

— Amico mio, monsieur Hughon voleva confessarmi e invece... io ho confessato lui. Egli mi ha rivelato tutto!

A questo, Saturno, che per dare enfasi a quel suo eh, eh!... gesticolava ancora, lasciò cascare il braccio, come se glie lo avessi stronco. "Vi ha tutto rivelato? Ciò è contrario ai Regolamenti e non avrebbe dovuto farlo." Stette alquanto ad occhi bassi, poi mormorò: "Dal momento che sapete tutto... orbene sì! ormai altro non vi resta se non che il morire virilmente, come avete vissuto."

Allora nel mio animo si fece improvvisamente un gran silenzio e quasi un gran buio; ma in quella specie di vuoto, la vita si manifestava potentemente ancora per un istinto che mi sospingeva ad anelare qualcosa di più buio, di più silenzioso. "Tanto meglio quanto più presto!..." è l'espressione d'Orsini; solo allora io ne compresi l'intero significato.

"Alle cinque precise! ho molto, molto da scrivere. I miei ultimi pensieri, dal carcere, ai miei parenti ed all'Elisa; i miei ultimi pensieri, dal palco, alla Patria... E tu?... Piglia!" (lo baciai). "Domani, a quest'ora, pensa a me. Dirai: Le miserie umane non lo toccano più!"

Saturno fu puntuale. La mia prima sensazione fu causata, quel giorno memorando, dal pallore estremo del suo volto. Scrissi a mio padre; scrissi ai parenti di mia moglie, raccomandando loro, dal profondo, quell'infelice. Le sei suonarono all'orologio della Roquette.

Poco dopo, dieci o dodici persone entrarono nel camerone attiguo. Riconobbi, tra essi, il cappellano Hughon e il Direttore del carcere. Si fecero alla cella di Orsini e li udii annunciargli la sua ultima ora. Il Direttore del carcere pronunciò poi, a bassa voce, poche frasi, il cui significato mi fu palese dalla risposta d'Orsini. Un giudice era pronto a ricevere le rivelazioni del morituro e lo si era esortato ad una confessione generale. La risposta del martire risuonò nel silenzio sepolcrale, sommessa ma distinta: "Felice Orsini sa morire, non sa essere delatore!"

Venne poi la volta del Pieri e i suoi nervi ebbero uno scatto. "Era vecchio, era legato; ma gli sarebbe bastato l'animo..."

Il Direttore del carcere lo ammonì cortesemente ed egli subito si rimise. "Tant'è — disse — morire subito, morire fra qualche anno, che importa? Anche i miei carnefici morranno..." Aggiunse che si proponeva di precipitare nell'eternità cantando.

Venne la mia volta. Risposi pacatamente che il Conte Carlo di Rudio era pronto; espressione che poi, sui resoconti ufficiali, venne attribuita all'Orsini. Chiuse appena le labbra, delineai più nettamente, nella penombra, l'uomo che aveva accompagnato il Direttore delle carceri entro la mia cella. Era un vecchio abate... Una perla, forse. Ma a me tanto mal predisposto per tutto quanto ricorda la chierica, fece l'effetto di un nemico venuto ad accertare, de visu, i miei ultimi tratti. E poiché la morte era vicina e sicura, ben potevo, senza tema di recar danno a chicchessia, dar libero sfogo al mio sentire. Egli mi si avvicinò e fece per parlare. Mi immaginai volesse esortarmi a morire cristianamente e lo prevenni: "Preferisco — dissi — consacrare a mia moglie e al bimbo mio, i miei ultimi pensieri. Quanto al vostro Dio, lo vedrò, se esiste, ma per narrargli da che razza di ministri ha la dabbenaggine di farsi rappresentare su questa terra."

Fummo tratti nel camerone. Le nostre mani vennero svincolate. Gravi e da gente tragicamente commossa, i volti... il resto, quasi scena d'amici che salutino amici, alla vigilia di un lungo viaggio.

Il Pieri, ben deciso a voler considerare la cosa come un avvenimento ordinario, non volle rinunciare alla sua colazione. Furono dati gli ordini opportuni. Nell'attesa, si svolse persino un po' di presentazione. Seppi per tal modo, che l'Hughon sarebbe il confortatore spirituale dell'Orsini; il cappellano della Conciergerie, quello del Pieri; l'abate messo alle mie costole era cappellano della Casa dei Discoli.

Fu portato un elegante cabaret, con caffè e rhum. Felice Orsini sorseggiò di quest'ultimo, brindando alla salute del Direttore delle carceri. A questo punto, il mio "confortatore", sorpassando umanamente sulla mia ruvida ripulsa, mi offerse un bicchierino; ma ebbe il torto di osservare che mi avrebbe infuso coraggio. Rifiutai per orgoglio, ribattendo che i patrioti italiani non avevan bisogno di coraggio artificiale. Poi fu una gara nel chiederci tenui ricordi. I tre carnefici, ritti e taciturni, lungo la parete, ci osservavano con una strana insistenza.

D'improvviso il Direttore trasse l'orologio e fece un cenno alle guardie. I Regolamenti, incuranti delle coscienze, ci imponevano di assistere alla Messa. Felice Orsini si avviò mestamente verso la cappella del carcere. Io protestai ed il Pieri aggiunse, al mio secco rifiuto, la sua irruente loquela. Gli astanti si guardarono in viso e il Direttore alzò le spalle e finì per transigere. L'assenza dell'Orsini fu brevissima. Non appena egli fece ritorno, ci avviarono verso la Cancelleria, d'onde passammo in una camera attigua. Nell'entrarvi il Pieri, che mi precedeva di alcuni passi, si volse e disse all'Orsini: "Ebbene, vecchio mio?" L'Orsini alzò il capo, lo guardò fisso e rispose: "Calma, calma!" Eran le prime parole che quei due uomini, affratellati dagli ideali e dal martirio, si scambiavano dopo il loro arresto... e furono l'ultime!

Eravamo del carnefice. Mi raserò la nuca; mi fecero indossare una camicia scollata; mi scalzarono colle loro proprie mani. Identici preparativi, eseguiti rapidamente, lasciarono l'Orsini assorto nei suoi pensieri; ma il facondo lucchese ne divenne quasi ilare... stranezza dei nervi! Egli volle specchiarsi e il suo carnefice, damerino probabilmente, malgrado il turpe mestiere, trasse uno specchietto dal taschino e glie lo porse. Si guardò a suo agio e, sorridendo, esclamò: "Mi avete conciato come una vecchia cocotte!" Mentre lo scalzavano diede in una risata schietta e disse: "Per fortuna mi sono lavato i piedi!"

Quanto a me perchè non dovrei dirlo! Quei momenti mi riuscirono estremamente penosi. Da parecchi anni la mia vita era una sequela di avvenimenti a forti tinte; ma ogni loro traccia parve improvvisamente cancellata dai ricordi dell'infanzia che mi assalirono in folla. E rividi la mia povera mamma tutta tenerezze e tutta sorrisi, come quando il dì di festa mi abbigliava, e contemplandomi, se ne compiaceva. Quale contrasto!

Ma il Conte di Rudio non doveva esser visto, in tal momento, cogli occhi piagnucolosi e se qualche lagrima volle spuntare, l'alberezza si prese cura di ricacciarla nel profondo e di disseccarla.

Mentre mi avvolgevano il capo nel velo nero, Giuseppe Pieri già usciva senza dir parola. Ma lo raggiunsi a tempo per udirlo pronunciare quella frase che, lievemente distorta, servì poi quasi di pretesto a dimostrare la sua mente megalomane e bislacca. A me sembrò invece, e sembra, la virile espressione di un patriota che, compiuta nel limite delle proprie forze la propria missione, si senta alleggerito da ogni sua parte caduca e volgare e quasi sublimare dal martirio.

Il cappellano della Conciergerie gli aveva preso il braccio, come se vi fosse bisogno di sorreggerlo. Egli, senza neppure voltare il capo: "Non si ha paura quando si va al Calvario!" E si avviò frammezzo ai due luridi sostegni d'ogni tirannide, il prete ed il carnefice... e più non lo rividi. Ma udii distintamente la sua voce intonare, nel cortile, l'inno dei Girondini: "Morire per la Patria". Andava al patibolo, come aveva promesso, cantando!... piove.

Sono nel cortile... piove e nevica. Un tempo che mi concilia coll'idea dell'imminente tiepido riparo sottoterra. Passo d'accanto ad una compagnia di guardie carcerarie, allineata, in atto di presentare le armi... i fucili hanno la bocca puntata a terra, il calcio all'aria. Il portone era spalancato; il patibolo, eretto a pochi passi dal marciapiede, intercettava la mia visuale; ma non tanto che non scorgessi sulla piazza un nerbo ragguardevole di fantaccini, di cavalleggeri, di artiglieria, disposto come nell'imminenza dell'azione.

Avevo le mani legate dietro la schiena e assicurate, per giunta, ai vincoli che mi stringevano i piedi. Procedevo a passi forzatamente piccoli e lenti. Il carnefice, alla mia destra, mi sovrastava di tutto il capo; il vecchio prete, alla mia sinistra, doveva, colla sua canizie, contrastare stranamente la mia bruna, irrequieta giovinezza. Egli mormorava preghiere, guardando un suo crocefisso. Avevo manifestato il desiderio di sorbire qualche boccata di fumo e mi era stato concesso. Ma l'acqua e la neve, che calpestavo a piedi nudi, che mi cadevano sulle nude spalle, provocavano tremiti che io indarno mi sforzavo di reprimere e quell'apparente fiacchezza, irritandosi, mi induceva a mordere rabbiosamente la cannuccia della pipa.

Presso il portone, il campo della mia visuale si allargò e vidi le finestre e vidi i tetti gremiti di persone. Lo spettacolo della virtù sventurata, dissero i Greci, è degno degli Dei e, per assistervi, gli uomini avevano pagato sin mille franchi il nolo di un balcone. L'umore di quella moltitudine? "Essa — dice la relazione ufficiale — è rimasta costantemente calma e silenziosa durante la grande e legittima espiazione." Per la verità, una voce robusta partita dall'alto gridò: "Popolo, giù il cappello! Questo è supplizio d'eroi!" Sulla piazza successe un parapiglia. Si tentava, credo, di circondare la casa d'onde quell'umana protesta era partita. Ma proprio allora, la mia attenzione fu rivolta altrove... l'inaspettato avvenne!

Un uomo a cavallo era entrato dal portone... vestiva in civile, il petto coperto da decorazioni. Saltò di sella e guardie e soldati lo salutarono militarmente. Era monsieur de Collet, maggiordomo dell'Imperatrice. Mi venne presso, mi fissò in viso e, accorgendosi del mio tremito, disse: "Fa freddo, signor Rudio!" Risposi: "Sì, ma non mi lasceranno il tempo di pigliare un'infreddatura". Una boccata di fumo lo colpì in pieno viso. "Scusi; ho le mani legate e poi... è l'ultima!" — "Oh niente affatto; fumerete ancora!" Mormorò qualche parola nell'orecchio del carnefice prima, del vecchio prete dappoi ed entrambi, lenti e silenziosi si allontanarono. Allora, volgendosi di bel nuovo a me, che l'interrogavo coll'espressione del volto: "Verrò verso le nove nella vostra cella e vi spiegherò". Girai sui calcagni e mi trovai di fronte Felice Orsini.

Egli rallentò il passo: "Che è questo?"

— Mi rimandano in carcere. Non so altro.

— E il Pieri?

— È lassù!

— Meglio in due che in tre!

Proseguimmo per opposta via... egli a fecondare, col suo sangue, l'ideale della Patria; io verso il carcere... e quel ritorno alla vita, mi giunse penoso come una prepotenza, tanto ero preparato alla morte. Fatti pochi passi, voltai la testa e, per una singolare corrispondenza delle anime, anche l'Orsini si voltò. "Addio!" disse accennando col capo. Il velo nero che glie lo avvolgeva, fatto qua e là denso ed opaco dalle pieghe, nascondeva il suo volto così che già mi parve mezzo perduto nell'ombra misteriosa dell'eternità.

Trovai il camerone e la cella ingombri di soldati e di ufficiali. Un gruppo era affaccendato d'attorno al mio letto, sul quale giaceva svenuta e convulsa la mia guardia fidata, Saturno. Mi apersi l'adito sin là e, mentre una mano pietosa mi svincolava le braccia, l'andavo chiamando per nome. La mia voce parve più efficace degli spruzzi d'acqua e dei sali... Aperse gli occhi, mi guardò come trasognato, poi la sua faccia si rischiarò di una luce che rivelava un'amicizia profondamente sincera. D'improvviso, mi assalì con queste parole: "La scommessa! La scommessa!... avete perso!... pagherete lo champagne!"

"Più tardi, amico. Questa è ora di cordoglio. Laggiù si muore!" Quasi a confermare le mie parole, l'orologio della Roquette suonò: sette rintocchi. Nel carcere si fece un silenzio sepolcrale. L'ora tragica dà strane chiaroveggenze... gli ufficiali si scopersero il capo. In quel momento, la lama della ghigliottina... le teste... il turpe canestro...

Il Conte di Rudio, sopraffatto dall'emozione, non potè continuare. L'insufficienza del cuore, che già minava la sua robusta vecchiezza, gli toglieva il respiro. Sapevo che l'osservarlo, in simili frangenti, lo irritava, peggiorando il male e fu con vero senso di sollievo che udii il passo celere della signora Elisa. Ella entrò e non si accorse della gravità del momento. Stendeva le braccia e la sinistra sovrapponeva alla palma destra. Sorrideva colle labbra, sorrideva cogli occhi azzurri, come se avesse una grande contentezza nell'animo e volesse sbocconcellarla e darcene parte. Scoperse la palma e mise in mostra... due pulcini rannicchiati, d'un candore luminoso. Chi se la ricorda la bianca chioccia che covava nel cortile? "Sono sgusciati!" disse la signora, sorridendo sempre. Quei due atomi di vita ci guardavano meravigliando, quasi dicessero: "Chi siete voi?"

Torsi il capo... il sole californese, vicino al tramonto, spiava attraverso il verde fogliame. I miei occhi tristi, intolleranti di luce, si chiusero; ma il disco rossastro mi rimase impresso sulla retina. Oh accesa fantasia! oh vividezza di rievocazione! In quel rossore sanguigno, la testa recisa di Felice Orsini apparve... Stretta pei capelli dal pugno del carnefice, boccheggiava, senz'alito, quel suo ultimo, fatidico augurio: "Viva l'Italia! viva la Francia!"

Le mie labbra non si mossero, ma l'anima ebbe un grido: "Oh quanto ci è costata la Patria!"

CHAPTER NINETEEN

ON the evening of the twelfth, the chaplain Hughon came to pay me his customary visit. He was grave, grave, grave. I spoke to him of our appeal that had been rejected, and he became embarrassed. Then the suspicion of the sad reality flashed upon me, and I resorted to several subterfuges to confirm it. But Hughon promptly assumed the air of the ingenuous man, and sheltered behind that simple artifice, he became unassailable. Perhaps my every suspicion would have evaporated had my visitor not, upon taking his leave, extended his hand to me. Those fingers seemed to me to trace mysterious signs in the air, so visibly did they tremble.

I waited for the return of Saturno who, of the two guards, struck me as the more straightforward, and without even giving him time to remove his cap: "Saturno," I said, "I know that tomorrow morning I shall be led to the scaffold. Heed me and do, you who are a good man, everything to carry out my last wishes to the letter. At five you will wake me."

At this he began to protest. Why had I lodged such nonsense in my head? With the appeal rejected, there still remained — thanks to our protections — a great deal of rope to twist, and before... eh, eh!... it would take a good while yet!

— My friend, Monsieur Hughon came to hear my confession and instead... I have confessed him. He has revealed everything to me!

At this, Saturno, who to lend emphasis to his eh, eh!... was still gesticulating, let his arm fall as though I had snapped it off. "He revealed everything to you? That is contrary to Regulations and he ought not to have done it." He stood for a moment with eyes downcast, then murmured: "Since you know everything... well then, yes! Nothing remains for you now but to die like a man, as you have lived."

Then in my soul there came suddenly a great silence and almost a great darkness; but within that species of void, life still asserted itself powerfully through an instinct that impelled me to yearn for something darker still, more silent. "Better sooner than later!" — that is Orsini's expression; only then did I comprehend its full meaning.

"At five precisely! I have a great deal, a very great deal to write. My last thoughts, from the prison, to my kinsmen and to Elisa; my last thoughts, from the scaffold, to the Patria... And you?... Take this!" (I kissed him). "Tomorrow at this hour, think of me. You will say: The miseries of men touch him no more!"

Saturno was punctual. My first sensation on that memorable day was caused by the extreme pallor of his countenance. I wrote to my father; I wrote to the relatives of my wife, commending that unhappy woman to them from the depths of my heart. Six o'clock struck at the clock of La Roquette.

Shortly afterward, ten or twelve persons entered the large hall adjoining. Among them I recognised the chaplain Hughon and the Director of the prison. They went to Orsini's cell and I heard them announce to him his final hour. The Director of the prison then pronounced, in a low voice, a few phrases whose meaning was made plain to me by Orsini's reply. A magistrate stood ready to receive the dying man's revelations, and he had been urged to make a full confession. The martyr's answer resounded in the sepulchral silence, subdued but distinct: "Felice Orsini knows how to die; he does not know how to be an informer!"

Then came Pieri's turn, and his nerves gave way. "He was old, he was bound; but he would have had spirit enough..."

The Director of the prison admonished him courteously and he recovered himself at once. "Be that as it may," he said, "to die now, to die in a few years — what does it matter? My executioners too will die..." He added that he intended to hurl himself into eternity singing.

My turn came. I replied calmly that the Count Carlo di Rudio was ready — an expression which, in the official reports, was afterwards attributed to Orsini. Barely closing my lips, I made out more clearly in the half-light the figure of the man who had accompanied the Director of the prison into my cell. He was an elderly abbé... a pearl, perhaps. But to me, so ill disposed toward everything that calls the tonsure to mind, he had the effect of an enemy come to ascertain, *de visu*, my final moments. And since death was near and certain, I could very well, without fear of bringing harm to anyone, give free rein to my feelings. He drew close to me and made to speak. I imagined he wished to exhort me to die as a Christian, and I forestalled him: "I prefer," I said, "to consecrate my last thoughts to my wife and my child. As for your God, I shall see Him, if He exists — but only to tell Him what manner of ministers He is simple enough to have represent Him upon this earth."

We were brought into the large hall. Our hands were unbound. The faces were grave and those of persons tragically moved... the rest was almost a scene of friends taking leave of friends on the eve of a long journey.

Pieri, firmly resolved to regard the business as an ordinary occurrence, would not forgo his breakfast. The appropriate orders were given. While we waited, there was even a round of introductions. I learned in this way that Hughon would serve as Orsini's spiritual comforter; the chaplain of the Conciergerie, Pieri's; while the abbé attached to my side was chaplain of the House of Correction.

An elegant tray was brought in, with coffee and rum. Felice Orsini sipped of the latter, drinking the health of the Director of the prison. At this point my "comforter," humanely overlook­ing my rough rebuff, offered me a small glass; but he made the mistake of observing that it would infuse me with courage. I refused out of pride, retorting that Italian patriots had no need of artificial courage. Then there was a contest in asking one another for small keepsakes. The three executioners, erect and silent along the wall, watched us with a strange insistence.

All at once the Director drew out his watch and made a sign to the guards. The Regulations, indifferent to men's consciences, required us to hear Mass. Felice Orsini set off sadly toward the prison chapel. I protested, and Pieri added his vehement volubility to my curt refusal. Those present looked at one another, the Director shrugged his shoulders and finally gave way. Orsini's absence was very brief. As soon as he returned, they led us toward the Chancellery, whence we passed into an adjoining room. On entering it, Pieri, who preceded me by a few paces, turned and said to Orsini: "Well then, old friend?" Orsini raised his head, looked at him steadily, and replied: "Calm, calm!" These were the first words those two men, united by their ideals and their martyrdom, had exchanged since their arrest... and they were the last!

We were in the hands of the executioner. They shaved the nape of my neck; they made me put on an open-necked shirt; they removed my shoes with their own hands. The same preparations, carried out swiftly, left Orsini absorbed in his thoughts; but the loquacious man from Lucca became almost merry at them... such are the vagaries of the nerves! He wished to look in a mirror, and his executioner — something of a dandy, no doubt, despite his vile trade — drew a small looking-glass from his pocket and held it out to him. He examined himself at his leisure and, smiling, exclaimed: "You have got me up like an old *cocotte*!" When they removed his shoes he burst into genuine laughter and said: "Fortunately I washed my feet!"

As for myself — why should I not say it! Those moments were exceedingly painful to me. For several years my life had been a succession of highly-coloured events; but every trace of them seemed suddenly effaced by the memories of childhood that assailed me in a throng. And I saw again my poor mother, all tenderness and smiles, as she was on feast-days when she dressed me and, contemplating me, was well pleased. What a contrast!

But the Count di Rudio was not to be seen, at such a moment, with tearful eyes; and if a tear threatened to well up, his pride took care to drive it back into the depths and dry it there.

While they were winding the black veil about my head, Giuseppe Pieri was already going out without a word. But I reached him in time to hear him utter that phrase which, slightly distorted, afterwards served almost as a pretext for demonstrating his megalomania and eccentricity. To me it seemed then, and seems still, the virile expression of a patriot who, having fulfilled his mission to the limits of his own strength, feels himself lightened of all his transient and common parts and almost sublimated by martyrdom.

The chaplain of the Conciergerie had taken his arm, as though there were need to support him. He, without even turning his head: "One feels no fear when one goes to Calvary!" And he moved forward between the two squalid props of every tyranny, the priest and the executioner... and I saw him no more. But I heard his voice distinctly, striking up in the courtyard the hymn of the Girondins: "Mourir pour la Patrie." He was going to the scaffold, as he had promised, singing!

...it is raining.

I am in the courtyard... it rains and snows. Weather that reconciles me to the thought of the imminent warm shelter underground. I pass alongside a company of prison guards drawn up presenting arms... the rifles have their muzzles pointed to the ground, their stocks to the air. The great gate stood wide open; the scaffold, erected a few paces from the kerb, intercepted my view; but not so entirely that I could not make out in the square a considerable body of foot-soldiers, cavalry, and artillery, disposed as though action were imminent.

My hands were bound behind my back and secured, moreover, to the bonds that constrained my feet. I moved in steps forcibly small and slow. The executioner, on my right, towered above me by a full head; the old priest, on my left, must, with his white hair, have formed a strange contrast to my dark, restless youth. He murmured prayers, gazing at a crucifix he held. I had expressed a wish to draw a few puffs of smoke, and this had been granted me. But the rain and snow, which I trod with bare feet and which fell upon my bare shoulders, provoked shivering that I strove in vain to suppress; and that apparent weakness, irritating me, induced me to bite savagely at the stem of my pipe.

Near the gateway, my field of vision widened and I saw the windows and the rooftops crowded with people. The spectacle of virtuous misfortune, the Greeks said, is worthy of the Gods; and to witness it, men had paid as much as a thousand francs for the hire of a balcony. The mood of that multitude? "It remained," says the official report, "constantly calm and silent throughout the great and legitimate expiation." In truth, a robust voice from above cried out: "People, off with your hats! This is the execution of heroes!" On the square there was a commotion. An attempt was being made, I believe, to surround the house from which that human protest had issued. But at that very moment my attention was drawn elsewhere... the unexpected occurred!

A man on horseback had ridden in through the gateway... he was dressed in civilian clothes, his breast covered with decorations. He leapt from the saddle and guards and soldiers saluted him with a military greeting. It was Monsieur de Collet, major-domo of the Empress. He came up to me, looked me full in the face, and, perceiving my trembling, said: "It is cold, Monsieur Rudio!" I replied: "Yes, but they will not leave me time enough to catch a chill." A puff of smoke caught him full in the face. "I beg your pardon; my hands are tied — and besides... it is the last!" — "Oh, not at all; you will smoke again!" He murmured a few words in the executioner's ear first, then in the old priest's, and both, slowly and silently, drew away. ON the evening of the twelfth, the chaplain Hughon came to pay me his usual visit. He was grave, grave, grave. I spoke to him of our rejected appeal and he became awkward. Then the suspicion of the sad reality flashed upon me and I resorted, to ascertain it, to several subterfuges. But Hughon promptly assumed the air of an innocent and, under the cover of that simple artifice, became unassailable. Perhaps my every suspicion would have evaporated if, in taking his leave, my visitor had not extended his hand to me. It seemed to me that those fingers traced mysterious signs in the air, so visibly did they tremble.

I awaited the return of Saturno who, of the two guards, seemed to me the more candid and, without even giving him time to take off his cap: "Saturno," I said, "I know that tomorrow morning I will be led to the scaffold. Heed me and see to it, you who are a good man, that you execute my last will to the letter. At five o'clock you will wake me."

At this he began to protest. Why had I gotten such foolishness into my head? The appeal rejected, there was still, thanks to our patrons, much fighting to be done and before... eh, eh!... that would take some doing!

— My friend, Monsieur Hughon wished to confess me and instead... I have confessed him. He has revealed everything to me!

At this, Saturno, who to give emphasis to his eh, eh!... was still gesticulating, let his arm fall, as if I had broken it off. "He has revealed everything to you? That is against the Regulations and he should not have done it." He stood for a while with his eyes downcast, then murmured: "Since you know everything... very well then, yes! now nothing else remains for you but to die manfully, as you have lived."

Then in my soul there suddenly fell a great silence and almost a great darkness; but in that kind of void, life still manifested itself powerfully through an instinct that impelled me to long for something darker, more silent. "The sooner the better!..." is Orsini's expression; only then did I comprehend its entire meaning.

"At five o'clock precisely! I have much, much to write. My last thoughts, from the prison, to my relatives and to Elisa; my last thoughts, from the scaffold, to the Fatherland... And you?... Take this!" (I kissed him). "Tomorrow, at this hour, think of me. You will say: Human miseries touch him no more!"

Saturno was punctual. My first sensation, on that memorable day, was caused by the extreme pallor of his face. I wrote to my father; I wrote to my wife's relatives, commending that unhappy woman to them from the depths of my heart. Six o'clock struck on the Roquette clock.

A little later, ten or twelve people entered the adjoining large room. I recognized, among them, the chaplain Hughon and the Prison Director. They went to Orsini's cell and I heard them announce his final hour. The Prison Director then pronounced, in a low voice, a few phrases, the meaning of which was made clear to me by Orsini's reply. A judge was ready to receive the revelations of the condemned man, and he had been exhorted to make a general confession. The martyr's reply resounded in the sepulchral silence, subdued but distinct: "Felice Orsini knows how to die, he does not know how to be an informer!"

Then came Pieri's turn and his nerves gave a jolt. "He was old, he was bound; but he would have had the heart..."

The Prison Director admonished him courteously and he immediately recovered himself. "So it is," he said, "to die at once, to die in a few years, what does it matter? My executioners too shall die..." He added that he intended to plunge into eternity singing.

My turn came. I replied calmly that Count Carlo di Rudio was ready; an expression which later, in the official reports, was attributed to Orsini. As soon as I had closed my lips, I made out more clearly in the semi-darkness the man who had accompanied the Prison Director into my cell. He was an old abbot... A pearl, perhaps. But to me, so ill-disposed towards all that recalls the clergy, he had the effect of an enemy come to ascertain, *de visu*, my final features. And since death was near and certain, I could well, without fear of causing harm to anyone, give free vent to my feelings. He approached me and made to speak. I imagined he wished to exhort me to die as a Christian and I forestalled him: "I prefer," I said, "to consecrate my last thoughts to my wife and my child. As for your God, I shall see him, if he exists, but only to tell him by what sort of ministers he has the foolishness to be represented on this earth."

We were led into the large room. Our hands were unbound. The faces were grave, as of people tragically moved... the rest, almost a scene of friends bidding farewell to friends on the eve of a long journey.

Pieri, quite determined to consider the matter an ordinary event, did not wish to renounce his breakfast. The appropriate orders were given. While waiting, there were even some introductions. THE following day, at La Roquette. They made us put on the grey uniform of prisoners; they fitted us with the straitjacket... not from any lust for torture, but to forestall attempts at suicide.

We had been conveyed to the new prison separately and confined separately; yet our cells were so situated that we could see and speak to one another. A rectangular chamber, rather spacious, bare... four white walls, four windows, four doors. Whoever enters by the door communicating with the courtyard perceives, directly opposite, the door and window of Felice Orsini's cell; Pieri's cell lay beyond the left-hand wall; beyond the right-hand wall, my own.

This constituted, for us, a great advantage over the Conciergerie; death was in prospect, yet it was pleased to be preceded by some measure of relief.

Another circumstance contributed to render the present sad hour more bearable to me. My guards had followed me to La Roquette. They would sleep in turns in order to keep me under continuous surveillance; but this did not trouble me in the least, for I had no intention whatsoever of anticipating my end by self-destruction.

On the very day of our removal, our lawyers came to counsel us to lodge an appeal. Nothing concrete was hoped for; nevertheless, both I and Pieri consented without raising any objection. But Orsini would hear nothing of it. He maintained that death would by now relieve him of a great burden, and that it would come the more welcome the sooner it arrived. Giulio Favre, to obtain his consent, was obliged almost to force his hand.

The days of agony began and passed swiftly. In a state of mind only too explicable, the memories of those days flash through my mind in sudden leaps, standing out like disconnected images against the sombre background of those hours in which, meditating, I prepared myself to die.

The chaplain of La Roquette, Monsieur Hughon, paid us a visit at least once every twenty-four hours. He was a former captain of dragoons and had retained the colouring of that life; thickset and robust, in spite of his years; liberal in spite of his position. From his very first appearance I told him plainly that he would always be welcome on one condition: never to speak to me of religious subjects. He promised, and kept his word.

Giuseppe Pieri, on the other hand, willingly acknowledged his right to exercise his ministry, but not to undergo it passively. From the very first evening he provoked him to disputation with an air of open defiance, and allowed himself in subsequent days to be carried away by a demolishing spirit, by a manner of arguing *ad hominem* that was in the extreme degree sharp and almost offensive. Constrained by the pitiable circumstances, the ex-captain was obliged to put a good face upon more than one lance-thrust full in the ribs.

The voice of Pieri was, moreover, the predominant note in that confined and austere place. And how indeed could he, already so loquacious even in the most colourless circumstances of life, have brought himself to silence, now that powerful emotions exalted him?

He talked and argued about everything with obstinate persistence... at times even with an abundant vein of comedy, especially when it fell to him to confute the extravagances of our judges. But turn it as he might, he returned by preference to his political and philosophical ideas; as though a vortex drew him inexorably into the narrow spout of that funnel.

A strange contrast with the bearing of our leader, from whose cell there issued, for hours on end, not the smallest sign of life; so that more than once I had asked myself whether it were not the tomb of Felice Orsini; whether Felice Orsini had not become the tomb of his own soul.

One hour each day we were permitted to walk in the great courtyard; but not simultaneously. Pieri was taken out for his breath of air alone; upon his return, Orsini and I went out together. We wore our straitjackets even while walking, and were watched not merely by sight but at musket-point.

During that brief hour, Orsini would expand his lungs like a man reborn; but the gaze he turned upon the blue sky was full of melancholy. He walked at a rapid pace and I, reared in military discipline, followed him, instinctively keeping slightly apart. This would occur: if I was in ill humour and did not speak, he would turn his head and question me with a look, as though to say: “A storm brewing?” But if I spoke, he would stride on as though absorbed in the vision of other worlds, or, if he answered, the words came from the depths, drawn out as with a corkscrew.

Those brief words sufficed to reveal to me the man who was undergoing rapidly a radical transformation.

Felice Orsini had brought into prison two resentments; one against Pieri and one against Giuseppe Mazzini. It is well known that in his *Memorie* the impetuous Romagnol had portrayed Mazzini as a modern Mahomet who presumed to model the destinies of Europe upon the caprices of his odalisques — a circumstance of which the monarchist Cavour made admirable use. He distributed those *Memorie* in thousands of copies, thus cunningly throwing a bridge which afforded the opportunity to cross the Rubicon, to furl the banner bearing the motto *“Dio e Popolo”* to all too many Mazzinians, either wavering from weakness of fibre, or eager to justify with any pretext their base and mercenary calculations.

But the old resentment against the Master dissolved on the day when the guards and Giulio Favre informed Orsini of the noble defence of him made by the “Prophet” on the morrow of the attempt, at a time when other patriots, fastidious regarding the means of redeeming the Fatherland, had well-nigh branded him as a common assassin.

To this transformation, which I might call regenerative, another and vaster one of quite a different order corresponded. Better to face the subject at once. Felice Orsini was the only one of the three *morituri* who listened to the exhortations of the chaplain-dragoon more than passively, with a complaisant ear. And our daily walks insinuated in me a presentiment of what afterwards came to pass. The convictions slowly acquired through a life of struggle were, in the man, gradually disintegrating, and allowed to reappear, nay to predominate, the underlying strata saturated with the fables absorbed in unknowing childhood. In the imminence of his end, he returned to those religious practices to which only the blind multitude should still attribute essential virtue.

He therefore deceives himself, and unwittingly belies the truth, who, admiring the stoic fortitude with which Orsini mounted the scaffold, would make of him a model of consistency to be set against the figure of Silvio Pellico and others, softened, as it were, by their sufferings. But he would be equally mistaken who should presume to safeguard his greatness by passing over his conversion in silence. Silence attenuates nothing and cancels nothing.

One may well say in his defence that he succumbed to a cause that was anything but ignoble. Capable of leonine daring under a fleeting impulse, he was powerless, by reason of his supremely sensitive nature, against a thought that was tormenting, insistent, obstinate, fixed... not the thought of the death that hung over him, but that other, springing from the consciousness of his own responsibility in the ruin of high ideals; from the vision of the tragic death of others. His hair had rapidly turned white with it, and more than one delicate fibre of his brain must have been shaken and, as it were, scarred by it.

Felice Orsini, moreover, never posed before his contemporaries as a discoverer of philosophical or moral truths. A patriot he called himself instead, and was so from earliest youth; a patriot he was and proclaimed himself until his head lay beneath the blade of the guillotine. He had pledged to Italy his every energy and the last drop of his own blood. Italy has had greater sons; she has had none more unconditionally faithful. I say that there is no need to glean in other fields in order to establish the glory of Felice Orsini!
Beyond the chaplain-dragoon, there appeared almost daily at La Roquette a very powerful personage, the Prefect of Police, Pietri. He treated us in a most affable manner; nor did he ever fail to exert himself to instil into our minds some ray of hope... now affirming that the Court of Appeal would in all probability annul our trial; now assuring us that the Empress and a powerful court party showed themselves so openly disposed to clemency that Napoleon's mind would end by being influenced and overcome thereby.

But neither his manner nor his intelligent countenance availed to dispel the instinctive distrust, to bridge the abyss that separates the conspirator from a man who must, by force of circumstance, hunt him down and derive his own satisfaction from his ruin. This distrust was in me strengthened by other circumstances. On several occasions I had sent to Elisa letters of the most passionate character. When I wished to write, the guard on duty would free my right hand more or less; then, having bound it again, would carry the letter to the prison Director to have his endorsement placed upon it. But my ardent appeals received no reply... absolute silence, as though Elisa had utterly abandoned me, or were dead. It is easy to imagine my torment. Then, all at once, there entered my heart the suspicion that it was a matter of refined cruelty on the part of the Police, in pursuit of ends I could not clearly comprehend but whose existence I divined. From that moment my torment ceased... to give place to a sullen rancour against those who had inflicted it upon me.

Moreover, Pietri had the fault of bringing to the prison, almost without exception, one personage or another. A morbid curiosity appeared to drive the upper classes to verify, who knows, perhaps whether those who had dared to provoke such disorder were indeed composed of flesh and bone. To me, that being put on show, like a wild beast in a cage, weighed terribly.

One day I found in my cell, with Pietri, a high-ranking military man who had his head bandaged. I do not clearly remember whether it was General Macan or General Roguet. Pietri introduced him to me simply as “one of your victims”; which caused my ill humour to break forth. I retorted sharply that I had sought to strike no one save the man who had been the ruin of my Country. And as for being a victim — I would most willingly have exchanged places with that wounded man.

At this the officer stepped forward and, extending his right hand, declared that he bore not the least grudge, either against me or against any other. And I clasped, as best I was able, that hand; but I made no reply and, turning to the Prefect, asked him what interest the Police could have in intercepting my wife's letters.

He appeared surprised; but contented himself with a shrug of the shoulders.

The following day he returned alone, and before entering the cell stood for some seconds upright upon the threshold, filling the doorway with his fine and tall person. I thought I perceived in that countenance something unwonted, something inquisitive. He entered and, after repeating in a manner more affable and, I was about to say, more paternal than usual, the customary phrases, rubbed his brow and, lowering his voice somewhat — the guard had, moreover, withdrawn at his appearance — spoke to me with intensity of his deep sympathy for my youth. And, lamenting the sad fate that lay in store for me, partly through force of circumstances and partly also — he said — through the supreme recklessness of my companions, he assured me that he had pondered long to find a means of saving me, and not me alone! I already knew how at Court there was no reluctance to adopt measures tempered with clemency. But I was unaware — he added — that His Majesty the Emperor was himself animated by the most lively sympathies for Italy, whose greatness he admired and whose misfortunes profoundly moved him. Once set upon such a course, a man of that stamp — who could predict where he would halt? Certainly no sovereign in Europe was in a position to render greater service to the redemption of Italy than Napoleon. So that if I viewed the matter with the eye of an enlightened patriot, I would not be slow to understand that I could serve myself and my Country better by aiding, and so to speak allying myself with, the man I had attempted to kill.

I believe my face expressed my profound astonishment in full, for Pietri laid a paternal hand upon my shoulder and, lowering his voice still further, said:

“I know that I am dealing with an intelligent young man and I therefore expect not to be considered a boor. Between His Majesty and his high designs stand the internal enemies of the Empire. Too weak to act openly, they have secretly allied themselves with the external enemies and have conspired. I have not come to propose anything dishonourable to you. The safety and welfare of States have their ineluctable demands. Aid the eminent personage upon whom the triumph of the Italian cause may depend; shield him against his internal enemies, and I give you my word of honour that you will not be long in rejoining your young wife. By what strange aberration would you leave her poor, abandoned, and alone to struggle in the world and against the world?”

Now the true reason for Elisa's pretended silence appeared to me not merely hinted at but manifest; now my indignation had twofold cause to break forth. “I know nothing,” I replied, “and have nothing to reveal. But even if I had... you yourself, if I were to accept your proposals, would be the first to feel horror for me. Nor should I have expected such proposals from you, a Corsican... Italians abhor traitors!”

My natural vivacity urged me to gesticulate, and since I could not, I trembled. But without intending it, I had touched a sensitive chord in a heart not entirely stripped of noble feeling.

I saw that man, hardened as he was to so many emotions, cover his face with his hands. A few seconds passed, and he looked me in the face... his eyes were red with tears. He departed, promising that he would do everything in his power to save our heads. Should he fail in his purpose, he would tender his own resignation.
I have no reason whatsoever to doubt the sincerity of that man; but what he actually did, or attempted to do, I cannot say. This much is certain: on the eleventh of March he came to La Roquette and communicated to Felice Orsini, if nothing else, his hopes. It was shortly before the hour appointed for our walk. The conversation between the two was still at its height when that hour struck and the guards led me into the courtyard, alone. On my return, Felice Orsini, always so taciturn and, one might almost say, sombre, appeared to me suddenly transformed and, I was about to say, cheerful. The moment he saw me he said in a loud voice: “Di Rudio, take heart. We shall not be executed!”

This occurred, I repeat, on the morning of the eleventh of March. That same evening, the Court of Cassation rejected our appeal. If Orsini's affirmation was destined to find its correspondence in reality, the events of the twelfth of March would have demonstrated it. The day passed and our expectations were disappointed. That very night of the twelfth, by torchlight, work was proceeding in the square of La Roquette to erect the scaffold.

The explanation of the enigma?

I received it later from the lips of Mathieu de la Drôme. Public opinion was exerting a powerful pressure in our favour. In defiance of the Police, in the popular meeting-places a stirring song was being sung in praise of “the heroes of the fourteenth of January.” The Empress and a powerful court party had declared themselves for clemency. Napoleon III called their demonstrations indecent; yet he lacked the heart to oppose them openly. To eliminate the enemies who had fallen into his power, to strike terror among those who had escaped him... such was the secret yearning of the liberticide; but it had to be done while affecting to have had his hand forced, as though from scrupulous respect for the Law, shifting the responsibility onto others.

The Council of State was convened to decide upon the greater or lesser expediency of the imperial pardon, and the Emperor made a brief appearance before it. “The Councillors of the Crown,” he said, “were free to vote according to their own conscience; but he could not refrain from expressing one conviction of his own. Within that Council, traitors lay hidden.”

It was the sombre threat. The Councillors were assembled not to give way to the tide of pity, but to render a service to their master. Could the dignitaries of an Empire whose very essence was sharp dealing and corruption — and which in such a vortex would have swept all France along with it — hesitate between their lucrative sinecures and the lives of a few outcasts, rendered incapable of reaction and of doing harm? And had not the reason of State dissimulated far other acts of cowardice?

As if this were not enough, the Archbishop of Paris, scenting the situation, deadened the mild influence of Eugenia Montijo by interposing between her and the Emperor the measured, subtle exhortations of the Church. Never had the hatreds and interests of clericalism and of the State shown themselves so closely akin... the conspirators smelled of Freemasonry; they had fought body and soul to put an end to that temporal power which is the ill-concealed and supreme goal of every religious imposture; they, were it in their power, would have renewed the struggle. It was well worth suppressing them, even if in doing so one disowned Christ yet again.

The Council of State and the clergy... Napoleon III obtained from them more than he had hoped, and his mind, which knew how to sound characters in spite of their masks, must have exulted darkly, and perhaps, like the despot of tragedy, was saying within himself: “Oh, how many are the consciences that are sold!”
