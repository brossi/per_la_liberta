## Chapter Eight

I wandered, almost adrift, through various cities, until I took refuge in Genoa. The "Superba" had upon her hands some ten to twelve thousand political refugees. She provided for them with generous largesse; but it was like casting water upon sand.

The city, saturated with political passions, was disturbed by frequent tumults, and the exiles, made more restless by their sufferings, added no little fuel to the flame. A child of my times, far from considering myself above my surroundings, I did as the others did. By degrees I fell into great straits. My father, learning of this, wrote a warm letter of recommendation to Alessandro Lamarmora, and I found myself unexpectedly offered the rank of sub-lieutenant in the Bersaglieri. I refused! Why? I refused!

Why?

Sudden conversions were not yet in fashion in Italy, and I had no wish to inaugurate the series. A Mazzinian to the marrow, I would sooner have served Satan than sworn fealty to a Monarchy. Let it be added that the Bersaglieri were, at that time, terribly hated in Genoa — the fatal aftermath of the Piedmontese cannonades!

I continued to live by humble labours and expedients, until an unforeseen event procured me the relief… of changing the nature of my embarrassments. A celebrated republican had died. His funeral proved memorable both for the extraordinary concourse of the agitated multitude, and for that, no less extraordinary, of the armed force bent on keeping them within prescribed limits. At the cemetery, passions broke loose. Bitter speeches were delivered, with open allusions to the monarchical objection and to the hopes placed in the future. They aroused a storm of approval. All at once, the banner bearing the motto "God and the People," concealed upon the breast of some Mazzinian, was unfurled high above the swaying heads. From the Sun it received its vivid colours, from the sea-breeze its trembling; from the generous and the impatient, a long, thunderous salutation.

That harmless banner precipitated matters. At the sight of it, the Bersaglieri launched themselves forward, resolute as though bent upon avenging some outrage. Balilla, ever alive in Genoa, greeted them with a shower of stones and drove them to fury. Then the veins of the rioters stained the enclosure of the dead with crimson.

Shortly afterwards, arrests were made en masse, and, as is ever the way on such occasions, at random. I, who, rightly or wrongly, had been among the first to lay hands, did not wait upon the pleasure of the constables, and that very day, seizing on the fly one of those opportunities that seem to fall from heaven, I was sailing toward free America.

I never arrived there. A storm, which forced us to put in at Cartagena, once again altered the course of my existence. Before us lay Spain with her manifold attractions; beyond Spain, France — political energy, life! I took counsel with a certain Trolli, a Lombard patriot who had gone into exile from Genoa under the same pressure and with the same purpose as myself. We had but a few farthings in our pockets; yet we were possessed of nimble legs. We concluded, with one unanimous cry: "To the Pyrenees!"

Trolli belonged to a most excellent family of Varese, which, proposing to make of him something in the nature of a Cardinal, had sent him to the priesthood. When the revolution broke out, he cast the cassock to the nettles and seized the musket. An ardent republican, he brought to his convictions the exuberance altogether proper to a robust constitution and a flourishing youth — so much so as to declare that the most righteous shots he had fired had been directed not against the Germans, but against that poor "execrated Carignano," whom he accused, among other things, of having betrayed the Milanese by abandoning them, without striking a blow, to Austrian vengeance; which had not prevented him — he would add — from making off with the jewels donated by the Lombard women for the defence of their metropolis.

Our few farthings having vanished after a few days of forced marches, the problem of appetite became for us at once as arduous as it was imperious. But my Cardinal — still in the bud — had an inspiration. He said to me, in substance:

"Garibaldian tactics: are provisions lacking? Wrest them from the enemy! We need bread? The enemy will give us bread and something to eat with it. I studied for the priesthood; Latin is my strong suit. We come from Rome; we are two ravens plucked bare by the revolution. I shall serve Mass; I shall preach in broken Spanish, but larded with excellent Latin. The curias and the monasteries will fling wide their doors — of the refectory. A comedy with a well-spread table; actors of distinction on both sides, and… let our bellies rejoice!"

My disapproval was lukewarm. At the sight of the first bell-tower, Trolli put an abrupt end to it by acting.

By this device we made our way from Cartagena to Alicante, then to Valencia, then to Saragossa, then to Tarragona. Trolli gave proof of inexhaustible quickness of wit. The seeds sown in his soul by the Seminary had not fallen upon ungrateful ground. With a single glance he could distinguish the simple, good-faith priest from the shrewd one who, versed in the hidden purposes of the Church, treated his "mission" as a comfortable trade. His transformations verged upon the phenomenal. Humble, steeped in a seraphic candour with the former, he assumed with the latter an undercurrent of guile that asserted itself while scrupulously respecting appearances. If he encountered any suspicion, he would resort to Latin, using and abusing it without stint. Those poor prelates, in their relative ignorance, felt themselves vanquished, all but annihilated. Trolli would afterwards say to me, in private: "You see that the tongue of the dead works miracles in the mouth of the living!"

We did not always live in this manner. In Barcelona, where Trolli was expecting a remittance from home which unfortunately never came, we adopted the counsel of a certain Piedmontese, a lame fellow who went on crutches and made his living selling saints and madonnas.

And in the Café of the Liceo, the haunt of political refugees, making good use of our brazen faces, we planted ourselves in the middle of the room and struck up, in thirds, the hymn of Mameli. For months we had allowed no legitimate outlet to our rebel souls, and that plunge into clear water had the virtue of lending to our singing an impetus it did not owe to art.

There was applause. A gentleman with large grey-streaked moustaches asked us, in French, who we were.

— Refugees from Rome.

That was enough. He turned to his friends and harangued them aloud. He told them that we had been driven from our homeland for the crime of having loved and defended her. And having depicted, in vivid colours, the sad life of the exile, he added: "Who was the cause of these evils? Who has made the hearts of so many generous men to bleed? We, we French, who, being strong, felt no shame in assailing the weak; we who, having risen in defence of a great principle, straightway disowned it, turning our arms against a people who had risen courageously, as we had done, for the vindication of that same principle; we who, having barely won our liberty, abused it by strangling the liberty of others!" He concluded by recalling the inescapable obligation of every Frenchman to exert himself individually in alleviating the sorrows called forth by the Nation.

And, removing his hat, he made the round of the room, table by table, soliciting and gathering oblations.

We departed from Barcelona with twenty-five duros in our pockets. Having crossed the Pyrenees with that provision, we arrived safely at Perpignan.

Here I was compelled to acknowledge that, even outside the world of old-fashioned tales, a good action may find a greater reward than that which derives from the mere satisfaction of having performed it.

The remittance from Varese, which we fingered, counted, and recounted precisely at Perpignan, had allowed us to transform ourselves from hat to shoe and to enter, for a little diversion, a café.

The spacious room was thronged with military men talking loudly of affairs in Algeria. I was listening in silence, sipping a refreshment, when I felt myself fixed, as it were, by invisible eyes. I turned sharply. At a nearby table sat several officers, one of whom was indeed scrutinising me.

— I beg your pardon. By your appearance… you are not French.

— Italian, and not of the worst sort.

— Were you at Rome?

— In the time of your siege.

— And… do you remember the bridge of San Siro? Oh, I am not mistaken! Your appearance, your voice…

And he embraced me.

In the room… profound silence. I felt myself borne, with affectionate force, towards the group of officers.

— I wish to present you to my comrades-in-arms. The right hand of the French soldier always cordially grasps that of the valiant — all the more so if, on a certain day, without hating me, they found themselves face to face with me.

It was my prisoner from Villa Corsini, the officer for whom I had forestalled a mortal leap into the Tiber. That evening we dined merrily in quarters. And since in conversation we disclosed our intention of making our way into the territory of the Republic, it was desired that we, honoured as guests, should accompany the Regiment — which was the 10th Light Infantry — as far as Marseilles, whither it was bound to embark for Africa.

There followed a succession of dinners and receptions which continued as far as Montpellier. The mysterious policy which already pervaded the affairs of France had sent thither an unexpected counter-order. The officer of Villa Corsini gave me one last, warm embrace and pressed on with his men toward Paris; I continued, with Trolli, my journey on foot as far as Marseilles — our paths and our souls were to meet no more.

My sojourn in this last city is memorable to me, for it was there that I pronounced the inspired oath of Mazzini and affiliated myself with the Giovane Italia. At the same time I became a member of the Circolo del Paradiso, a French subversive association which, in many affairs, acted in concert with it. However, a month later… fresh troubles! Obedient to the reactionary wind blowing in secret from the capital, the Police surrounded the headquarters of the Circolo. All those found within it did not sleep, that night, in their own beds. The foreign members were peremptorily expelled from France. To Trolli and me, the Police delivered a passport, with a fixed itinerary, which was supposed to conduct us, stage by stage, as far as England.

At Dijon, we slipped, by the first train, through the meshes of the net. The authorities either took no thought for us, or could not recover our traces, and, undisturbed, we entered the great vortex of Paris.

It was June of 1851, and stormy days were being prepared for France; yet to the eye of the superficial observer nothing of this appeared. Louis Napoleon, having assassinated the Roman Republic, had contracted a taste for blood and was now girding himself for a vaster crime, if not a greater one — the assassination of the French Republic. His Presidency expired on the 4th of March 1852. As the fateful date drew nearer, his hopes, his fears, his hatreds, and his appetites were all sharpened. But in dissembling his workings, in dissipating mistrust, he reveals himself a master. His ideal — to aim at the heights of Napoleon I and to attain them by the arts of Julius Caesar. The careful study of the life of the great Roman had persuaded him that a new tyranny is not established when, having flung aside the mask, one proclaims it audaciously as such. The favour of the masses was indispensable to him. How was he to secure it? He had begun by causing himself to be thought solicitous for the disinherited classes, publishing on the subject of pauperism pages which did not appear hostile to the doctrines of the socialists. Openly accused of ambitious designs, he replied with his famous comparison between Napoleon and Washington, concluding therefrom that, being unable to possess the genius of the former, his sole ambition was to imitate the virtue of the latter. And he drove home the nail by affirming, on every fitting occasion, that he would consider as an enemy of his country whosoever should attempt to make himself great by violating the Laws.

Such prattle lulled not only the great multitude to sleep; it had its effect likewise upon the National Assembly. This body was composed of the most disparate elements, which, while extolling the excellence of the prevailing régime, were zealously devoted to quite other ideals. And since, in the political field also, men readily believe what they desire, the honourable hotchpotch was, by those vanities, easily brought to the persuasion that the President of the Republic, more than satisfied with his rapid career, had no thought of attempting any dangerous novelty. On the other hand, Legitimists, Orleanists, reactionaries, and radicals all expressed their prudence and exhausted their energy in watching one another, and took very little account of Louis Napoleon, who, despite the warnings of his dark past, was held to be a man too feeble and ineffectual to meditate either virtuously great enterprises or great crimes.

Amid the general foolishness, there was no lack of clear-sighted men who foresaw the danger; but being conscious of their own weakness, they prepared themselves for the surprises of the future by working silently, in the shadow.

Then, unexpectedly, the National Assembly, disowning its own origins, abolished universal suffrage. Louis Napoleon had found his way!

He could henceforth undermine the ground beneath his enemies' feet; plunge into a cowardly crime and find historians to exalt its virtue; enslave the multitude and, posing as its champion, secure at once its neutrality, and perhaps even its goodwill! He set in motion the sinister forces long held in readiness, and struck the Coup d'État!
