THANKS to our relations with the Circolo del Paradiso, we were, from our arrival in Paris, fraternally welcomed by the Club of Batignolle. We were employed in the manufacture of cartridges.

The rooms of the Club were permanently occupied by some forty revolutionaries... not a corner to lodge us. But we ended up finding a place to lay our heads in the house of a hatter from Torino, who dwelt in the Saint-Antoine suburb, at the opposite end of the metropolis.

To rise before the Sun, to stride out cheerfully, recounting past adventures; to nourish ourselves with Spartan frugality; to subject ourselves to the life of the exploited, to understand their sorrows and their hopes, to be partakers in them; to return love for pure love; to fraternally cultivate the capable, but benighted, intellect; to lend a hand in preparing for its vindication... such was our life until the 2nd of December.

Quickening our pace on the second of December, 1851, as we made for our usual meeting place, we had occasion to be greatly surprised. The Place de la Bastille was militarily occupied; we stopped. Infantry and cannon. The cannoneers stood with lit fuses, as if a battle were imminent. We found other soldiers lined up along the boulevard. In the suburb, outside the barrier, there was a sudden coming and going of gravely proceeding troops... a silence that savoured of a storm.

Hastening our steps and working by conjecture, we soon reached the mouth of the Rue de la Paix. Here we stopped short.

Opposite the Club's headquarters was a hedge of Guards. We saw the brutally satisfied men assigned to the manufacture of cartridges coming out of the great door in twos and fours, brutally shoved by the gendarmes.

It was necessary to warn the allied Clubs. In retracing our steps, we had to persuade ourselves that the soldiers were not stationed merely for an ostentation of force. In isolation, but with an ill-omened frequency, shots rang out. We were headed for the Club of Saint Denis. Before arriving, we almost stumbled over several corpses... workmen in blouses, unarmed, who had gone out without suspicion, launched, by treachery, into eternity!

We found, at the Club, some citizens, nervous from the uncertainty of the situation, pale, incapable of resolving upon a course of action. I recounted what I had seen... and exhorted them not to let themselves be slaughtered like sheep, but to act. Immediately the furniture is piled in the street; the paving stones are torn up, we labour feverishly to raise a barricade. A red handkerchief, secured to a stick, flutters over that improvised bulwark and is transformed into a banner of Liberty. But even before our work was finished, a battalion of the Chasseurs of Vincennes, having turned the corner at the end of the street, advanced, running, to the assault.

We were sixteen Frenchmen and two Italians. No one moved a muscle until the assailants were at a sure range... then we fired simultaneously. As soon as the smoke cleared, we began to fire again, with a disorderly fusillade.

The assault was attempted twice and twice repulsed. On the third, the battalion, closing ranks, rushed forward with an evidently infuriated spirit and a determination equal to the tension of its soul. It expected a third spray of lead... which did not come, and it scaled the barricade, shouting, blindly thrusting with bayonets. It found not a living creature before it!

The few had stood up to the many because they were sustained by the hope that the people of Paris would hasten to reinforce the resistance, with that admirable impetus which is their characteristic. The shops, the windows, the doors had remained closed; the street desperately deserted. Having exhausted our last cartridge, we consulted. To what end would our sacrifice serve? We decided to disperse. The red handkerchief, continuing to flutter, had deceived the assassins. The officer who commanded them, deeming himself mocked, took his revenge by seizing it with a kick... not, perhaps, the last of the superhuman merits that would later earn him the ribbon of the Legion of Honour!

But what precisely had happened; what was being prepared in the other parts of the city? By what arts did Louis-Napoléon consummate the Coup d'État?

It was in the dead of night that he began his tenebrous work. The "suspect" generals, those he had not been able or had not dared to corrupt; those who, enjoying some popularity, would have, in due course, placed themselves at the head of an insurrection, were arrested in their own beds. Simultaneously, every printing press in the metropolis was seized and placed under seal; a trusted guard was stationed at the foot of every bell tower. The people of Paris had not yet awakened and had already lost every means of promulgating any protest whatsoever, of calling to arms; of sounding, from on high, a resonant tocsin of alarm. Nor was this to suffice. Louis-Napoléon had the typographers of the National Printing Office work with pistols to their throats and forced Paris, forced France, to speak for him, to give the semblance of legality to his brigandage. From the early hours of the morning, the still-drowsy population of the city read on the street corners three documents that have remained famous: the Proclamation to the People, the Proclamation to the Army, the Decree by which the President of the Republic dissolved the National Assembly, re-established universal suffrage, convened the electoral colleges, declared a state of siege, and dissolved the Council of State.

This last document bore, associated with the name of "Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte," that of "de Morny," Minister of the Interior. Those two men, strangers before the Law, blood relatives through the mysterious embraces of adultery, had reinforced the bonds of blood by becoming brothers in crime as well.

That same day the militia cleared, with fixed bayonets, the hall of the National Assembly and the Police took it upon themselves to disperse the Supreme Court, which had gathered to protest, in homage to the Constitution, against the new parricide. The prisons of Mazas were crammed with representatives of the People.

But the Deputies of the Left had remained at large, for, by a misunderstanding, they had attended the Assembly only in very small numbers. To them was entrusted the arduous enterprise of erasing the outrage, of saving the Republic. Not a printing press, not arms, not powder; a bewildered people, paralysed by a fatal astonishment; a forest of bayonets all around, an entire Army Corps in control of the strategic points, a Police force sold to the prevaricator. To escape being shot, they had to disguise themselves and change their meeting place every day. They acted manfully, they knew also how to face martyrdom serenely... a blaze of heroism! but they did not know how, nor could they, overcome the greatest obstacle: their own prejudices!

The nightmare of legality dominated them; the spectre of the Constitution bound their arms, and they deemed it greatness not to cause its collapse—in such moments!

They were seen confronting there, with speeches, soldiers drunk on brandy, thirsty for blood; opposing to lead... dissertations on the duties of a good citizen. They trusted in the insignia of their own dignity, like devotees who expect portents from a relic; they put forward, with great conviction, the inviolability of their persons... Language appropriate on the lips of a despot; ridiculous on those of people who did not possess the force to sustain their claim.

In brief: indomitable courage, sublime abnegation... senile childishness!

The arid political questions left the crowds cold, for they sensed, instinctively if not with full consciousness, their vacuity. People who toiled from morn till night for a meager sustenance would not have shown themselves sensitive except to immediate improvements in their material existence. Certainly universal suffrage interested, more or less, a good number of non-intellectuals as well, and the Assembly or, to be more precise, its remnants, hastened to erase the error committed and to re-establish it. But Louis-Napoléon had preceded them... theirs seemed a forced concession and was derided.

Victor Hugo, in whose vast mind the truth flashed for an instant, proposed to his colleagues the immediate abolition of the city toll and the tax on liquors. For some time, the People had been clamouring against these fiscal measures. The great lyricist obtained that a decree for them be drafted. But it was of no avail. Once again the practical men showed that their vision was shorter than the poets'. They wished to postpone its publication until the morrow of a problematic victory, thus spitting, virtually, on the most effective means of ensuring it. "If the People do not understand," they said, "that we are risking our heads for the defence of their rights; if they do not fight, they are unworthy of any concession, they are unworthy of liberty." Madness of a haughty and disdainful pose, but madness all the same. Perhaps those men who serenely faced death every day, every hour, feared to give the impression of evoking the spectre of the red republic.

Another great intellect, reputed to be a fantasist, released from prison by chance, on leave (he languished there for an offence against Louis-Napoléon), found a way to launch a coldly positive note among those perplexed men. I allude to Proudhon.

"You are victims of your illusions. The People have let themselves be duped. They will not move, or they will move to help Bonaparte. A foolishness, the restoration of universal suffrage, has hooked the fools who are always the multitude. The usurper passes for a socialist. He wants to be emperor and he will be. It is a shameless crime, but shamelessness triumphs when it is backed by bayonets. He has on his side the cannon, popular prejudices, the errors and the unpopularity of the Assembly. Your attempts are destined to fail. You, Deputies of the Left, are honest; he is a scoundrel and this too is a great advantage for him. You have scruples, he has none. Desist from the struggle. Given the circumstances, much might perhaps be attempted, but nothing obtained. And you will do nothing but drag one thousand, two thousand, ten thousand generous souls to your ruin."

Prophetic words! But was it in human nature to accept them; to thus smooth the main road for the enemy?

The trouble is that the most logical, the most certain, the most expeditious, the least cruel means to paralyse the parricide, did not cross anyone's mind or, at the very least, no one dared speak of it. Louis-Napoléon had meditated the Coup d'État; he was the pivot around which the foul conspiracy revolved. It was necessary to strike the pivot; it was necessary to suppress Louis-Napoléon. By what strange aberration did the blood of a man who, by perjuring himself, was enslaving the Fatherland, weigh more, in the conception of those representatives of the People, than the lives of thousands upon thousands of innocents?

Victor Hugo wrote, from exile, masterly pages on the terrible power of the "octopus." He knew that the knife's blade slips on the monster's tentacles. The creature releases its prey and falls like a rag as soon as the iron turns about the only truly vulnerable point, the mouth that creates the vacuum and gives strength to the tentacles. But if in the Assembly a voice had arisen to summon the hero Gilliatt against the nefarious "octopus" of the Empire—he, Victor Hugo, first among all, would have launched the thunderbolts of his indignant eloquence to deprecate "the shame of a vulgar assassination." A great and ingenuous dreamer, after having incited the people of Paris to raise barricades, to fight and to kill, he pushed his audacity to the point of facing, unarmed, the enemy's lead; he pushed his sentimentalism to the point of rebuking the friend who offered him a weapon to defend himself and to strike. Puerile contradiction of a sublime intellect!

But the horror of blood was not to restrain the "man without scruples." The Regiments, the batteries, stationed in the suburbs, were, by his disposition, concentrated on the Boulevard Montmartre. To sharpen the militaristic enthusiasm, ten thousand francs' worth of brandy had been distributed to each brigade. The hordes of passive obedience do not reach the high spheres of heroism without the fumes of alcohol. As for the bigwigs, there had been broad assurances of more respectable epaulettes and swollen envelopes "to face any eventuality." And for fear that this would not suffice, the influence of some high-class prostitutes was also skilfully brought into play... the prostitutes who would later form one of the most salient characteristics of the Second Empire. The soldiers remained for several hours, with weapons in hand, in silence, forcibly motionless, almost like greyhounds on a leash. Suddenly, fired from who knows where, shot by who knows precisely whom, a rifle shot resounded. It was a signal.

All, simultaneously, began to kill. It was not arms levelled against arms; it was the pitiless hunt for the harmless citizen quietly attending to his own affairs; it was the premeditated and accomplished slaughter to terrorize, for the lust of slaughter. The drunkenness of blood was soon added to that of alcohol, and the havoc and death spread from the boulevard to an entire district. Foot soldiers, lancers, cuirassiers proceed in close order, finishing off all who are guilty of being in the street. Nor is not being there a sufficient defence... they shoot into courtyards; through grates, down into cellars; they launch volleys of lead at the windows. Little by little the indiscriminate killing becomes a monotonous enterprise. How to liven it up? The human beasts make a game of it, they wager, laughing, on the sureness of their aim; the targets for the infernal contest, women and children. Soldiers were seen inflaming the frenzy of assassination by torturing the dying; widening their wounds with steel.

After the killing with small arms, came the cannon. The slaughter lasted three hours... a holocaust of several thousand people: Napoléon announced to Europe that he had... saved "social order." Blessed, with cowardly gratitude, by Pius IX, he set out, from so glorious a beginning, dragging France behind him, towards the dark abyss of Sedan!

But Louis-Napoléon never dared to publish revealing figures. A cowardly malefactor, having struck his victims dead, he turns livid before the enormous quantity of corpses and takes every care to conceal them from his own eyes, no less than from others'.

Night fell... The centre of Paris is obstructed by barricades; but the People are almost annihilated by terror and do not stir. To defend them stand, unarmed and despairing of victory, a few virile consciences... an ephemeral obstacle to the tyrant's artillery.

Nor, with victory, does the executioner's frenzy abate. The prisons overflow with the unfortunate. How to make room for the arriving throngs? The answer, which came from on high, is brief and terrible: "Kill!" At night they are abruptly awakened and chosen at random. They are asked neither their name, nor whence they come, nor what position they occupy in the social order... what matters is to reach the fatally established number. Three hundred and thirty-seven are thus taken in a single go from the cellars of the Tuileries, led, between a triple file of soldiers, to the Champ de Mars, struck down, buried that same night in the cemetery of Montmartre, with their heads protruding from the ground... a lugubrious expedient for identification!

The horrible tragedy is repeated for twelve consecutive nights, at the Champ de Mars, in the courtyard of the Prefecture, in the adjacent Rue de Jérusalem. The slaughter is perpetrated near the opening of the sewer so that the human blood, flowing into it, leaves few traces and does not reveal the enormity of the hecatomb.

Then came the persecutions sanctioned by Law. What Law? Louis-Napoléon creates the Military Commissions and the Mixed Commissions. It will suffice to mention the manner in which the former functioned. They hold their sessions behind closed doors. They receive from above a list of names, they run through it—the accused is absent—to each name they affix, without further investigation, a mark which, according to the case, has the power to absolve, or to send a wretch to Lambessa or, worse, to the dry guillotine of Cayenne.

In a short space of time, the Military Commissions and the Mixed Commissions plunged forty thousand families into mourning! With such deeds, one acquires the sceptre "by divine right!"
