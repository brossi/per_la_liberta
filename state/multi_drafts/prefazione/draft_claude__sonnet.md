# Preface

THE PRESENT volume might make some contribution to History; but it is not history in the sense in which that word is today understood and written... Something less for those who demand the methodical concatenation of facts and the cold judgment upon men and events; something more for those who, regarding events as a mere resultant, should aim to grasp "the soul of the times," rummaging among the passions that stirred them.

The Count di Rudio, so distant, in time and in space, from the events he recounts, frequently protests that the miseries of politics no longer move him... Doubtless he deceives himself. Like a Minos who

*"Giudica e manda secondo che avvinghia"* ["Judges and sentences according as he winds his tail"]

he might have, in the estimation of many of my countrymen, a value entirely relative; but he acquires a great one as a "human document," and it is precisely as such that he is especially to be considered.

Why did I resolve to write of him?

Consider: In this extreme West, in this land of vulgar *affarismo* [money-grubbing], to find a specimen of that epoch now long swept away by the centuries, in which the third Italy had its gestation; to find him pulsing with intense life, still distilling a potent poetry, still capable of indignation and of tears... How could I have failed to be tempted, and all but mastered, by him? I wrote because my existence was thereby enriched with edifying and pleasurable sensations.

And I did not cast the manuscript to the bottom of a trunk, specially if not exclusively, out of regard for the Count di Rudio, whom I know to be either calumniated or forgotten. In the surge of men and events that welled forth from the European revolution which preceded my times, he possessed virtues that predestined him to be swept under. The triumph of political men is almost always a consequence of their most wretched qualities. It neither dazzles nor moves me. My sympathies are with those who succumb with virility.

I surmised that this book would see the light when di Rudio would be no more. That no doubt whatsoever might arise as to its authenticity, I not only sought and obtained written approval of each individual chapter; but I asked as well, and obtained, that every page which might be the object of presumable controversy should be authenticated by di Rudio with his own signature.

I might have softened a certain dissonance, rendered the more jarring by so much lofty and virile language; might have erased certain heterogeneous marks that insinuated themselves into the volume through the influence of those authors whom di Rudio held most dear... I did not. To adulterate the word issued from lips silenced for ever may be a venial sin to a Minister of Public Instruction; for a simple mortal it is cowardice.

I have nothing further to premise. *Salute!*

Cesare Crespi.
