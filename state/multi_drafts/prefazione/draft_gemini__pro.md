THE PRESENT volume might make some contribution to History; but it is not history in the sense in which it is understood and written today... Something less, for him who might demand the methodical concatenation of facts and a cold judgment upon men and events; something more, for him who, considering events as a mere resultant, might seek to grasp "the soul of the times," by delving into the passions that stirred them.

Count di Rudio, so far removed in time and space from the facts he narrates, frequently protests that the miseries of politics no longer move him... Undoubtedly, he deludes himself. Like a Minos who

"Judges and sends according as he entwines" he might have, in the opinion of many of my countrymen, a value altogether relative; but he acquires a great one as a "human document," and it is precisely as such that he should be especially considered.

Why have I resolved to write of it?

Imagine: In this farthest West, in this land of vulgar commercialism... to find an exemplar of that age, now engulfed by the centuries, in which the third Italy was gestated; to find him pulsating with intense life, still exuding a potent poetry, still capable of indignation and of tears... How could I not have been tempted and almost subjugated by it? I wrote because my own existence was thereby intensified by edifying and pleasant sensations.

And I have not thrown this manuscript to the bottom of a trunk, especially if not exclusively, out of regard for Count di Rudio, whom I know to be either calumniated or forgotten. In the great outpouring of men and events that sprang from the European revolution which preceded my own times, he possessed virtues that predestined him to be swept away. The triumph of politicians is almost always a consequence of their worst qualities. It neither dazzles nor moves me. My sympathies are for those who manfully succumb.

I sensed that this book would see the light of day only when di Rudio was no more. So that no doubt whatsoever might arise as to its authenticity, not only did I procure the approval, in writing, of every single chapter, but I also requested and obtained that every page liable to controversy be authenticated by di Rudio with his own signature.

I could have softened certain jarring notes, made more strident by so much lofty and virile language; I could have erased some heterogeneous imprints which have crept into the volume through the influence of the authors whom di Rudio most cherished... I did not do so. To adulterate the word spoken from lips now silenced forever may be a venial sin for a Minister of Public Instruction; for a simple mortal, it is cowardice.

I have nothing more to add by way of preface. Farewell!

Cesare Crespi.
