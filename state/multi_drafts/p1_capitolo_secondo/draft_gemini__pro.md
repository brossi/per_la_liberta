Half an hour of discussion in the study, under the effigy of Mazzini and of Fortunato Calvi who, it seemed, were listening to us. And I was already almost disposed to renounce my idea when, to avoid future regrets, I said vivaciously: "But do you not understand, Major, that this is a duty to be fulfilled? The event had a historical motive and to History it belongs. Why not clarify its circumstances and its intentions? Why allow inaccuracy and falsehood to be superimposed upon the truth? If you persist in your silence, the final word will remain, unchallenged, with your political adversaries, and you would make yourself a willing accomplice to their lies. It would be a grave wrong to the martyrs whose memory you are obliged to defend; to your children, to whom you must leave, in all its entirety, the good name you had from your father."

The Major passed a hand over his brow and ground his teeth. It was his way of showing strong emotions. Suddenly he fixed his eyes on my face and began:

I was born in Belluno on the 26th of August 1832 to Count Ercole and Countess Elisabetta de Domini.

My family has old roots in that farthest strip of Italy. Angelo Biave wrote of it and he wrote—God rest his soul!—in Latin hexameters. A great-uncle of mine took the trouble to translate them into the vernacular, thus preparing an instrument of torture for his great-nephews. For every little escapade of ours, if punishment came, it was mild for the times, but terribly tedious: to learn by heart a translated passage of Biave. Papa presumed in this way to re-forge you in the glory of your ancestors. I still remember the opening lines:

"I gird myself for an arduous enterprise; I narrate of ancient personages, descendants of an illustrious lineage, who gave to the Peoples most just laws.

"In the IXth century, the emperor Otto the Great decreed that the celebrated Nosadana family should govern Belluno and endow it with a wise order of Laws. A just reward granted by Caesar to virtue.

"For a long time the Nosadano ruled the city that our ancestors, with good reason, called Belluno; for here Bellona makes her trials and the citizens are adept in arms, unterrified in the face of dangers.

"The Nosaidana family boasts personages of high renown in war and for counsel. It changed its name and was called Rudia."

The poet then proceeds to narrate the deeds of a good company of Nicolai, of Ercoli, of Eustachi, some of them *podestà* of their native city; others celebrated for books dripping with doctrine; still others distinguished as advocates for the plebs, or as leaders of the Venetian infantry, or for hoary heads made venerable by holy works and by the mitre...

But let us leave our ancestors to sleep in peace and speak of my father, from whom I believe I inherited something more than blood and a name. An anecdote to clarify my meaning.

At the beginning of 1848, when the gazes and hopes of Italian patriots were converging on Rome and popular songs repeated the praises of Mastai-Ferretti, the citizens of Belluno organized a demonstration in homage to the "liberator Pontiff". They paraded through the streets, carrying in triumph the bust of Pius IX.

My father was seated at a table in a café. He sees, he trembles, and plants himself boldly before the demonstrators, and cries out to them: "O deluded ones, you carry in triumph the greatest enemy of our Fatherland." And without adding another word, with a mighty blow of his stick, he sends the multitude's idol into smithereens.

Intense was the excitement that followed the momentary bewilderment, and my father owed it to his well-established popularity that he did not live, then and there, his final quarter of an hour.

Pius IX, by renouncing the holy crusade for the independence of his Country and fornicating with the foreigner, took it upon himself, shortly thereafter, to reinforce that popularity, to demonstrate the perspicacity of the old and proud patrician.

A man of such temper was bound to be odious to Austria, somber and suspicious. The fact is that he never obtained permission to practice law, though he was very well-versed in legal disciplines. He consoled himself with the sweetness of fatherhood and had fourteen children. I was the fifth-born.

They called me the "*Moretto*" from the color of my skin. I was the favorite, and I do not preface this with "modesty aside" because I believe I was so more for my mischief than for my virtues. My maternal grandfather, Fortunato de Domini, a Colonel in the Austrian infantry, blinded by intense affection, believed he saw in me the stuff to "make something of," and to remove me from the influence of the liberal ideas that had infiltrated the family, he wished to keep me constantly at his side. First in the barracks of Belluno, an old building that had in its fine days echoed with the psalm-singing of friars; then in the fort of Palmanova where he had been transferred. The good man died before realizing that liberal ideas were more nimble than his vigilance, stronger than the walls of any fortress.

The simple and rude education he had imparted to me was then continued at the Military Academy of San Luca in Milano, at that time designated for the cadets of the Lombardo-Venetian Kingdom. And I would have remained there, with my brother Achille, until my studies were complete, if, suddenly, there had not arisen to overwhelm me the fierce tempest that, having gathered in the North, crossed the Alps and ran through and bloodied all of Italy.

Here, at the cost of narrating things to some already too well known, I believe it opportune to summarize the Italian question as it presented itself in that fateful epoch. Memory is more fragile than one might believe among the fervid Latin race, and recalling the evils that afflicted us will make it better understood why those who wanted a United and Independent Italy could not and should not have remained in doubt as to the greater or lesser correctness of the means.

Milano was the first center where our wrath erupted. No plan prepared long beforehand, no understanding among the citizens. The news of the insurrection in Vienna predisposed minds to attempt new things; a yellow and black cloth displayed at an ill-chosen moment, on her own windowsill, by a woman of loose morals, fanned, as if agitated by destiny, the flame that refines men and transforms them into heroes.

He who had prepared the material to feed the insurrection long beforehand was the aggressor himself; it was the folly of the wise Metternich.

That sad incarnation of the subtle arts already esteemed himself the most powerful intellect to appear for centuries on the world stage, even before the day when, taking advantage of the embarrassment of Napoleon the Great, he had managed to leap at his throat and hasten his ruin. Imagine afterward!

Napoleon, from his height, had treated peoples like a flock of sheep; why could he not do likewise?

Thus the one and the other, through an excess of confidence in their own strength, fell, by different paths, into the identical fatal error.

Napoleon I, by the very nature of his genius, upon enslaving a People, would redouble its activity and foment its ambition. Not for nothing does one trample on the prejudice of birth and reserve the highest offices of the State for positive merits. Son of the Revolution, he had usurped its thunderbolts; but only through it and on the ruins of the past had he been able to rise as a giant.

Wherever his eagles appeared, a powerful spirit of modernity blew; wherever he passed with his victorious armies, he necessarily spread the ferment of new ideas that smoothed the way for the future. And on the other hand, his entire life was a good lesson: "one must become strong and be daring, to vanquish the strong."

Thus in the very yoke he imposed was the seed of liberty and redemption; the very good that accompanied his evil works was destined—strange to say!—to undermine his power and precipitate him into the abyss of Leipzig.

Metternich was too fine an observer not to perceive that weak point. In his titanic enterprise of driving the human flock back toward the past, he took every care to be a tyrant, a somber tyrant and nothing else.

In 1814, when the power of Napoleon, though declining rapidly, still appeared menacing and the aid of the peoples seemed indispensable to prevent surprises, Metternich and the other European diplomats who took their cue from him made the most ample promises of liberty to the Italians. It was enough, to obtain it, that they show themselves deserving by helping the Holy Alliance to break the bloody yoke of the French adventurer; by rallying around a banner that signified the happiness, the regeneration of their Country.

With the Napoleonic danger gone, the deluded, though divided in their choice of a prince, were nevertheless in agreement in openly demanding their own independence.

The replies that came, at long intervals, from the mouth of Metternich, mark the various phases through which the nefarious work of subjugation passed and would be enough, by themselves, to arouse hatred in the heart of any people that feels.

— The affairs of the Italians will be decided in Vienna.

— Austria will never consent to renounce her rights of conquest.

— Italy is a geographical expression.

Unfortunately, Metternich could give force to his assertions, not only with diplomatic intrigues and the arts of the Church, but also and above all with the armies of the Holy Alliance.

The debt of gratitude toward the peoples having been considered non-existent, there remained for Austria that toward the princes. And perhaps, disregarding this last one as well, she would have appropriated half the Peninsula if the jealousies of Prussia and of Czar Alexander I had not arisen, at a good moment, to play, to some extent, the game of the petty tyrants.

Forced to divide Italy into morsels to amortize that debt, Metternich took care to create around himself a group of satellites that would necessarily gravitate to the great center of Vienna. He wished to make a half-exception for Murat, for whom he was reserving the kingdom of Naples. Murat's wife, Napoleon's sister, had delighted him with kisses that were worth a kingdom... but the impetuosity of the French general, who wished to pose, intempestively, as the champion of the Italians, deprived Metternich of the opportunity to fulfill the only phosphorescent purpose of his entire political life.

The kingdom of Naples fell to Ferdinand I, who had, for that matter, prepared the way by lavishing nine million francs in the antechambers of the Congress of Vienna. It was an Austrian army that accompanied him triumphantly to his capital.

Rome and the Legations fell to Pius VII, the restorer of the power of the Jesuits; the future compiler of the Bull "Ecclesiam Jesu Christo," which excommunicated any of the faithful who, knowing a member of the Carboneria, did not degrade himself to denounce him, to be a spy.

The grand duchy of Tuscany and the principality of Piombino fell to Ferdinand III, an Austrian archduke; the duchy of Modena to Francesco IV, nicknamed the "executioner" by his subjects. Maria Luisa, daughter of the Emperor of Austria and wife of Napoleon I, had to content herself with ruling in Parma; while the other Maria Luisa, Infanta of Spain, was placed in charge of the principality of Lucca.

Genova, despite its protests and the eloquence of the Marchese Brignole, who attempted to assert its long centuries of liberty and past greatness, was incorporated into Piemonte, upon whose throne, returning from his refuge in Sardegna, sat Vittorio Emanuele I.

Austria retained under her direct dominion Lombardia, Venezia, the Tirolo, Istria, and Dalmazia.

Having seen to assuring Austrian preponderance by surrounding the empire with weak states, Metternich strove to increase its power ever more by casting a dense net of secret treaties. And by imposing, along with the obligation to adopt reactionary measures, the other of reciprocal aid in case of turmoil, he reserved for himself, in substance, the right to intervene, with arms, in the affairs of every single state.

The sovereigns of Italy, so haughty and cruel toward their subjects, had, in order to reign, accepted the role of Austria's assassins. Austria, having seen the direct dominion of the entire Peninsula taken from her by the jealousy of her faithful allies, had circumvented the difficulty and had managed to secure an indirect, but no less substantial, dominion. And the chains that weighed upon the wrists of the august Latin Mother were no less heavy and no less firm for it.

By virtue of those Treaties, every Italian movement, even a peaceful one, intended to provoke liberal reforms, was suffocated in blood by the nefarious work of the army of white uniforms and cosmetically treated moustaches; nor was Piemonte itself immune from the barbaric deluge, whose Kings, not lacking in courage, had yet given some fine proof of virile independence.

In great historical periods, happy or sad, incidents unfold that synthesize them and can serve for art to depict them. The symbol of the Italy of those times was as if incarnated and put into action in the torture practiced under the diabolical inspiration of the Bourbon of Naples. An imprudent word, a suspicion, the hatred of an informer, all the more prone to slander as the accused and the accuser were never confronted, and there was an innocent man torn from his family, stripped to the waist, tied astride a donkey, driven through the streets of the city.

A company of Austrian soldiers preceded him to the sound of the drum, another followed him; at his side was the executioner whirling the nail-studded scourge, and at every step, there were blows that penetrated the flesh, that carried it away in shreds.

The torture was suspended when the unfortunate man, scarlet with blood, bowed his pale face and seemed on the verge of breathing his last... Suspended, but to greater agony. It began again as soon as a groan, a convulsive movement of the muscles, revealed the revival of sensibility.

The passersby did not react; they covered their faces and fled in horror... At the end of the long Calvary, the gallows! It was the torment of Christ renewed daily by the work of most Christian Kings allied, under the auspices of the Pontiff, in a holy alliance!

More refined, but no less inexorable, was the oppression weighing on the Italians subject to the direct dominion of the Empire. The air, impregnated with lies, began to envelop tender childhood, which was taught, as a supreme duty, as a religious obligation, servile submission to the tyrant; it penetrated the lungs of the University student, who was required to provide the State not so much with learned men as with faithful subjects; it hung, subtle, corrosive, over every phase of social life. The well-beloved subjects of His Majesty were free to move from house to house, from place to place; to attend to their business, upon whose profits the Fisc extended its rapacious claws... but in all else, they were serfs; men resigned or swollen with rage... citizens never!

Not for this did the repression of refractory souls proceed without due respect for the forms prescribed by strict legality... an easy task, where the Laws are promulgated by despotism, without the collaboration and consent of the governed. And of generous Italians, legally stricken, the fortress of Spielberg in Moravia was populated, which for the groans suppressed by its walls, for the noble intellects that were sterilized and extinguished there, for the crimes against humanity that it covered in the night of its dungeons, will rise horrible in the memory of men even when the last of the Habsburgs has been mixed with the dust for centuries.

The deluge of spies, high and low, that inundated the Kingdom provided egregiously for the supplying of innocent flesh to that Austrian bastille and to the re-established pillory and flagellation. Not distinguished by any signs of recognition, disguised by plausible occupations, they insinuated themselves into every family, drew up reports on the tendencies, the habits, the conversations of every individual, even on the style of hat preferably worn; they violated the secrecy of the post; they wove plots; they sullied, or attempted to sully, the reputation of the most noted and authoritative men, whose words could have incited souls to virile reclamations.

The population of Lombardo-Veneto, emaciated and corroded by a monstrous fiscal system, monstrously applied, had to provide not only for the expenses of the local Administration, but also to lavishly replenish the imperial coffers. So true is it that if you scratch the tyrant, the predator comes to light!

And the predator disregarded the refined and reptilian means, to resort, like the other oppressors of Italy, to barbarously ferocious ones, as soon as the Milanese, by refusing to smoke and to wear cloth manufactured by the foreigner, tried to harm him in his purse. The prison doors were thrown open and the social refuse, mixed with policemen, roamed the streets of the metropolis, smoking and forcing others to smoke. The daring who refused to clench between their lips the leaf impregnated with German spittle were mutilated or run through with the sword; neither sex nor age was a defense.

Martial law became ordinary law; treason, punished with harsh torments, was the wearing of cloths of certain colors, the singing of certain songs, the applauding of certain phrases of this or that drama; the adorning of oneself with a beard or hair cut in a certain way; the allowing of oneself to be carried away by any act that the Authorities, of their own full discretion, deemed a demonstration of a subversive nature. "Three days of slaughter ensure thirty years of peace," said old Marshal Radetzky. And the pretext to perpetrate the slaughter was sought with the pertinacity of one who pushes the mania for dominion to the point of lust: of one who knows no other inspiration than the murky interests of the dynasty.

Such were the delights that the dapper and smiling Metternich dispensed, with a more or less generous hand, to a good part of Europe. And the Peoples escaped him by the virtue inherent in the very arts that kept them enslaved. For they necessarily aroused the hatred which is a regenerating energy, which impels toward the luminous affirmations of Right, as much as and more than love.

And so intensely did this energy stir the hearts of the Milanese, that on the 18th of March 1848 they found themselves united as brothers by a supreme purpose; without distinction of class, without distinction of sex, without distinction of age... to kill or be killed; to win or remain buried under the ruins of their own metropolis.

No exhortations were needed, no watchword, no leaders. The one hundred and sixty thousand inhabitants of the city became a terrible unity... the PEOPLE; just as the billions of grains of dust constitute, in exploding, the terrible unity of the powder magazine.

The weapons?

They were drawn from underground, from the bowels of the walls, from the hiding places where the courageous, yearning for the day of vengeance, had secreted them... old implements of every kind, rusted; to which the armourers, emptying their shops, soon added a contingent of hunting rifles. Then came the maces, the rapiers, the halberds already wielded by soldiers of fortune; the greatswords that perhaps broke the impetus of Barbarossa in the fine days of the Lombard League... the past emerging from the penumbra of the silent Museums, rushing into the vortex of the revolt, bringing its contribution to the redemption of a People.

That formidable tumult of erupting passions did not refract at the walls of the military Academy of San Luca; but, penetrating them, it aroused in our youthful souls a fever which, though repressed, lost none of its intensity.

On the first day of the insurrection, the Officers spoke to us contemptuously of four brigands whom the Austrian force and the good sense of the "honest" population itself had very quickly brought to heel. Then... a grim face and silence. But that silence was worth, concentrated, the eloquence of several volumes.

The dynamic that stirred the living and made them greater than themselves had suddenly been transfused into things. In little more than a night, over a thousand barricades arose and blocked the narrow streets of the city. The pavement had given its stones; from the palaces artistic furniture was thrown down; the houses of the workers provided their household goods, their mattresses, their pillows. Sanctified by the feverish hand of a people that willed it, even the humblest utensils had been transformed into a bulwark of liberty... Formless bulwarks; their disorder revealed the fury of souls.

The fighter who, pale, fidgeting with his carbine, lies in wait for you, rediscovers, as if refined, the purest emotions. It was among that furniture that his fathers, correcting him, had taught him to secretly stammer the name of Italy. And now he would shout that name at the appearance of the enemy as a supreme provocation, as a challenge to the death; now he would demonstrate that the ancient valor may languish in Italian fiber, but never be extinguished. Victor or vanquished, he would have accomplished a work not without greatness.

The assault came, and at a hundred different points, simultaneously. But the Austrian found everywhere men to whom desperation had taught the art of defense. The barricades creak, change shape under the fury of the aggressors and the attacked, they drink of the blood of both... blood... but they echo the imprecation of the barbarian who succumbs, the battle cry of the citizen who triumphs.

While the patriots fight in the front lines, the mothers, the sisters, the children share the care of the wounded and prepare new means of offense. The cobblestones and tiles, piled on the roofs, are transformed into deadly projectiles; the spider's web of barricades extends and closes in on the barracks, the detached garrisons. Besieged by the oppressor, the Milanese thus become, in part, besiegers. And under the leaden sky, more insistent, more resonant than the battle, spread the tolling of the bells rung *a martello* as an alarm. Across the wide plain they spread the certainty that Milano is not vanquished, that she still hopes and fights.

On the morning of the fourth day, we knew that the garrisons within the circle of the Naviglio had surrendered; that Radetzky was forced to keep to the periphery of the city; that a Provisional Government had been formed; that the insurgents, certain of victory and impatient to achieve it, had rejected the offer of an armistice; that the wind and the hot-air balloons (some had even flown over our Academy) had spread the incitements to revolt throughout the countryside; that the cities of Lombardia and Veneto were in full revolt; that the Piedmontese army was already moving to lend a strong hand to the Italian cause; that armed bands roamed the countryside and made even the problem of retreat arduous for the old marshal.

In the afternoon of the same day, a committee of citizens, preceded by a white flag, came to the College and demanded the return of the Milanese cadets to their families. Then discipline was no longer able to place a dam before our enthusiasm. Those who left and those who stayed embraced fraternally and not without tears. Brief and solemn promises were murmured that not a few, among those beardless youths, were later to seal with their blood.

Not many hours passed and we were concentrated at Monte Tabor, near Porta Romana. The order was to place ourselves at the tail of the imperial army that was filing along the bastions. Radetzky, the iron Radetzky, despite the resources of his own genius, saw himself forced to avert ultimate ruin with a retreat, disguised but precipitous.

Five days the clash of virtue against the barbarous oppressor had lasted. The latter, brutalized by his own unexpected impotence, massacred old men and children, buried prisoners alive; bound lovers and spouses body to body and burned them alive; bloodied the castle of the Sforza by cutting off, for amusement, the extremities and the breasts of the gentle and majestic Lombard women.

The Milanese people had, by winning, begun the period of history exuberant with enthusiasms and disillusions, with victories and defeats, with tumults and silences, from which, as from a writhing matrix, our Nation gushed forth to new life, to the tremors of Liberty and of Glory
