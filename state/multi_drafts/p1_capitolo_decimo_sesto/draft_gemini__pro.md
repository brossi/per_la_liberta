Here is the sorrowful page: The secret of the vast conspiracy, jealously guarded; the Austrian Authorities absolutely unprepared; the distribution of pay to the soldiers having taken place; the targeted points stripped of defenders; the garrison scattered and carousing. Eugenio Brizzi himself, on the verge of the planned assault, penetrated the inner courtyard of the Castello and conferred with the two armourers. He found them firm in their given word; nor did any surprise attack ever have better auspices.

But at the moment of action, Assi and some other leaders, instead of convening the squads, vanished. The few brave men posted, seeing themselves too few in number, waited for some time, seething; then, suspecting betrayal, or thinking that all was postponed to another day, they dispersed.

Of Fortunato Calvi's plan, only so much succeeded as is sufficient to demonstrate that it was not built on clouds and that it would have had a far different outcome if in Milano, individuals of great influence, while also desiring the expulsion of the foreigner, had not, under the colour of prudence, had their eye fixed upon the interests of their own caste.

The assault on the Gran Guardia, led by a simple charcoal-burner, had a successful outcome. The insurrection would have had its centre and two howitzers to defend it. Except that the guiding minds were conspicuous by their absence, and the popular impetus, intolerant of delays, paid no heed to the instructions received. Instead of barricading themselves in the palace, those few strong men rushed tumultuously through the streets. They killed a few officers, then fell back to Porta Romana, where the innkeeper Giuseppe Monti organized a defence.

To suppress it, an entire battalion was sent. And, while a handful of common folk generously exposed their own lives, in the hope of being able to resist until the middle and upper classes decided to descend, armed, into the revolutionary vortex... the rich, the respectable, closed their gates, closed their windows, gave to four-fifths of the capital the aspect of a deserted and silent city.

The insurrection failed because the Milanese intellectuals lacked the ardour, the heart, the spirit of sacrifice, the faith of the People. Already in the preliminary dealings between them and Giuseppe Mazzini, Emilio Visconti Venosta, the future Minister of the monarchy, and his ilk, had applied the keenness of their intellect to raising objections. Forced by the demonstrations to retreat from one to the next, they ended by declaring that, before deciding to lend a hand, they intended to seek the opinion of a competent military man. And they turned to Giacomo Medici, who was then residing in Genova.

The hero of the Vascello, future general of Vittorio Emanuele II, had the identical thought that had informed the conduct of Giuseppe Mazzini: "Discourage the movement; but aid it strenuously should it prove inevitable."

It did not please them, and they did not follow it. I think that from Medici's lips they expected the phrase that would sanction their fearful egoism. The conspiracy took shape and began its fatal course; but they gave not a penny, not a man, not a rifle. And they possessed them!.. but with an expression heralding the short-sighted and corrosive opportunism of times still too vivid in the memory of Italians, they declared "that they reserved the right to make good use of them on the second day!"

The flight of Assi and the others, however blameworthy, had no other motive than these subtle tergiversations. The Milanese working class was not yet accustomed to the present-day, subtle distinctions between proletariat and capitalism; it had not yet learned to stand erect on its own vertebrae. It had heard and read so many times that all Italians are brothers and, in its naive good faith, it considered them as such. The first doubts are always the most bitter. Why ever did the *marsine* (frock coats) now refuse to make common cause with the *blouse* (workman's smock)? Did the sons of the People not risk their lives for a holy common cause? Why for them all the risks of the initiative?

I knew Assi in exile. He was atoning, with a dull ache, for his own error and for those which, wrongly, were attributed to him. And he defended himself, saying: "They wanted to push us alone to the slaughter so they could disavow us, if we were defeated. If victory had smiled upon us, without risk to themselves, they would have stolen the fruit of it from us!"

The bourgeois and aristocratic inaction of 1853 recalled that, in 1848, the direction of public affairs had been usurped by those who had not made the revolution. Deplorable logic, that of Assi, but logic nonetheless!

Giuseppe Monti and his men fought, from house to house, until not a crumb of powder remained to them. And so effective was the resistance, that a good one hundred and fifty enemies lay on the cobblestones and ceased to torment Italy.

Then came the martyrdom! A military tribunal, hastily constituted, hastily condemned to the gallows Giuseppe Monti and fifteen other generous men. (1)

They faced the ordeal with a serene spirit, teaching the indolent how one dies, after having taught them how one rises up.

Then came the arts of Tartuffe. It was necessary to diminish the heroism of the good men in order to give their own defection the character of an enlightened decision of supermen. There is no shortage of "connoisseurs" who would qualify the verses of Dante as insipid, were they presented for their examination with the signature of an unknown. The plans of Fortunato Calvi, attributed to Mazzini, an intellect deemed refractory to all military discipline, were declared a blunder more than puerile. And they were not ashamed, to prove it, to borrow, with a foolish air of triumph, the Croatian phrases spewed forth during the heroic hours spent at the tavern: "If the Milanese had killed our officers, we would have put the Milanese to the sword... men, women, and children!"

And the people of the "Five Days," to please the Croats and the Visconti Venostas, would have resigned themselves to being skewered on a stick, like a plate of flaccid gnocchi!

Then came infamy!.. To vilify the honest man; to reduce, if possible, the level of the giant so that the People, disillusioned and deceived, might decide to gravitate around the will-o'-the-wisps. The tactic is an ancient one..

And it was insinuated and written and given for certain that Giuseppe Mazzini was a spy in the pay of Austria. He provoked the excesses, prudently keeping his distance, to offer the oppressor the means to terrorize the timid by strangling the audacious.

Then came the sickening cynicism!..

To numb the conscience, revived intermittently by the tormenting thought of one's own guilt; to attempt to deceive oneself and others by disowning even one's own martyrs. It came to this: they circulated a protest in which, the roles being reversed, Giuseppe Monti and the other unfortunates were portrayed as assassins; and as martyrs the assassins righteously struck down in that desperate, glorious attempt to give us back a Fatherland! Never had creatures, born and raised under the fair sky of Italy, prostrated themselves so low!

I insist: The failure of the movements of the 6th of February 1853 is due principally to the fact that a handful of rich, privileged, and influential young men were horrified at the possibility of a popular Regiment and already preferred not to make Italy, if it were not to be conservative and monarchical.

Nevertheless, those movements did not remain sterile. They made Austria more obstinate and, making her ever more blind to her true interests, pushed her back to rash sequestrations, to new persecutions; to remedy our errors with the dynamic of her own errors, greater and irreparable.

Giuseppe Monti and his companions in martyrdom thus indirectly rendered impossible any accord between the oppressed and the oppressors. The holy work of redemption quickened its course and became inevitable!
