CHAPTER FIFTEEN

THUS on the fateful 6th of February I found myself neither at the assault on the Gran Guardia in Milan, nor aboard the Radetzky on Lake Maggiore.

Instead, early in the morning, I was climbing a steep path which, branching off from the Ceresio, a few steps from Lugano, led up to a villa gleaming white as the snow that surrounded it. Its green shutters were the sole contrasting note upon that vast whiteness. Having reached the esplanade facing the villa, I paused to contemplate it, and through my mind passed the words of Fortunato Calvi: "Let the arms do their duty, the mind keeps watch and will do its own!"

But if the climbing up there had been an easy enough undertaking, being presented to the man was not to prove so easy for me. The surroundings, outwardly solitary and tranquil, concealed hearts beating fast, and were as though saturated with a busy silence. I felt, beyond the walls, the suspicious eye that watched me, and in the sturdy manservant who approached me, stooping and low-voiced, I glimpsed the conspirator ready to draw himself up and, if need be, to attempt even a sudden stroke. Rather than answer his questions, I pronounced the password and extended my hand to him as to a brother. I added that I was the bearer of a letter of introduction to the Master, on behalf of Colonel Calvi.

But the Master, being most exceedingly occupied, had given positive orders not to be disturbed. And since I persisted and urged the urgency of the matter, the pretended manservant snatched the letter from my right hand, assuring me he would deliver it in person. It was a piece of luck that he invited me to enter.

I am in a ground-floor room, spacious and cold… on the mantelpiece were four pieces of firewood neatly arranged, but unlit. A writing-desk, a few bamboo chairs… bare walls. Here too the predominant note is whiteness. In the recess of the wide window, between two white curtains, hangs a cage of brass wire, wrapped in a rosy gauze… within, a canary was hopping about. I planted myself before it; but my thoughts turned at once elsewhere… to the little great man, toward whom there converged, from every quarter of Europe, so much impetus of hatred, so much impetus of love. I expected to see the stalwart "manservant" reappear at any moment to announce that I was expected, that I should be welcome. I heard the door creak on its hinges and turned. The man standing before me was slight, dressed in black, with a black kerchief at his throat. He had a high, smooth brow; pale cheeks, almost olive-tinged. I felt my soul as though enveloped in the warm and serene gaze of Giuseppe Mazzini.

Between his fingers he held the letter which had informed him of our failure, and he had hastened out to receive a fuller account of the particulars. He listened, with great composure, to my narration, and when, by way of conclusion, I drew out the money which I had wrested from avaricious selfishness, he took it and put it away, without counting it, in the drawer of the writing-desk. Not a word, not a flutter of an eyelid!… whether he deemed honesty unworthy of praise, or whether weighty thoughts prevented him from pausing over lesser matters.

He sat down and motioned me to do likewise. But I remained standing and apart, and while he meditated, I observed with wonder that slender, spare frame, the pallor of the face, the intellectual profile, so exquisitely delicate. The greying head seemed to me too heavy for the neck that bore it — an impression perhaps accentuated by his attitude; for the little great man was supporting his broad brow with lean fingers.

I felt perplexed. How had a man such as this been able to sustain so many hardships, so many intellectual struggles, such fury of battles? Whence came the strength that had roused so many from inertia, formed so many consciences, kindled so many fires, breathed new life into the Fatherland?

No sooner did he begin to speak than I was persuaded that a great heart has great impulses, even within a slender breast. One is commonly counselled: "Write as you speak." Giuseppe Mazzini, in grave moments, spoke instead as he wrote.

I summarise and piece together the various parts of his discourse:

"Nothing has been attempted… therefore nothing is lost! We must set ourselves to the work again… but not with such men as these. He who meditates an enterprise and trembles and cavils, labours for his own enemy and unknowingly digs the abyss that will swallow himself and the hopes of the Fatherland.

"Today's sun is destined to illuminate bold new deeds. A people that has never been sparing of its own blood will hurl itself, with the courage of desperation, unarmed against the powerful oppressor. The streets of its city, full of gaiety, will be transformed into a pit of groans and slaughter. If heroism avail not, if the brute force of lead and numbers triumph once more over right, Milanese youth will tread alone the road of exile, will climb alone the steps of the scaffold. But when the hatred of present oppression, the virile impulses, the disregard of suffering, shall have the power to ripen fate, we too shall gather the fruit of victory, we brothers of Italy. The Milanese rise and sacrifice themselves for a cause that is ours. And should we confine ourselves to the part of idle spectators? Shall we demonstrate to the enemy and to the world, through our selfishness, that our chains are well fitted to us; that the dignity of free men is unbecoming to our souls?

"Let Milan be certain that beyond her walls she has aroused sympathy of purpose and of passion… and not barren sympathy! In arduous trials, destinies hang by a thread; nor is succour limited to arms and munitions. Let our solidarity sustain the arm of the bold: let our ardour strengthen the sacred flame that drives them to action. It is imperative, it is imperative!"

He remained a moment in thought, and his lean fingers tormented his smooth brow, as though to wrest from it, by main force, an inspiration. "How to provide," he murmured, "in such straits of time?" Then suddenly, with great emphasis:

"Varese is the point! Access to reinforcements is easy there; the garrison small; the citizens patriotic and impetuous, the *Giovine Italia* well rooted, the assurances I have received serious and almost tangible. The thing is to cast a spark —

*'Parva favilla gran fiamma seconda.'*"

"A thorny undertaking, not a hopeless one. Let a man depart from Lugano this very day, and tomorrow he is on the spot; he meets with the leaders, distributes manifestos, spreads the news of the Milanese insurrection, revives men's spirits, calls them to arms, takes the enemy in detail, organises the barricades, sounds the tocsin, rouses the countryside.

"For this, a legion is not needed — one man suffices, but a man who has the cunning and the audacity of the feline, the sacred enthusiasm of heroes; a man who would prefer, if need be, to die with arms in hand rather than live a slave. Where is he to be found?"

Such words, spoken by such a man and at such a moment, could not glide over my soul without mark. My youthful exuberance overflowed and, grown bold, I dared to presume upon myself. I said:

"Master, I began to serve my Country when on these lips the first hairs had scarcely appeared. I put my life at hazard in Venice, in Rome. Emigrated to Paris, I did my utmost to oppose the fratricidal arms of Napoleon. And Fortunato Calvi can attest that, even recently, in Lombardy-Venetia, I more than once confronted, for the good cause, the danger of dying on the gallows. Assuredly, I have not accomplished great things, but—"

— "I understand, I understand! The spirit of sacrifice cannot be inexhaustible."

— "No, no, Master! Have you not taught us that right proceeds from a duty fulfilled? I have fulfilled mine, as best I knew. Now I claim the right to be deemed worthy of offering once more my arm and my blood for the benefit of my country."

He raised his head, and once again I felt myself as though enveloped in the warm and serene gaze of the Master. He said simply: "The enterprise of Varese is yours!"

I departed at dusk, escorted by several smugglers… the smugglers who gave Austria so much trouble and whom resurgent Italy, prodigal as she is of marble and bronze to the heroes… of the sixth day, appears to have wholly forgotten. We walked over crags and precipices in silence; I marvelling at their agility, they in all likelihood at mine. At daybreak we were at the frontier.

There stood in those days, near the point where we were to cross, a small cottage. At the top of its door was fixed a branch of pine. This signified, in the figurative language of those parts: "Here one drinks;" just as myrtle signified in ancient times "Here one makes love!" The leader of the party directed his steps toward it and all of us, one at a discreet distance from another, followed in his wake.

That innkeeper, positioned astride two states, sold wine and grappa in order to give himself… a social standing. His greater earnings derived from the secret understandings he had formed with the bold spirits who defrauded the customs. The smugglers descending into Lombardy and those who, from the Republic, penetrated into the Kingdom, made a halt at his cottage… to spy out the land, to receive orders, to deposit there merchandise and subversive literature.

Around the innkeeper gathered those who had escorted me. They fell to talking in low voices, in an agitated manner; I, prudently apart, was contemplating several pictures pasted upon the wall. Then we drank to one another's health and drank again. Grown impatient, I went out into the open air and cast an eager eye outward, far and wide across the plain below, in the direction of Varese.

But just as I was laying plans for days of endeavour, and in some small measure also of heroism, there arrived at full gallop a courier who was destined to thrust me back into grey life, among the resigned, among the unknown. I was so absorbed that I noticed nothing until I heard my name called. "Ah, it is you! I had already despaired of reaching you. I bring you a most urgent letter!" He rummaged in his greatcoat and handed it to me. As I broke the seal, my hand was trembling. I read:

*"Return immediately, your sacrifice would be in vain. In Milan all is lost."* "Mazzini."

Brief lines, immense calamity! To my eyes they seemed traced by a pen dipped in the blood of martyrs. I retraced my steps in haste and, before the setting of the sun, I was looking upon Giuseppe Mazzini once more.
