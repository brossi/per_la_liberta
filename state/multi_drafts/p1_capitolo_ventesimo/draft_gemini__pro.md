WHAT I am about to narrate, I derived from the letters Giustiniano wrote to me. I have them impressed upon my memory in indelible characters. To hearten our mother, Giustiniano would sketch the situation for her, tempering it with a good dose of optimism. Luisa would undergo some interrogations, but would soon be set at liberty. Papa had the reputation of an old patriot, an irreducible man, and would not have been able to get off so easily. But how could they condemn him without proof? The judicial authorities were rigorous beyond all telling; but they were keen to appear most upright and would not dare commit a blatant piece of knavery. As for me, he himself had accompanied me as far as the mines of Agordo. From that point, I had chosen paths that only I and the chamois knew how to travel. I must have already arrived beyond the borders, yet the Police still believed me to be in Belluno and were searching for me in Belluno. Shortly, they would receive letters from me from Switzerland, or from some other country where the yellow and black did not fly. Everything therefore depended on not losing heart, so as to preserve one's health, so necessary in that tempest.

But the confidence he strove to instill in our mother's soul was far from being in his own. The newspapers, subject to rigorous censorship, seemed agreed in treating the latest conspiracy with the conspiracy of silence. But news knows how to travel a long way even without the help of newspapers. Now from the lips of trusted friends he met along the way; now at the café, around the small table, between one phrase and another about the fine weather or the price of agricultural products, there slipped into his ear the words that could not be openly spoken without danger, and from them, as always in such cases, hopeful assurances appeared but faintly delineated, while the sinister predictions were painted in bold strokes.

One evening, these predictions seemed to have found, in facts, a tragic conclusion. "Among the gorges of the Alps, a patrol had blocked the path of the fugitive patriot... the summons to surrender was disregarded... the noble heart broken by Austrian lead... the series of generous impulses cut short forever. That very night, the corpse would arrive in Belluno for the legal verifications."

Giustiniano hurried home to prevent the sad news from reaching our mother from some incautious mouth. He found her in bed. Wake her? To what purpose?

She would have more than enough time for tears. He paced the room back and forth until a very late hour; then, overcome by weariness, he let himself fall into an armchair and fell asleep.

Through the half-closed shutters, the first light of dawn was barely peeking, and already our mother, impatient for information, was bringing him his customary cup of coffee. She saw him and, guessing the sleepless and anguished night he had passed, instead of waking him, she went to the untouched bed, took a blanket from it, and spread it over his knees and chest. Just then, the bell rang furiously.

Giustiniano awoke with a start, saw, remembered, understood. He threw his arms around the poor woman's neck and said to her:

"Mamma, my poor mamma, be strong and prepare for the worst."

They departed in a carriage, a gendarme on the box, an inspector at their side. No hysterical fits, no sobs, no silent tears. Giustiniano held fast to the grieving woman's right hand, but she paid him no mind, remaining motionless, her eye fixed on some distant point, perceptible only to her.

She was led into a large, cold room. From the opposite wall stood out a plank, one end of which rested on a trestle; upon the plank was spread a sheet; beneath the sheet, the form of a man in the rigidity of death was outlined with a sinister uncertainty. A soldier, with his rifle at rest, stood erect and puffed-chested beside it. Another, in a simple waistcoat, his sleeves rolled up, his left hand plunged deep into a large boot upon which his eyes were fixed, was furiously plying his brush and scrubbing it. Four officers, arranged in a circle, were conferring in low voices. A burning brazier adulterated the clearness of the morning with its glare.

At our mother's entrance, the officers interrupted their conversation and turned their faces toward that severe apparition, who proceeded to the foot of the corpse with a firm, almost resolute, step.

Someone spoke. He alluded to the painful necessity imposed upon him by duty and lamented the untimely end of the ill-advised who attempt to subvert the sacred majesty of the Laws... She seemed not to hear; her soul was concentrated in her eyes, which flashed from beneath her black veil.

Someone removed the sheet with a sudden gesture. A livid face appeared. Then a single sob resounded in the sepulchral silence, just one!... but it was enough to resolve the tempest in a heart.

Mamma lifted her veil, looked intently at the officers, and with a voice in which vibrated the muffled threat of an entire People, she said: "No, this poor wretch is not my son! Carletto lives!... he lives and he will avenge the anguish I have suffered, and he will avenge his Country!"

In the general stupor, maternal majesty had its triumph. She started toward the exit with the same firm step with which she had come. The soldier in the waistcoat breathed his warm breath upon the boot and down came the brushstrokes, more furious than ever... perhaps he wished, in that moment, that the barracks and the Empire were concentrated upon that muddy boot-upper. The puffed-chested soldier beside the corpse did not move an eyelid... an automaton in uniform, he awaited a command that did not come.

The four officers, who had suddenly grown pale, perhaps had their thoughts drawn by force to a distant country, beyond the Alps, where other mothers were suffering.

Into the cold atmosphere of the barracks, into the chests hardened by iron discipline, accustomed to the fierce emotions of arrogance and blood, there evidently crept the gentle remembrances of childhood, and they reawakened a stunned pity.
