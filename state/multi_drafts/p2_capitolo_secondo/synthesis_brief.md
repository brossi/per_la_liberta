LA GRATA sorpresa notturna continuò, con matematica regolarità... un pacchetto ben legato e ben fornito, ogni ventiquattro ore. Il conte di Rudio decise d'andare al fondo della cosa, e, un bel mattino, rassettata come di consueto la camera, si rannicchiò nel vano della finestra, dietro il cortinaggio, risoluto a rimanere appostato e silenzioso magari il giorno intero. Di lassù dominava, per buon tratto, il marciapiede a destra ed a sinistra ed egli pensava, a ragione, che le polpette non gli potevano giungere per l'aria a volo. Avrebbe scoperto il suo misterioso benefattore.

Un pò prima del mezzogiorno, mentre appoggiato al davanzale fotografava coll'occhio il bruno via vai che si agitava confusamente ai suoi piedi, un subito destarsi delle vibrazioni vitali, un dolce tepore, un senso di luce penetrante, lo scossero. Egli si volse di scatto... non era più solo!

Avvolta in un bianco accappatoio, coi capelli in parte annodati, in parte fluenti, stava ritta al di qua della soglia, la signorina Elisa. Le dita della destra tenevano, per la cordicella, il pacco rigeneratore e gli occhi azzurri erravano in un indicibile turbamento.

"Signor Carlo!... mormorò. Ignoravo che fosse qui!" Subitamente si rinfrancò e soggiunse con calma: "Hanno portato questo pacco per lei!"

— Quando? Per la porta non è entrata anima viva.

— Stamane per tempo. Se avessi saputo che era a casa, glie l'avrei subito consegnato.

— Stamane, ha detto?

— Per tempo. Un signore ben vestito, italiano all'aspetto, all'accento. Gli ho chiesto il nome; ma mi ha risposto che, per ragioni tutte sue particolari, non desiderava di darsi a conoscere. Non mi parve lecito l'insistere. D'altronde egli parlava tanto male l'inglese ed io so così poco d'italiano!...

Non aggiunse altro e rimase ad occhi bassi, in attesa di qualche frase, di una parola allusiva, non foss'altro, al bel tempo. E come la parola assolutamente non venne, fece nervosamente arco delle sopracciglia e rialzò gli occhi. La blanda interrogazione che vi brillava, si smarrì presto in un'espressione di penosa meraviglia. Il conte di Rudio pareva assorto nel pensiero di cose lontane... la sua mente correva presso l'italiano ben vestito che, senz'essere uno stregone, conosceva tanto bene i segreti del suo stomaco. E si irritava del mistero che, malgrado il tedioso appostamento, non era riuscito a risolvere. Quando s'accorse d'essere osservato... silenziosamente, dignitosamente, si spolverò, colla destra, la giacchetta imbiancata dal lungo contatto col davanzale.

La situazione diventava imbarazzante quando, nel corridoio, risonò un calpestio di passi precipitosi ed ecco sopraggiungere la signorina Sara. Ella sorreggeva, serrandolo al seno, un gran vaso di rose.

Lo depose presso la finestra e dopo qualche secondo di muta contemplazione: "Ci sta a meraviglia!" Si rivolse all'Elisa e soggiunse: "L'idea è tua e a te spetta il dare una spiegazione!" Ma non glie ne lasciò il tempo e fissando il conte: "Una sera ha detto che la rosa è l'emblema della sua famiglia e che, anche per questo, è da Lei preferita sopra ogni altro fiore. Elisa ebbe un'ispirazione. «Procuriamone un cespo, forse l'avrà caro.» Veda che rigoglio! Per non darle fastidi continueremo ad innaffiarlo noi. Elisa mi faceva osservare che le rammenterà la mamma e la Patria... perchè l'Italia è il paese delle rose. Non è così?"

Qui le parve, o finse, che qualcuno la chiamasse e corse via. La signorina Elisa salutò con un cenno del capo e svelta e leggera, come un'ombra, scomparve.

Il conte di Rudio se ne stette a lungo immobile, fissando l'occhio nel vuoto, come se qualcosa delle due giovinette vi fosse rimasto. Da quel giorno esse occuparono maggior parte delle sue ore fantasiose. Si insinuarono, non chiamate, ne' suoi pensieri e non appena vi apparivano erano accolte con amorevolezza più che fraterna. Gli occhi tristi, che non avevano mai visto altro se non che la tortura dell'innocente ed il trionfo della canaglia, si rinfrancarono in quella interna contemplazione. E per la prima volta, il Conte sentì profondamente che, al disopra del mondo astioso, degli uomini che si rodono scambievolmente, della insaziabile cupidigia, della spietata prepotenza, della melma varia e nauseabonda in cui si affonda e si imbraca il nostro vivere civile, sta sospesa nel cielo azzurro, che ne è saturo, una forza serena che trasforma, che redime, che crea, che ci può far buoni e felici... l'Amore!

## Chapter the Second

THE AGREEABLE nocturnal surprise continued with mathematical regularity... a well-tied and well-stocked parcel, every twenty-four hours. The Count di Rudio resolved to get to the bottom of the matter, and one fine morning, having set his room in order as usual, he tucked himself into the window recess behind the curtain, determined to remain posted there in silence were it to take the whole of the day. From that vantage above he commanded a good stretch of the pavement to right and left, and he reasoned, not without justice, that the morsels could hardly reach him through the air on the wing. He would discover his mysterious benefactor.

A little before midday, while leaning against the sill and photographing with his eye the dark to-and-fro that stirred in confused motion at his feet, a sudden awakening of vital vibrations, a gentle warmth, a sense of penetrating light, startled him. He turned with a jerk... he was no longer alone!

Wrapped in a white *accappatoio* — a combing-cloth — her hair in part knotted, in part flowing, Miss Elisa stood upright on the near side of the threshold. The fingers of her right hand held by its cord the restorative packet, and her blue eyes wandered in an unspeakable perturbation.

"Signor Carlo!..." she murmured. "I did not know you were here!" She gathered herself at once and added with composure: "Someone brought this parcel for you!"

— When? Not a living soul has entered by the door.

— Early this morning. Had I known you were at home, I should have brought it to you at once.

— This morning, you say?

— Early. A well-dressed gentleman, Italian in appearance and in accent. I asked his name; but he answered that, for reasons entirely his own, he did not wish to make himself known. It did not seem proper to insist. Besides, he spoke English so very ill, and I know so little Italian!...

She added nothing more and remained with eyes downcast, waiting for some phrase, for a word — were it only an allusion — to the fine weather. And as the word absolutely did not come, she nervously arched her brows and raised her eyes again. The mild enquiry that glimmered in them was soon lost in an expression of pained wonder. The Count di Rudio appeared absorbed in thought of distant things... his mind was running upon the well-dressed Italian who, without being a sorcerer, knew the secrets of his stomach so passing well. And he chafed at the mystery which, despite his tedious vigil, he had not succeeded in resolving. When he became aware that he was being observed... silently, with dignity, he brushed from his jacket with his right hand the whitish dust acquired by its long contact with the sill.

The situation was growing awkward when, in the corridor, there sounded the tread of hurrying steps, and there appeared Miss Sara. She was carrying, clasped to her bosom, a great vase of roses.

She set it down by the window and after a few seconds of mute contemplation: "It looks wonderfully well there!" She turned to Elisa and added: "The idea is yours and it falls to you to give the explanation!" But she allowed her no time for it, and fixing her eyes upon the Count: "One evening you said that the rose is the emblem of your family and that, for that reason too, it is your favourite flower above all others. Elisa had an inspiration. 'Let us procure a bush; perhaps he will hold it dear.' See what luxuriance! So as not to trouble you, we shall continue to water it ourselves. Elisa observed to me that it will put you in mind of your mother and your Fatherland... for Italy is the land of roses. Is it not so?"

Here she thought — or feigned — that someone was calling her, and ran off. Miss Elisa took her leave with a nod of the head, and swift and light as a shadow, vanished.

The Count di Rudio remained a long while motionless, fixing his gaze upon the void, as though something of the two young women had lingered there. From that day they occupied the greater part of his dreaming hours. They insinuated themselves, unbidden, into his thoughts, and no sooner did they appear there than they were received with a tenderness more than brotherly. His sad eyes, which had never beheld aught save the torment of the innocent and the triumph of the base, found renewal in that inward contemplation. And for the first time, the Count felt profoundly that, above the world of spite, of men who gnaw one another, of insatiable cupidity, of pitiless arrogance, of the manifold and nauseating mire in which our civil life sinks and wallows, there floats suspended in the blue sky — of which it is the very substance — a serene force that transforms, that redeems, that creates, that can make us good and happy... Love!

AS an introduction to this second part, a page essentially of love claims its rightful place. What I here relate I had, in part, from the Count di Rudio himself, and in part from his wife. Nor was I without the aid of certain intimate letters, yellowed by time, yet still exhaling, fresh as ever, the fragrance of the past.

The little politics that peeps in through the doorway carries the soul of Giuseppe Mazzini. For what, after all, is di Rudio, if not a reflection of that great conspirator? Setting aside the disproportion of their genius, the only substantial difference between the two seems to me this: the one held that without faith in God neither a spirit of sacrifice nor an enduring honesty is possible; the other managed to sacrifice himself and to live honestly, having set aside that mighty hypothesis altogether.

In London, di Rudio made his way to the innkeeper already known to us, at whose establishment Fumagalli had preceded him. Tressoldi introduced him to the chorus-master of Drury Lane, a Milanese who engaged him as a chorister in the Italian Opera company. Thus, as in other days from flowers, di Rudio drew his sustenance from music — that is, music sung by others; for, on the whole, he was obliged to content himself with opening his mouth wide and waving his arms. The important thing was to pocket five shillings each evening, and in this he succeeded marvellously well, as any seasoned chorister could have done.

Let it be added that he was paid to listen to Madame Glassier and to the tenor Armandi; whilst, to be transported into ecstasy by the high notes of this Bolognese cobbler, the wealthy paid relatively fabulous sums.

When the season ended, the Opera Company drifted back, one by one, beneath the fair skies of Italy; but di Rudio remained beneath the leaden skies of London. He persisted in reading the newspaper advertisements and at last found work at the Wapping Docks, a locality situated at the eastern extremity of the metropolis. The work consisted of handling rushes and fashioning coverings for bottles, making use, for this purpose, of a newly invented machine, the contrivance of a Frenchman.

The excessive distance of the workshop obliged him to forgo the cordial hospitality of the good Tressoldi. The necessity of bread had, in any case, already thinned out that pleasant company. To follow the manifold threads of their lives, to trace worthily the stubborn, exhausting struggle sustained by our exiles in order to keep afloat, would require a volume unto itself.

We should encounter feet peering out between sole and upper, bearing witness to the long and fruitless hunt for employment; bellies pinched by the belt, betraying the enormous disproportion between appetite and the means of satisfying it. I should have to tell of nocturnal hunts across the rooftops and the mewing of innocent cats destined for the pot; to describe the cramped lairs shared between half a dozen human creatures, each instinctively reclaiming, in sleep, their portion of the blanket — a blanket sometimes pieced together with the art of a mosaicist, compounded of newspapers gathered from the benches in the public gardens.

The drama would not fail to chill us with its cold breath... virile constitutions corroded by hunger; sensitive natures ruined beyond all hope of recovery; poems vanished silently amidst the clamour of a thieving, pleasure-seeking world; the death-rattles of the consumptive; corpses cut to pieces upon the cold marble of the mortuary slab... acts of heroism without witnesses, unrecorded, yet no less worthy of history than those which unfolded in the light of great occasions.

Against this backdrop, rendered now in strokes of lamentation, now of comedy, now of the sublime, stands out the pensive figure of Giuseppe Mazzini who, to feed the generative flame of divine conceptions, of genius and of virtue, goes knocking at the moneylender's door and pledges... his own boots.

Nor should we want for hearty laughter hurled, like defiance, in the face of misery... frequent dreams of glory; sentimental love affairs piercing to the heart through threadbare elbows; long fasts forgotten in the reading of Byron... and likewise those occupations dripping with grease that know nothing of poverty. Angelo Fumagalli, to cite one example, made an excellent living manufacturing sausages.

The Count di Rudio had found lodging at Baldwin's Garden, in the house of one Mancherini, a figurine-maker from Lucca. Fumagalli and a certain Leoni were the only exiles with whom he maintained, in those days, relations of close friendship.

He had come to know Leoni at Tressoldi's inn, and they had grown intimate through a characteristic incident which I am pleased to recall.

Austria and the petty tyrants of Italy, who had been given so many headaches by the exiles, devised a new means of defence. They granted to the birds of prey chained in the prisons of their homelands full freedom of flight, on the condition that they should pass themselves off as political refugees and insinuate themselves amongst the honest company.

The calculation was that a change of sky would not change their ways, and that the public, implicating the good in the blame provoked by the wicked, would cease to pity the exiles and to lend moral support to their noble aspirations.

A veritable flock of such gentlemen descended upon Tressoldi's inn. They were led by one Bonacina, a Milanese, a swaggering instigator who, backed by his confederates and emboldened by the innkeeper's soft character, soon turned that congenial establishment into a sinister den of ruffians.

Tressoldi's comical despair, and the fact that he had caught the scent of the troublemakers' special mission, induced di Rudio to resort to an extreme expedient. He found willing souls in Leoni, a kind of athlete, and in other well-built young men; then, having wrested from Tressoldi the promise of the strictest neutrality, he awaited a favourable occasion.

One evening Bonacina and four or five fellows of the same stamp were demanding to drink at no cost and threatening to turn everything upside down if they were not served instanter. Di Rudio stepped forward and ordered them to clear the room... and never to set foot in it again.

"This beardless boy smells of good health!" replied Bonacina, making as if to deal him a backhanded blow. It was the signal for a furious brawl... Fumagalli had his head broken; but the ruffians, overwhelmed and badly mauled, ended by beating a retreat, leaving their leader to make the best of things as he could. Alone, with one eye closed and the other barely open, his mouth bloody, Bonacina backed away to the window and, swearing he would have his revenge, leaped out and vanished into the darkness of the street.

Not a single grim face, that evening, at the sign of Tressoldi's! But the affair had, for di Rudio, an unexpected sequel. One foggy night, returning from the Opera, with his head full of trills and high notes, turning a corner into a deserted lane, he found himself face to face with Bonacina, heavily muffled, fist raised and in that fist a large knife.

"Ah, ah!" said the scoundrel mockingly. "Now we shall see whether your liver is sound!" But instead of delivering the blow, he drew back... he had caught the gleam of a pistol barrel.

Then di Rudio was seized by a singular impulse: to add, to the silent eloquence of the weapon, that of a brief homily. He recalled to Bonacina the heroism and greatness of his fellow Milanese, in such striking contrast to his own conduct. And probing that heart, to rekindle beneath the ashes the gentle sentiments of childhood, he asked him, among other things, whether he did not believe that his mother was ashamed to have brought into the world, nursed and raised a son who was surely betraying her finest hopes; who mocked the sufferings of his fellow citizens; who spat upon her grey hairs; who renounced the Fatherland.

Bonacina, hard and contemptuous at first, ended by absorbing, head bowed, his dose of medicine. Then, flinging away the knife with a sudden movement, he fled headlong, sobbing like a child. The love of the Fatherland had known how to kindle some glimmer even in that base and debased soul!

The Count di Rudio, from his lodging at Baldwin's Garden, went every evening to exchange a few words with Fumagalli and Leoni. After their customary long walk, they pooled what few coins they had and allowed themselves a bottle... of concentrated sunshine. Such was the name they gave to the product of the Lombard vineyards; the only available means by which they could warm themselves at the sun of Italy.

The first to appear rarely at the accustomed gathering was di Rudio.

Apropos of these absences, Leoni asked him one evening, with an expression that revealed the unspoken meaning, whether he was lodging in a house of young ladies. "Without a doubt!" replied the Count. "At the Mancherinis' there are two. One is called Sara and is the daughter of the household; the other goes by the name of Elisa and is their niece. Her parents live in some corner of the County of Surrey and have sent her to London, if I was correctly informed, to train in the art of artificial flowers."

Leoni pressed to know more, and the Count di Rudio, who had picked up the musical jargon at Drury Lane, replied:

"Miss Sara has a soul tuned in the minor key, soft and caressing; Miss Elisa, on the other hand, has vibrations in the major, impetuous, conquering."

And Leoni, looking fixedly at Fumagalli and winking at him: "Let us recite the *de profundis*. The *Moretto* is ill and... we shall soon lose him!"

The following evening and the next and many more besides, di Rudio was nowhere to be seen. His two friends grew anxious and resolved to go to Baldwin's Garden to investigate. They found the door ajar. To ring the bell, or to step boldly forward without asking leave? Whilst they stood in two minds, they heard a sweet, gentle voice saying: "In my garden there grows a pear..."

A masculine voice, that of di Rudio, interrupted: "I have already told you that the names of trees are masculine, and the names of fruits feminine. Excepting *dattero*, *limone*, *ananassa*..."

— Except *dattero*, *limone*, *ananassa* and *fico*! — observed another woman's voice, more vibrant than the first.

Leoni drew Fumagalli away. Then he said: "I foresaw it. The fellow is done for!"

Signora Mancherini, a Booth by birth, a distant relation of the founder of the Salvation Army, was English through and through; but her husband's occupation had dusted her, not to say plastered her, with art more or less classical. She spoke with evident ostentation of Venuses and Dianas. And with the Gods of Olympus she had overburdened every piece of furniture, every shelf, every projecting surface of her little apartment. She was also accustomed to dressing her hair in a fashion that recalled times long since vanished.

Her daughter Sara was a fair flower of eighteen years. In the judgement of the Count di Rudio, she took a little from her father and a little from her mother... a blend of English and Italian. But the northern disposition, active and assertive, had, by an apparent contradiction of nature, ended by remaining as if atrophied, whilst the gentle and submissive disposition of the Italian woman, developing and all but bursting forth, stamped the whole of her character in a very marked degree. She did not seek to shine; she did not imperiously demand love; she preferred to love without being loved in return to being loved without loving in return. Not yet having reached the age at which feeling, fully developed, knows what the tempests of passion are, she was content with the tepid breath of friendship, and lavished upon her cousin Elisa the tender devotion which she would later reserve for a husband; in her cousin she rejoiced, suffered at her vexations, and exulted in her joys. Happy in her own half-light, she asked for nothing more, provided that Elisa should triumph.

This latter, meanwhile, as if to make it so deliberately, was endowed with a temperament that tended to accentuate Sara's disposition. Of the English people it is generally said that they have strong teeth and that once they succeed in seizing their prey they never let it go. Beneath Miss Elisa's fair hair, beneath the rosy skin, behind the blue eyes that even today call forth a sense of sweetness, there nestled a tenacity of purpose and a courage by no means common. These qualities, finding occasion to manifest themselves, appeared as a paradox and astonished the superficial observer; but not one who, accustomed to analysis and deduction, had noted the significance of that broad, firmly drawn chin and the energy of her gesture, however rare and restrained as befitted every young English woman.

The Count di Rudio told me that, as a girl, she possessed a certain suppleness of character and intellect; but she could never have been brought, even had she wished it, to the point of self-negation. To love strong souls; to open to them, as a corolla to the kisses of the sun, the most hidden gradations of her heart; but in order to be stronger, greater together as two. To conclude a treaty of alliance — that yes, with all her ardour, and to remain faithful to it unto the last; to sign even an honourable surrender, never!

Di Rudio had resolved to teach Italian to the two young women at the urging of Signora Mancherini. Why refuse? To study our language becomes fatally synonymous with loving our Fatherland, and our friends are always something of enemies to our enemies. The sympathy of the English was greatly coveted by the Italians, and even the arts of peace may serve to win a battle. Besides, every small stone has its value in great undertakings!

And so it came about that the Count di Rudio, every evening, after having laboured prosaically among rushes, applied himself to instilling the sweetest and the strongest of modern tongues into the charming heads of two young women born and raised upon the foggy banks of the Thames; and perhaps the Gods of Olympus, from the heights of their shelves, smiled down upon the scene.

Accustomed to the blunt directness of military life, the Count di Rudio proved a rather brusque master, and the hour of the lesson assumed, so to speak, the rigidity of a geometrical problem. But no sooner had that hour passed than Signora Mancherini set her steaming tea service upon the table and the atmosphere changed as if by enchantment... books and notebooks vanished, and animated conversation flowed without interruption, punctuated and, as it were, annotated by sips of the warm beverage. Signora Mancherini, who was stout, filled the large armchair entirely with her own person. Sara sat, by a habit consonant with her inclinations, upon a low footstool, resting her forearm upon Miss Elisa's lap. The Count di Rudio, lean, nervous, dark, amidst so much triumph of fair complexions and rosy hues — like a grain of pepper — sat at first calm and composed; then, little by little, he grew all gestures and starts, and his countenance, most mobile, flashed and sparkled.

Before long, through his doing, the titanic figures who had striven for our *Risorgimento* were embroidered upon the fibres of those souls, all the more clearly for the fact that the fabric receiving them was sensitive and unversed in powerful emotions. And they were transformed by it: they felt, suffered, hoped and willed in the Italian manner.

What was bound to happen, happened. The heroes were dead or far away; the Count di Rudio was close at hand, young, attractive. He too had suffered for the sacred cause. In the eyes of the two young women he ended by growing to heroic stature, and they blended about that head his own prestige and that of others.

Angelo Fumagalli had a singular expression to characterise that situation: "Venice," he would say, "had the Moor; Belluno, being smaller, has only the *Moretto*. But the Moor was loved by a single Desdemona; that rascal di Rudio has two. Perhaps... and why not?... with not a shadow of malice, mind you!... perhaps he has three!"

The only one who seemed unaware of this was the Count di Rudio himself. Love knows no tropics; hearts burn at the Pole as furiously as at the Equator. It is the manner of expressing affection that varies with latitude. The Italian impetuosity is unknown to the young English woman, who perhaps senses instinctively how discordant it would sound in that world of subdued tones. Free and mistress of herself, she adorns herself, when she loves, with a composed dignity... the distinguishing mark of the free. Hence her unjust reputation for coldness. That charm glided over the spirit of the Count, absorbed and all but saturated with a wholly different passion.

True it is that in the long hours spent at the workshop, whilst his practised hands carried out the habitual labour mechanically, the mind of di Rudio strayed toward the two young women. Often the daydream absorbed him so completely that the noise of the tools and the faint odour of the rushes were, as it were, suppressed. In those moments — so he affirmed to me — a mysterious fragrance of youth and health penetrated his lungs and gave him a subtle intoxication. Sometimes he had caught himself yearning for the evening... But was it not in the evening that, having scraped away the scales of vulgarity, he conversed with his great ones, upon great things? The explanation seemed more than sufficient to him, and he did not trouble to enquire any further.

Unexpectedly, his employer, a Frenchman, withdrew from the business. A new proprietor, new methods: the foreign labour was dismissed, and the Count di Rudio found himself once more without immediate resources, very nearly destitute. For a time he went forward on the small savings he had put aside; then he found himself reduced to utter penury. The day he turned out his pockets and searched his every pocket within, he reflected, not without dismay, that he already owed a month's rent to Signora Mancherini. For several evenings past, at the lesson, he had appeared pale and listless. Miss Elisa asked him whether he was unwell. "Not precisely," he replied, "but I must say something that weighs upon me. I have most pressing obligations. Tomorrow evening I shall be engaged elsewhere; and I do not know when we shall be able to resume our lessons."

The festival of rosy and azure hues was over... the *via crucis* of the hero reduced to the level of the pariah began, the one to whom society denies salt and fire. He went out early in the morning, and returning at a very late hour, he managed to slip along the corridor, avoiding unpleasant encounters — he had come to call them such. He was of such a temper that he would have preferred to face the mouth of a cannon to the face of a creditor.

Unfortunately, one cannot leave hunger on the threshold of one's own house, nor hurl it into the street like an awkward burden. He hurled instead to the moneylender his few superfluous possessions, one by one. He had left behind his old friends, and to approach them now, when he was in extreme need, seemed to him a deliberate courting of rebuff. He would have been glad of any work; but these were times of crisis and the unemployed formed an army. In the newspapers the "Help Wanted" columns were conspicuous by their absence; those of "Situation Wanted" were measured by the yard. He went about soliciting here and there; but to compound his misfortune, he had the appearance of a gentleman. The more humane contented themselves with answering with some pleasantry. Others, having barely glanced at him, grunted the laconic "Go on!" which to Italian ears seems saturated with so much contempt. He walked as long as his legs would support him; then he resorted to the recourse of those without recourse... sitting upon a bench in some park and waiting for the late night... a painful repose.

Relief came to him unexpectedly, one night by mysterious ways, when he had not tasted a morsel in forty-eight hours. He had returned home with his customary precautions. He did not light the lamp, and before undressing, from the force of habit, turned down the bedclothes. Something heavy rolled upon the bed. Shortly afterwards he was unwrapping a parcel tied with great care, and a delicious fragrance tickled his nostrils. Before him, entirely at his disposal, was a marvel of roasted viands. And he bit into them with canine voracity, without reflection, without thought. Only when he had wiped his mouth did he, wondering that he had waited so long, ask himself: "Whence has all this abundance rained down upon me? Who can have taken care of me?"

His first suspicion fell upon Signora Mancherini and her two gentle creatures whom she, in the manner of Providence, sheltered beneath her ample wings. But he reflected, not without logic, that Signora Mancherini would have laid out a proper table, and that a parcel tied with care must have come from a distance. None of his old friends knew of his critical circumstances.

What mystery, then, was this?

To put it briefly, he had already stretched himself beneath the bedclothes and the outer world was already receding, and even the consciousness of his own being had grown dim; but a small part of his brain still worked on mechanically... Silence; the limitless and tenebrous void. Now and then, within it, the two questions flashed unresolved: "Whence this abundance? Who is taking care of me?"

And since life and the universe, in practical terms, exist only insofar as they are reflected in our minds, we may gauge to what minimal terms they may be reduced even within the skull of a hero!
