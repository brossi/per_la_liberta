ALLE nove precise, Monsieur de Collet era nella mia cella. Non si diffuse in grandi particolari, ma quanto mi narrò fu più che sufficiente a metter fine alle mie congetture. Influenti personaggi inglesi avevano intercesso, per me, presso la sua augusta Imperatrice. Ricorreva, appunto in quei giorni, il secondo compleanno del principe ereditario ed ella, che era sopratutto donna e madre, si era compiaciuta di solennizzare il fausto avvenimento, accordando una grazia che avrebbe sollevato dalla sua afflizione una donna, una madre. Mia moglie era già stata avvisata telegraficamente della mia buona ventura.

Monsieur de Collet si profuse, a questo punto, in congratulazioni; poi si incamminò per andarsene, ma ritornando sui suoi passi: "Fu l'Imperatrice che mi inviò stamane. Ella volle accertare che la sua volontà non fosse violata. Tuttavia è bene il rammentare che molto di bene e molto di severa giustizia può procedere dal suo augusto consorte. Una frase di gratitudine a lui rivolta, certo non vi potrebbe nuocere. Dico questo di mia propria iniziativa; ma sono persuaso di interpretare il pensiero di Sua Maestà l'Imperatrice, a cui siete pur debitore della vita."

Che dovevo rispondere? Da un paio d'ore operava in me una profonda reazione e, a quel mio primo, strano rincrescimento per l'inaspettato ritorno alle sensazioni dei vivi, già succedeva un anelito disperato verso la vita intensa, verso la libertà! La speranza, violentemente espulsa dall'animo, ritornava a torrenti, né forza umana avrebbe potuto resisterle. Dico, che se l'esprimere sentimenti mentiti, poteva contribuire anche solo un fuscello ai progetti privi di qualsiasi forma, ma indubbi nella loro tendenza, che già mi germogliavano nel cervello, io non dovevo titubare a scrivere una, dieci, cento lettere... all'Imperatore, al Papa, al- Ero senza difesa alcuna nelle mani di nemici onnipotenti... qualsiasi sotterfugio, qualsiasi menzogna diventava arte legittima di guerra e di salvezza. Sgusciare da quegli artigli, a qualunque costo, per rizzarsi più fieramente a mani slegate; per combattere ancora... questo il problema!

Promisi!

Fui tratto una volta ancora, al Palazzo di Giustizia, d'innanzi a quel Presidente Delangle che tanto del suo veleno aveva istillato nel nostro processo. Per una di quelle ipocrisie care ad una società che annette più importanza alle apparenze che non alla sostanza, si volle che io sembrassi libero in Tribunale e mi tolsero le catene. Fu letta la parte dei resoconti processuali che mi riguardava; in seguito l'avvocato generale Oscar de Valle, chiese la ratificazione delle lettere patenti imperiali che commutavano la mia pena di morte, nell'ergastolo a perpetuità. Tali lettere portavano la data del 12 Marzo. Perchè mai, dunque, fui tratto sino ai piedi del patibolo? Erano da incolparsi le lentezze burocratiche, o il proposito deliberato di assoggettarmi ad una pressione morale che potesse indurmi a fare delle rivelazioni?

Debbo notare che il resoconto ufficiale dell'esecuzione si diffonde a parlare del Pieri e dell'Orsini... sul conto mio, non una parola! Penso che non si volesse urtare la suscettività di un popolo, ammettendo implicitamente che la Francia cavalleresca aveva abolito la tortura fisica, unicamente per sostituirvi quella dell'intelletto. Ratificata la grazia, il Presidente Delangle si provò a calare un ultimo colpo a favore dell'ordine sociale, quale egli l'intendeva, o fingeva d'intenderlo, domandandomi se ero pentito "del mio delitto". Alla protesta del mio avvocato, che temeva qualche fiera risposta, non insistette e si accontentò di dire ai gendarmi: "Conducetelo via!" Le manette mi furono rimesse. Venni condotto alla Conciergerie e di là, in vettura cellulare, alla Roquette, ove fui sospinto nel cortile, cogli altri prigionieri. L'un d'essi mi venne subito incontro, offrendomi la destra. Era Antonio Gomez.

Quale sguardo fu il nostro e quanti pensieri ci scambiammo, in pochi secondi, senza far parola! Fu il Gomez che ruppe il silenzio. La sventatezza che aveva provocato il mio arresto, gli rimordeva ed egli me ne chiese scusa.

"Non accorartene, gli dissi. Errori ne abbiamo commesso un po' tutti. Ma ben si può affermare che il nostro animo non conobbe moti malvagi".

Finimmo per appoggiarci ad un muro, quasi ad esporre le nostre membra al Sole... triste Sole che non preludiava ancora la primavera. Parlammo dei due martiri... essi che non avevano sparso sangue, erano i decapitati! Nel mio petto si insinuò e crebbe una sensazione più fredda dell'inverno e le parole ci morirono sulle labbra. Fui tolto ai miei pensieri da un secondino che, passando, scosse violentemente un mazzo di chiavi.

Trascorsero parecchi giorni, resi più penosi dagli umilianti regolamenti carcerari; resi lunghi dalla febbrile attesa di notizie da parte di mia moglie. Io mi immaginavo che la Polizia non frapponesse, ormai, ostacolo alcuno alla nostra corrispondenza e l'Elisa, ritardando a darmi sue nuove, aveva risollevate le mie ansietà. Forte fu, perciò, il mio sussulto quando mi sentii annunciare che la mia presenza era richiesta alla Cancelleria.

Fui condotto proprio d'innanzi al soprintendente, il Marchese Lassalle, il quale mi porse una lettera procurandomi, con quel semplice atto una triplice sorpresa. La prima perchè, contrariamente alla ferrea disciplina, essa era tuttora suggellata; la seconda perchè i caratteri fini, che apparivano sulla busta, non erano dell'Elisa... essi mi giungevano assolutamente ignoti; la terza perchè, guardando in faccia il soprintendente, come per trarne una spiegazione, mi avvidi che, malgrado la sua serietà superficiale, egli, sotto la pelle, sorrideva.

Lacerai la busta e provai una quarta sorpresa, di fronte alla quale le tre antecedenti diventarono sbiadite ed insignificanti. La lettera incominciava esortandomi a leggere sino alla fine prima di giudicare. Procedeva poi annunciandomi che chi scriveva era la viscontessa d'Hasting, una scozzese protestante, convertitasi alla religione cattolica e diventata suor Teresa delle Carmelitane scalze. Era rinchiusa nel convento di Montauban, presso Tolosa. "Debbo anche premettere — diceva suor Teresa — che le regole del nostro Ordine ci impongono di recarci la notte, ogni due ore, a pregare nella chiesa del Convento". Una notte, mentre ella pregava, le era inaspettatamente apparso il Cristo. Il quale, con voce ineffabile, le aveva comunicato che, nelle carceri della Roquette, era un giovine di famiglia patrizia, coraggioso, con l'animo nutrito a buoni ed elevati sentimenti. Il contatto di persone malvagie lo aveva deviato dal retto sentiero e sospinto alla perdizione. Il Cristo aveva concluso affidandole la santa missione di salvare l'anima di quel traviato caro, malgrado i suoi trascorsi, al Padre celeste.

Seguiva una minuta descrizione della vita e della conversione di suor Teresa e una calda esortazione perchè l'imitassi; perchè ritornassi nel grembo della vera chiesa di Dio.

La lettera aveva anche una postilla, nella quale suor Teresa mi assicurava d'aver ottenuto dall'Arcivescovo di Parigi, il permesso di attivare con me una fraterna corrispondenza. L'Arcivescovo stesso aveva poi indotto le Autorità carcerarie a concedermi di suggellare le mie risposte, che le verrebbero recapitate senz'essere lette, né da esse Autorità, né da chicchessia.

Non occorreva soverchia perspicacia per comprendere, che i caratteri della Carmelitana scalza, fini, eleganti, dissimulavano il sozzo connubio della Chiesa e dell'Impero. Per compiacere all'adultero, la sposa del Cristo aveva assunto il turpe mestiere della spia.

Porsi la lettera al marchese Lassalle osservandogli, ironicamente, che se certe castronerie erano ancora possibili, in pieno secolo XIX, sotto l'Impero, nella Francia degli Enciclopedisti, esse non avevano presa alcuna sull'animo dei patrioti italiani. Il marchese Lassalle si strinse nelle spalle senza rispondere; ma il suo mal dissimulato sorriso non si tenne e trasudò da ogni poro della pelle.

Tuttavia io considerai che da quella farsa, insinuata nella pienezza del dramma, avrei potuto trarre qualche vantaggio per la mia famiglia; lasciando anche stare che lo scrivere mi servirebbe di distrazione. Suor Teresa ebbe la mia risposta.

Che le dissi? Ringraziai innanzi tutto, dell'interesse che ella si era preso per me. Il Cristo, che ha le cose conte, certo doveva averla informata che io ero un libero pensatore e che non credevo punto né in lui, né nelle sue dottrine. Se però esisteva realmente una Divina Misericordia, disposta a fare qualche cosa per me, lasciasse momentaneamente da parte l'anima e pensasse agli estremi bisogni della mia esistenza terrena. Avevo moglie ed un bimbo, al cui benessere ero necessario. Se il Cristo operava il miracolo di rimettermi in libertà, forse il mio scetticismo ne rimarrebbe scosso e... da cosa nasce cosa. A piede libero, avremmo parlato con maggior agio dell'anima mia!

Ora vedi sottigliezza dei servi di Dio! Essi si guardarono bene dall'indurre il loro principale a fare il miracolo; ma comprendendo che qualcosa bisognava pur concedermi, non foss'altro per adescarmi colla speranza del meglio, tirarono segretamente i fili e... Un giorno (ne eran passati solo cinque o sei, dacché avevo scritto a suor Teresa) mentre passeggiavo, meditando, una mano, amichevolmente ruvida, calò sulla mia spalla. Era il capo secondino che m'invitava a seguirlo in Cancelleria. Aveva l'aria misteriosa di un uomo che conduca il bimbo colà dov'è nascosto il pomo.

Pochi minuti dopo, avevo anch'io la mia apparizione. Non immaginaria, non mentita; ma vera e viva e trepidante più di me che di sé stessa. Stava ritta nell'ombra del camerone, come la Madonna di un quadro fiammingo e, non appena entrai, mi corse incontro con l'irruenza della passione e mi abbracciò. Elisa!... Elisa!... Non era sola. Aveva con sé il suo bambino: non più avvolto nei lini candidi, come quando l'avevo lasciato a Londra; ma fatto grandicello, tentava nel carcere i suoi primi passi malcerti. Svincolatomi dalle braccia d'Elisa, lo sollevai e lo baciai sonoramente; egli rabbuiò il viso tondo e fissò i grandi occhi in volto alla mamma, come per accertare, dalla sua espressione, che non correva alcun pericolo. Diceva, nel suo muto linguaggio: "Debbo piangere, o debbo ridere?"

Il guardiano, seduto presso l'uscio, una gran chiave nella destra, mi avvertì che, pel nostro ultimo addio, non ci era concesso più di un quarto d'ora. Potevamo — disse — parlare liberamente in inglese.

Il nostro breve colloquio fu estremamente concitato. Ella mi informò che, seguendo il mio consiglio, era riuscita ad interessare alla nostra sorte certo Sir Walter, della sua stessa Nottingham, il quale era una delle colonne del "Times" di Londra. Il potente giornale aveva fatto circolare una supplica, firmata poi, per umana influenza di Sir Richard Main, da parecchi personaggi influenti a Corte. La Regina Vittoria, piegata da una pressione abilmente dissimulata, aveva autorizzato l'ambasciatore inglese a Parigi, ad adoperarsi perchè la supplica fosse recapitata personalmente all'Imperatrice Eugenia.

L'Elisa aveva saputo della grazia accordatami sin dalla notte del 12. Un telegramma disceso, per lungo ordine gerarchico, sino al capo dei detectives Sanders e, da questi, risalito a lei, le annunciava che "non per mio riguardo, poiché non ne meritavo punto, ma per pietà verso una giovane madre ed un bimbo innocente, l'Imperatrice aveva disposto perchè la mia vita fosse risparmiata".

Elisa aveva accennato alla prima parte del telegramma sorridendo... sorridendo tra le lagrime, sorriso ineffabile! Del resto, come nessuna lettera sua mi era stata recapitata, così nessuna delle mie era giunta in sue mani.

Io la guardavo e pensavo: Che farà la poverina, legata dalle Leggi e più dall'amore ad un uomo destinato, forse, a non più rivederla? Sentii che dovevo, lì per lì, scioglierla da ogni suo obbligo e l'esortarla a cercare un appoggio degno di lei, mi parve virtù. Ma non si rinuncia, senza tentare qualche sforzo supremo, al dominio di un nobile cuore. Incominciai col l'informarla della lettera di suor Teresa e, proseguendo: "Invieranno certo — dissi — qualche monaca, o qualche prete, anche da te. Qualunque cosa ti dicano sul conto mio, non rilevarla. Se ti fanno domande indiscrete, rispondi costantemente che non sai nulla. Se ti offrono danaro, accettalo. Se ti invitano a recarti in qualche posto, non andarvi mai sola... mai! Fra un anno sarò libero; penserò io a trovare il modo di esserlo. Se passato l'anno non ti avrò raggiunta... agisci come se fossi morto e lo sarò! Un di Rudio non può vivere a lungo in catene!"

Il quarto d'ora — ahi quanto presto! — era trascorso. Ci abbracciammo e le sue lagrime riprincipiarono... ma io ben m'accorsi che esse erano di pietà, non di disperazione. Evidentemente, ella poneva ancora tutta la sua fiducia in me e si aspettava che mantenessi, alla lettera, la mia promessa. A maggiore suo conforto, rincalzai: "Sì, mi rivedrai libero... fra un anno!" Poi cercai con l'occhio il bimbo mio. Era sulle ginocchia del guardiano e stringeva, estasiato, tra le sue esili dita, la grossa chiave, quasi fosse un giocattolo. Il guardiano stesso, porgendolo al mio abbraccio, osservò la bizzarria della sorte che divertiva il piccino colla chiave che gli rubava il padre, che ne faceva un orfano!...

L'uscio si aperse. Sulla soglia mi volsi per incidere l'imagine d'Elisa nel cuore.

La rividi, ritta tra l'oscurità, come la Madonna di un quadro fiammingo. Il bimbo anelava i suoi baci e le tirava la veste. Un secondo e la mia apparizione era svanita!...

AT precisely nine o'clock, Monsieur de Collet was in my cell. He did not dwell upon great particulars, but what he narrated to me was more than sufficient to put an end to my conjectures. Influential English personages had interceded on my behalf with his august Empress. It so happened that those very days fell upon the second birthday of the crown prince, and she, who was above all else a woman and a mother, had been pleased to solemnise the auspicious occasion by according a grace that would lift from her affliction a woman, a mother. My wife had already been advised by telegraph of my good fortune.

Monsieur de Collet lavished congratulations upon me at this point; then he turned to take his leave, but retracing his steps: "It was the Empress who sent me this morning. She wished to ensure that her will had not been violated. Nevertheless, it is well to bear in mind that much good, and much of stern justice, may proceed from her august consort. A phrase of gratitude directed toward him could certainly do you no harm. I say this of my own initiative; but I am persuaded that I interpret the thought of Her Majesty the Empress, to whom you are moreover indebted for your life."

What was I to answer? For a couple of hours a profound reaction had been at work within me and, in place of that first, strange reluctance at the unexpected return to the sensations of the living, a desperate yearning toward an intense life, toward liberty, had already taken hold! Hope, violently expelled from the spirit, was returning in torrents, and no human force could have resisted it. I say that if the expression of feigned sentiments could contribute even the smallest straw toward plans devoid of any definite form, yet unquestionable in their tendency, which were already germinating in my brain, I ought not to hesitate to write one, ten, a hundred letters — to the Emperor, to the Pope, to the — I was entirely without defence in the hands of all-powerful enemies; any subterfuge, any falsehood became a legitimate art of war and of self-preservation. To slip from those claws, at whatever cost, in order to stand more proudly erect with hands unbound; to fight on — that was the problem!

I gave my promise!

I was brought once more before the Palais de Justice, before that President Delangle who had instilled so much of his venom into our trial. By one of those hypocrisies dear to a society that attaches more importance to appearances than to substance, it was deemed proper that I should appear free in the Tribunal, and my chains were removed. The portion of the trial proceedings that pertained to me was read aloud; thereafter the advocate-general Oscar de Valle requested the ratification of the imperial letters patent commuting my death sentence to perpetual life imprisonment. These letters bore the date of the 12th of March. Why, then, had I been led to the very foot of the scaffold? Were the delays of bureaucracy to blame, or was it the deliberate intention to subject me to a moral pressure that might induce me to make revelations?

I must note that the official account of the execution dwells at length upon Pieri and Orsini — concerning myself, not a word! I believe they did not wish to offend the susceptibilities of a people by implicitly acknowledging that chivalrous France had abolished physical torture only to replace it with that of the intellect. The pardon having been ratified, President Delangle attempted to deliver a final blow in favour of the social order as he understood it, or pretended to understand it, by asking whether I was repentant "of my crime." Upon the protest of my advocate, who feared some fierce reply, he did not insist, and contented himself with saying to the gendarmes: "Take him away!" My manacles were replaced. I was conveyed to the Conciergerie and from there, in a prison van, to La Roquette, where I was thrust into the courtyard among the other prisoners. One of them came toward me at once, offering me his right hand. It was Antonio Gomez.

What a look passed between us, and how many thoughts we exchanged in a few seconds, without uttering a word! It was Gomez who broke the silence. The carelessness that had provoked my arrest still gnawed at him, and he begged my pardon for it.

"Do not trouble yourself over it," I told him. "We have all committed errors enough. But one may well affirm that our spirit knew no base impulses."

We ended by leaning against a wall, as though to expose our limbs to the sun — a cheerless sun that gave no foretaste of spring. We spoke of the two martyrs — they who had shed no blood, they were the ones who had been beheaded! Into my breast there crept and grew a sensation colder than winter, and the words died upon our lips. I was drawn from my thoughts by a warder who, passing by, rattled a bunch of keys with a violent clatter.

Several days elapsed, made the more painful by the humiliating regulations of the prison; made long by the feverish wait for news from my wife. I imagined that the Police would now interpose no obstacle to our correspondence, and Elisa, in delaying to send me word of herself, had revived all my anxieties. My start was therefore violent when I was informed that my presence was required at the Governor's office.

I was brought directly before the superintendent, the Marchese Lassalle, who handed me a letter, furnishing me by that simple act with a threefold surprise. The first, because, contrary to the iron discipline of the place, it was still sealed; the second, because the fine characters appearing on the envelope were not Elisa's — they were entirely unknown to me; the third, because, looking the superintendent in the face as though to draw some explanation from it, I perceived that, despite his outward gravity, he was, beneath the surface, smiling.

I tore open the envelope and experienced a fourth surprise, before which the three preceding ones grew pale and insignificant. The letter began by urging me to read to the end before passing judgement. It then announced that the writer was the Viscountess d'Hasting, a Scottish Protestant who had converted to the Catholic faith and become Sister Teresa of the Discalced Carmelites. She was enclosed in the convent of Montauban, near Toulouse. "I must also premise," said Sister Teresa, "that the rules of our Order require us to go every two hours through the night to pray in the church of the Convent." One night, while she was praying, Christ had appeared to her unexpectedly. He had communicated to her, with an ineffable voice, that within the prisons of La Roquette there was a young man of patrician family, courageous, with a spirit nourished upon good and elevated sentiments. The contact of wicked persons had led him astray from the right path and driven him toward perdition. Christ had concluded by entrusting her with the sacred mission of saving the soul of that erring one, who was dear, notwithstanding his transgressions, to the Heavenly Father.

There followed a detailed account of Sister Teresa's life and conversion, and a fervent exhortation that I should emulate her; that I should return to the bosom of the true Church of God.

The letter had also a postscript, in which Sister Teresa assured me she had obtained from the Archbishop of Paris permission to establish with me a fraternal correspondence. The Archbishop himself had then prevailed upon the prison authorities to permit me to seal my replies, which would be delivered to her without being read — neither by said authorities nor by any person whatsoever.

No very great perspicacity was needed to understand that the fine, elegant handwriting of the Discalced Carmelite concealed the foul union of the Church and the Empire. To please the adulterer, the bride of Christ had assumed the vile trade of the spy.

I handed the letter to the Marchese Lassalle, observing to him, with irony, that if certain absurdities were still possible in the full light of the nineteenth century, under the Empire, in the France of the Encyclopaedists, they had no purchase whatsoever upon the spirit of Italian patriots. The Marchese Lassalle shrugged his shoulders without replying; but his ill-concealed smile would not be contained and seeped from every pore.

Nevertheless I considered that from that farce, insinuated into the fullness of the drama, I might draw some advantage for my family; leaving aside, moreover, that the act of writing would serve me as a diversion. Sister Teresa had my reply.

What did I say to her? I thanked her, first of all, for the interest she had taken in me. Christ, who has knowledge of all things, must surely have informed her that I was a free-thinker and that I believed not in the least in him, nor in his doctrines. If, however, there truly existed a Divine Mercy disposed to do something for me, let it lay the soul momentarily to one side and think upon the extreme necessities of my earthly existence. I had a wife and a child, to whose welfare I was indispensable. If Christ were to work the miracle of restoring me to liberty, perhaps my scepticism might be shaken thereby and — one thing leads to another. At liberty, we should speak of my soul with greater ease!

Now observe the subtlety of the servants of God! They took good care not to induce their principal to work the miracle; but understanding that something must nonetheless be conceded to me, if only to bait me with the hope of better things, they drew the strings secretly and — One day (only five or six had passed since I had written to Sister Teresa), as I was walking in meditation, a friendly, rough-hewn hand descended upon my shoulder. It was the head warder, inviting me to follow him to the Governor's office. He had the mysterious air of a man leading a child to where the apple is hidden.

A few minutes later, I too had my apparition. Not imaginary, not feigned; but true and living and trembling more on my behalf than on her own. She stood erect in the shadow of the great hall, like the Madonna in a Flemish painting, and no sooner had I entered than she ran toward me with the impetuosity of passion and embraced me. Elisa! — Elisa! — She was not alone. She had with her her child: no longer wrapped in white linen, as when I had left him in London; but grown somewhat larger, he was attempting within the prison his first uncertain steps. Freeing myself from Elisa's arms, I lifted him and kissed him resoundingly; he clouded his round face and fixed his large eyes upon his mother's countenance, as though to ascertain from her expression that no danger threatened. He was saying in his mute language: "Ought I to weep, or ought I to laugh?"

The warder, seated near the door, a great key in his right hand, informed me that for our final farewell we were permitted no more than a quarter of an hour. We might, he said, speak freely in English.

Our brief meeting was exceedingly agitated. She informed me that, following my advice, she had succeeded in interesting in our fate a certain Sir Walter, of her own Nottingham, who was one of the pillars of the London Times. The powerful journal had circulated a petition, subsequently signed, through the humane influence of Sir Richard Main, by several personages of note at Court. Queen Victoria, moved by an adroitly dissembled pressure, had authorised the English Ambassador in Paris to endeavour to have the petition delivered personally to the Empress Eugenia.

Elisa had learned of the grace accorded me from the very night of the 12th. A telegram descended, through a long chain of hierarchy, to the head of the detectives, Sanders, and from him relayed to her, announcing that "not out of any regard for me, since I merited none whatsoever, but out of compassion for a young mother and an innocent child, the Empress had disposed that my life should be spared."

Elisa had referred to the first part of the telegram with a smile — smiling through her tears, an ineffable smile! For the rest, just as no letter of hers had been delivered to me, so not one of mine had reached her hands.

I looked at her and thought: What will the poor creature do, bound by the Laws and still more by love to a man perhaps destined never to see her again? I felt that I ought, there and then, to release her from every obligation, and to urge her to seek a support worthy of her seemed to me a virtue. But one does not relinquish, without some supreme effort, dominion over a noble heart. I began by informing her of Sister Teresa's letter, and continuing: "They will surely send," I said, "some nun or priest to you as well. Whatever they may say to you on my account, take no notice of it. If they put indiscreet questions to you, answer steadily that you know nothing. If they offer you money, accept it. If they invite you to go somewhere, never go alone — never! Within a year I shall be free; I shall find the means of being so. If by the time the year has passed I have not rejoined you — act as though I were dead, and I shall be! A di Rudio cannot live long in chains!"

The quarter of an hour — how swiftly it passed! — was over. We embraced, and her tears began afresh — but I perceived clearly that they were tears of compassion, not of despair. Evidently she still placed all her trust in me and expected that I would keep my promise to the letter. For her greater comfort I added: "Yes, you shall see me free again — within a year!" Then I sought out my child with my eyes. He was upon the warder's knee, and was clasping the great key between his slender fingers with a look of rapture, as though it were a toy. The warder himself, handing him to my embrace, remarked upon the bizarre caprice of fate that amused the little one with the very key that robbed him of his father, that made an orphan of him!

The door opened. Upon the threshold I turned to engrave the image of Elisa upon my heart.

I saw her, standing in the darkness, like the Madonna in a Flemish painting. The child was reaching for her kisses and tugging at her dress. One instant more and my apparition had vanished!

TWENTIETH CHAPTER


AT nine o'clock precisely, Monsieur de Collet was in my cell. He did not go into great detail, but what he related was more than sufficient to put an end to my conjectures. Influential English personages had interceded for me with his august Empress. It was, in fact, in those very days, the second birthday of the crown prince, and she, who was above all a woman and a mother, had been pleased to solemnize the auspicious event by granting a grace that would relieve a woman, a mother, of her affliction. My wife had already been advised by telegraph of my good fortune.

Monsieur de Collet, at this point, was profuse in his congratulations; then he started to leave, but returning on his steps: "It was the Empress who sent me this morning. She wished to be certain that her will would not be violated. Nevertheless, it is well to remember that much good and much severe justice can proceed from her august consort. A phrase of gratitude addressed to him could certainly do you no harm. I say this of my own initiative; but I am persuaded that I interpret the thought of Her Majesty the Empress, to whom you are, after all, indebted for your life."

What was I to answer? For a couple of hours a profound reaction had been at work in me, and my initial, strange regret for the unexpected return to the sensations of the living was already succeeded by a desperate yearning for an intense life, for freedom! Hope, violently expelled from the soul, returned in torrents, nor could any human force have resisted it. I say that if expressing feigned sentiments could contribute even a single straw to the projects, devoid of any form but doubtless in their tendency, that were already germinating in my brain, I must not hesitate to write one, ten, a hundred letters... to the Emperor, to the Pope, to— I was utterly defenseless in the hands of omnipotent enemies... any subterfuge, any lie became a legitimate art of war and of salvation. To slip from those claws, at any cost, to rise up more fiercely with hands unbound; to fight again... this was the problem!

I promised!

I was brought once more to the Palace of Justice, before that President Delangle who had instilled so much of his venom into our trial. By one of those hypocrisies dear to a society that attaches more importance to appearances than to substance, they wished me to appear free in the Tribunal, and they removed my chains. The part of the trial records that concerned me was read; subsequently, the attorney general, Oscar de Valle, asked for the ratification of the imperial letters patent which commuted my death sentence to imprisonment for life. These letters bore the date of March 12th. Why, then, was I brought to the very foot of the scaffold? Was the slowness of bureaucracy to blame, or the deliberate intention to subject me to a moral pressure that might induce me to make revelations?

I must note that the official account of the execution speaks at length of Pieri and Orsini... on my account, not a word! I think they did not wish to offend the susceptibilities of a people by implicitly admitting that chivalrous France had abolished physical torture only to replace it with that of the intellect. Once the pardon was ratified, President Delangle attempted to strike a final blow in favour of the social order, as he understood it, or pretended to understand it, by asking me if I repented "of my crime." At the protest of my lawyer, who feared some fierce reply, he did not insist and contented himself with saying to the gendarmes: "Lead him away!" The manacles were put back on me. I was taken to the Conciergerie and from there, in a cellular carriage, to la Roquette, where I was pushed into the courtyard with the other prisoners. One of them came to me at once, offering his right hand. It was Antonio Gomez.

What a look was ours, and how many thoughts we exchanged, in a few seconds, without a word! It was Gomez who broke the silence. The thoughtlessness that had provoked my arrest was a remorse to him, and he begged my pardon for it.

"Do not grieve over it," I told him. "We have all made our share of errors. But it can well be said that our souls knew no wicked impulses."

We ended by leaning against a wall, as if to expose our limbs to the Sun... a sad Sun that did not yet prelude the spring. We spoke of the two martyrs... they who had shed no blood were the ones decapitated! A sensation colder than winter crept into my breast and grew, and the words died on our lips. I was roused from my thoughts by a turnkey who, in passing, violently shook a bunch of keys.

Several days went by, made more painful by the humiliating prison regulations; made long by the feverish wait for news from my wife. I had imagined that the Police would no longer place any obstacle in the way of our correspondence, and Elisa, by delaying to give me her news, had revived my anxieties. My start was therefore a strong one when I heard it announced that my presence was required at the Chancellery.

I was led right before the superintendent, the Marchese Lassalle, who handed me a letter, causing me, with that simple act, a triple surprise. The first because, contrary to the iron discipline, it was still sealed; the second because the fine characters that appeared on the envelope were not Elisa's... they were utterly unknown to me; the third because, looking the superintendent in the face, as if to draw an explanation from him, I perceived that, despite his superficial seriousness, he, under the skin, was smiling.

I tore open the envelope and experienced a fourth surprise, before which the three preceding ones became pale and insignificant. The letter began by exhorting me to read to the end before judging. It then proceeded to announce that the writer was the Viscountess d'Hasting, a Scottish protestant, converted to the Catholic religion and become Sister Teresa of the Discalced Carmelites. She was enclosed in the convent of Montauban, near Tolosa. "I must also premise," said Sister Teresa, "that the rules of our Order require us to go at night, every two hours, to pray in the Convent church." One night, while she was praying, Christ had unexpectedly appeared to her. He, with an ineffable voice, had communicated to her that in the prisons of la Roquette there was a youth of a patrician family, courageous, with a soul nourished on good and lofty sentiments. The contact of wicked persons had diverted him from the straight path and driven him to perdition. Christ had concluded by entrusting to her the holy mission of saving the soul of that stray one, dear, despite his past transgressions, to the heavenly Father.

There followed a minute description of the life and conversion of Sister Teresa and a warm exhortation that I should imitate her; that I should return to the bosom of the true church of God.

The letter also had a postscript, in which Sister Teresa assured me she had obtained from the Archbishop of Paris the permission to enter into a fraternal correspondence with me. The Archbishop himself had then induced the prison Authorities to grant me leave to seal my replies, which would be delivered to her without being read, either by those Authorities or by anyone else.

It did not require excessive perspicacity to understand that the fine, elegant characters of the Discalced Carmelite concealed the filthy union of the Church and the Empire. To please the adulterer, the bride of Christ had taken up the foul trade of the spy.

I handed the letter to the Marchese Lassalle, observing to him ironically that if such nonsense were still possible, in the middle of the 19th century, under the Empire, in the France of the Encyclopedists, it had no hold on the souls of Italian patriots. The Marchese Lassalle shrugged his shoulders without replying; but his ill-concealed smile could not be contained and oozed from every pore of his skin.

Nevertheless, I considered that from that farce, insinuated into the fullness of the drama, I might be able to draw some advantage for my family; leaving aside also that writing would serve me as a distraction. Sister Teresa had my reply.

What did I say to her? I thanked her, first of all, for the interest she had taken in me. Christ, who has all things numbered, must certainly have informed her that I was a freethinker and that I believed not at all either in him or in his doctrines. If, however, a Divine Mercy truly existed, disposed to do something for me, let it for the moment leave my soul aside and think of the extreme needs of my earthly existence. I had a wife and a child, to whose well-being I was necessary. If Christ performed the miracle of setting me at liberty, perhaps my skepticism would be shaken and... one thing leads to another. Once free, we would speak with greater ease of my soul!

Now behold the subtlety of the servants of God! They took great care not to induce their principal to perform the miracle; but understanding that something must be conceded to me, if only to bait me with the hope of better, they secretly pulled the strings and... One day (only five or six had passed since I had written to Sister Teresa) as I was walking, meditating, a hand, amicably rough, fell upon my shoulder. It was the head turnkey, inviting me to follow him to the Chancellery. He had the mysterious air of a man leading a child to where the treat is hidden.

A few minutes later, I too had my apparition. Not imaginary, not false; but real and alive and more trembling for me than for herself. She stood erect in the shadow of the great room, like the Madonna in a Flemish painting, and, as soon as I entered, she ran towards me with the impetuousness of passion and embraced me. Elisa!... Elisa!... She was not alone. She had her child with her: no longer wrapped in white linens, as when I had left him in London; but grown a little, he was attempting in the prison his first uncertain steps. Freeing myself from Elisa's arms, I lifted him up and kissed him soundly; he darkened his round face and fixed his large eyes on his mother's face, as if to ascertain from her expression that he was in no danger. He was saying, in his mute language: "Should I cry, or should I laugh?"

The guard, seated near the door, a great key in his right hand, warned me that for our last farewell we were allowed no more than a quarter of an hour. We could—he said—speak freely in English.

Our brief colloquy was extremely agitated. She informed me that, following my advice, she had succeeded in interesting in our fate a certain Sir Walter, from her own Nottingham, who was one of the pillars of the London *Times*. The powerful newspaper had circulated a petition, signed then, through the humane influence of Sir Richard Main, by several personages influential at Court. Queen Victoria, swayed by a skillfully concealed pressure, had authorized the English ambassador in Paris to see to it that the petition was delivered personally to the Empress Eugénie.

Elisa had learned of the pardon granted to me as early as the night of the 12th. A telegram, descended through a long hierarchical order down to the chief of detectives Sanders, and from him sent up to her, announced that "not for my regard, for I deserved none, but out of pity for a young mother and an innocent child, the Empress had arranged for my life to be spared."

Elisa had alluded to the first part of the telegram smiling... smiling through her tears, an ineffable smile! For the rest, just as no letter of hers had been delivered to me, so none of mine had reached her hands.

I looked at her and thought: What will the poor thing do, bound by the Laws and more so by love to a man destined, perhaps, never to see her again? I felt that I must, then and there, release her from all her obligations, and to exhort her to seek a support worthy of her seemed to me a virtue. But one does not renounce, without attempting some supreme effort, the dominion of a noble heart. I began by informing her of Sister Teresa's letter and, continuing: "They will certainly send," I said, "some nun, or some priest, to you as well. Whatever they tell you on my account, take no notice of it. If they ask you indiscreet questions, reply constantly that you know nothing. If they offer you money, accept it. If they invite you to go somewhere, never go there alone... never! In a year I will be free; I will see to finding the way to be so. If after a year I have not reached you... act as if I were dead, and I shall be! A di Rudio cannot live long in chains!"

The quarter of an hour—ah, how quickly!—had passed. We embraced and her tears began again... but I could see well that they were of pity, not of desperation. Evidently, she still placed all her trust in me and expected that I would keep, to the letter, my promise. For her greater comfort, I added: "Yes, you will see me free again... in a year!" Then I searched with my eye for my child. He was on the guard's knee and was clutching, ecstatic, between his slender fingers, the large key, as if it were a toy. The guard himself, handing him to my embrace, remarked on the bizarreness of fate that amused the little one with the key that was robbing him of his father, that was making an orphan of him!...

The door opened. On the threshold I turned to engrave the image of Elisa in my heart.

I saw her again, standing in the darkness, like the Madonna in a Flemish painting. The child yearned for her kisses and pulled at her dress. A second and my apparition had vanished!...

# Chapter the Nineteenth

ON the evening of the twelfth, the chaplain Hughon came to pay me his customary visit. He was grave, grave, grave. I spoke to him of our rejected appeal, and he became embarrassed. Then the suspicion of the grim truth flashed upon me, and I resorted to several subterfuges to confirm it. But Hughon promptly assumed the air of the ingenuous, and sheltering behind that simple artifice, became unassailable. Perhaps every suspicion of mine might have dissolved, had my visitor not extended his hand as he took his leave. Those fingers seemed to me to trace mysterious signs in the air, so visibly did they tremble.

I awaited the return of Saturno who, of the two guards, seemed to me the more candid, and without even giving him time to remove his cap: "Saturno," I said, "I know that tomorrow morning I shall be led to the scaffold. Heed me and see to it, you who are good, that my last wishes are carried out to the letter. At five o'clock you will wake me..."

Here he began to protest. Why had I put such nonsense into my head? The appeal being rejected, there remained still, thanks to our connections, a great deal of fighting to be done, and before anything... eh, eh!... it would take much more than that!

"My friend, Monsieur Hughon intended to hear my confession and instead... I have confessed him. He has revealed everything to me!"

At this, Saturno, who was still gesticulating to give emphasis to that *eh, eh!* of his, let his arm fall as though I had snapped it off. "He revealed everything to you? That is against the Regulations and he ought not to have done it." He stood for a moment with eyes downcast, then murmured: "Since you know everything... well, then — yes! there is nothing left for you now but to die manfully, as you have lived."

Then, all at once, a great silence fell within my soul, and with it a kind of great darkness; but in that emptiness, life still manifested itself powerfully through an instinct that drove me to crave something yet darker, yet more silent. *"Tanto meglio quanto più presto!"* — "The sooner the better!" — is Orsini's expression; only then did I understand its full meaning.

"At five precisely!... I have a great deal, a very great deal to write. My last thoughts, from the prison, to my kinsmen and to Elisa; my last thoughts, from the scaffold, to the Patria... And you?... Take this! (I kissed him.) Tomorrow, at this hour, think of me. Say to yourself: Human miseries touch him no more!"

Saturno was punctual. My first sensation on that memorable day was caused by the extreme pallor of his face. I wrote to my father; I wrote to my wife's kinsmen, commending that unhappy woman to them from the depths of my heart. Six o'clock struck at the Roquette.

Shortly after, ten or twelve persons entered the large room adjoining. Among them I recognised the chaplain Hughon and the Governor of the prison. They made their way to Orsini's cell, and I heard them announce to him his final hour. The Governor of the prison then pronounced, in a low voice, a few phrases, the meaning of which was plain to me from Orsini's reply. A magistrate stood ready to receive the dying man's disclosures, and he had been urged to make a full confession. The reply of the martyr rang through the sepulchral silence, subdued yet distinct: "Felice Orsini knows how to die; he does not know how to be an informer!"

Then came Pieri's turn, and his nerves gave way. "He was old, he was bound; but he would have had spirit enough..."

The Governor of the prison admonished him courteously, and he recovered himself at once. "Be it as it may," he said, "to die now, to die in some few years — what does it matter? My executioners too shall die..." He added that he intended to plunge into eternity singing.

My turn came. I replied calmly that the Count Carlo di Rudio was ready — an expression which, in the official reports, was afterwards attributed to Orsini. As my lips closed, I made out more clearly, in the dim light, the man who had accompanied the Governor of the prisons into my cell. He was an elderly abbé... A fine soul, perhaps. But to me, so ill-disposed toward everything that recalls the tonsure, he had the effect of an enemy come to ascertain, *de visu*, my last moments. And since death was near and certain, I could well, without fear of giving harm to any living soul, give free rein to my feelings. He drew close to me and made to speak. I imagined he wished to exhort me to die like a Christian, and I forestalled him: "I prefer," I said, "to consecrate my last thoughts to my wife and my little child. As for your God, I shall see Him, if He exists, but only to tell Him from what manner of ministers He has the simplicity to let Himself be represented upon this earth."

We were conducted into the large room. Our hands were unbound. Grave, and belonging to people who were tragically moved, the faces... the rest, almost like a scene of friends who bid farewell to friends, on the eve of a long journey.

Pieri, firmly resolved to treat the matter as an ordinary event, would not renounce his breakfast. The appropriate orders were given. While waiting, a round of introductions even took place. In this way I learned that Hughon was to be the spiritual comforter of Orsini; the chaplain of the Conciergerie, that of Pieri; the abbé fastened to my side was the chaplain of the House of Correction.

An elegant *cabaret* — a tray with coffee and rum — was brought. Felice Orsini sipped from the latter, toasting the health of the Governor of the prisons. At this point, my "comforter," humanely overlooking my rough rebuff, offered me a small glass; but he made the mistake of observing that it would give me courage. I refused out of pride, retorting that Italian patriots had no need of artificial courage. Then there was a rivalry in asking one another for small keepsakes. The three executioners, upright and silent along the wall, watched us with a strange persistence.

Suddenly the Governor drew out his watch and made a sign to the guards. The Regulations, heedless of conscience, required us to attend Mass. Felice Orsini moved sadly toward the prison chapel. I protested, and Pieri added to my curt refusal his own impetuous torrent of words. Those present glanced at one another; the Governor shrugged his shoulders and in the end consented to compromise. Orsini's absence was very brief. No sooner had he returned than they led us toward the Chancellery, whence we passed into an adjoining room. On entering, Pieri, who preceded me by a few paces, turned round and said to Orsini: "Well then, my old friend?" Orsini raised his head, looked at him steadily and replied: "Calm, calm!" Those were the first words those two men, united by their ideals and their martyrdom, had exchanged since their arrest... and they were the last!

We were in the hands of the executioner. They shaved my nape; they made me put on a low-cut shirt; they removed my shoes with their own hands. The same preparations, carried out rapidly, left Orsini absorbed in his thoughts; but the voluble man from Lucca grew almost cheerful from it all... the strangeness of nerves! He wished to see himself in a mirror, and his executioner — something of a dandy, most likely, despite his foul trade — drew a small looking-glass from his waistcoat pocket and held it out to him. He examined himself at his leisure and, smiling, exclaimed: "You have got me up like an old *cocotte*!" While they were removing his shoes he broke into a frank laugh and said: "Lucky thing I washed my feet!"

As for me... why should I not say it? Those moments were extremely painful to me. For several years my life had been a succession of events in vivid colours; but every trace of them seemed suddenly effaced by the memories of childhood that assailed me in a throng. And I saw again my poor mother, all tenderness and all smiles, as on a feast-day when she dressed me and, gazing at me, took her pride in it. What a contrast!

But the Count di Rudio was not to be seen, at such a moment, with tearful eyes, and if a tear or two wished to spring forth, his pride took care to drive them back into the depths and dry them there.

While they were wrapping my head in the black veil, Giuseppe Pieri was already walking out without a word. But I reached him in time to hear him utter that phrase which, slightly distorted, afterwards served almost as a pretext for demonstrating his grandiose and eccentric mind. To me it seemed, and still seems, the virile expression of a patriot who, having fulfilled his mission to the utmost limit of his own powers, feels himself lightened of every perishable and vulgar part of himself, and almost sublimated by martyrdom. The chaplain of the Conciergerie had taken his arm, as though there were need to support him. He, without even turning his head: "One does not feel fear when one goes to Calvary!" And he moved away between the two foul props of every tyranny, the priest and the executioner... and I saw him no more. But I heard his voice distinctly strike up, in the courtyard, the hymn of the Girondins: *"Mourir pour la Patrie"* — "To Die for the Fatherland." He went to the scaffold, as he had promised, singing!... it rains.

I am in the courtyard... it rains and snows. Weather that reconciles me to the thought of the imminent warm shelter underground. I pass alongside a company of prison guards drawn up in line, presenting arms... the rifles have their muzzles pointed to the ground, their stocks in the air. The great gate stood wide open; the scaffold, erected a few paces from the pavement, cut off my view; but not so much that I could not make out on the square a considerable body of foot-soldiers, of light cavalry, of artillery, drawn up as if in the imminence of action.

My hands were bound behind my back and fastened, moreover, to the bonds that constrained my feet. I advanced in steps necessarily small and slow. The executioner, on my right, stood a full head taller than I; the old priest, on my left, must with his white hair have contrasted strangely with my dark, restless youth. He was murmuring prayers, his eyes upon a crucifix. I had expressed the desire to draw a few puffs of smoke, and this had been granted me. But the water and snow, which I trod with bare feet, which fell upon my bare shoulders, provoked shiverings that I strove in vain to suppress, and that apparent weakness, vexing me, drove me to bite savagely at the stem of my pipe.

Near the gate, the field of my vision widened, and I saw the windows and saw the rooftops thronged with people. The spectacle of ill-starred virtue, the Greeks declared, is worthy of the Gods, and to witness it, men had paid as much as a thousand francs for the hire of a balcony. The mood of that multitude? "It" — says the official report — "remained constantly calm and silent throughout the great and legitimate expiation." In truth, a robust voice from above cried out: "People, bare your heads! This is the execution of heroes!" A commotion broke out on the square. They were attempting, I believe, to surround the house from which that human protest had come. But just at that moment my attention was drawn elsewhere... the unexpected occurred!

A man on horseback had entered through the gate... he was dressed in civilian clothes, his chest covered with decorations. He dismounted from the saddle and guards and soldiers saluted him in military fashion. It was Monsieur de Collet, the Empress's major-domo. He came up to me, looked me full in the face, and noticing my trembling, said: "It is cold, Monsieur Rudio!" I replied: "Yes, but they will not leave me time to catch a chill." A puff of smoke struck him full in the face. "I beg your pardon; my hands are bound and besides... it is the last!" — "Oh, not at all; you will smoke again!" He murmured a few words in the ear of the executioner first, then of the old priest, and both, slowly and silently, withdrew. Then, turning once more to me, as I questioned him with the expression of my face: "I shall come to your cell towards nine o'clock and explain everything." I spun on my heels and found myself face to face with Felice Orsini.

He slowed his step: "What is this?"

— They are sending me back to prison. I know nothing more.

— And Pieri?

— He is up there!

— Better two than three!

We continued on our opposite ways... he to fecundate with his blood the ideal of the Patria; I toward the prison... and that return to life came upon me as something grievous as a violence, so fully was I prepared for death! After a few steps, I turned my head, and, by a singular correspondence of souls, Orsini turned his as well. "Farewell!" he said, with a nod of his head. The black veil that wrapped it, grown thick and opaque here and there in its folds, concealed his face, so that he already seemed to me half lost in the mysterious shadow of eternity...

I found the large room and my cell crowded with soldiers and officers. A group was busily gathered about my bed, upon which lay, in a dead faint and in convulsions, my faithful guard, Saturno. I made my way through to him, and while a compassionate hand unbound my arms, I kept calling him by name. My voice proved more efficacious than the sprinklings of water and the smelling-salts... He opened his eyes, looked at me as one lost in a dream, then his face brightened with a light that revealed a friendship profoundly sincere. All at once, he assailed me with these words: "The wager! The wager!... you have lost!... you shall pay the champagne!"

"Later, my friend. This is an hour for grief. Down there, men are dying!" Almost as though to confirm my words, the clock of the Roquette struck: seven strokes. In the prison a sepulchral silence fell. The tragic hour bestows strange clairvoyance... the officers uncovered their heads. At that moment, the blade of the guillotine... the heads... the vile basket...

The Count di Rudio, overcome by emotion, could not continue. The insufficiency of the heart, which was already undermining his robust old age, took away his breath. I knew that to observe him at such moments irritated him and worsened the ailment, and it was with a true sense of relief that I heard the quick step of Signora Elisa. She entered and did not perceive the gravity of the moment. She stretched out her arms, and her left hand was laid over her open right palm. She was smiling with her lips, smiling with her blue eyes, as though she carried a great happiness within her soul and wished to parcel it out and share it with us. She uncovered her palm and displayed... two chicks nestled there, of a luminous whiteness. Who remembers the white hen that was brooding in the courtyard? "They have hatched!" said the Signora, still smiling. Those two little atoms of life looked at us in wonder, as though asking: "Who are you?"

I turned my face away... the Californian sun, near its setting, peered through the green foliage. My sad eyes, intolerant of light, closed; but the reddish disc remained imprinted on my retina. O ardent imagination! O vividness of evocation! In that blood-red glare, the severed head of Felice Orsini appeared... Gripped by the hair in the executioner's fist, it gasped, breathless, that last, fateful wish of his: *"Viva l'Italia! Viva la Francia!"*

My lips did not move, but my soul gave a cry: "Oh, how dearly the Patria has cost us!"
