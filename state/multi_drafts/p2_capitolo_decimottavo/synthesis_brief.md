IL giorno seguente, alla Roquette. Ci fecero indossare il grigio uniforme dei prigionieri; ci misero la camicia di forza... non per libidine di tortura, ma per prevenire tentativi di suicidio.

Ci avevano trasportati al nuovo carcere separatamente e separatamente rinchiusi; ma le nostre celle erano situate in modo che potevamo vederci e parlarci. Una camera rettangolare, piuttosto ampia, nuda... quattro pareti bianche, quattro finestre, quattro usci. Chi entra da quello che comunica col cortile, scorge di fronte l'uscio e la finestra della cella di Felice Orsini; la cella del Pieri era oltre la parete di sinistra; oltre la parete di destra, la mia.

Ciò costituiva, per noi, un grande vantaggio sulla Conciergerie; la morte era in prospettiva, ma si faceva precedere da qualche sollievo.

Un'altra circostanza contribuiva a rendermi più sopportabile la triste ora attuale. Le mie guardie mi avevano seguito alla Roquette. Avrebbero dormito alternativamente per tenermi sotto continua sorveglianza; ma ciò non mi contrariava affatto, perchè io non avevo intenzione alcuna di anticipare, suicidandomi, la mia fine.

Il giorno stesso del nostro trasloco, i nostri avvocati vennero a consigliarci di ricorrere in appello. Non si sperava nulla di concreto; tuttavia tanto io quanto il Pieri, acconsentimmo senza sollevare alcuna obbiezione. Ma l'Orsini non ne voleva sapere. Egli affermava che la morte lo avrebbe ormai sollevato da un gran peso e che gli sarebbe giunta tanto più accetta quanto più presto. Giulio Favre, per ottenerne l'assenso, dovette quasi forzargli la mano.

Principiarono i giorni dell'agonia e trascorsero veloci. Per uno stato d'animo anche troppo spiegabile, i ricordi di quei giorni mi lampeggiano nella mente a sbalzi, risaltando quasi disegni non concatenati sullo sfondo cupo dell'ore in cui, meditando, mi preparavo a morire.

Il cappellano della Roquette, monsieur Hughon, ci faceva una visita almeno ogni ventiquattro ore. Era un ex capitano dei dragoni e ne conservava le tinte; traverso e forte, malgrado l'età; liberale malgrado la posizione. Sin dal suo primo apparire gli dissi, in modo esplicito, che sarebbe sempre il benvenuto a un patto; non parlarmi mai di soggetti religiosi. Promise e mantenne la parola.

Giuseppe Pieri, invece, gli riconobbe di buon grado il diritto di esercitare l'opera sua, ma non per subirla passivamente. Sin dalla prima sera egli lo provocò alla discussione con aria aperta di sfida e si lasciò trasportare, nei giorni successivi, da uno spirito demolitore, da un argomentare ad hominem estremamente acre e quasi offensivo. Costretto dalle pietose circostanze, l'ex capitano dovette far buon viso a più di un colpo di lancia in pieno costato.

La voce del Pieri era, del resto, la nota predominante in quell'ambiente ristretto e severo. E come mai egli, già tanto loquace anche nelle circostanze più incolori della vita, avrebbe potuto rassegnarsi al silenzio, ora che forti emozioni lo esaltavano?

Parlava e discuteva di tutto ostinatamente... talora anche con vena comica abbondante, specie se gli capitava di confutare gli sproloqui dei nostri giudici. Ma gira e rigira, ritornava di preferenza sulle sue idee politiche e filosofiche; come se una voragine trascinasse inesorabilmente nel cannoncino di quell'imbuto.

Strano contrasto col contegno del nostro capo, dalla cui cella non usciva, per ore ed ore, il minimo indizio di vita; tanto che parecchie volte mi ero domandato se essa non fosse la tomba di Felice Orsini; se Felice Orsini non fosse diventato la tomba dell'anima sua.

Un'ora al giorno, ci era concesso di passeggiare nel gran cortile; ma non contemporaneamente. Il Pieri era tratto alla sua boccata d'aria, solo; al suo ritorno, io e l'Orsini uscivamo assieme. Portavamo la nostra camicia di forza anche passeggiando ed eravamo guardati, più che a vista, a punta di moschetto.

In quella breve ora, l'Orsini allargava i polmoni come uomo che rinasca; ma lo sguardo che rivolgeva all'azzurro era pieno di melanconia. Passeggiava celeramente ed io, allevato a disciplina militare, lo seguivo, istintivamente un po' in disparte. Avveniva questo: Se io ero di malumore e non parlavo, egli rivolgeva il capo e mi interrogava collo sguardo quasi dicesse: "Tempesta?" Ma se parlavo, tirava via come assorto nella visione d'altri mondi o, se rispondeva, le era parole tratte dal profondo, col cavaturaccioli.

Quelle parole brevi, bastarono a rivelarmi l'uomo che subiva rapidamente una trasformazione radicale.

Felice Orsini aveva portato in carcere due risentimenti; quello contro il Pieri e quello contro Giuseppe Mazzini. E' noto che nelle sue "Memorie" l'irruente romagnolo tratteggiava il Mazzini come un moderno Maometto che presuma modellare i destini d'Europa sui capricci delle sue odalische — circostanza questa, di cui si servì a meraviglia il monarchico Cavour. Egli diffuse quelle Memorie a migliaia di copie, gettando così astutamente un ponte che offerse il destro di passare il Rubicone, di ripiegare la bandiera dal motto "Dio e Popolo" a troppi e troppi mazziniani, o titubanti per mollezza di fibra, o ansiosi di coonestare, con qualsiasi pretesto, calcoli bassi e venali.

Ma l'antico risentimento contro il Maestro dilegua il giorno che le guardie e Giulio Favre informarono l'Orsini della nobile difesa di lui fatta dal "Profeta" il indomani dell'attentato, quando altri patrioti, meticolosi sui mezzi di redimere la Patria, lo avevano quasi stigmatizzato come un assassino volgare.

A questa trasformazione, che potrei chiamare rigenerante, un'altra più vasta e di tutt'altro genere fece riscontro. Meglio affrontar subito l'argomento. Felice Orsini fu l'unico dei tre morituri che ascoltasse, più che passivamente, con compiacente orecchio, le esortazioni del cappellano-dragone. E le nostre passeggiate giornaliere mi insinuarono il presentimento di quello che poi avvenne. Le convinzioni lentamente acquistate nella combattuta esistenza si andavano, nell'uomini, disintegrando e lasciavano riapparire, anzi predominare, i sottostrati saturi di fole assorbite nella ignara fanciullezza. Nell'imminenza della sua fine, egli fece ritorno a quelle pratiche religiose a cui solo la cieca moltitudine dovrebbe attribuire ancora virtù essenziali.

Si inganna perciò e mentisce, inconsciamente, al vero, chi ammirando la stoica fortezza colla quale l'Orsini salì il patibolo, volle farne un modello di coerenza da contrapporre alla figura di Silvio Pellico e d'altri, rammolliti, quasi, dai patimenti. Ma sbaglierebbe del pari chi presumesse di tutelarne la grandezza sorvolando sulla sua conversione. Il silenzio nulla attenua e nulla cancella.

Ben si può dire a sua difesa, che egli soccombette ad una causa tutt'altro che ignobile. Capace di ardimenti leonini sotto un impulso fugace, egli nulla poteva, grazie alla sua natura superlativamente sensitiva, contro un pensiero tormentoso, insistente, ostinato, fisso... non quello della morte che gli sovrastava; ma l'altro derivante dalla coscienza della propria responsabilità nella rovina degli alti ideali; dalla visione della tragica morte altrui. I suoi capelli n'erano rapidamente incanutiti e più d'una tenue fibra del suo cervello ne doveva essere rimasta scossa e quasi scalfita.

Felice Orsini non posò mai del resto, d'innanzi ai suoi contemporanei, come uno scopritore di verità filosofiche e morali. Patriota si disse, invece, e fu sin dalla prima giovinezza; patriota fu e si proclamò sinché ebbe il capo sotto la lama della ghigliottina. Egli aveva promesso all'Italia ogni sua energia e sin l'ultima stilla del proprio sangue. L'Italia ebbe figli più grandi, non più incondizionatamente fedeli. Io dico che non c'è bisogno di spigolare in altri campi per costituire la gloria di Felice Orsini!

Oltre il cappellano-dragone faceva quasi giornalmente la sua comparsa alla Roquette un personaggio molto potente, il Prefetto della Polizia Pietri. Ci trattava in modo molto affabile; né mai tralasciava di adoperarsi per insinuare nell'animo nostro qualche raggio di speranza... ora affermando che il Tribunale d'Appello avrebbe, con tutta probabilità, annullato il nostro processo; ora assicurando che l'Imperatrice e un forte partito di Corte si mostravano propensi alla clemenza così apertamente che l'animo di Napoleone avrebbe finito per rimanerne influenzato e vinto.

Ma né i suoi modi, né la sua fisionomia intelligente valevano a dissipare la diffidenza istintiva, a colmare l'abisso che separa il cospiratore da un uomo che deve, per forza di cose, dargli la caccia e trarre il proprio soddisfacimento dalla sua rovina. Questa diffidenza era in me avvalorata da altre circostanze. A più riprese, io aveva inviato ad Elisa lettere appassionatissime. Quando volevo scrivere, la guardia di turno mi liberava, più o meno, la destra; poi, rilegatala, portava lo scritto al Direttore del carcere perché vi apponesse il nulla osta. Ma le mie calde invocazioni non ebbero risposta... silenzio assoluto, come se l'Elisa mi avesse completamente abbandonato, o fosse morta. E' facile immaginarsi le mie torture. Poi, d'improvviso, mi entrò nel cuore il sospetto che si trattasse di una raffinata crudeltà della Polizia, per fini che non potevo ben comprendere, ma la cui esistenza intuivo. Da quel momento le mie torture cessarono... per far posto ad un sordo rancore contro chi me le aveva procurate.

Inoltre il Pietri aveva il torto di condurre al carcere, quasi costantemente, ora questo, ora quel personaggio. Una morbosa curiosità pare sospingesse le alte classi a verificare, che so io, forse se eran proprio composti di carne e d'ossa coloro che avevano osato provocare tanto scompiglio. A me quell'esser messo in mostra, come una belva in gabbia, pesava terribilmente.

Un giorno mi trovai nella cella, col Pietri, un militare d'alto bordo che aveva la testa fasciata. Non rammento bene se fosse il generale Macan o il generale Roguet. Il Pietri me lo presentò semplicemente come "una delle vostre vittime"; il che fece prorompere il mio mal umore. Ribattei aspramente che io non avevo cercato di colpire se non che l'uomo che fu la rovina del mio Paese. E quanto all'essere vittima ben volentieri avrei mutato panni con quel ferito.

A questo il militare si fece innanzi e, porgendomi la sua destra, affermò che non conservava il minimo rancore, né con me, né con altri. Ed io strinsi, come meglio mi venne dato, quella mano, ma non risposi e, rivolgendomi al Prefetto, gli chiesi quale interesse potesse avere la Polizia nell'intercettare le lettere di mia moglie.

Parve meravigliarsi; ma si accontentò di stringersi nelle spalle.

Il giorno seguente ritornò solo e, prima di entrare nella cella stette per qualche secondo, ritto sulla soglia, ingombrando il vano colla sua bella ed alta persona. A me parve di notare in quella fisionomia qualcosa di insolito, di inquisitivo. Entrò e, dopo aver ripetuto, in modo più affabile e, sto per dire, più paterno del solito, le solite frasi, si fregò la fronte e, abbassando alquanto la voce — la guardia era, del resto, uscita al suo apparire — mi parlò intensamente della sua profonda simpatia per la mia giovinezza. E, compiangendo la triste sorte che mi era riserbata, un po' per forza di circostanze, un po' anche — disse — per la suprema leggerezza de' miei compagni, mi assicurò d'aver molto meditato per trovar modo di salvarmi e non me solo! Già io sapevo come a Corte non si rifuggisse dall'adottare misure temperate a clemenza. Ma io ignoravo — soggiunse — che Sua Maestà l'Imperatore era pure animato da vivissime simpatie per l'Italia, la cui grandezza ammirava, le cui sventure profondamente lo commuovevano. Una volta messo su simile via, un uomo simile, chi potrebbe predire dove si arresterebbe? Certo nessun sovrano d'Europa era in grado di poter giovare alla redenzione d'Italia, quanto Napoleone. Cosicché se io consideravo le cose coll'occhio del patriota illuminato, non tarderei a comprendere di poter giovare meglio a me stesso ed alla Patria aiutando, alleandomi per così dire, all'uomo che avevo tentato d'uccidere.

Credo che la mia faccia esprimesse tutta la mia profonda meraviglia, perchè il Pietri mi mise paternamente una mano sulla spalla e abbassando sempre più la voce, disse:

"So che tratto con un giovanotto intelligente e mi attendo perciò di non essere considerato come uno zotico. Tra Sua Maestà e i suoi alti disegni, stanno i nemici interni dell'Impero. Troppo deboli per agire apertamente, si sono segretamente alleati ai nemici esterni ed hanno congiurato. Io non sono venuto a proporle nulla di disonorevole. La salvezza, il benessere degli Stati hanno le loro esigenze ineluttabili. Aiuti l'alto personaggio, da cui può dipendere il trionfo della causa italiana; lo premunisca contro i suoi nemici interni ed io le do la mia parola d'onore che Ella non tarderà a raggiungere la sua giovane sposa. Per quale strana aberrazione, vorrebbe lasciarla povera, abbandonata e sola a lottare nel mondo e contro il mondo?"

Ora la ragion vera del preteso silenzio di Elisa mi appariva, più che adombrata, evidente; ora il mio sdegno aveva doppia ragione di prorompere. "Non so nulla — risposi — e nulla posso rivelare. Ma quand'anche... voi, voi stesso, se accogliessi le vostre proposte, sareste il primo a provare orrore per me. Né da voi, Corso, me le sarei aspettate... gli Italiani aborrono i traditori!"

La mia naturale vivacità mi sospingeva a gestire e, non potendolo, tremavo. Ma senza volerlo, avevo toccato una corda sensibile in un cuore non al tutto spoglio di nobili sentimenti.

Io vidi quell'uomo, rotto a tante emozioni, coprirsi la faccia colle mani. Pochi secondi e mi guardava in viso... i suoi occhi erano rossi di lagrime. Partì promettendo che avrebbe fatto tutto quanto dipendeva da lui per salvare la nostra testa. Se falliva nell'intento, avrebbe rassegnato le proprie dimissioni.

Io non ho motivo alcuno per dubitare della sincerità di quell'uomo; ma dire ciò che realmente abbia fatto, o tentato di fare, non so. Questo è certo: L'undici Marzo egli venne alla Roquette e comunicò a Felice Orsini, se non altro, le sue speranze. Fu poco prima dell'ora destinata alla nostra passeggiata. La conversazione fra i due ferveva ancora, quando quell'ora suonò e le guardie mi trassero nel cortile, solo. Al mio ritorno, Felice Orsini, sempre così taciturno e quasi cupo, mi apparve d'un subito trasformato e sto per dire ilare. Non appena mi vide, disse ad alta voce: "Di Rudio, sta di buon animo. Noi non saremo giustiziati!"

Questo avveniva, ripeto, la mattina dell'undici Marzo. La sera stessa, la Corte di Cassazione respingeva il nostro appello. Se l'affermazione dell'Orsini era destinata ad avere riscontro nella realtà, gli avvenimenti del 12 Marzo lo avrebbero dimostrato. La giornata trascorse e le nostre aspettative rimasero deluse. La stessa notte del 12, al chiarore delle torcie, si lavorava sulla piazza della Roquette ad erigere il patibolo.

La spiegazione dell'enigma?

L'ebbi più tardi dalla bocca di Mathieu de la Drôme. L'opinione pubblica esercitava una forte pressione a nostro favore. A dispetto della Polizia, nei ritrovi popolari si cantava una canzone esaltante, "gli eroi del 14 Gennaio". Per la clemenza si erano dichiarati l'Imperatrice e un forte partito di Corte. Napoleone III chiamava indecenti le loro manifestazioni; ma non gli bastava l'animo d'opporvisi apertamente. Eliminare i nemici caduti in suo potere, gettare lo spavento tra quelli che gli erano sfuggiti... tale il segreto anelito del liberticida; ma bisognava farlo, dandosi l'aria d'aver la mano forzata, quasi per scrupoloso rispetto alle Leggi, addossandone ad altri la responsabilità.

Il Consiglio di Stato venne radunato per decidere sulla maggiore o minore opportunità della grazia sovrana e l'Imperatore vi fece un'apparizione fugace. "Liberi i Consiglieri della Corona — disse — di votare secondo la propria coscienza; ma non poteva esimersi dal manifestare una sua convinzione. In quel Consesso, si celavano i traditori!"

Era la fosca minaccia. I Consiglieri venivano radunati non per assecondare la marea della pietà, ma per rendere un servigio al padrone. Potevano i dignitari di un Impero, la cui essenza era l'affarismo e la corruzione e che, in tal vortice, avrebbe travolta la Francia intera, titubare tra le laute sinecure e la vita di pochi reietti, fatti incapaci di reagire e di nuocere? E non aveva la ragion di Stato dissimulate ben altre codardie?

Quasi non bastasse, l'Arcivescovo di Parigi, subodorata la situazione, ammortì la mite influenza di Eugenia Montijo, ponendo tra essa e l'Imperatore, le ponderate, sottili esortazioni della Chiesa. Mai gli odi e gli interessi del clericalismo e dello Stato si erano mostrati tanto affini... i congiurati odoravano di Massoneria; essi avevano combattuto anima e corpo per metter fine a quel potere mondano che è lo scopo mal celato e supremo d'ogni imbroglio religioso; essi, potendolo, avrebbero rinnovata la lotta. Ben valeva la pena di sopprimerli anche se, per farlo, si sconfessava un'altra volta il Cristo.

Il Consiglio di Stato ed il clero... Napoleone III n'ebbe più di quanto sperava e il suo animo, che sapeva scandagliare i caratteri malgrado le maschere, deve aver esultato sinistramente e forse, come il despota della tragedia, esclamava entro sé stesso: "Oh quante sono le coscienze vendute!"

THE following day, at La Roquette. They made us put on the grey uniform of prisoners; they fitted us with the straitjacket... not from any wanton cruelty, but to forestall attempts at suicide.

We had been conveyed to the new prison separately and confined separately; yet our cells were so situated that we could see and speak to one another. A rectangular chamber, rather spacious, bare... four white walls, four windows, four doors. Whoever enters by the door communicating with the courtyard perceives, directly opposite, the door and window of Felice Orsini's cell; Pieri's cell lay beyond the left-hand wall; beyond the right-hand wall, my own.

This constituted, for us, a great improvement upon the Conciergerie; death was in prospect, yet it was pleased to be preceded by some measure of relief.

Another circumstance contributed to render the present sad hour more bearable to me. My guards had followed me to La Roquette. They would sleep in turns in order to keep me under continuous surveillance; but this did not trouble me in the least, for I had no intention whatsoever of anticipating my end by self-destruction.

On the very day of our removal, our lawyers came to counsel us to lodge an appeal. Nothing concrete was hoped for; nevertheless, both I and Pieri consented without raising any objection. But Orsini would hear nothing of it. He maintained that death would by now relieve him of a great burden, and that it would come the more welcome the sooner it arrived. Giulio Favre, to obtain his agreement, was obliged almost to force his hand.

The days of agony began and passed swiftly. In a state of mind only too explicable, the memories of those days flash through my mind in sudden leaps, standing out like disconnected images against the sombre background of those hours in which, meditating, I prepared myself to die.

The chaplain of La Roquette, Monsieur Hughon, paid us a visit at least once every twenty-four hours. He was a former captain of dragoons and had retained the colouring of that life; thickset and robust, in spite of his years; liberal in spite of his position. From his very first appearance I told him plainly that he would always be welcome on one condition: never to speak to me of religious subjects. He promised, and kept his word.

Giuseppe Pieri, on the other hand, willingly acknowledged his right to exercise his ministry, but not to do so without challenge. From the very first evening he provoked him to disputation with an air of open defiance, and allowed himself in subsequent days to be carried away by a demolishing spirit, by a manner of arguing *ad hominem* that was in the extreme degree sharp and almost offensive. Constrained by the pitiable circumstances, the ex-captain was obliged to put a good face upon more than one lance-thrust full in the ribs.

The voice of Pieri was, moreover, the predominant note in that confined and austere place. And how indeed could he, already so loquacious even in the most colourless circumstances of life, have brought himself to silence, now that powerful emotions exalted him?

He talked and argued about everything with obstinate persistence... at times even with an abundant vein of comedy, especially when it fell to him to confute the extravagances of our judges. But turn it as he might, he returned by preference to his political and philosophical ideas; as though a vortex drew him inexorably into the muzzle of that funnel.

A strange contrast with the bearing of our leader, from whose cell there issued, for hours on end, not the smallest sign of life; so that more than once I had asked myself whether it were not the tomb of Felice Orsini; whether Felice Orsini had not become the tomb of his own soul.

One hour each day we were permitted to walk in the great courtyard; but not simultaneously. Pieri was taken out for his breath of air alone; upon his return, Orsini and I went out together. We wore our straitjackets even while walking, and were watched not merely by sight but at musket-point.

During that brief hour, Orsini would expand his lungs like a man reborn; but the gaze he turned upon the blue sky was full of melancholy. He walked at a rapid pace and I, reared in military discipline, followed him, instinctively keeping slightly apart. This would occur: if I was in ill humour and did not speak, he would turn his head and question me with a look, as though to say: "A storm brewing?" But if I spoke, he would stride on as though absorbed in the vision of other worlds, or, if he answered, the words came from the depths, drawn out as with a corkscrew.

Those brief words sufficed to reveal to me the man who was undergoing rapidly a radical transformation.

Felice Orsini had brought into prison two resentments; one against Pieri and one against Giuseppe Mazzini. It is well known that in his *Memorie* the impetuous Romagnol had portrayed Mazzini as a modern Mahomet who presumed to model the destinies of Europe upon the caprices of his odalisques — a circumstance of which the monarchist Cavour made admirable use. He distributed those *Memorie* in thousands of copies, thus cunningly throwing a bridge which afforded the opportunity to cross the Rubicon, to furl the banner bearing the motto *"Dio e Popolo"* to all too many Mazzinians, either wavering from weakness of fibre, or eager to justify with any pretext their base and mercenary calculations.

But the old resentment against the Master dissolved on the day when the guards and Giulio Favre informed Orsini of the noble defence of him made by the "Prophet" on the morrow of the attempt, at a time when other patriots, fastidious regarding the means of redeeming the Fatherland, had well-nigh branded him as a common assassin.

To this transformation, which I might call regenerative, another and vaster one of quite a different order corresponded. Better to face the subject at once. Felice Orsini was the only one of the three condemned men who listened to the exhortations of the chaplain-dragoon more than passively, with a complaisant ear. And our daily walks insinuated in me a presentiment of what afterwards came to pass. The convictions slowly acquired through a life of struggle were, in the man, gradually disintegrating, and allowed to reappear, nay to predominate, the underlying strata saturated with the fables absorbed in unknowing childhood. In the imminence of his end, he returned to those religious practices to which only the blind multitude should still attribute essential virtue.

He therefore deceives himself, and unwittingly misrepresents the truth, who, admiring the stoic fortitude with which Orsini mounted the scaffold, would make of him a model of consistency to be set against the figure of Silvio Pellico and others, softened, as it were, by their sufferings. But he would be equally mistaken who should presume to safeguard his greatness by passing over his conversion in silence. Silence attenuates nothing and cancels nothing.

One may well say in his defence that he succumbed to a cause that was anything but ignoble. Capable of leonine daring under a fleeting impulse, he was powerless, by reason of his supremely sensitive nature, against a thought that was tormenting, insistent, obstinate, fixed... not the thought of the death that hung over him, but that other, springing from the consciousness of his own responsibility in the ruin of high ideals; from the vision of the tragic death of others. His hair had rapidly turned white with it, and more than one delicate fibre of his brain must have been shaken and, as it were, scarred by it.

Felice Orsini, moreover, never posed before his contemporaries as a discoverer of philosophical or moral truths. A patriot he called himself instead, and was so from earliest youth; a patriot he was and proclaimed himself until his head lay beneath the blade of the guillotine. He had pledged to Italy his every energy and the last drop of his own blood. Italy has had greater sons; she has had none more unconditionally faithful. I say that there is no need to glean in other fields in order to establish the glory of Felice Orsini.

Beyond the chaplain-dragoon, there appeared almost daily at La Roquette a very powerful personage, the Prefect of Police, Pietri. He treated us in a most affable manner; nor did he ever fail to exert himself to instil into our minds some ray of hope... now affirming that the Court of Appeal would in all probability annul our trial; now assuring us that the Empress and a powerful court party showed themselves so openly disposed to clemency that Napoleon's mind would end by being influenced and overcome thereby.

But neither his manner nor his intelligent countenance availed to dispel the instinctive distrust, to bridge the abyss that separates the conspirator from a man who must, by force of circumstance, hunt him down and derive his own satisfaction from his ruin. This distrust was in me strengthened by other circumstances. On several occasions I had sent to Elisa letters of the most passionate character. When I wished to write, the guard on duty would free my right hand more or less, and then, having bound it again, would carry the letter to the prison Director to have his endorsement placed upon it. But my ardent appeals received no reply... absolute silence, as though Elisa had utterly abandoned me, or were dead. It is easy to imagine my torment. They would sleep in turns to keep me under continuous surveillance; but this did not vex me at all, for I had no intention whatsoever of anticipating my end by committing suicide.

Giuseppe Pieri, on the other hand, readily granted him the right to exercise his work, but not to undergo it passively. THE twenty-fifth of February arrived — the day appointed for the proceedings of the trial. I was brought to the Palais de Justice and for the first time, since the evening of the attempt, I saw my companions in misfortune fully and plainly.

The assembly-hall of the Tribunal was packed with men and ladies. There was a great display of fine apparel... people flocked to the trial as to the theatre. Yet, through the perception of I know not what mysterious sense, I understood at once that the manifold soul of that audience was not opposed to us. Hostile, by contrast — deliberately hostile — were the individuals seated upon the bench, and the handful of hired servants who surrounded them. These men perhaps imagined themselves to be the Empire and its might, and regarded our attempt as one regards a personal affront.

The trial was, at its core, the repetition of the preliminary examination... but revised and corrected. The right of defence exists for the virtuous and exists for the wicked, and I should be a fool were I to reproach the French judicial authorities for not having composed a panegyric upon the accused; but equally foolish would he show himself who, blinded by the prestige inherent in social position and with the instincts of a fetish-worshipper, took the opinions expressed by those authorities as a measure by which to judge us.

From the earliest unfolding of History down to our own days, privilege has been obliged, in order to persist, to fortify oppression with artifice. Oppression is, by its very nature, intermittent and does not reach everywhere. Its deficiency is repaired by a discipline refined across the centuries, which manifests itself in various ways according to the times and the customs, but which aims constantly at portraying the heads of a State or of a religion — at least while they live — as very arks of ingenuity, as very arks of sanctity; which aims, at the same time, at portraying the recalcitrant who refuse to enter the orbit of those supreme ones, those saints, as fanatics, as degenerate and wicked creatures.

This discipline creates the larvæ devoid of substance, yet rosy and phosphorescent, which draw the multitudes along tortuous ways; it creates the fearsome phantoms that drive them back, with wild terrors, from the direct ways. It was this discipline that rendered the Grand Lama inaccessible, lest the eye that sees, analyses and judges, should discern, in his flesh and in his spirit, the defects inseparable from human nature. It induced the Sultans to have themselves preceded and followed by a troop of assassins whirling the scimitar. The audacious who dared raise inquisitive eyes to the demigod provoked their own swift death.

This discipline has taught, on the other hand, that the rancour against rebellious souls must continue even after the punishment. To sever the heads that would not bow is not enough; they must be exposed, bloodied, to the mockery of the unthinking. To burn the thinker at the stake is not enough; a fierce malediction must be pronounced over his ashes — they must be scattered to the winds!

Austria would have been ingenuous had she contented herself with hanging Italian patriots. She buried them without funeral rites and without a bier, in out-of-the-way pits, as was the practice with the carcasses of quadrupeds.

It is this baleful discipline, modified by the times, that brands and persecutes as crime the honest expression of an honest thought; and where certain extremes are no longer possible, the livid war against reputation and memory is ever in fashion. Which war, if it proves useful to privilege more or less legally constituted, becomes an indispensable necessity for the privilege that has attained such standing by violence and by crime.

At my age one records facts; one does not vent rancours. In the trial constructed against the authors of the attempt of the 14th of January, the denigration mounted against us was all the more necessary inasmuch as the very origin of the Empire was, more or less openly, in question. We sat upon the bench of the accused before Napoleon; but Napoleon sat upon the bench of the accused before France and the civilised world. Every nobility of spirit in him, every loftiness of purpose, became an aggravating circumstance for us; every loftiness of purpose in us tended to diminish him — it was a condemnation upon him.

Nor should it pass unmentioned that violent action sometimes provokes incidents which demonstrate it more efficacious than any other manner of propaganda. To denigrate us, to have us passed off as malefactors of the basest sort, to lessen the likelihood of finding imitators — this, therefore, had to be the unconfessed yet essential aim of the trial. I acknowledge that, from their point of view, the French judicial authorities acted wisely in attempting to decapitate, together with our bodies, our reputations. I acknowledge, at the same time, that they plunged into the enterprise with all the sinister dexterity of the servants of the Second Empire.

The bill of indictment, formulated and read by the Imperial Procurator Chaix d'Est-Ange, glided deftly over the initial uncertainties and the police machinations; it portrays the examining authorities in the attitudes of genius that perceives, probes and vanquishes. It suppresses, by contrast, every impulse of ours that might reveal a character above the vulgar; and, dwelling upon the most easily censurable aspects and giving emphasis to our weaknesses, it loads us down with commentaries — often absolutely fantastic — that savour of cowardice.

The President of the Tribunal, Monsieur Delangle, for his part, laboured with viperous persistence to lend the Procurator's semi-literary creation its proper embellishments, dissembling its improbabilities by introducing incidents which, even if true, would have had no connection whatsoever with our conspiracy. Worse still, he cast "compassionately" over the threadbare garment of disinterestedness the ample cloak of the hired assassin; he branded with the mark of the charlatan a virtue in its death-throes.

An easy task, withal! We have lived in times of intense passions — times in which the attractions and repulsions of souls alternated with the violence of the tempest. What man, having taken part in them, to whatever degree, has not found himself treated, to his own astonishment, now worse than the devil, now better than a saint; the object, often simultaneously, of profound veneration and immeasurable hatred! One can well understand that the Police should have been in no want of mud to pour forth.

Thus upon Giuseppe Pieri the President Delangle poured, in Paris in 1858, the hatred of the Tuscan reactionaries which had pursued him since 1830 — the hatred that, portraying him as a beast of liberalism, had succeeded in having him stripped of his colonelcy; the hatred that had seized upon a prank among bohemians, in which a group of young scapegraces had relieved another scapegrace of a watch worth a few *baiocchi*, as a pretext to defame him.

The generosity of spirit, the haughtiness, the disdainful impulses of Felice Orsini were represented as a "ridiculous ostentation." And when Orsini persisted in maintaining that the third bomb had not been thrown by him but by the Italian to whom he had handed it but a few minutes before the attempt, Delangle demonstrated his serene impartiality in the search for truth by bursting out and portraying him as a liar seeking to make one believe that his hands were clean of the blood of the children, the women, the old men wounded and killed; "for the odour of that blood rose to him and mounted to his brain."

To complete his meritorious work in the service of lofty and moral principles, President Delangle then lent a complaisant ear to the sacristy whisper; and Felice Orsini — Commissioner of the Roman Republic in a remote province, who, surrounded by foreign enemies, weakened by enemies within, maintained his phalanx by means of requisitions — was depicted to the jurors as a common thief who had then escaped, by flight, the delights of the papal penitentiary.

As for what concerned me personally, Delangle had not the shadow of a pretext for reproaching me with any irregular family connection, nor with any real or apparent misappropriation; but this was not to render me immune to his venom. I had revealed how and why, on the evening of the 14th of January, Felice Orsini had handed us the sum of 200 francs. My revelation aimed at making our Judges understand that venality was not in our hearts. But I was compelled to realise that I had not reckoned with the refined malice of my Judges. That meagre drop, in such a moment of crisis, was sufficient for the upright Magistrate, striking the pose of indignant surprise, to accuse me — I who belonged to an honourable family — of having abandoned school and taken a loathing to work until I had so degraded myself as to become, step by step, a mercenary assassin for the sum of 200 francs!

A word or two upon the official trial reports. The tactic of denigration could not arrest itself at the margin of its papers, as the storied progenitors of our race at the threshold of paradise lost. They were retouched and mutilated and distorted, like all the rest, at the arbitrary pleasure of His Excellency. One example: when President Delangle asked whether we had anything to submit on the imminent application of the sentence, Gomez and Orsini replied: "Nothing!" Pieri observed that he had shed no blood. I pointed out Kim, the municipal street-sweeper who, filing past with the other witnesses, had given his false deposition. I denounced him aloud, protesting that in pronouncing the sentence, justice demanded that no account be taken of a perjured deposition.

The official report states that, contrary to my three companions, unanimous in pronouncing their "nothing," I invoked "the clemency of my judges."

The meeting between Francesco Crispi and Orsini? I should be lying were I to say I did not deny it. But one must remember that Orsini himself had, a few seconds before, declared that the Italian with the third bomb was not known to any of his accomplices, not even to Pieri; that he had revealed his existence to no one; that none of us had suspected him.

Was this not an almost open suggestion to me of my line of conduct? And was there not, moreover, in my denial, the most expeditious means of avoiding insidious questioning and machinations that might have compromised a man who remained unsuspected and at liberty?

Had the charlatans of the Empire not felt within their souls the terror of the truth, they would not have imposed upon the newspapers the obligation to adhere strictly, in their reports, to what was published in *Le Droit* and the *Gazzetta dei Tribunali*. A half-truth is no longer truth, and the reason of State that does not scruple to adulterate it by paring it down cannot restrain itself out of scruples when it is convenient to deck it out like a harlot, or to line its skirts with some tendentious little falsehood.

Let him believe blindly in the official reports who swears upon the infallible sanctity of the powerful, with that simplicity of soul which induces others to believe in the infallibility of the hypocrite into whose ear a dove might coo the truth. Infinite and manifold is human folly!

But the discerning man who does not let any broth drain through his intellect without first filtering it will certainly not fail to reflect that we find ourselves, if nothing else, under the unfavourable light of those whose history has been written by their own enemies, in times and circumstances which made those enemies believe any refutation on our part impossible.

The sentence was pronounced on the night of the 26th of January; and, as if to heighten the theatricality of that denouement, when the Court, having concluded its deliberations, re-entered the great hall of the audience-chamber, the clock of the Palais de Justice was striking its chimes.

For Gomez, imprisonment for life; for Pieri, for me, for Felice Orsini, the penalty reserved for parricides. Luigi Napoleone had become, after having cannonaded it, the father of his people — History is full of such bloody ironies! And lest any doubt remain to us as to the nature of that penalty, its full description was read aloud: "led to the scaffold in a shirt, barefoot, the head covered by a black veil; then the reading of the sentence; then the decapitation."

I looked full in the face of my companions in faith and in misfortune. Gomez, exceedingly pale, maintained a marble immobility — the grief of the condemnation and the entirely natural satisfaction of having escaped the worst seemed to cancel each other out within his heart. Pieri was shaking his head and kept his eye fixed upon the President, with an indefinable mingling of scorn and commiseration. I noticed that his lips were moving, and received the impression that he was pressing home, at that moment, a certain phrase he had addressed to Delangle himself during the interrogation: "I knew well enough that we should never come to any good end with that pigheaded man!"

Felice Orsini was slowly turning his eyes from one judge to another. He seemed to say, without the shadow of bravado: "Your pettiness does not touch me!"

As for my own state of mind... what am I to say? I had long foreseen the condemnation; now that the foresight had been borne out, I struggled to be fully conscious of it, to grasp its full import. It was to assure myself that I was indeed awake that I had looked my companions in the face. All at once there rose vividly before my mind the image of Elisa — not radiant and smiling, but as I had kissed her that last time, fallen back upon her chair, her eyes extinguished, insensible, as though the tribunal of the Seine had pronounced her sentence and not mine.

That image remained so fixed in my mind as to allow me to see or think of nothing else. As I traversed the underground passage that led us back to the Conciergerie I roused myself, and, turning to Orsini, who followed a few paces behind me, I said: "You who have means — remember them!" He understood, and murmured: "I promise you!" The door of the prison had barely closed behind me when I felt a strange sensation, as of being plunged into a quiet that was almost familiar. Saturno and the other guard already knew everything... they had lost their wager; they had made ready the champagne. But they wanted the chance of a return match. Should the sentence not be quashed by the Supreme Court or by a decree of pardon, they would pay another bottle; otherwise I should pay. Never did a wagerer so ardently desire to lose!

Thus, by a caprice of fate, chatting merrily and raising our glasses, I passed the night on which I had been condemned to the guillotine. I have wished many times over that I might know how my Judges fared in the end — Napoleon III, the street-sweeper Kim, and the others who had contributed to saving the social order and the usurped Empire... but my wish has remained always unsatisfied.
