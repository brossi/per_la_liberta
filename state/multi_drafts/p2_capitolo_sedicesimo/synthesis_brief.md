Natura soverchiamente nervosa e sensitiva, oscillava fatalmente dall'uno all'altro estremo, mettendo a nudo deficienze strane che io tenevo, sin da quei tempi, in conto di malattie... di quale precisa natura non sapevo e non so definire.

La congiura contro Napoleone III rivela in lui ingegno destro nel concepire un disegno a grandi linee. Felice Orsini, all'atto pratico, ha mostrato tanto scarso riguardo alle più elementari precauzioni, che io non mi perito ad affermare essere le doti essenziali del cospiratore cospicue, in lui, per la loro completa assenza.

Giuseppe Mazzini, censurato dall'Orsini sino alla denigrazione, sino a non riconoscergli altro genio fuorché quello della parola, certo non avrebbe commesso la dabbenaggine di lasciare l'Inghilterra, di ingolfarsi in un'impresa di tanto calibro, dimenticandosi dietro intatta, reperibile, in vista quasi, la corrispondenza che rivelava i preliminari tutti dell'attentato.

Questa imprudenza apparirà tanto più madornale, quando si sappia che egli stesso, scrivendomi, mi raccomandava, spesso, di distruggere le sue lettere, di non lasciarne sussistere la minima traccia.

Mi pesa il dirlo ma debbo dirlo ad onor del vero: il guaio della mente di Felice Orsini consisteva nella fantasia traboccante che gli impediva di rilevare l'esatto contorno delle cose, malgrado la sua pretesa d'essere uomo positivo.

Sobbarcatosi in una difficile impresa, egli costruiva, colla fantasia, gli avvenimenti che avrebbero dovuto, con lunga catena, condurre al trionfo e, soffermandosi su questo, se ne esaltava così, da ritenerlo come inevitabile, da pregustarlo prima che fosse.

Dirò anche: Nessuna anima umana potrebbe essere in tutte le molteplici facce del suo prisma, levigata e perfetta. A costituire la grandezza basta, talora, un solo lato granite. Felice Orsini ne aveva più d'uno.

Felice Orsini, dico, dà prova di un coraggio il cui tempra oscura qualsiasi eroismo chiassoso e battagliero.

V'è poi un tratto sfuggito, per quanto io sappia, a chi scrisse sinora di lui, che rivela un coraggio ancora più raffinato e sublime. L'impossessarsi di segreti che intuiva di importanza vitale, era questione a cui essa annetteva carriera, ricchezza, potenza, gloria tutto!

Né il suo cuore era muto ad affetti più modesti, non meno intensi... era padre; sulle rive del Tamigi, una donna sperava e trepidava per lui!... ma fra la tragica ora presente e quel tripudio, era sangue di generosi, eran lagrime d'orfani. Preferì incamminarsi pallido e silenzioso verso il patibolo. Grandezza austera, degna d'un Romano antico!

La difesa di Felice Orsini scaturiva spontanea dalle circostanze e Giulio Favre non era ingegno da guastare, per pusillanimi riguardi, lo splendido materiale che esse gli offrivano.

Egli vi pose la mano poderosa da artista che sa il magistero degli effetti; da uomo di cuore che fa suo il fremito di un Popolo glorioso ed infelice.

Suo colpo da maestro fu la lettera indirizzata da Felice Orsini a Napoleone III. Senza nulla rivelare, senza compromettere alcuno, essa gli permette di far balenare l'altezza del nostro scopo ai Giudici, ai giurati, al mondo civile; essa blandisce il leone che esercita gli artigli nelle nostre carni; ma tra le lusinghe prudentemente inserita la sorda minaccia. Una semplice frase onestamente espressa, basta a trapanare un cuore malvagio. Nell'ore silenziose, nell'ore del rimorso, essa rinfaccerà i mancati giuramenti, trassuderà l'ira perenne di una razza santamente vendicativa, che fu sua, che egli ha tradito!

Giulio Favre al disopra dei piccoli raggiri legali e delle cose meschine. Spaziando tra cose grandi, potè compiere una grande difesa. Non strappò un uomo al carnefice, ma fece di meglio... ne reintegrò il carattere. Egli tramandava ai posteri un animo, già per sé stesso tanto virile, trasfigurato quasi, da tratti di affascinante bellezza!

AND here we come to our chief. I shall call up old impressions; I shall express judgments that fifty years of meditation have not availed to alter.

We find ourselves before a character capable, in the highest degree, of generous impulses. Yet, by the mysterious reactions of his spirit, the frequent exaltations of feeling were always, or nearly always, followed by profound depressions. A nature excessively nervous and sensitive, he oscillated fatally from one extreme to the other, laying bare strange deficiencies which I held, even in those times, to be maladies... of what precise nature I did not know, and cannot now define.

The conspiracy against Napoleon III reveals in him a talent adroit in conceiving a design in broad outline. But the success of such enterprises, when analysed, proves to be constituted by the sum of trifles patiently worked to the chisel. Felice Orsini, in practical execution, showed so little regard for the most elementary precautions, that I do not hesitate to affirm that the essential gifts of the conspirator were conspicuous in him by their complete absence.

Giuseppe Mazzini, censured by Orsini to the point of denigration, to the point of acknowledging in him no other genius than that of the spoken word, would certainly never have committed the blunder of quitting England, of plunging headlong into an enterprise of such magnitude, leaving behind him intact, discoverable, almost in plain sight, the correspondence that revealed every preliminary of the attempt.

This imprudence will appear all the more egregious when it is known that he himself, writing to me, would often recommend me to destroy his letters, to leave not the slightest trace of them.

It grieves me to say it, but say it I must, in honour of truth: the trouble with Felice Orsini's mind lay in an overflowing imagination which prevented him from perceiving the exact contours of things, despite his pretension to being a positive man.

Having shouldered a difficult enterprise, he would construct, through the imagination, the events that ought, by a long chain, to have led to triumph, and, dwelling upon this triumph, he would become so exalted as to regard it as inevitable, to relish it before it was.

With an irony that seemed cutting, it was observed how Felice Orsini, on the eve of one of his desperate attempts in the Lunigiana, had carried his faith in success to the point of procuring for himself a general's uniform. It is repugnant, before the majesty of martyrdom, to be compelled to recall how, setting out for Paris to attempt the life of an Emperor, to initiate a revolution, he — who had such great need of obscurity and silence — purchased a saddle-horse and rode frequently, on horseback, through the streets of the capital, almost anticipating in advance the emotions of the epic moments...

This explains so many other things. The excessive development of one mental tendency is accomplished at the expense of other faculties, which are left all the more deficient the more that tendency manifests itself swollen and overbearing. Excessive faith in success — which derives, in substance, from an exaggerated estimate of one's own powers — is matched, too often, by scant regard for the strength and character of others. When catastrophe brutally cuts short the fantastic dreams, the proud and haughty spirit, overflowing with bitterness, will strive, in perfect good faith, to demonstrate that said catastrophe is principally owing to the worthlessness of one's companions in action.

What reason induced Felice Orsini to detach himself so wrathfully from the Master? Historians may seek it in this or that incident and, from their point of view, they are right to do so. But the primary cause is entirely psychological. Historical incidents are, in reality, nothing other than the expression taken, within the surrounding circumstances, of the deplorable defect of an otherwise noble and great soul.

In a different field and in different measure, but with identical sap, Felice Orsini was destined to treat also us, his companions in danger and misfortune. The bitter words spoken during the brief absence of Gomez, in the house in the Rue Monthabor, on the evening of the 14th of January; the no less stinging phrase launched, shortly after, in the direction of Pieri, as we made our way to the Opera, demonstrate that a dark reaction was already fermenting in his soul, even before the catastrophe.

An ill-starred incident, provoked by Gomez, served to lend some substance to the phantoms that were troubling him. Before resolving upon a complete confession, Gomez had attempted to persuade the examining magistrate that, irritated by his hesitation, Felice Orsini, at the approach of the imperial carriage, had brusquely snatched the bomb from his hands and that it was Orsini himself who had hurled it against the Emperor. Brought face to face with him, he retracted immediately, and the matter had no legal consequence whatsoever. But it certainly did not serve to recall Orsini to a just perception of reality.

He was unquestionably in such a state when he was surrounded and beguiled by the arts of the police. The insinuation contrived against the integrity of his companions made a wide breach in his soul, already so open to suspicion. And whereas, before that time, following his generous impulses, he had shown a disposition to take all responsibility for what had occurred upon himself, he now repented of it and dictated a detailed account in which, assigning to their proper author those acts already known to the Police, he suffered words full of acrimony to escape him with regard to us.

He then repented of having repented, and attempted, by means of an energetic counter-declaration, to take all responsibility upon himself once more; but at bottom the doubt remained, and the acrimony found, from time to time, the means of breaking forth impetuously — overcome then, always, and with equal impetuosity, by sentiments more consonant with his greatness.

A see-saw that disoriented us in a most distressing fashion!

Even today, I could not interpret with assured judgment certain of his denials, certain of his retractions. These might have been inspired equally by the fleeting determination to dissociate himself from us and to stand apart, or by the ardent desire to open a path of safety for us, to offer us the means of attenuating the rigour of the fate that was about to strike us.

Here, to avoid misunderstanding, I must insist upon one point: the insinuations of the Police were undoubtedly intended to irritate Orsini, so as to wrest from him a full confession. Orsini had his faults, but he was not a coward. He did indeed acknowledge all that the Police showed they knew with certainty; but he took great care not to disclose particulars unknown to them, unless those particulars concerned himself alone.

I will say further: no human soul could be, in all the multiple facets of its prism, polished and perfect. To constitute greatness, a single granite side sometimes suffices. Felice Orsini had more than one.

It is easy enough, for instance, to show oneself courageous in the light of day upon a field of battle. So easy, that I know of men transformed into heroes by a single ricocheted leaden ball, who were incapable of standing before a snarling mongrel without trembling. The disciplined multitude carries along even the most timid and feeble-spirited individual... to keep him on his feet he has, or supposes he has, fixed upon him, by the thousands, the eyes of his fellow-citizens.

But Felice Orsini who, alone in his room, spreads out the mercury fulminate before the fireplace and dries it over the flame; who, with a watch in one hand and a thermometer in the other, watches for the spark that might blow him to pieces, without casting forth from his work a single atom to earn him the applause of men; nothing, not even the memory of his generous intentions — Felice Orsini, I say, gives proof of a courage whose temper darkens all noisy and martial heroism.

There is, moreover, one trait — escaped, so far as I know, by all who have hitherto written of him — which reveals a courage yet more refined and sublime. The seizure of his correspondence had, among other things, revealed him as the centre from which radiated all the clandestine activity; to which, in turn, the dissembled hatreds, the subterranean forces working to undermine the Empire, must all converge.

To suppress Napoleon for the mere satisfaction of suppressing him would have been the work of madmen. The French political police had well understood that the blow must, through diligent preparation, impart the impulse to broad forces, ready to gather its fruit. To lay hold of secrets which it divined to be of vital importance was a matter to which it attached career, wealth, power, glory — everything!

Felice Orsini found himself, therefore, in a position not of one who must submit, but of one who might impose terms. A single thread of the vast web, secretly sacrificed, would have led him to safety, to opulence perhaps...

He would have seen again the white mountain ridges, the green fragrant plains, the incomparable sky of Italy; he would have conspired and struggled still in her behalf; he might have flattered himself with witnessing her third rebirth. Nor was his heart silent to more modest, no less intense affections... he was a father; on the banks of the Thames, a woman hoped and trembled for him!... but between that tragic present hour and that rejoicing lay the blood of the generous-hearted, the tears of orphans. He chose instead to set forth, pale and silent, towards the scaffold. An austere greatness, worthy of an ancient Roman!

The defence of Felice Orsini sprang spontaneously from the circumstances, and Jules Favre was not the intellect to spoil, through pusillanimous scruples, the splendid material they offered him.

He brought to it the powerful hand of the artist who knows the mastery of effects; of the man of heart who makes his own the trembling anguish of a glorious and unhappy People.

His masterstroke was the letter addressed by Felice Orsini to Napoleon III. Without revealing anything, without compromising anyone, it allowed him to flash before the judges, the jurors, and the civilised world the height of our purpose; it caresses the lion who exercises his claws in our flesh; but amid the blandishments there is prudently inserted the muffled threat. A single phrase honestly expressed is enough to pierce an evil heart. In the silent hours, in the hours of remorse, it will cast in his teeth the broken oaths, will exude the undying wrath of a race holy in its vengefulness, which was once his own, which he has betrayed!

Jules Favre stood above petty legal stratagems and mean concerns. Ranging among great things, he was able to accomplish a great defence. He did not snatch a man from the executioner, but he did something better... he restored a character. He was handing down to posterity a spirit, already so virile in itself, transfigured almost, by traits of captivating beauty!

So easy, that I know of men transformed into heroes by a simple ricocheted lead ball, who were incapable of standing, without trembling, before a snarling cur. The disciplined multitude carries along even the most timid and unwarlike individual... to keep him standing straight he has, or supposes he has, fixed upon him, by the thousands, the eyes of his fellow citizens.

But Felice Orsini who, alone in his room, spreads the fulminate of mercury before the fireplace and dries it by the fire; who with a watch in one hand, a thermometer in the other, watches for the spark that could blow him to bits, without casting forth, from his work, a single atom that might earn him the applause of men; nothing, not even the memory of his generous intentions... Felice Orsini, I say, gives proof of a courage whose temper obscures any clamorous and warlike heroism.

There is, moreover, a trait that has escaped, as far as I know, those who have written of him until now, which reveals a courage still more refined and sublime. The seizure of his correspondence had, among other things, revealed him as the center from which all the clandestine labor radiated; to which, in turn, were to lead the dissimulated hatreds, the subterranean forces, working to undermine the Empire.

To eliminate Napoleon for the sake of eliminating him would have been the work of madmen. The French political Police had well understood that the blow must, by diligent preparation, impart the impulse to large forces, ready to gather its fruit. To possess itself of secrets that it intuited to be of vital importance was a matter to which it attached career, riches, power, glory—everything!

Felice Orsini found himself, therefore, in a position not to submit, but to impose. A single thread of the vast plot, secretly sacrificed, would have led him to safety, to opulence perhaps...

He would have seen again the white mountain ridges, the fragrant green plains, the incomparable sky of Italia; he would have conspired and fought for her again; he could have flattered himself that he would witness her third renaissance. Nor was his heart mute to more modest affections, no less intense... he was a father; on the banks of the Thames, a woman hoped and trembled for him!... but between the tragic present hour and that jubilation, there was the blood of generous men, there were the tears of orphans. He preferred to set forth, pale and silent, towards the scaffold. An austere greatness, worthy of an ancient Roman!

Felice Orsini's defense sprang spontaneously from the circumstances, and Giulio Favre was not a mind to spoil, out of pusillanimous considerations, the splendid material that they offered him.

He laid upon it the mighty hand of an artist who knows the mastery of effects; of a man of heart who makes his own the tremor of a glorious and unhappy People.

His masterstroke was the letter addressed by Felice Orsini to Napoleon III. Without revealing anything, without compromising anyone, it allows him to flash the loftiness of our aim before the Judges, the jurors, the civilized world; it coaxes the lion that exercises its claws in our flesh; but amidst the flatteries was prudently inserted the muffled threat. A simple phrase honestly expressed is enough to pierce a wicked heart. In the silent hours, in the hours of remorse, it will reproach him for his broken oaths, it will exude the perennial wrath of a sacredly vengeful race, which was his, which he has betrayed!

Giulio Favre stood above the petty legal stratagems and paltry things. Ranging among great things, he was able to accomplish a great defense. He did not snatch a man from the executioner, but he did better... he restored his character. He handed down to posterity a soul, already in itself so virile, transfigured, almost, by traits of fascinating beauty

## Chapter Fifteen

WHEN the door of the cell closed behind me, it was as though the turbid passions that had agitated me remained on the other side of the threshold. Saturno and the other questioned me with the expression of their faces. "It is all over! Every aspiration I had towards liberty is cut short from this day. One must prepare oneself to die." So I said; but within, I felt that the pride of having sacrificed myself for my Country would sustain me.

No sooner had I finished relating what had passed than they strove, as if by tacit agreement, to drag me back from my convictions. They put forward, among other things, the sympathies of the population and the favourable dispositions which, as it was asserted, animated the imperial Court. To this I replied with a shake of my head and a smile. A strange dispute arose from this, and we ended by making a wager. If the Tribunal pronounced a sentence of death, they would undertake to pay me a bottle of champagne, and of the finest. If not, I should pay it. We sealed the compact by exchanging a vigorous handshake...

Mathieu de la Drôme paid me his first visit that same day. He was a man of dignified appearance, tall, already more than greying, placid in his manner and speech. For several days past he had spontaneously offered to defend me; but we had not been permitted to meet until the Police were well assured of having their claws fast about my throat. He drew near, so to speak, to my soul with the delicacy of a physician who knows he must touch limbs that are sensitive and in pain. And I related to him at once, without reserve (the guards had withdrawn) the whole of my past.

When I had finished, he lowered his eyes and withdrew into his reflections for some seconds. Then, with a paternal affability: "The damaging admissions might be retracted. It is within your right. But to what extent it would serve you, I cannot say. You are accustomed to facing danger, and I prefer to speak plainly. The direct evidence gathered by the Police makes an acquittal beyond your reach. Were it a matter of considering only yourself and your bearing before your countrymen, I should say to you: Go forth to meet death majestically, whole and unbroken, blessing your Country, cursing her oppressors. The People will accord you, in their great heart, the place that is your due. But the wise man—"

Here he drew out at length, setting before me what, in his view, the wise man would have done in my situation. To hurl oneself headlong towards the scaffold was not to serve one's Country, nor would my sacrifice improve the position of my fellow conspirators. And how would my aged parents, who had already suffered so greatly, endure a blow so desperately past all remedy? A young wife trembled for me, and I had made her a mother. Why not attempt every honourable means of living, when, by cheating the executioner, I should spare needless grief to those unhappy souls?

I ended by asking him for an explicit explanation of what he required of me. "A free hand," he answered, "in attempting the means which might save you, and a formal promise to moderate your impulses, whatever the part I might judge it expedient for you to assume."

I consented. And this explains why, in the accounts of the trial, I emerge colourless, almost without a will of my own, easily swayed, driven to the terrible act by the iron mind of the leaders and by a morbid anxiety not to appear pusillanimous, not to be confounded with Rossi of Mantua, not to be taken for a spy.

I have never harboured towards Mathieu de la Drôme anything other than sentiments of filial gratitude. Unfortunately, his noble efforts to "cheat the executioner" served afterwards as a convenient instrument of defamation for the ill-disposed, blinded alike by personal rancour and by partisan rancour — not the least of the motives that, for fifty years, shut me in a disdainful silence!

My fellow conspirators were caught in the selfsame vice, sunk to the throat in the selfsame quicksand.

The evidence of which the Authority had possessed itself was, of itself, sufficient to secure a conviction in perfect accordance with the Law. To reinforce it with the admissions of the accused would give bolder relief, greater theatrical effect, to the cunning of the Police.

The better to attain its end, it had begun its subtle work with me, who was the youngest; then came the turn of Antonio Gomez, who was my elder by some years. To this man the Examining Magistrate placed in triumph before his eyes a letter of recommendation issued to him by Pieri, addressed to Orsini. Worse still, a letter written in his own hand to Orsini himself. In it he laboured to dispel certain suspicions that had insinuated themselves into the mistrustful mind of the chief, and, pressing for confirmation of the charge promised to him, he declared his desire to take part in the conspiracy, inspired solely by the love he had always borne and still bore to their common Country.

Italians, reborn as a Nation, have the moral obligation to set such solicitude and the sentiments that inspired it to Gomez's credit — but, in the face of the French Police, could there be a more compromising document?

As though this were not enough, Gomez found himself required to account for his every movement, step by step, from the moment when, on the 6th of January, he set out with Pieri from Birmingham, halting first in London, at the house of Miss Cheney, then at Lille, awaiting Pieri, who was making his dash to Brussels; and so on until the moment when he set foot for the first time in Orsini's furnished lodgings in the rue Monthabor, No. 10.

And here, as a finishing stroke, came the artful insinuation: "his fellow conspirators had confessed their own part and his. What purpose was there in persisting in denial?"

After various equivocations and various manoeuvres too desperate to savour of sound tactics, hearing it said openly that I, di Rudio, in admitting to having thrown the second bomb, had revealed everything else, he ended by confessing to having thrown the first. But even in this extremity, he was not forsaken by an instinct very widespread among the people of southern Italy: that which leads a man to draw from his pocket the packet of feigned simplicity and to smear it over his face. He, who had been a soldier nonetheless, attempted to give the impression of having thrown his projectile as a faithful servant, without forming any clear notion of its deadly effects. It was upon this supposed simplicity that his defence was constructed...

More arduous proved the struggle, amid similar straits, for Giuseppe Pieri. The police had placed before his eyes several revealing letters; had recounted to him, with indisputable precision, step by step, his movements in the period preceding the attack; his relations with Orsini, with Gomez, with Simon Bernard, with me; his complicity in importing the empty projectiles into France, in the purchase of certain revolvers found near the scene of the attack! Elisa Cheney identifying him and Hartmann denouncing him had, by their personal testimonies, corroborated the written evidence. The arrest effected within minutes and paces of the attack, the loaded bomb in the pocket of his overcoat, the boasts that had escaped him after his arrest — could there be a more enveloping net, a more complete case?

He added nothing to it, because nothing remained for him to add. He attempted instead to adorn the facts with fantastic fabrications and to draw from them deductions tending to distort their significance.

"He had come to Paris solely to settle some family business, and intended to proceed to Italy, where a revolution was imminent. He had discussed bombs with Orsini, but solely with a view to putting them to use at home, on the barricades. And since he had expressed some doubt as to their efficacy, Orsini had handed one to him; but with the understanding that they would cause it to explode, by way of experiment, at the Barrière des Martyrs. Only Pieri had gone to the rendezvous; for which reason the bomb had remained in his pocket and had been found upon him there. If on the evening of the attack he had gone to the rue Le Pelletier, it was simply because he had been drawn thither by the extraordinary illumination."

A shield assembled with sufficient cleverness; but wasted labour! — a few of his written phrases and a little analysis sufficed to shatter it. He had the merit of perceiving this in time. Thereupon, pleading as a pretext "the inquisitorial manner of the Examining Magistrate," he declared that he would answer no further questions; that he would refute the charges solely during the trial proper, before those whom he considered his natural judges.

He conjured away, by this means, the danger of compromising himself yet further. But was the worst truly past?

Nugent de Saint Laurent, who afterwards fully approved of Pieri's tactics, attempted to lend them greater weight by laying great emphasis on the animosity existing between Pieri himself and Orsini; as though to infer that a conspiracy, and the fraternal relations it entails, were impossible between two men who cordially hated one another. Unfortunately, the reason for the disagreements was never made clear; while it became sufficiently plain that they were of too recent a date to have had any power of dissolution upon the conspiracy.
