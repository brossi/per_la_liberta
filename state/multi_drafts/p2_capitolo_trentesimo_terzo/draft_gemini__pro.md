THE sufferings of the *ergastolo* were by now erased by my long immersion in the light of freedom and in the affections of my family, which had almost reconciled me with inertia, when the announcement of the crime of Aspromonte came to shake me.

The conferences and rallies organized to protest against the Temporal Power of the Popes became a daily event in Londra, and I, so to speak, plunged into them.

An incident occurred that I recall with satisfaction. The Irish element, which clouds its fine qualities by subordinating them too easily to the watchword of the tonsured camarilla, took umbrage at this newest agitation. One day, as an imposing international rally was taking place at the Park, they broke the cordons of the Police that protected us and furiously assailed us. The demonstrators were as if swept away and the flags struck down. Only two remained upright and waving: that of the French republican delegation, which was at my side, and the one that I clasped... upon it blazed, in scarlet letters, the motto: "O Roma o Morte."

This modest triumph was due to the short bladed weapon, which had escaped the eye of the French Police. On that occasion too it remained virgin of blood... its menacing glitter had been enough!

Resolved to rededicate my activity to greater things, I set about finding a way to return to my homeland. Giuseppe Mazzini, though he aided the movements that tended toward the liberation of Roma when they seemed inevitable, preferred, for practical reasons, to first bring about the liberation of the Veneto. And it was toward this that his efforts were bent after Aspromonte. I hoped, by returning home, to enter fighting into the lands to which so many intimate memories bound me; to crown my modest work under the very eyes of the two good creatures from whom I drew, with my blood, my entire soul.

For a moment, it seemed that the Italian Government was destined to realize my desires. In English waters was a warship of ours; perhaps the "Vittorio Emanuele". It was to set a course for Genova, and a ministerial provision granted free passage to any refugees who wished to be repatriated. At the Consulate there was a crowd of individuals of every stamp, among whom I made out some who, notoriously, had both their own honour and the Fatherland in their pocket. Consul Corti, an official who later had a fine career and with whom I was to find myself face to face in other lands and in circumstances so changed for me, received me with courteous manners, but concluded by enjoining me to return the following morning... a suspicious delay! Nevertheless, at the appointed hour I appeared before him. At his side was a man who, seeing me enter, whispered a few words in his ear. The consular mask darkened: "Why did you state a false name yesterday?"

— Not false, but simply incomplete. I said my name is Carlo Nosadano; my name is Carlo Camillo di Rudio of the Counts of Nosadano.

— I am sorry, but for you there is no place on the royal ship.

— I understand! You do not wish to mix me with the common delinquents whom you have nonetheless welcomed with open arms.

— Take it as you please. I must, however, inform you that where the King's flag flies, the Revolution and the wind no longer blow propitiously for certain methods and for certain people. I believe, in all conscience, that if you were to be surprised within the borders of the Kingdom, you would be imprisoned and handed over to the French authorities, with whom, unless I am mistaken, you have accounts to settle...

Consul Corti was, on this point, perfectly right. The King of Italy and Napoleon III were joined, and he who, under the Emperor's pressure, did not hesitate to have the man to whom he owed a crown shot by firing squad, could not remain in doubt between that pressure and the unarmed rights of a simple, obscure citizen.

They did not want me to have a Fatherland! For a few days I remained as if stunned by it; then I thought: "Despots are all leagued together even without need of concrete pacts, just as all the peoples of the world are brothers. I will find bullies to fight everywhere, good causes to defend."

From this I concluded that what I had attempted for the benefit of my country, with all that depended on my strength, did not exonerate me from my obligations toward the rest of mankind. I would, if nothing else, have demonstrated that they were wrong to spurn the cooperation of a patriot who, if not among the best, was not among the lowliest either.

Heroic Polonia, overrun time and again by Cossack hordes, was then struggling in the spasms of its death throes. I asked to be welcomed among the ranks of its defenders... but this time too, the pacts were to be stronger than my resolutions. Right in those days, unexpectedly, a letter from Giuseppe Mazzini invited me to a meeting.

Carried along by the whirlwind of events, he had spent the last years now in Italia, now in Lugano, now in Londra, but gravely ill and accessible only to his most intimate friends. And the profound emotions and sufferings had so aggravated the signs of old age on that noble face that, upon seeing him, I could not manage to conceal a gesture of sorrowful surprise.

He noticed it and, shaking my hand, said: "Everything passes, everything breaks. It grieves me that our ideals have been realized only in part, and even that part disfigured by a fatal materialism that drives men to renounce the most vital principles for the brief advantage of the moment. Oh how many, without being vulgar, have acted vulgarly!"

He lowered his large eyes, still beautiful, still serene in his face that had become almost earthen, and remained for a few seconds absorbed in his thoughts. Then he roused himself and added: "Let the consciousness of having spent our lives in action suffice for us, and the certainty of leaving an Italia better than the one we found."

I wanted to say to him: "To leave it when the hour of rest arrives... can be sad. But to be cast out from it like a leper, after having loved it so much and when, young and full of good will, you feel you have every right to witness its resurrection, to fight to secure new triumphs for it... this is a sorrow that knows no comfort!"

He forestalled me by asking for detailed information about Felice Orsini's last moments. He insisted on knowing if, in the imminence of his martyrdom, he had thought of his old friend, if he had recognized that their souls were made not to tear each other apart, but to understand and to love each other.

Having been reassured, he exclaimed: "How I regretted not having known in time what you were plotting!"

Then I reminded him of our last encounter in Cheapside. It was precisely to inform him of our conspiracy that I had asked him, but in vain, for a meeting. He seemed vexed by a disquieting thought and, after a brief silence: "I had not forgotten you, and if I had suspected even remotely... But in the welter of commitments... What would you have? There is a fatality in things!"

With an abrupt transition, he launched, with wings unfurled, into a completely different subject. He had been informed of my intention to fight for the independence of Polonia. It was to dissuade me from it that he had written to me.

And concerning that unhappy People, he expressed the following concepts: "The Polish cause is as holy as any other; but it is a lost cause. Russia has managed to divide the interests of the working multitude from those of the landowners... a tactic fatal to the Revolution. Nothing is more ruinous to Liberty than a tyranny that hypocritically waves the banner of liberal reforms. Between the countrymen who urged it to continue an uncertain, long, bloody struggle, and the foreigner who offered it the fields fertilized by its own sweat, the agricultural class did not hesitate at all. The intellectual Polonia finds itself completely isolated, and the hour of catastrophe is imminent. Sad is the Fatherland that fattens the minority to the detriment of the sons who, with incessant toil, produce its wealth. The appeal to national solidarity, launched in the moment of danger, sounds to the disinherited like a mockery, and rapacious egoism ends by reaping, in a short span of time, the storm accumulated over centuries. And perhaps the lesson will be of use! Russia has undertaken a dangerous game for its internal tranquility; but in the tenacious struggle waged by the various Powers to overwhelm one another, that one could triumph which, by seriously setting its hand, in its own house, to wise social reforms, finds itself in a position to guarantee their implementation, in case of war, and without internal dangers, in the house of another. The multitudes would welcome it as a liberator and its own armies would be, virtually, quintupled."

He fell silent and after a brief recollection: "Be that as it may, why do we wish to bring to a desperate cause a contribution of energy and blood destined to remain sterile?"

I shrugged my shoulders, and he then, with fervent words, alluded to the epic struggle that a part of the Stati Uniti del Nord America was sustaining to maintain the integrity of the Union and for the abolition of slavery. Despite this detestable anachronism, Liberty has deep roots over there. Great is the ferment of human activity there, and the vast environment favors great ideas and colossal enterprises. Once the sad present moment is overcome, once the shame that has driven them one against the other is erased, the Stati Uniti will proceed without impediment and will achieve, in short order, such progress as will astonish the world. You who will live, you shall see. I advise you to sail toward the West. We of the old party of action must persevere in the old school: to fight even at the cost of adding a new triumph to the enemy; but supported by the certainty that every drop of shed blood hastens the advent of the ideal, and it matters little if others, not we, will gather the fruit of our sacrifice. I maintain that every sword-stroke is an active coefficient in the titanic war sustained by the American Union, and even if you were to leave your bones there, you would not have acted in vain."

He looked at me, as in other times, fixedly in the eyes and exclaimed: "It is settled, you are leaving for the Stati Uniti!"

Without another word, he sat down at his desk and set about penning the letters that were to put me in a position to distinguish myself honourably in the task I had tacitly assumed. His pen glided rapidly, scratching on the paper, while I, standing in the recess of a window, observed him lovingly and had thoughts, old and new, that time has been able to expand, but that were not to be contradicted:

"What nobility and what force of intellect in that slender body that with a slight effort of my arm I could have overpowered!"

"Why on earth do the Italians turn their backs on him, disown him with such haste?"

"A dreamer incapable of practical views," yet he alone, with almost mathematical precision, knew how to intuit events whose possibility the great minds of the age did not even suspect. He alone knew how to photograph, as if they already were, the hidden effects of the vices and virtues of his generation.

Humble, not rich, without arms, he escaped for half a century the combined Police forces of all Europe; he tore jealously guarded secrets from every Chancellery; he undermined the pedestal of Metternich, the cynical colossus, ultra-positive, who weighed upon the Peoples worse than a nightmare. He devised and applied Laws that, renewed, would even today be the boast of any civilised Nation; he raised armies sacred to Liberty where only slaves had swarmed; he added, by his actions, to the modern history of Roma pages worthy of ancient Roma; he combined war plans that the swaggering Generals of the King disdainfully rejected only to run, head down, to meet defeat; but which Bismarck and Moltke sanctioned. He treated on equal terms with Kings and robbed every despot of his self-assurance. He cast with full hands upon the multitude, sick with apathy, a ferment that released flashes of light; he set the fated course of peoples upon a broader path. Not one year of his youth, his manhood, his old age that is not a whole poem of fruitful action and of daring and of sacrifice. How many practical results, for a man incapable of practical views!

In the writings of Giuseppe Mazzini—who dares deny it?—we collide with excessive frequency with mystical phrases, with ascetic invocations to the vacuous phantoms created by human hallucination and human pride. And that strong and gentle soul does not perceive that the very facts upon which he stumbles are the irrefutable negation of those phantoms. For if a Mind ruled all things and aimed at the triumph of the good and the just, it would have simplified its work by not creating Evil, and it would not have permitted the delinquents who live in Royal Palaces to annihilate the untainted consciences aspiring precisely to the good and the just. If nothing else, with a very simple act of His omnipotence, He would have broken the noose around the necks of the victims.

And what good does it do to bow to a Most High who is always with the strongest? Would it not be more logical to work at eliminating, by acting manfully, the causes of our weakness in order to compel him, with the power thus acquired, to declare himself for us?

In the belief in God every privilege, every rapine, every political and social injustice becomes entrenched and is nourished. Giuseppe Mazzini, by a singular aberration, consecrated so large a part of his energy to reinforcing the error that served as a picklock for his most bitter enemies.

But setting this weakness aside, what integrity in the pages of that great man, what passion and what profundity of conceptions and what an ocean of truth!

Certainly, human thought is not crystallized in a few formulas and in a few volumes, and the human race tends toward ideals that are ever more evolved. But I affirm that the writings of Giuseppe Mazzini will have the virtue of educating to high sentiments even when our political institutions and our social order have reached all the perfection of which they are susceptible. But if the rising generation does not nourish its soul with similar food, let it not say that it has progressed so far as to be able to disdain it with impunity; let it rather say that the school of sacrifice is repugnant to invasive careerism; let it say that from the Master's pages there transpires, on this subject, a reproach that, for being tacit, is no less irksome to it.

Then he rose and came toward me, smiling a smile that revealed all the sadness of a farewell. He handed me several letters addressed to American political figures, doling out a few instructions. In his right hand he retained a small sheet of very thin paper, of the kind he used to use for correspondence in the time of his classic conspiracies: "You will keep this in your wallet. It will be a good viaticum for you." And he wished to read it. It said:

"I take the liberty of recommending to all those American citizens who sympathize with me, Carlo Rudio, valiant, active, resolute, and disposed to unite with the defenders of the cause of Emancipation. He deserves to be helped. Giuseppe Mazzini."

That spontaneous certificate of esteem from the most venerable figure of our risorgimento, I still preserve it, and if I have ever acted and suffered for my Country, from it I received that sole, but inestimable, compensation, and it is enough for me!

I turned my last gaze to the Master from the foot of the short staircase. He was leaning with both hands on the railing. He too was looking at me intensely. I understood his thought and I intuited that he understood mine: "Everything passes, everything breaks!..."

On the 8th of February 1864, when in Londra preparations were already being contemplated for the triumphal welcome of Giuseppe Garibaldi, I, having parted once again from my Elisa and my child, embarked at Liverpool on the steamer "Virginia," bound for Nuova York. I left behind me peoples torn by grave turbulence and by ferocious wars... before me, beyond the vast Ocean, other Peoples were tearing each other apart, no less ferociously, in a titanic fratricidal war. In antiquity and in modern times, in the old and in the new Continent, Liberty is reached by paths slick with blood.

Since my arrival in Nuova York, half a century dense with events has now passed... but even in the moments of greatest fortune, even in the hours of triumph, a secret sorrow has persisted in the substratum of my soul... I have never seen my Fatherland again!
