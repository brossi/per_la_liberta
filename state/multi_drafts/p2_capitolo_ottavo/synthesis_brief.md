Quella sera stessa, ero tranquillamente riparato all'Hotel France et Champagne, nella camera del Pieri.

Giuseppe Pieri non ebbe sinora biografi. Gli scrittori che incidentalmente ne parlarono, basandosi sui documenti processuali, ne ingigantirono i difetti così da eclissare le belle e non comuni virtù. Io stesso, per un momento, lo ritenni la causa principale, semi-volontaria della nostra rovina e non sempre lo trattai colla serena imparzialità a cui aveva pure diritto. Ora non ne farò l'apoteosi; ma dico che, contrariamente alla maggioranza degli uomini civili, i quali hanno levigata la superficie e bacato l'interno, egli di volgaruccio non aveva se non che la superficie.

Nei brevi giorni che passammo in compagnia, seguendo la sua natura aperta, fiduciosa, impulsiva, mi rivelò ogni suo pensiero, ogni segreto della sua vita intima e pareva felice d'aver trovato un terreno propizio a rovesciare il torrente della sua loquela, più che toscana, prettamente lucchese. Ad onta di quella strana, monotona sua voce, il suo dire era tutt'altro che uggioso; spesso anzi riesciva interessantissimo. Sapeva farsi perdonare il fraseggiare poco castigato, frammischiandovi fiori di filosofia pratica che rivelavano l'ingegno bizzarro e la vita tumultuosamente vissuta.

Le sue idee filosofiche liberamente espresse e, troppo di frequente con soverchia enfasi, non contribuivano a moltiplicarli d'attorno gli amici. Anche oggigiorno, malgrado il fatto che certe dottrine si siano tanto democratizzate, il suo dire sarebbe generalmente accolto col viso dell'armi. Eppure quella sua fenomenale sincerità da libro aperto e quel suo agire in piena armonia coi propri convincimenti, dovrebbero bastare a sollevarlo al di sopra di una legione di minuscoli, zavorra della Storia, del cui nome si sono pur riempite le carte, la cui nullità ebbe onore di piedestallo.

Voglio addurre un saggio di quel suo modo d'essere. La moglie del Pieri dimorava a Parigi; egli se n'era diviso. Ma la completa cessazione d'ogni rapporto coniugale, non gli impediva di nutrire per essa stima profonda e di farle visita, come ad amica provata e fedele. A me che, sposo da poco e imbevuto, in proposito, di tutt'altri ideali, lo guardavo, in certa occasione che me ne parlava, con occhi meravigliati, egli battè la mano sulla spalla e disse a un dipresso: "Giovanotto, l'amore che cerca la felicità nella soddisfazione dei sensi a danno della felicità altrui, non è amore, è egoismo da selvaggi. Tu lavori perchè un Popolo possa diventare arbitro dei propri destini e sei degno di lode. Ma vorresti, dio birbettone! che la più bella, la più nobile metà di quel Popolo non sia arbitra neppure del proprio cuore e della propria persona! La tirannia del maschio degrada la donna che se ne vendica ripagandoli di baci mentiti e di tradimento; essa conduce a dolori tanto più terribili quanto meglio dissimulati; a dolori che dissolvono l'integrità dell'animo e si ripercuotono sull'animo dei figli; alle tragedie domestiche che si risolvono col colpo di pistola. La mia teoria? Combina la libertà colla dignità umana e, all'amore che tramonta, sostituisce la riconoscenza basata sui dolci ricordi del passato, l'amicizia basata sulla reciproca stima."

Nelle cose religiose il Pieri mi rivelò una mente scevra d'ogni pregiudizio, non per convinzioni sovrapposte dalla lettura; ma per naturale tendenza. Si era accomodato nel sereno, credo, per necessità, come un corpo leggero staccato dal fondo tenebroso di una palude deve necessariamente elevarsi alla luminosa superficie. Questo suo ateismo lo mise talora in urto coll'Orsini, il quale dall'educazione avuta nell'infanzia aveva, come il Mazzini, ritratto qualcosa di nebuloso che persistette, in entrambi, quanto fu lunga la loro vita.

I giorni brevi che precedettero l'attentato, tanto io quanto il Pieri, procurammo di vivere come se nulla di grave fosse nell'aria. Si pranzava, di solito, in una trattoria della galleria Montmartre e il pasto frugale era preceduto e seguito da passeggiate lunghe e senza meta. Facevamo qualche visita all'Orsini; ma egli si mostrava pensieroso, taciturno e le eran visite brevi. Il pomeriggio del Sabato (12 Gennaio) doveva formare eccezione. Noi trovammo l'Orsini ed il Gomez vestiti di tutto punto, col soprabito addosso ed il cappello in testa. Non appena ci vide, l'Orsini si lasciò sfuggire un'esclamazione che parve si portasse via l'impazienza accumulata da una lunga attesa. Ci pregò d'accompagnarlo. Uscimmo e, strada facendo, disse che voleva condurci sulla piazzetta dell'Opera per studiare, sul posto, un suo piano d'azione. Ci incamminammo lentamente lungo la via Le Pelletier e, giunti di fronte al teatro, ci fermammo, quasi per ammirarne l'architettura. Vi fu un lungo silenzio. Felice Orsini lo ruppe con queste parole: "Il mio piano si attaglia perfettamente alla località." E, senza fare alcun cenno colle mani, che egli tenne costantemente sprofondate nelle tasche del soprabito: "Tu, Gomez, ti apposterai di là della via, a destra, più vicino al peristilio che ti riuscirà possibile. Pieri si metterà precisamente al lato opposto. Io ed il Moretto ci pianteremo qui di fronte, più o meno dove ci troviamo adesso. So che l'Imperatore si recherà, tra breve, ad una rappresentazione della Ristori. La carrozza imperiale deve procedere, indisturbata, sino al teatro. Non appena Napoleone smonta, Gomez lancerà la sua bomba. Se ha effetto, ognuno sguscia per la sua strada, come meglio può e ci troviamo poi tutti sulla piazza della Concorde, ove darò le istruzioni sul da farsi. Se non ha effetto, delle due l'una: o l'Imperatore tenta di salvarsi riparando in teatro e allora Pieri gli lancia ai piedi la seconda bomba e lo finisce; oppure egli si ritrae nella carrozza e questa tira via al galoppo, e allora il Pieri la colpisce alle spalle, io e il di Rudio di fianco. Ciò dovrebbe bastare a frantumare un fortino. Ma dato anche che l'Imperatore possa procedere, sia a destra, sia a sinistra, lungo la via Le Pelletier, prima che arrivi al Boulevard Montmartre o alla via Rossini, troverà i patrioti che sapranno inviargli il saluto che si merita. Comunque sia, compiuto l'attentato, tutti, per vie diverse, sulla piazza della Concorde!"

Il giorno seguente, Domenica, il Pieri mi propose un'escursione a Passy. L'orizzonte vasto della campagna parve aumentasse nel patriota toscano il bisogno di espandersi e, alla rigidezza eccezionale della giornata, egli oppose un eccezionale calore di frasi, di immagini, di rievocazioni. In quella sequela interminabile di parole, l'uomo dai fieri propositi anelante a libertà, il filosofo pratico ora si sovrapponeva all'uomo sensuale, sereno nei suoi facili amori, ora se ne mostrava sottoposto e come eclissato. Mai mi ero trovato dinanzi ad amalgama tanto complesso di sodezza e di vanità.

Di tutte le sue rivelazioni, quella mi impresse maggiormente che, per sé stessa, era forse la più insignificante. Pieri aveva conosciuto l'Orsini a Birmingham ed era proprio da lì che aveva preso le mosse per venire a Parigi. Nel suo viaggio si era fermato a Bruxelles per salutare una sua amante. Sotto l'impulso di emozioni che fanno chicchessia propenso al facile abbandono, egli era diventato, ancora più del solito, spensieratamente espansivo. All'amante che lo pregava di fermarsi più a lungo, aveva risposto che la sua immediata partenza per Parigi era irrevocabile.

— Come! vai in Francia? E non temi di cascare in qualche brutta rete, tu che ne fosti espulso?

E il Pieri, di rimando, affermava che, questa volta, vi andava colla speranza di ripagare, a misura di carbone, coloro che lo avevano perseguitato.

La sera io battevo all'uscio di Felice Orsini. Gli bastò uno sguardo per comprendere che egli era più pensieroso che io non fossi. Ciò malgrado, insistetti per un colloquio immediato. Mi guardò con occhi scrutatori, ma nulla disse. Ed io, saltando di piè pari nell'argomento, gli rammentai la sua prima, saggia determinazione di mantenere i congiurati affatto estranei l'uno all'altro. Tale determinazione, soggiunsi, era stata irrisa e quasi soffiata via dalle circostanze... la nostra sicurezza n'era diventata precaria ed effimera. Non parlai, in modo particolare, dell'imprudenza del Pieri, ma insistetti sulla probabilità che qualche imprudenza fosse stata commessa. Bisognava tener calcolo delle eventualità impreviste, ma indubbiamente molteplici, che potevano mettere a repentaglio, non solo la nostra esistenza, ma il destino di due Popoli e ne conclusi coll'affermare che, a mio parere, si aveva il torto di tentare l'effettuazione dell'impresa con un numero superfluo di addetti. Due uomini risoluti, uno solo poteva bastare.

Gli rammentai pure che il proposito di colpire nel cuore la reazione francese, sopprimendo Napoleone, non era nuovo in me... egli ben lo sapeva che aveva piena contezza della lettera da me scritta sin dal 1854, a Giuseppe Mazzini. Perchè non lasciarmi compiere da solo un'impresa, sulla quale vantavo un certo diritto di precedenza? Se riuscivo, la nostra causa non avrebbe avuto nulla da perdere; se fallivo, altre forze ignote, intatte, rianimate dal desiderio della riscossa, avrebbero rinnovata la prova. La certezza del successo era nella persistenza.

— L'imminenza dell'azione, rispose l'Orsini, non solo mi persuade che i tuoi timori siano infondati; ma rende anche impossibile il pentimento. Come vuoi, del resto, che ci sfugga un nemico destinato a dare nell'imboscata al trotto e quasi corra a trionfo? Che si aspetta! Che l'Imperatore, determinando la sera, ci dia, colle proprie mani, il segnale di colpirlo. Ecco tutto!

Insistetti e gli feci osservare che il mio piano si innestava mirabilmente sul suo, diminuendo, per la sua stessa semplicità, i rischi. Se egli mi procurava (e non doveva riuscirgli difficile) un'uniforme da "sargent de ville", io mi sarei confuso, travestito, tra le guardie delegate dalla città a proteggere l'Imperatore. Ogni sezione ne forniva un contingente e quelle di una parte della città non conoscevano personalmente quelle venute dalle altre... Quando l'Imperatore vuol smontare dalla sua carrozza, una di queste guardie, per una consuetudine diventata diritto, si affretta ad aprirne lo sportello. Vediamo ora: Luigi Bonaparte procede, tra i suoi sbirri, sino al peristilio del teatro. Io mi lancio allo sportello. Sono un graduato. Ma scende! È questione di un attimo. Alla Repubblica Romana, ai mitragliati del 2 Dicembre. La storia delle rivendicazioni popolari ripiglia, da quel momento, il proprio corso!

Felice Orsini passeggiò la camera pel lungo e pel largo. Era agitato e sulla sua faccia mobilissima, i pensieri si incalzavano, come lampi. Finì per piantarmisi dinanzi... prese e gettò nuovamente sul tavolo parecchie carte, poi proruppe: "È troppo tardi, è troppo tardi! E, d'altra parte, pari la gloria, pari il rischio. Non parliamone più!"

Con quel rifiuto, egli segnava irrevocabilmente il proprio destino!

That same evening, I had quietly taken shelter at the Hotel France et Champagne, in Pieri's room.

Giuseppe Pieri has had no biographers until now. The writers who touched upon him in passing, drawing upon the documents of the trial, exaggerated his faults so as to eclipse his fine and uncommon virtues. I myself, for a time, held him to be the principal, half-voluntary cause of our ruin, and did not always treat him with that serene impartiality to which he too had a right. I shall not here compose his apotheosis; but I say that, contrary to the majority of civilised men, who have polished the surface while rotting within, he had nothing of the vulgar about him save the surface alone.

In the brief days we spent together, following his open, trusting, impulsive nature, he revealed to me his every thought, every secret of his intimate life, and seemed happy to have found a favourable ground upon which to pour forth the torrent of his speech — more than Tuscan, purely Lucchese. For all that strange, monotonous voice of his, his talk was anything but tedious; often, indeed, it proved most interesting. He knew how to make himself forgiven for his somewhat uncouth phrasing by interspersing it with flowers of practical philosophy that betrayed an eccentric intelligence and a life tumultuously lived.

His philosophical ideas, freely expressed and all too frequently with excessive emphasis, did not help to multiply his friends about him. Even today, despite the fact that certain doctrines have become so democratised, his manner of speaking would generally be met with a cold countenance. Yet that phenomenal sincerity of his — open as a book — and his habit of acting in complete harmony with his own convictions ought to be enough to raise him above a legion of petty creatures, the ballast of History, whose names have nonetheless filled so many pages, whose nullity was honoured with a pedestal.

I wish to offer a specimen of that manner of his. Pieri's wife lived in Paris; he had separated from her. But the complete cessation of all conjugal relations did not prevent him from holding her in deep esteem and from calling upon her as upon a tried and faithful friend. Once, when he spoke to me of this, I — newly married and imbued with altogether different ideals on the matter — looked at him with wondering eyes; whereupon he clapped his hand upon my shoulder and said, roughly to this effect: "Young man, the love that seeks happiness in the gratification of the senses at the cost of another's happiness is not love — it is a savage's egoism. You labour that a People may become master of its own destiny, and you are worthy of praise. But you would have it, you little rogue of a god, that the fairest, the noblest half of that People should not even be mistress of its own heart and its own person! The tyranny of the male degrades the woman, who takes her revenge by repaying him with feigned kisses and betrayal; it leads to sorrows all the more terrible for being the better concealed; to sorrows that dissolve the integrity of the soul and reverberate upon the souls of children; to those domestic tragedies that end with the pistol shot. My theory? It reconciles liberty with human dignity, and in the place of love that is waning, substitutes gratitude founded upon the sweet memories of the past, friendship founded upon mutual esteem."

In religious matters, Pieri revealed to me a mind free from every prejudice — not through convictions imposed by reading, but by natural bent. He had settled into serenity, I believe, out of necessity, as a light body detached from the murky depths of a swamp must of necessity rise to the luminous surface. This atheism of his brought him at times into conflict with Orsini, who, from the upbringing received in childhood, had — like Mazzini — retained something nebulous that persisted in both men throughout the whole of their lives.

During the brief days that preceded the attempt, both Pieri and I endeavoured to live as though nothing of gravity hung in the air. We generally dined at a *trattoria* in the Galerie Montmartre, and the frugal meal was preceded and followed by long, aimless walks. We paid some visits to Orsini; but he showed himself pensive and taciturn, and these were short visits. The afternoon of Saturday, the 12th of January, was to prove an exception. We found Orsini and Gomez fully dressed, overcoats on and hats upon their heads. No sooner did he see us than Orsini let slip an exclamation that seemed to carry away with it the impatience accumulated by a long wait. He asked us to accompany him. We went out, and as we walked he said that he wished to take us to the little piazza of the Opéra to study, on the spot, his plan of action. We made our way slowly along the Rue Le Peletier, and upon arriving opposite the theatre we halted, as though to admire its architecture. There was a long silence. Felice Orsini broke it with these words: "My plan fits perfectly to the location." And, without making any gesture with his hands, which he kept thrust constantly into the pockets of his overcoat: "You, Gomez, will post yourself on the far side of the street, to the right, as close to the peristyle as you can manage. Pieri will take up position precisely on the opposite side. Moretto and I will plant ourselves here in front, more or less where we now stand. I know that the Emperor will shortly be attending a performance with La Ristori. The imperial carriage must be allowed to proceed, undisturbed, up to the theatre. The moment Napoleon alights, Gomez will hurl his bomb. If it takes effect, each one slips away by his own route, as best he can, and we meet afterwards on the Place de la Concorde, where I shall give instructions as to what is to be done. If it does not take effect, then one of two things: either the Emperor attempts to save himself by taking refuge in the theatre, in which case Pieri hurls the second bomb at his feet and finishes him; or else he retreats into the carriage and it drives off at the gallop, in which case Pieri strikes it from behind, whilst di Rudio and I strike it from the side. That ought to suffice to shatter a small fortress. But even supposing the Emperor should be able to proceed, whether to the right or to the left along the Rue Le Peletier, before he reaches the Boulevard Montmartre or the Rue Rossini, he will find patriots who will know how to send him the salutation he deserves. In any event, once the attempt is carried out — all of us, by different routes, to the Place de la Concorde!"

The following day, Sunday, Pieri proposed an excursion to Passy. The broad horizon of the countryside seemed to augment in the Tuscan patriot his need to expand himself, and against the exceptional chill of the day he opposed an exceptional warmth of phrases, of images, of evocations. In that interminable succession of words, the man of fierce resolve who yearned for freedom, the practical philosopher, now rose above the sensual man serene in his easy loves, now appeared subject to him and as though eclipsed by him. Never before had I found myself confronted with so complex an amalgam of solidity and vanity.

Of all his revelations, that which impressed me most was perhaps, in itself, the least significant. Pieri had come to know Orsini at Birmingham, and it was precisely from there that he had set out to come to Paris. On his journey he had stopped in Brussels to call upon a mistress of his. Under the impulse of emotions that dispose any man to easy expansiveness, he had become, even more than was his wont, heedlessly candid. To the mistress who begged him to stay longer, he had replied that his immediate departure for Paris was irrevocable.

— What! You are going to France? And you have no fear of falling into some ugly trap, you who were expelled from it?

And Pieri, in reply, declared that this time he was going thither with the hope of repaying, *a misura di carbone* — in full and burning measure — those who had persecuted him.

That evening I knocked at Felice Orsini's door. A single glance sufficed for me to understand that he was more preoccupied than I was myself. For all that, I insisted upon an immediate conversation. He looked at me with searching eyes but said nothing. And I, leaping straight into the matter, recalled to him his first, wise resolve to keep the conspirators entirely unknown to one another. That resolve, I added, had been flouted and well-nigh blown away by circumstances — our security had become precarious and ephemeral. I did not speak, in particular, of Pieri's imprudence, but I dwelt upon the likelihood that some imprudence had been committed. Account must be taken of unforeseen contingencies — unquestionably numerous — that might imperil not only our own lives but the destiny of two Peoples; and I concluded by affirming that, in my opinion, we were wrong to attempt the execution of the enterprise with a superfluous number of participants. Two resolute men — or even one alone — might suffice.

I also reminded him that the resolve to strike at the heart of the French reaction by suppressing Napoleon was not new in me — he well knew it, having full knowledge of the letter I had written as far back as 1854 to Giuseppe Mazzini. Why not allow me to accomplish alone an enterprise upon which I could claim a certain right of priority? If I succeeded, our cause would have nothing to lose; if I failed, other forces unknown, intact, reanimated by the desire for redress, would renew the attempt. The certainty of success lay in persistence.

— The imminence of the action, Orsini replied, not only persuades me that your fears are groundless, but also renders repentance impossible. How do you expect, moreover, that an enemy destined to ride into our ambush at the trot should escape us — and almost as though hastening to his own triumph? What is there to wait for? That the Emperor, deciding upon the evening, should give us, with his own hands, the signal to strike him. That is all!

I persisted, and pointed out to him that my plan grafted itself admirably upon his, diminishing, by its very simplicity, the risks. If he could procure for me — and it ought not to be difficult for him — the uniform of a *sargent de ville*, I should mingle, thus disguised, among the guards assigned by the city to protect the Emperor. Each section furnished a contingent, and those from one part of the city did not personally know those from the others. When the Emperor wishes to alight from his carriage, one of these guards, by a custom become a right, hastens to open the door for him. Let us consider: Louis Bonaparte proceeds, amid his police, up to the peristyle of the theatre. I fling myself to the carriage door. I am a non-commissioned officer. But he steps down! It is a matter of an instant. For the Roman Republic — for those cut down by grapeshot on the 2nd of December. From that moment, the history of popular retribution resumes its course!

Felice Orsini paced the room its full length and breadth. He was agitated, and upon his most mobile face thoughts pressed upon one another like lightning flashes. At last he planted himself before me, picked up and threw back down upon the table several papers, then burst out: "It is too late, it is too late! And besides — equal the glory, equal the risk. Let us say no more of it!"

With that refusal, he sealed his own destiny irrevocably.

If it has its effect, everyone slips away by their own road as best they can, and we will all meet later on the piazza della Concorde, where I will give instructions on what is to be done. If it has no effect, one of two things will happen: either the Emperor will try to save himself by taking refuge in the theatre, and then Pieri will throw the second bomb at his feet and finish him; or he will retreat into the carriage and it will pull away at a gallop, and then Pieri will strike it from behind, and I and di Rudio from the side. That should be enough to shatter a small fort. But even assuming that the Emperor is able to proceed, either to the right or to the left, along the rue Le Pelletier, before he reaches the Boulevard Montmartre or the rue Rossini, he will find patriots who will know how to send him the greeting he deserves. Be that as it may, once the attack is accomplished, everyone, by different routes, to the piazza della Concorde!"

The following day, Sunday, Pieri proposed an excursion to Passy. The vast horizon of the countryside seemed to increase in the Tuscan patriot the need to expand and, to the exceptional bitterness of the day, he opposed an exceptional warmth of phrases, of images, of recollections. In that interminable sequence of words, the man of proud resolves yearning for liberty, the practical philosopher, now superimposed himself upon the sensual man, serene in his easy loves, now showed himself subordinate to him and as if eclipsed. Never had I found myself before such a complex amalgam of soundness and vanity.

Of all his revelations, the one that impressed me most was that which, in itself, was perhaps the most insignificant. Pieri had known Orsini in Birmingham, and it was from there that he had set out to come to Paris. On his journey, he had stopped in Bruxelles to greet a mistress of his. Under the impulse of emotions that make anyone prone to easy abandon, he had become, even more than usual, thoughtlessly expansive. To his mistress, who begged him to stay longer, he had replied that his immediate departure for Paris was irrevocable.

— What! you are going to France? And are you not afraid of falling into some ugly trap, you who were expelled from there?

And Pieri, in reply, affirmed that, this time, he was going with the hope of repaying, with a measure of coal, those who had persecuted him.

That evening, I was knocking at the door of Felice Orsini. A single glance was enough for me to understand that he was more pensive than I was. Despite this, I insisted on an immediate interview. He looked at me with searching eyes, but said nothing. And I, jumping into the subject with both feet, reminded him of his first, wise decision to keep the conspirators completely unknown to one another. That decision, I added, had been mocked and almost blown away by circumstances... our security had become precarious and ephemeral. I did not speak, in particular, of Pieri's imprudence, but I insisted on the probability that some imprudence had been committed. One had to take into account the unforeseen, but undoubtedly multiple, eventualities that could place in jeopardy not only our existence but the destiny of two Peoples, and I concluded by affirming that, in my opinion, we were wrong to attempt the execution of the enterprise with a superfluous number of participants. Two resolute men, even one alone, could suffice.

I also reminded him that the resolve to strike at the heart of the French reaction by suppressing Napoleon was not new in me... he knew it well, for he had full knowledge of the letter I had written as far back as 1854 to Giuseppe Mazzini. Why not let me carry out alone an enterprise to which I could claim a certain right of precedence? If I succeeded, our cause would have had nothing to lose; if I failed, other unknown, untouched forces, reanimated by the desire for revenge, would have renewed the attempt. The certainty of success lay in persistence.

— The imminence of the action, replied Orsini, not only persuades me that your fears are unfounded, but also makes second thoughts impossible. How, besides, do you expect an enemy destined to fall into the ambush at a trot, almost as if running to a triumph, to escape us? What remains is to wait! For the Emperor, by setting the evening, to give us, with his own hands, the signal to strike him. That is all!

I insisted, and pointed out to him that my plan grafted itself admirably onto his, diminishing the risks by its very simplicity. If he were to procure for me (and it should not be difficult for him) the uniform of a "sargent de ville", I would have mingled, in disguise, among the guards delegated by the city to protect the Emperor. Each section furnished a contingent, and those from one part of the city did not personally know those who came from the others... When the Emperor wishes to dismount from his carriage, one of these guards, by a custom that had become a right, hastens to open its door. Now let us see: Luigi Bonaparte proceeds, among his henchmen, to the peristyle of the theatre. I rush to the carriage door. I am a man of rank. He is getting out! It is a matter of an instant. To the Roman Republic, to the victims of the grapeshot of December 2nd! The history of popular vindications resumes its course from that moment!

Felice Orsini paced the length and breadth of the room. He was agitated, and on his most mobile face, thoughts chased one another, like flashes of lightning. He ended by planting himself before me... he picked up and threw back on the table several papers, then burst out: "It is too late, it is too late! And, on the other hand, equal the glory, equal the risk. Let us speak of it no more!"

With that refusal, he was irrevocably sealing his own destiny

## Chapter the Seventh

THE next morning, I was pressing forward at full steam towards my destiny. To face it, I had my carpet-bag... between the leather and the wooden panel of the bottom, it concealed mercury fulminate and a blade which had already, in the insurrectionary attempts of 1853, known the beating of my heart. I had moreover a passport in the name of Giovanni da Sylva, a Portuguese, travelling salesman for a brewery, and some ten shillings. Money and passport had been placed in my hands by Dr. Bernard during our last colloquy. I had, above all, my youth and my courage. What would come of all this? The spark capable of altering history, or nothing other than desolation and death?

Leaving the mouths of the Thames behind us, we encountered a very rough sea, a veritable tempest. The stomach's distress and the terror cast a great confusion aboard. But the infinite, which roared on every side, filled me with an exaltation that redoubled my consciousness of life. For some time a certain quietism had seemed to impose itself upon my nature, and that kind of immersion in force and motion seemed to regenerate me.

Yet the storm itself, which I almost blessed, was, unknown to me, the answer of things to the question I had put to myself, and which pressed upon my mind with great insistence. The steamship, slowed by wind and billows, reached Calais at five in the morning of the ninth of January... twelve hours late! And when, in Paris, I descended from the train, I sought in vain, among the crowded throng, the noble figure of Felice Orsini. This mishap made a visit to his lodgings in the rue Monthabor imperative — a circumstance which was to have consequences far graver than its apparent trifling nature led me to suppose.

Here, in brief, is what came to pass during that first visit of mine. Orsini's apartment was on the ground floor. The porter accompanied me to the door, knocked, and when the door opened, discreetly withdrew. I entered with a bound and was on the point of embracing Orsini as a brother — he stood erect and waiting — when I noticed the presence of a third person... a young man sprawled with easy familiarity in an armchair. He was fair-haired, with cheeks and lips cleanly shaven, and had the look of a good-natured priest. I thereupon assumed the deferential air of the travelling salesman and, giving the name from the passport, offered to present “Tomaso Allsop” with a few samples of beer.

I was rummaging in the carpet-bag, already turned inside out by the customs officers, when the false Allsop broke into a loud laugh. “We are among friends,” he said. And he introduced me, by my true name, to Antonio Gomez. He then asked me to explain my delay, and, helping things along, said: “How did Miss Cheney receive you! Did she hand over the affair to you!”

I turned out my few belongings upon the table and set about removing the panel that concealed the mercury fulminate. A moment later it lay spread upon a newspaper — *Le Droit*, if memory does not deceive me. Felice Orsini was running his fingertips over it, as though to judge the degree of its moisture. His face, ordinarily so mobile and open, had assumed a rigid expression of deep melancholy. At last he said: “The supply is now complete, and not for nothing shall we have cast twelve apostles.” And he sighed.

Struck by that melancholy air and by that sigh, I recalled his original plan, inspired by his horror of bloodshed. Evidently, he still clung to the idea of entrusting to the blade the liberation of France.

“This devilish stuff is very damp,” he resumed, “we shall have to make use of the fireplace and the thermometer. But this does not concern you. I owe it to your wife and your son not to expose you beyond what is strictly necessary.”

At that moment there came a knock at the door... undoubtedly a prearranged signal, for Orsini did not stir and left the fulminate on the table, in full view.

There entered a man of my own stature, with a grizzled beard and an intelligent eye; the pallor of his face lent extraordinary intensity to his dark pupils. He stopped short on seeing me and contented himself with uttering a simple, commonplace greeting. But his words resounded in the room in an extraordinary manner. A voice that recalled the monotonous sound of a bag-pipe. It was the first time I had heard it, yet it would have sufficed for me to recognise it among a thousand, even many years hence. And, there and then, it struck me as the most distinctive mark of the man. Undoubtedly the beard concealed a full throat.

Felice Orsini made the introduction. In this manner I came to know personally Giuseppe Pieri, with whom I had already exchanged, by post, a brief correspondence. He at once struck up conversation with great familiarity, as though for long years we had shared good and ill fortune alike. Such, in practice, was the outcome of Orsini's prudent resolve to reserve to himself alone the knowledge of those taking part in the attempt.

I had intended to speak to him of this at the first opportunity, when he, as though to accentuate the unfortunate turn of affairs, broke in upon the boisterous loquacity of Pieri: “By the way!” he said to me. “Where have you taken lodgings?”

I observed that I had arrived but a short while ago... barely time enough to eat a bite at the nearest eating-house and make my way here. I had been left with few coins in my pocket. Since the understanding had been that I should lodge, as a greater precaution, in a first-rate hotel, I was awaiting his instructions.

“You know!” he resumed, after scratching his head. “Our friend Pieri is lodging at the Hôtel France et Champagne, rue Montmartre. He has a room with two beds. Go with him, and matters will be kept simple.”

He gave me no time to raise objections. He leant his person backwards and, letting his gaze pass in turn over each of us, said: “A Venetian, a Tuscan, a Neapolitan, a Romagnole. And other, other shreds of Italian soil shall be represented in this proud enterprise! It is not this or that individual, not this or that region, but all Italy that awakens, that labours to open for herself the road to redemption. The significance is great, and Europe will comprehend it. And this union of hands that seek one another, today compelled to do so in secret, pledges us that other union which shall be accomplished in the sight of Peoples and in the light of the Sun... which shall come to pass, shall come to pass!” And I bowed my head, and a powerful tremor ran through my bones, as though in that place there had suddenly passed the great soul of a prophet!
