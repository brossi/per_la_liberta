ON the evening of the following day, January 14th, Pieri and I dined, as usual, at the trattoria in the galleria Montmartre. We repaired, immediately after, to the hotel and the doorkeeper, handing Pieri the key to the room, announced to him that half an hour before, a gentleman had come asking for him. He had not revealed his name, but had assured him that he would return shortly.

Here we were in our room, in a frantic search for a match to light the gas. We had left the door open, to take temporary advantage of the light coming from the corridor. Turning instinctively I perceived, silhouetted in black against that light, the figure of Felice Orsini.

His words were brief: "Compatriots, the Emperor has given the signal. Go to my house to get the implements. I shall be there in an hour. Everything is arranged for the best... success cannot fail. But given the unforeseen... here are the means to facilitate your escape and your safety." And drawing forth a purse, he counted out two hundred francs for each of us. Then he continued: "I should have liked to provide better for your needs, but it is impossible for me. One contributes to the triumph of great unacknowledged principles more with the integrity of the soul than with the force of gold. It is lacking to the persecuted and is more properly the weapon of despotism. Remember that on your punctuality, on your firmness, depends the redemption of the Fatherland. I say no more to you." He saluted me with a powerful handshake and disappeared. But something remained.

A tormenting spirit that seemed to saturate the air, and my heart was nearly dismayed by it. The moments that precede the action are always more trying than those in which the action is accomplished. The act of doing gives rise to a fever that increases with the danger and leaves no room for reflection or repentance. Be that as it may, from the tumult of thoughts that assailed me, the sentiment of duty emerged triumphant, and my soul remained as if armoured by it.

I cast a glance at Pieri and, just then, to my astonishment, I saw him throw the money Orsini had left him onto the bed, as if he scorned it. A moment later he drew from a case a revolver (the marvellous American invention had recently been introduced in Europe) and, handing it to me, said: "Take it! it contains six shots... six friends, true friends! they could be of use to you!" He threw the case into the drawer of the chest-of-drawers, locked it, picked up his money again, and we went out.

We went out to traverse, in silence and for the last time, the road that led to the well-known dwelling on the rue Monthabor. Having arrived before Felice Orsini's apartment, and as we were about to knock on the door, it opened and two men crossed the threshold. They had the collars of their overcoats raised, their faces turned towards the darkness of the courtyard. I did not recognize them.

We found ourselves reunited once more, I, Pieri, Gomez, and Orsini, as on the day I wished to submit my beer samples to Tomaso Allsop. But Gomez, as soon as he saw us, asked leave to absent himself to make, he said, a small purchase. We waited for him in silence, Orsini leaning, almost sitting, on the table. Those long moments of waiting made him nervous. He stood up and said: "It occurs to me that that man has gone to sell the mice to the cat."

But Gomez returned, and Orsini smiled. He then began to distribute the bombs. He said simply: "To you... to you..." So that they would not slip from our hands, he had wrapped them in a black handkerchief that, held by its knotted corners, would have gained the force of a sling. Gomez and I each received a large one; Pieri a small one; Orsini cautiously thrust two small ones, one in the right pocket, the other in the left of his overcoat. We departed, I at Orsini's side; Gomez and Pieri preceded us. At this point I could not help but notice that Pieri had kept his lips constantly sealed, as if the imminence of the action, or some secret thought, had rendered him suddenly mute. I drew from this rather uncharitable conclusions. Soon we lost sight of him, and Orsini remarked to me, without my saying a word, that "the loquacious Tuscan had all the air of a man who intended to desert."

Now I must recount an incident that was related to me later by Orsini himself and which Pieri confirmed. At a certain point, Pieri turned back on his path and passed beside us; but he was not alone. A stranger accompanied him, properly dressed, with a contented, almost cheerful, expression. This man went on his way, without paying us any mind; Pieri, instead, winked at Orsini. Absorbed in my thoughts, or distracted, I could not well say, I noticed nothing, and Orsini did not deem it opportune to speak of it to me. He imagined that an entirely harmless nuisance had latched onto "the loquacious Tuscan"; who would undoubtedly have known how to be rid of him, perhaps in some liquor shop, so as to then be at his post at the designated hour. That silence had a decisive influence on my own actions immediately after the attempt.

We are now about to set foot on the rue Le Pelletier. Felice Orsini stops and looks around as if he were anxious to see an expected person. A few seconds, and a man with a hurried step, with long moustaches protruding from his raised collar, emerges from the crowd and moves towards him. Out of politeness, I drew a few steps aside; but the ear of the conspirator acquires habits that are not precisely those of a polite man. For this reason, perhaps, and not by deliberate intent, I caught the following brief dialogue:

— How goes the business?

— All is well!... Tonight!

And they shook hands. Immediately, the man with the long moustaches departed, in haste, as he had come. We resumed our path and Orsini said: "Do you know that man?" — "Certainly! It is Francesco Crispi!" Whereupon he, with a slight tinge of vexation: "The devil! I thought you did not know him!"
