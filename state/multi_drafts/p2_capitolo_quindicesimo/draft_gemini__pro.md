WHEN the cell door closed again, it was as if the turbid passions that stirred within me remained beyond the threshold. Saturno and the other questioned me with their expressions. "All is finished! Every aspiration of mine for liberty is cut short from this day. We must prepare ourselves to die." We, I said, but within, I felt that I would be sustained by the pride of having sacrificed myself for my Country.

No sooner had I finished my account than they endeavoured, as by a tacit understanding, to drag me back from my convictions. They put forward, among other things, the sympathies of the populace and the good dispositions which, it was assured, animated the Imperial Court. To this I replied by shaking my head and smiling. A strange squabble arose from it, and we ended by making a wager. If the Tribunal pronounced a sentence of death, they would submit to paying me for a bottle of champagne, and of the best. If not, I would pay for it. We sealed the pact by exchanging a vigorous handshake...

Mathieu de la Drôme paid me his first visit that very day. He was a man of dignified appearance, tall, already more than grey, placid in his actions and his speech. For several days he had spontaneously offered to defend me; but we were not permitted to confer until the Police were quite certain they had their claws tight at my throat. He approached, so to speak, my very soul with the delicacy of a physician who knows he must touch sensitive and aching limbs. And I immediately recounted to him, without reticence (the guards had left), the whole of my past.

When I had finished, he lowered his eyes and for a few seconds was lost in his reflections. Then with a paternal affability: "The damaging admissions could be retracted. It is your privilege. But to what extent it would benefit you, I do not know. You are accustomed to facing danger, and I prefer to speak plainly to you. The direct evidence gathered by the Police makes an acquittal inaccessible to you. If one were to consider only yourself and your attitude before your countrymen, I would tell you: Go to meet death majestically, all of one piece, blessing the Fatherland, cursing her oppressors. The People will grant you, in their great heart, the place that you deserve. But the wise man"

Here he launched into a long explanation of what, in his way of seeing things, the wise man would have done in my case. To rush headlong towards the scaffold was neither to benefit the Fatherland, nor did my sacrifice improve the situation of my accomplices. And how would my old parents, who had already suffered so much, have borne a blow so desperately without remedy? A young bride trembled for me, and I had made her a mother. Why not try every honest path to live, when, by cheating the executioner, I would have spared those unfortunate ones useless sorrow?

I ended by asking him for an explicit explanation of what he required of me. "A free hand," he replied, "in attempting the means that might save you, and a formal promise to moderate your impulses whatever part I might deem it opportune for you to assume."

I consented. And this explains why in the trial records I emerge as a faded figure, almost without a will of my own, easily suggestible, driven to the terrible act by the iron will of the leaders and by the morbid preoccupation of not seeming pusillanimous, of not being confused with the Mantuan Rossi, of not being taken for a spy.

I have not and never have had for Mathieu de la Drôme anything but feelings of filial gratitude. Unfortunately, his noble efforts to "cheat the executioner" later became a convenient document for the denigration of the malevolent, blinded as much by personal grudges as by partisan rancour... not the least among the motives which, for fifty years, locked me in a disdainful silence!

My accomplices were caught in the identical vise, plunged up to their throats in the identical quicksand.

The evidence of which the Authorities had possessed themselves was sufficient, in itself, to secure a conviction in perfect accordance with the Law. Corroborating it with the admissions of the accused would have given more energetic emphasis, greater theatricality, to the shrewdness of the Police.

To better achieve its aim, it had begun its subtle work on me, who was the youngest; then came the turn of Antonio Gomez, who was a few years my senior. Before this man, the Examining Magistrate triumphantly placed a letter of recommendation given to him by Pieri, addressed to Orsini. Worse still, a letter written in his own hand to Orsini himself. In it he took pains to dissipate certain suspicions that had crept into the suspicious mind of the leader and, soliciting confirmation of the assignment promised him, he affirmed his desire to participate in the conspiracy, inspired solely by the love which he had always borne and still bore for the common Fatherland.

Upon the Italians, risen to a Nation, falls the moral obligation to place that solicitation and the sentiments that inspired it to Gomez's credit... but before the French Police, could there have been a more compromising document?

As if that were not enough, Gomez had every one of his movements pointed out to him with precision, from when, on the 6th of January, he departed with Pieri from Birmingham, to stop first in Londra, at the house of Miss Cheney, then in Lille, awaiting Pieri who was making his dash to Bruxelles; all the way to the moment when he set foot for the first time in Orsini's furnished apartment, at No. 10 via Monthabor.

And here, as a final blow, the honest insinuation: "Your accomplices have confessed their part and yours. To what purpose persist in denial?"

After various reticences and various moves too desperate to savour of sound tactics, hearing it said openly that I, di Rudio, in admitting to having thrown the second bomb, had revealed all the rest, he ended by confessing to have thrown the first. But even in this extremity, an instinct quite widespread among the population of southern Italy did not abandon him: that which leads one to pull from his pocket the paper cone of foolishness and smear his face with it. He, who had indeed been a soldier, tried to make it seem that he had thrown his projectile like a faithful servant, without realizing the exact nature of its deadly effects. It was upon this supposed foolishness that his defence was based...

More arduous was the struggle for Giuseppe Pieri, in such a tight spot. The police had placed before him several revealing letters; they had narrated to him, with indisputable precision, step by step, his movements prior to the attack; his dealings with Orsini, with Gomez, with Simon Bernard, with me; his complicity in importing the empty projectiles into France, in the purchase of certain revolvers found near the scene of the attack! Elisa Cheney pointing him out and Hartmann denouncing him had, with their personal testimonies, corroborated the written evidence. The arrest carried out just minutes after and a few paces from the attack, the live bomb in his overcoat pocket, the boastful claims that escaped him after his arrest... could there have been a more enveloping net, a more complete story?

He added nothing to it, for there was nothing left for him to add. He tried instead to embellish the facts with fantastic lies and to draw from them deductions that tended to distort their significance.

"He had come to Parigi solely to settle some family business and intended to go to Italia, because a revolution was imminent there. He had discussed bombs with Orsini, but solely to make use of them in the Fatherland, on the barricades. And since he had expressed some doubt as to their efficacy, Orsini had given him one; but with the understanding that they would explode it, as an experiment, at the Barriera dei Martiri. Only Pieri had gone to the appointment; for that reason the bomb had remained in his pocket, and in his pocket they had found it. If on the evening of the attack he had gone to via Le Pelletier, it was simply because he was drawn there by the extraordinary illumination."

A defence put together with sufficient cunning; but a wasted effort!... a few of his written phrases and a little analysis were enough to break through it. He had the merit of realizing this in time. Then, using as a pretext "the inquisitorial manners of the Examining Magistrate," he declared that he would no longer answer any questions; that he would refute the accusations only during the actual trial, before those whom he considered his natural judges.

He thus averted the danger of compromising himself ever further. But was worse really possible?

Nugent de Saint Laurent, who later fully approved of Pieri's tactic, attempted to add weight to it by placing great emphasis on the animosity that existed between Pieri himself and Orsini; as if to deduce that a conspiracy, and the fraternal relations it entails, were impossible between two men who cordially hated one another. Unfortunately, the reason for the disagreements was not clarified, whereas the circumstance that they were of too recent a date to have had any dissolving force upon the conspiracy seemed, instead, quite clear.
