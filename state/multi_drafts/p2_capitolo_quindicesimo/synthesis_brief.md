Ogni mia aspirazione alla libertà è troncata da oggi. Bisogna prepararsi a morire". Noi dissi, ma dentro, sentivo che m'avrebbe sorretto l'orgoglio d'essermi sacrificato pel mio Paese.

Non appena ebbi terminato di narrare, essi si sforzarono, come per tacita intesa, di trascinarmi a ritroso delle mie convinzioni. Accamparono tra l'altro, le simpatie della popolazione e le buone disposizioni che, a quanto si assicurava, animavano la Corte imperiale. A questo risposi dondolando il capo e sorridendo. Ne nacque uno strano bisticcio e finimmo col fare una scommessa. Se il Tribunale pronunciava sentenza di morte essi si sarebbero assoggettati a pagarmi una bottiglia di champagne e del migliore. Se no, l'avrei pagata io. Suggellammo il patto scambiandoci una energica stretta di mano...

Mathieu de la Drôme mi fece la sua prima visita quel giorno stesso. Era un uomo dignitoso all'aspetto, alto, già più che brizzolato, placido negli atti e nella parola. Da parecchi giorni si era spontaneamente offerto di difendermi; ma non ci venne concesso d'abboccarci sinché la Polizia non fu ben certa d'avermi le unghie strette alla gola. Egli si avvicinò, per così dire, all'animo mio colla delicatezza di un medico che sa di dover toccare membra sensitive e doloranti. Ed io gli narrai subito, senza reticenze (le guardie erano uscite) tutto quanto il mio passato.

Quand'ebbi finito, abbassò gli occhi e s'internò, per qualche secondo, nelle sue riflessioni. Poi con una affabilità paterna: "Le ammissioni dannose potrebbero essere ritrattate. E' nel vostro privilegio. Ma sino a qual punto vi gioverebbe, ignoro. Siete avvezzo a tener fronte al pericolo e preferisco parlarvi chiaro. Le prove dirette raccolte dalla Polizia vi rendono l'assolutoria inaccessibile. Se fosse da por mente solo a voi stesso ed alla vostra attitudine di fronte ai connazionali, vi direi: Andate incontro alla morte maestosamente, tutto d'un pezzo, benedicendo alla Patria, maledicendo ai suoi oppressori. Qui tirò via spiegandomi a lungo ciò che, a suo modo di vedere, l'uomo saggio avrebbe fatto nel caso mio. Precipitarsi a testa bassa verso il patibolo, non era un giovare alla Patria né il mio sacrificio migliorava la situazione dei complici. E come avrebbero sopportato i miei vecchi genitori, che già tanto soffersero, un colpo disperatamente senza rimedio? Una giovane sposa trepidava per me ed io l'avevo fatta madre. Perchè non tentare ogni onesta via per vivere, quando, deludendo il carnefice, avrei risparmiato inutili dolori a quegli infelici?

Finii per domandargli una spiegazione esplicita su quanto richiedeva da me. "Mano libera — rispose — nel tentare i mezzi che potrebbero salvarvi e promessa formale di moderare i vostri impeti qualunque sia la parte che io stimassi opportuno di farvi assumere."

Acconsentii. E ciò spiega perchè dai resoconti processuali io emerga scolorito, quasi senza volontà propria, facilmente suggestionabile, sospinto al terribile atto dalla ferrea mente dei capi e dalla morbosa preoccupazione di non sembrare pusillanime, di non essere confuso col Rossi mantovano, di non essere ritenuto per una spia.

Io non ho e non ebbi mai per Mathieu de la Drôme se non che moti di filiale riconoscenza. Disgraziatamente i suoi nobili sforzi per "deludere il carnefice" furono poi comodo documento di denigrazione ai malevoli, accecati sia da rancori personali, sia da rancori di parte... non ultimo fra i motivi che, per cinquant'anni, mi chiusero in uno sdegnoso silenzio!

I miei complici erano presi nell'identica morsa, precipitati sino alla gola nell'identica sabbia mobile.

Le prove di cui l'Autorità si era impossessata, bastavano, per sé stesse, a conficcarsi a perfetto tenore di Legge. Il corroborare con le ammissioni degli imputati avrebbe dato più energico risalto, maggiore teatralità alla scaltrezza della Polizia.

A meglio raggiungere il suo intento, essa aveva incominciato da me, che ero il più giovane, il suo sottile lavorìo; poi venne la volta di Antonio Gomez che mi sorpassava di qualche anno. A costui, il Giudice Istruttore mise trionfalmente sott'occhio una commendatizia rilasciatagli dal Pieri, diretta all'Orsini. Peggio ancora, una lettera scritta di suo proprio pugno all'Orsini stesso. In essa si industriava di dissipare certe diffidenze insinuatesi nell'animo sospettoso del capo e, sollecitando la conferma del promessogli incarico, affermava di voler partecipare alla congiura, ispirato unicamente dall'amore che aveva sempre portato e che portava alla Patria comune.

Agli Italiani, risorti a Nazione, corre l'obbligo morale di mettere tale sollecitazione e i sentimenti che l'ispirarono all'attivo del Gomez... ma di fronte alla Polizia francese, poteva darsi documento più compromettente?

Quasi non bastasse, si vide, il Gomez, indicare appuntino ogni sua mossa, da quando, il 6 Gennaio, partiva col Pieri, da Birmingham, per sostare dapprima a Londra, in casa della signorina Cheney, poi a Lille, in attesa del Pieri che faceva la sua punta a Bruxelles; via sino al momento in cui metteva, per la prima volta, il piede nell'appartamento ammobiliato dell'Orsini, in via Monthabor No. 10.

E qui, come colpo finale, l'onesta insinuazione: "i suoi complici avevano confessato la loro e la sua parte. A qual prò ostinarsi nella negazione?"

Dopo varie reticenze e varie mosse troppo disperate per saper di sana tattica, sentendosi dire apertamente che io, di Rudio, ammettendo d'aver lanciato la seconda bomba, avevo svelato tutto il resto, finì per confessare d'aver lanciato la prima. Ma anche in tale estremità, non lo abbandonò un istinto molto diffuso tra la popolazione dell'Italia meridionale: quello che induce a trarre, dal taschino, il cartoccio della dabbenaggine ed a spalmarsene il viso. Egli, che pure fu soldato, si provò a far credere d'aver lanciato il suo proiettile da servo fedele, senza darsi esatto conto de' suoi effetti micidiali. Fu su questa supposta dabbenaggine che venne basata la sua difesa...

Più arduo riuscì il dibattersi, fra simili strette, a Giuseppe Pieri. La polizia gli aveva messo sott'occhio parecchie lettere rivelatrici; gli aveva narrato, con indiscutibile precisione, a passo a passo, le sue mosse antecedenti l'attentato; i suoi rapporti coll'Orsini, col Gomez, con Simon Bernard, con me; la sua complicità nell'importare in Francia i proiettili scarichi, nell'acquisto di certe rivoltelle trovate presso il luogo dell'attentato! Elisa Cheney additandolo e la Hartmann denunciandolo avevano, colle loro testimonianze personali, corroborato le prove scritte. L'arresto operato a pochi minuti e a pochi passi dall'attentato, la bomba carica nella tasca del soprabito, le millanterie sfuggitegli dopo l'arresto... poteva darsi rete più avvolgente, storia più completa?

Egli nulla vi aggiunse, perchè nulla gli restava d'aggiungere. Tentò invece di ornamentare i fatti con fandonie fantastiche e di trarne deduzioni che tendessero a svisare la portata.

"Era venuto a Parigi unicamente per sistemare qualche affare di famiglia e intendeva recarsi in Italia, perchè vi era prossima una rivoluzione. Aveva discusso di bombe coll'Orsini, ma unicamente per trarne partito in Patria, sulle barricate. E poiché aveva manifestata qualche dubbio sulla loro efficacia, l'Orsini glie n'aveva consegnata una; ma coll'intesa che l'avrebbero fatta esplodere, così per esperimento, alla Barriera dei Martiri. Solo il Pieri si era recato all'appuntamento; perciò la bomba gli era rimasta in tasca ed in tasca glie l'avevano trovata. Se la sera dell'attentato si era recato in via Le Pelletier, fu semplicemente perchè vi venne attratto dalla straordinaria illuminazione."

Usbergo messo assieme con sufficiente scaltrezza; ma fatica sprecata!... poche sue frasi scritte e un po' d'analisi bastavano a sfondarlo. Ebbe il merito di avvedercene a tempo. Allora, accampando a pretesto "i modi da inquisitore del Giudice d'Istruzione", dichiarò che non avrebbe più risposto a domanda alcuna; che avrebbe confutato le accuse unicamente durante il processo vero e proprio; dinanzi a quelli che riteneva i suoi giudici naturali.

Egli scongiurava, per tal modo, il pericolo di compromettersi sempre più. Ma era il peggio realmente possibile?

Nugent de Saint Laurent che approvò poi pienamente la tattica del Pieri, tentò d'aggiungervi peso, dando grande enfasi all'animosità esistente tra il Pieri stesso e l'Orsini; quasi a dedurne che una congiura e i fraterni rapporti che essa comporta, erano impossibili tra due uomini che cordialmente si odiavano. Disgraziatamente, la ragione dei dissapori non venne chiarita, ben chiarita parve invece la circostanza che essi erano di data troppo recente per avere sulla congiura, forza dissolvente alcuna.

WHEN the door of the cell closed behind me, it was as though the turbid passions that had agitated me remained on the other side of the threshold. Saturno and the other questioned me with the expression of their faces. "It is all over! Every aspiration I had towards liberty is cut short from this day. One must prepare oneself to die." I did not say this aloud, but within, I felt that the pride of having sacrificed myself for my Country would sustain me.

No sooner had I finished relating what had passed than they strove, as if by tacit agreement, to drag me back from my convictions. They put forward, among other things, the sympathies of the population and the favourable dispositions which, as it was asserted, animated the imperial Court. To this I replied with a shake of my head and a smile. A strange dispute arose from this, and we ended by making a wager. If the Tribunal pronounced a sentence of death, they would undertake to pay me a bottle of champagne, and of the finest. If not, I should pay it. We sealed the compact by exchanging a vigorous handshake.

Mathieu de la Drôme paid me his first visit that same day. He was a man of dignified appearance, tall, already more than greying, placid in his manner and speech. For several days past he had spontaneously offered to defend me; but we had not been permitted to meet until the Police were well assured of having their claws fast about my throat. He drew near, so to speak, to my soul with the delicacy of a physician who knows he must touch limbs that are sensitive and in pain. And I related to him at once, without reserve (the guards had withdrawn) the whole of my past.

When I had finished, he lowered his eyes and withdrew into his reflections for some seconds. Then, with a paternal affability: "The damaging admissions might be retracted. It is within your right. But to what extent it would serve you, I cannot say. You are accustomed to facing danger, and I prefer to speak plainly. The direct evidence gathered by the Police makes an acquittal beyond your reach. Were it a matter of considering only yourself and your bearing before your countrymen, I should say to you: Go forth to meet death majestically, whole and unbroken, blessing your Country, cursing her oppressors. The People will accord you, in their great heart, the place that is your due. But the wise man—"

Here he drew out at length, setting before me what, in his view, the wise man would have done in my situation. To hurl oneself headlong towards the scaffold was not to serve one's Country, nor would my sacrifice improve the position of my fellow conspirators. And how would my aged parents, who had already suffered so greatly, endure a blow so desperately past all remedy? A young wife trembled for me, and I had made her a mother. Why not attempt every honourable means of living, when, by cheating the executioner, I should spare needless grief to those unhappy souls?

I ended by asking him for an explicit explanation of what he required of me. "A free hand," he answered, "in attempting the means which might save you, and a formal promise to moderate your impulses, whatever the part I might judge it expedient for you to assume."

I consented. And this explains why, in the accounts of the trial, I emerge colourless, almost without a will of my own, easily swayed, driven to the terrible act by the iron mind of the leaders and by a morbid anxiety not to appear pusillanimous, not to be confounded with Rossi of Mantua, not to be taken for a spy.

I have never felt, and have never felt, towards Mathieu de la Drôme anything other than movements of filial gratitude. Unfortunately, his noble efforts to "cheat the executioner" served afterwards as a convenient instrument of defamation for the ill-disposed, blinded alike by personal rancour and by partisan rancour — not the least of the motives that, for fifty years, shut me in a disdainful silence.

My fellow conspirators were caught in the selfsame vice, sunk to the throat in the selfsame quicksand.

The evidence of which the Authority had possessed itself was, of itself, sufficient to be used to the full letter of the Law. To reinforce it with the admissions of the accused would give bolder relief, greater theatrical effect, to the cunning of the Police.

The better to attain its end, it had begun its subtle work with me, who was the youngest; then came the turn of Antonio Gomez, who was my elder by some years. To this man the Examining Magistrate placed in triumph before his eyes a letter of recommendation issued to him by Pieri, addressed to Orsini. Worse still, a letter written in his own hand to Orsini himself. In it he laboured to dispel certain suspicions that had insinuated themselves into the mistrustful mind of the chief, and, pressing for confirmation of the charge promised to him, he declared his desire to take part in the conspiracy, inspired solely by the love he had always borne and still bore to their common Country.

Italians, reborn as a Nation, have the moral obligation to set down such solicitude and the sentiments that inspired it to Gomez's credit — but, in the face of the French Police, could there be a more compromising document?

As though this were not enough, Gomez found himself required to account for his every movement, step by step, from the moment when, on the 6th of January, he set out with Pieri from Birmingham, halting first in London, at the house of Miss Cheney, then at Lille, awaiting Pieri, who was making his way through Brussels; and so on until the moment when he set foot for the first time in Orsini's furnished lodgings in the rue Monthabor, No. 10.

And here, as a finishing stroke, came the artful insinuation: "his fellow conspirators had confessed their own part and his. What purpose was there in persisting in denial?"

After various equivocations and various manoeuvres too desperate to be sound tactics, finding himself told openly that I, di Rudio, in admitting to having thrown the second bomb, had revealed everything else, he ended by confessing to having thrown the first. But even in this extremity, he was not forsaken by an instinct very widespread among the people of southern Italy: that which leads a man to draw from his pocket the packet of *dabbenaggine* (feigned simplicity) and to smear it over his face. He, who had been a soldier nonetheless, attempted to give the impression of having thrown his projectile as a faithful servant, without forming any clear notion of its deadly effects. It was upon this supposed simplicity that his defence was constructed.

More arduous proved the struggle, amid similar straits, for Giuseppe Pieri. The police had placed before his eyes several revealing letters; had recounted to him, with indisputable precision, step by step, his movements in the period preceding the attack; his relations with Orsini, with Gomez, with Simon Bernard, with me; his complicity in importing the empty projectiles into France, in the purchase of certain revolvers found near the scene of the attack. Elisa Cheney identifying him and Hartmann denouncing him had, by their personal testimonies, corroborated the written evidence. The arrest effected within minutes and paces of the attack, the loaded bomb in the pocket of his overcoat, the boasts that had escaped him after his arrest — could there be a more enveloping net, a more complete case?

He added nothing to it, because nothing remained for him to add. He attempted instead to adorn the facts with fantastic fabrications and to draw from them deductions tending to distort their significance.

"He had come to Paris solely to settle some family business, and intended to proceed to Italy, where a revolution was imminent. He had discussed bombs with Orsini, but solely with a view to putting them to use at home, on the barricades. And since he had expressed some doubt as to their efficacy, Orsini had handed one to him; but with the understanding that they would cause it to explode, by way of experiment, at the Barrière des Martyrs. Only Pieri had gone to the rendezvous; for which reason the bomb had remained in his pocket and had been found upon him there. If on the evening of the attack he had gone to the rue Le Pelletier, it was simply because he had been drawn thither by the extraordinary illumination."

A shield assembled with sufficient cleverness; but wasted labour! — a few of his written phrases and a little analysis sufficed to shatter it. He had the merit of perceiving this in time. Thereupon, pleading as a pretext "the inquisitor's manner of the Examining Magistrate," he declared that he would answer no further questions; that he would refute the charges solely during the trial proper, before those whom he considered his natural judges.

He conjured away, by this means, the danger of compromising himself yet further. But was the worst truly past?

Nugent de Saint Laurent, who afterwards fully approved of Pieri's tactics, attempted to lend them greater weight by laying great emphasis on the animosity existing between Pieri himself and Orsini; as though to infer that a conspiracy, and the fraternal relations it entails, were impossible between two men who cordially hated one another. Unfortunately, the reason for the disagreements was never made clear; while it became sufficiently plain that they were of too recent a date to have had any power of dissolution upon the conspiracy.

## Chapter Fourteen

FROM that moment I harboured no further doubt as to the fate that lay in store for me; and if I resolved to struggle to the end, it was solely, as one says, for honour of signature — so that I might cry out, upon reaching the bottom of the abyss: "I have done everything humanly possible to avoid plunging into it!"

That very same day I was compelled to convince myself that an overbearing will was working in the contrary direction, reinforcing the means honourably at its disposal with fraud and perjury. I was confined in a ground-floor cell. A companion was assigned to me — an agent of the secret Police, dressed in civilian clothes, who informed me that he would pass the day and night in my company. Certainly not to keep my spirits up, I thought — but to catch some word that might escape me, in the anguish of the soul, during sleep.

He appeared to divine my thought and begged me not to judge by appearances. If he could do me no good, he would certainly do me no harm.

Half an hour later, the bolts grated and the heavy door swung upon its hinges. Two persons entered: the examining magistrate and a man dressed as a municipal street-sweeper, with one empty socket, horribly scarred. Two guards remained standing on the threshold.

— Well then! said the magistrate, after a brief silence.

— It is he!

— Are you quite certain?

— As certain as I am baptised.

— Repeat your account.

— I was on duty at the Opera and was spreading sand at the entrance reserved for His Majesty. It would have been seven o'clock, or a quarter past at the most. I saw two gentlemen who were conversing in undertones and gesticulating. They had the air of studying the locality. I warned them that this was no place for the public and asked them to move along. They told me to mind my own business. The gentleman who is here said: "Shut your mouth, you scoundrel!" I made as if to strike him and both of them cleared off.

— And this took place?

— Yesterday evening, the fourteenth of January.

— An hour, or a little before, the attempt upon the life of His Majesty the Emperor?

— An hour, or a little more.

I had understood — perfectly understood! The object was to establish, in a manner that should appear irrefutable, our presence at the scene of the attempt. For Orsini there was his wound; for Gomez the arrest effected in front of the Opéra theatre. To bring about my own destruction and that of Pieri, they had placed in the mouth of that miserable wretch a tale invented out of whole cloth.

Nevertheless I did not feel that anger which drives a man to outbursts, and I contented myself with asking my strange accuser how much he had been promised in payment for his falsehoods. He turned aside the one greenish eye remaining to him and made no reply.

Late in the evening, a second agent was shut into my cell. Scarcely had he entered when he greeted me and, offering me his right hand, said that he too would not leave me either by day or by night. They were locking him in as well, as far as he could judge, in order to prevent any communication between myself and the accomplices whom I was suspected of having outside. He hoped that all three of us would, like good comrades, endeavour not to embitter our captivity with needless frictions.

Several days of relative quiet passed. No contact with the outside world, of which little air penetrated into my cell, still less sunlight, and the turnkey but rarely. He had orders to speak neither with me nor with my guards, who thus became the only human beings with whom I was permitted to exchange my thoughts. And I availed myself of this largely, telling them the history of Austria's horrible persecutions and of the suffering they visited upon my Country. One makes propaganda where one can and as occasion serves.

But this was not enough to deliver me from periods of deep prostration, in which the image of Elisa and of the child would rise before me, vivid and clear — the child to whose innocent years and maternal smile I had brought ruin. And my thoughts would wander at such times to my companions in misfortune as well, of whom I knew nothing save that they were, like me, confined at Mazas; subject to the same snares, plunging inexorably toward an identical fate. In those moments I would sit apart on the plank bed, motionless, my eyes cast down. And here I owe my guards a word of thanks — somewhat belated, it is true — who, so as not to disturb my meditations, paced on tiptoe.

Another of my tormenting preoccupations arose from the certainty that my enforced inaction must be matched by feverish activity on the part of the Police. I endeavoured to put myself, so to speak, in the place of the Authorities, and to school myself in my own defence. But every effort mocked every faculty I possessed, and the subtle conjectures and elaborate reconstructions ended in this: nervous irritation and exhaustion. There remained, then, nothing but to resort to the great remedy of youth: either I slept, in whatever fashion I might, or I abandoned the effort altogether.

The first certain sign of the inextricable net that was closing about me came when, conducted under heavy guard to the Chancellor's office, I found placed before my eyes, first a certified copy, and then the original itself, of a letter I had written from London to Felice Orsini. In it I informed him how a certain Carlotti, a man from Brescia, had, greatly to my surprise, made mention to me of our conspiracy. I had good reason to suspect this person of being a spy in the service of the French Government. He ought to be left well alone. If he already knew too much — one must either be rid of him by some means, or abandon the enterprise altogether.

Felice Orsini, more ardent patriot than prudent conspirator, had preserved among his papers so compromising a document, and the English Police, urged on from Paris, had carried out a search of his lodgings and taken possession of it.

My close connexion with the conspirators, my knowledge of their plots, appeared only too firmly established. Another good step further down the fatal slope! And when I attempted, pro forma, to deny the undeniable, the examining magistrate, by way of reply, smiled in a mocking fashion and sent me back to the darkness, launching after me, by way of a parting valediction, the shattering phrase: "Oh, we have far stronger evidence than this! and your accomplices have fully corroborated it!"

The following day I was transferred from Mazas, in a closed carriage, to the Conciergerie. I was assigned cell No. 1, and it was not long before I learned that Pieri was confined in No. 2, Gomez in No. 3, and Orsini in No. 4.

My new lodgings were approximately ten feet by fifteen in extent. The light entered timidly through a small window set with thick iron bars, situated near the ceiling. In the door, at a man's height, was a circular aperture — a five-franc piece would barely have passed through it. It permitted the warders to keep watch over my every movement; it permitted me to observe, within certain limits, what passed in the corridor. On the walls, the hands of prisoners who had preceded me had doubtless engraved verses and maxims, scraped thereafter inexorably away by the warders' knives.

What fragments of human hearts, and what reflections of souls refined by suffering, withdrawn from my meditation by the petty observance of duty! The story of one such soul I had known in all its particulars since my childhood. It was from my very cell that Marshal Ney had walked forth to have his patriot breast shattered by Bourbon lead!

At the Conciergerie I too had two guards. Of them I must say something. The more agreeable was a former petty naval officer. The State, having wrung his youth from him, had rescued him from penury by entrusting him with duties of dubious character that clearly revolted his disposition. A large family, poor and without connexions, kept him in thrall to his own fate; but his resignation was only apparent, and I was not long in perceiving that his secret rancour, finding no outlet, was slowly accumulating, drop by drop, within his heart. Nature had denied him the resolution and the impulse that drive men to virile acts; unless, indeed, a foolish discipline too long endured had severed every thread of his will. But he could still admire in others the virtues that were not in him, and his instincts betrayed themselves, beneath the surface and perhaps without his own knowledge, as rebellious. The years have erased his surname from my memory. His Christian name was, if I am not mistaken, one very common among the people of Paris — a mythological name: Saturno!

The other guard was a former soldier of the marine infantry. A rather stout man of few words, he would sit for hours with his hands upon his belly, fingers interlaced, placidly revolving his two thumbs one about the other, now in one direction, now in the reverse. He was fond of dozing.

At the Conciergerie I received my first intimation of what was passing in the world outside. My two guards kept their lips sealed on this subject. But other persons employed in the service of the prison attended to my thirst by the simplest of expedients: planting themselves in front of my cell and conversing with one another in loud voices.

In this manner I came to learn how public opinion, which had at first shown itself hostile to us, had rapidly shifted in our favour. The substance of entire newspaper articles drifted through the small circular aperture to my ear. Among them, those translated from the English press struck me as particularly explicit and almost laudatory.

But why did the staff of the Conciergerie take such pains? I believed I divined the reason on the day I heard a warder declare that "in this affair" — our affair — "mysterious and powerful influences were at work." He had added: "It is not difficult to foresee that not one of the four prisoners will be executed." I inferred from this that he who had spoken thus was not altogether unconnected with the direct inspiration of the mysterious influences to which he had permitted himself to allude.

And now we approach the precipice! From the far end of the corridor a voice cried: "Prisoner of cell No. 4!" It was Felice Orsini's cell. I ran to press my eye against the circular aperture. Let it now be seen how capable a thing it is of gnawing away a man's very existence — even in the most intrepid — to be tormented by the ruin of a great enterprise and by the consciousness of one's own responsibility! Felice Orsini passed rapidly across the narrow field of my vision; but that was enough for me to discern, upon that sculptural head, the traces of the storm that must have been raging within. His hair had turned almost entirely white.

Half an hour of sad reflection elapsed, and I heard Orsini's step returning. At once, from the far end of the corridor, the same voice: "Prisoner of cell No. 1!"

I was conducted into a long and narrow room. The single window that lit it was directly opposite me. At a writing-desk beside the window sat the usual examining magistrate and another individual dressed in black — the Imperial Prosecutor. But my attention was violently drawn to a third figure, seated a little way from the desk, to the right of the magistrate. Her profile stood out sharply against the light of the window. I knew that profile — it was of a woman small, slight, dark, not beautiful in the classical sense, but very attractive; and at the sight of her there awoke in my memory the impression of her persistent smile. It was Miss Elisa Cheney, the friend of Felice Orsini.

I dissembled the agitation that seized me so intensely as to prevent my distinguishing the other persons around me, and barely noticed a second lady who, seated at a table near the entrance, rested her arm upon it and upon that arm buried her face.

The examination proceeded with a swiftness that struck me as lightning-like. The Magistrate addressed himself first to the English young woman, speaking to her in her own language.

— Is this the man who brought the letter found upon your mantelpiece, and to whom you handed a travelling bag belonging to Signor Felice Orsini?

— It is he.

— Signor Count, what have you to say to that?

Genteel people, seated at table in a warm room with their feet upon soft carpets, may perhaps be unable to forgive me my ungallantry. But the question was one of saving my head from the guillotine and preserving it for better employment. I replied: "How can that woman claim to know me? She may have caught a glimpse of me some night, in passing, as she was walking the pavements of Haymarket. The testimony of a woman of doubtful character amounts to nothing."

The young woman turned pale and, in a voice broken by anger: "Let the travelling bag be examined. On the leather, at the bottom — in indelible characters—"

She could not continue. And the magistrate: "Compose yourself, compose yourself, Miss!"

"— in indelible characters, the name of Felice Orsini is written!"

At a sign from the magistrate, the Chancellor flashed before my eyes, produced I know not whence, the travelling bag with the embroidered tiger. Time and dust had blackened the bottom. But it was immediately washed, and the indelible characters appeared. Mere participation in a conspiracy that had led to the shedding of blood was sufficient — those characters were my death sentence.

But I did not concede defeat, and attempted a desperate move. I had clearly understood on the spot that Miss Cheney was acting neither from venality nor from hatred. The spirit that animated her must be love. "The Police, past masters in the art of turning passions to account," I thought, "must have promised her the salvation of the man she adored, in exchange for a complete revelation." I learned later that I had guessed the truth. To the detective Sanders, who was urging her to disclose all she knew, she had replied: "Everything, everything, to save that man!" And she had allowed herself to be brought to Paris.

Be that as it may, impelled by my own conviction, I raised my voice as I had never raised it before my judge, and turning to my accuser: "You are attempting to save Felice Orsini, and to that end you are betraying the innocent!"

What I had not expected then occurred. My voice, vibrating through the room, had entered a heart, had shaken a creature, had lifted a head bowed upon its arm in painful languor.

I found myself clasped by two trembling arms, and my muscles were forced to make an effort to support the weight of a being who surrendered herself to me. And I, who still did not comprehend, felt something like a wave of rose and blue penetrate to my depths and temper my storm; whilst a cloud of hair, fair and light, caressed my face and circumscribed it, as though to hide it from profane eyes. A sonorous, long kiss —

For a moment, the magistrate, the Imperial Prosecutor, the room, the conspiracy, France, the whole world ceased to exist. I was in the arms of my wife.

They parted us. The magistrate was the first to break the silence. How shrill his voice seemed to me! He said: "Is the Count di Rudio your husband?" And she, with honest pride: "I should not be here if he were not!"

We were then permitted ten minutes for a private interview. But when we drew aside, Sanders, the head of the Scotland Yard detectives, who had accompanied my poor Elisa to Paris, followed us and planted himself at our side. I foiled him by opening our conversation in Italian.

— The child?

— A darling! Mamma is taking care of him. Carlo, Carlo, what can I do for you?

And Sanders, brusquely: "Speak in English!"

— Go to the devil!

He lodged a protest with the magistrate, who, thinking perhaps that speaking the language of Shakespeare presented difficulties for me, gave orders that a Corsican interpreter should be present at our conversation. Sanders withdrew. And I then resumed speaking — but in English.

"Elisa, this is no time for illusions. All is lost, and I shall be brought to the scaffold. What can you do? Nothing, but bear your fate with fortitude. Unless—"

Here, strong protests from the Corsican, who demanded that we speak Italian. And thereupon the magistrate gave orders that both interpreters should stand at our side. But before Sanders had returned, I had already expressed my meaning — "unless you can manage to engage the interest of some English personage of great influence in our case. In that event, perhaps—"

At this point, the arrival of the second intruder exasperated me, and my overwrought emotions could no longer be contained. Which was the stronger impulse? Whether it was horror at the prolonged dissimulation and the need to vindicate, in that supreme moment, my own share in events; or whether it was the presence of Elisa that rekindled my pride — the fact is that, raising my voice once more, I flung into the astonished face of the magistrate these words: "Yes, I too attempted to destroy the assassin of my Fatherland and of the French People. I have but one regret: that I did not know how to strike. The second bomb was thrown by me!"

The magistrate, pale and grave, made as if to speak. But I forestalled him: "Do not trouble me with needless questions. What I have said is sufficient for your Justice!"

I saw the gesture slicing the air that dismissed me back to prison, and I turned to my wife to embrace her once more. Alas! the rose had vanished; the blue eyes had lost their brightness. Elisa, who had fainted, lay abandoned in a chair, her arms hanging loose, her head thrown back against the back of it. My kiss — the last, perhaps — I pressed upon those lips grown pale and insensible, yet still trembling.

Then I hurried away, so swiftly that the guards who came after me seemed almost to be giving chase!
