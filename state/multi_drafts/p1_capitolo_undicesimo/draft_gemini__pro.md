IN APRIL of 1852 I was once again in Genova and working for a certain Ferrari, a merchant tailor, when I was informed that don Bastiano Barozzi, parish priest of San Pietro in Campo, was living as a refugee in Torino. Barozzi, during the siege of Venezia, was chaplain of a Legion composed almost exclusively of students from Padova. He was one of those priests in whom the cassock did not suffocate the love of the Fatherland and who, to passive obedience, preferred to follow the impulses of their own conscience. Italy boasts a good number of them, and the Church of Rome could have drawn great prestige from them if, to please the powerful, it had not preferred to confess them and even lend a hand in their torment.

Extremely well-versed in ancient history, don Bastiano judged men through the prism of the Roman world; to the fine level of Attilio Regolo, of the Scipios, of the Gracchi he would have wished to raise our entire race. During my school holidays, while walking along the Ardo and without seeming to, he would steep my mind in heroic episodes. I believe that I owe to him, in no small part, my cult of Liberty.

The priests of murky waters, who did not dare to wage open war against him for his patriotic principles, dragged the Decretals into it and took issue with his moustache and his goatee, which smacked of the modernist. He stubbornly refused to have them shaved and lost his Mass for it. In his straitened circumstances, he was aided by my father, and that was enough for don Bastiano to hold for him a gratitude that bordered on veneration.

To such a man I decided to turn, in the hope of re-entering, with his help, the vortex of action, and thinking that two words spoken face to face are worth more than four long letters, I set off for Torino… on foot, just so as not to lose the habit.

My first care, when I arrived, was to make my way to the Caffè Nazionale, a favourite haunt of political refugees. Except that, before arriving there, I spied, planted before the playbill of a theatre, a gentleman with his face so close to the print that he seemed to be reading it with the tip of his nose. The extreme myopia of don Bastiano Barozzi was known to me, and that clue was enough to reveal him to me.

Great was his astonishment at finding me, immeasurable his joy. He could not contain it and, right then and there, without any regard for the passers-by, imprinted two sounding kisses upon my cheeks: "Here... this on my account; this on your father's account!" He then asked me what good wind had brought me to Torino. And I:

"Don Bastiano, you know that the di Rudios are not cowards. What is being contemplated, what is being prepared? I have come so as not to grow rusty, and I am placing my reliance upon you."

He lowered his eyes and did not answer. But, after a brief silence, as if concluding his thoughts: "Fortunato Calvi is here. In a few leaps, we can be at his house. Come!"

Fortunato Calvi, the glorious organiser of the Cacciatori delle Alpi, my Colonel, my family's intimate friend, received me with folded arms, without moving a step, with an almost disdainful expression. The flood of affection that was about to burst forth remained painfully compressed in my breast. I did not even dare to provoke an explanation. But the explanation came of its own accord.

Calvi had been informed by a person "worthy of belief" that I, having left Venezia on his orders, instead of proceeding to Chioggia, had gone over, with arms and baggage, to the Austrian. He even cited the name of one who had sworn to have seen me, behind the trenches, firing upon my own countrymen.

Calumny!.. what man has not been, to some extent, its victim? Little by little, the breast hardens to its blows so that, in the long run, they no longer manage to stir up any storm in me. I know bold men who, by their demeanour, almost provoke it on purpose, for the acrid pleasure of showing themselves superior. But that was the first time I had found myself face to face with it, and its bite was extremely cruel to me.

Nevertheless, I found it easy to demonstrate its absurdity.

Fortunato Calvi, who had no less a desire to find me innocent than I had to prove it to him, smilingly extended his right hand, which I clasped with feverish emotion. While don Bastiano, a mute witness to that scene and an exceptionally sensitive man, turned on his heels to hide a tear.

The consequences of that conversation were, as far as I am concerned, of no small moment. Giuseppe Mazzini, Luigi Kossuth and Ledru Rollin had openly founded, in London, a European Revolutionary Committee, to which countless branches were soon attached. Every People that, having vanquished that nefarious apathy, the cryptogam of souls, understood how regeneration springs from agitation and sacrifice, organised its own. Italy was a beehive of them and, by force of circumstances, the Committee of Torino held predominance over all our others. To it especially fell the task of inciting the spirit of rebellion that snaked through the Peninsula and of coordinating its movements. Its head, in 1852, was Count Grillanzoni; but the command of eventual military operations fell to Fortunato Calvi.

I was tasked by Calvi and Count Grillanzoni with traversing, as a special envoy, the principal cities of Lombardo-Veneto; at first to distribute subversive manifestos and bonds of the Mazzinian national loan; then, on a subsequent journey, to inspect the various garrisons and to report precise data on their strength, their nationality, and their morale.

The system adopted was simple. A passport was procured for me in the name of Carlo Moretti, a silk merchant. It was enough for me to write: In such-and-such a place I have sold two, three, four bales of white, or green, or yellow silk. The number of bales corresponded to that of the Regiments; the colour indicated their uniform and branch of service. A studied allusion to the special quality of the merchandise, or to the nature of the client, was enough to make understood whatever other circumstances I deemed it necessary to point out.

I recall the admonition Calvi gave me on the very day of my departure. I was hiding in the stem of my pipe the list of the various Sub-Committees, and I smiled. Calvi suspected that this smile came from light-mindedness and said sternly: "Do not delude yourself, young man! A slight imprudence, a simple fortuitous incident, and you would be hanged!" I did not fall upon the gallows, but I skirted it more than once.

My first stop was at Stradella, where I shook hands with Nino Bixio (who had survived the wound sustained at Villa Corsini) and with that Winkler, formerly head of a Hungarian legion in Venezia, who was, a few years later, to end his heroic existence fighting on the banks of the Volturno. The contact with men of such temper was a cause of pleasant excitement for me. But having crossed, with the help of a smuggler, the blue Ticino and barely advanced into the dominions of Austria, it seemed as if an iron hand were closing around my throat. The oppressor had, after 1849, sharpened its torpid wits to transform those lands into a pit of hell.

As a general rule, Austria considered the Provinces broken by its arms, and the others reconquered by the allied arms of Russia, as a den of brigands. To vent a barbaric hatred upon them, to gratify the most atrocious vengeances seemed, in courtly circles, high political wisdom. Convinced that he was carrying out a mission entrusted to him by God, Francesco Giuseppe did not even consider himself bound by his own word and by the stipulated capitulations. General Klapka, the heroic defender of Komorn, had obtained excellent terms of surrender for the garrison. They were all violated. A code of honour exists even among thieves; but on the pretext of *raison d'État*, none existed for Francesco Giuseppe.

Of the thirty-four Hungarian generals, elect intellects, heroic fibres, who had repeatedly forced the imperial soldiers to show their backs, three were condemned to perpetual imprisonment, twelve Christianly shot, or hanged. And the slaughter would have been greater, had the others not saved themselves through flight and exile. For several months a swarm of executioners, dressed, as if in greater mockery, in the colour of hope, busied themselves in Hungary piling up the corpses of patriots. All this now seems erased from the memory of the men who shout in the streets their *evvivas* to the reprehensible old man. And yet one never ceases to curse the generous ones who, taking into their own souls the spasms of the victims, risk their lives to administer to despots a pinch of their own medicine.

In Italy, to tell the truth, Francesco Giuseppe had his claws blunted by Vittorio Emanuele II who, having ascended the throne amid the glacial diffidence of his subjects, knew how to bend to the terrible impending necessities without compromising the future. He would not sign the peace treaty with Austria unless it assumed the obligation to grant full amnesty to the one hundred thousand Italians who, from Lombardo-Veneto, had taken refuge in Piemonte, whether to make war on it, or to escape its cruelties.

Vittorio Emanuele II, though defeated, commanded a not inconsiderable force, and the Emperor kept the pact. Except that his "pardon" was tacitly conditioned on complete submission to the Austrian yoke. Were pretexts lacking? Seizures, arbitrary impositions, chains and the gallows were not sparingly doled out by the paternal solicitude of Francesco Giuseppe, and the rod was applied to the backs even of those, men or women, who, without rebelling, manifested virile sentiments.

Poor young Italian girls, with souls open to all that is beautiful and gentle; and you mothers, aristocratic or common, who passed, with commanded slowness, between two files of henchmen, bringing down the rod with all their might upon your bare limbs, lend me your voices, that I might render a worthy apotheosis to the apostolic beast!

And I deliberately refer to Francesco Giuseppe and not to Austria; because when the sum of power is concentrated in one man, on his head, as a debt of justice, must likewise be concentrated the sum of hatred and infamy.

Mixed among the Italians, I found, in my peregrinations, several Hungarian officers, degraded because they had loved their Fatherland and defended it. Vexed with ferocious pertinacity, they nonetheless had the courage to rebel against the odious commands issued to the detriment of a People made brothers by misfortune. And there were those among them who, despairing of better times, put an end, by suicide, to a life that had become unbearable.

Fortunately, as if to disappoint the transgressors, Nature has so arranged her affairs that death itself is the great sower of life, and tyranny itself the great fertiliser of all liberty!

Despite the defeats, the blood drawn from it, and the persecutions, life was pulsating in our Fatherland, subterranean, but intense. Italians who were organising, who were conspiring, or who revealed a feverish impatience to do so, I found them in abundance in every centre. The logical deduction drawn from the horrible situation was that it was necessary to begin anew, to do better, and to win at all costs.
