CHAPTER ELEVEN

IN APRIL of 1852 I was once again in Genoa, working for a certain Ferrari, a merchant tailor, when I was informed that Don Bastiano Barozzi, parish priest of San Pietro in Campo, was living as a refugee in Turin. Barozzi, during the siege of Venice, had served as chaplain to a Legion composed almost exclusively of students from Padua. He was one of those priests in whom the cassock did not stifle the love of the Fatherland, and who, rather than passive obedience, preferred to follow the impulses of their own conscience. Italy can boast a good number of such men, and the Church of Rome might have drawn great prestige from them, had she not preferred, in order to please the overbearing, to hear their confessions and even lend a hand to their punishment.

Deeply versed in ancient history, Don Bastiano judged men through the prism of the Roman world; to the level of Attilio Regolo, of the Scipios, of the Gracchi he would have wished to raise our entire race. During my school holidays he would walk with me along the Ardo, and without seeming to make anything of it, he would fill my mind with heroic episodes. I believe I owe him, in no small part, my devotion to Liberty.

The priests of muddy water, who dared not openly make war upon him for his patriotic principles, dragged in the Decretals and took exception to his moustache and pointed beard, which smacked of the modernist. He stubbornly refused to have them shaved off, and for it he lost the right to say Mass. In his straitened circumstances, he was assisted by my father, and that alone had been sufficient for Don Bastiano to cherish towards him a gratitude that verged upon veneration.

It was to this man that I resolved to turn, in the hope of re-entering, with his help, the vortex of action; and reckoning that two words spoken face to face are worth more than four long letters, I set off for Turin — on foot, so as not to lose the habit.

My first care, upon arriving there, was to make my way to the Caffè Nazionale, the favoured gathering-place of political refugees. Before I reached it, however, I caught sight of a gentleman standing planted before the playbill of a theatre, his face so close to the printed page that he seemed to be reading it with the tip of his nose. Don Bastiano Barozzi's extreme short-sightedness was well known to me, and that single sign was enough to reveal him.

Great was his astonishment at finding me again; boundless his joy. He could not contain it, and, there and then, without the least regard for the passers-by, he pressed two resounding kisses upon my cheeks: "There — this one is from me; this one is from your father!" He then asked me what lucky wind had brought me to Turin. And I replied:

"Don Bastiano, you know that the di Rudios are no idlers. What is being planned, what is being prepared? I have come so as not to grow rusty, and I am counting upon you."

He cast his eyes down and did not answer. But after a brief silence, as though concluding his own thoughts: "Fortunato Calvi is here. In four steps we can be at his house. Come!"

Fortunato Calvi, the glorious organiser of the Cacciatori delle Alpi, my Colonel, the intimate friend of my family, received me with his arms folded, without moving a step, with an expression that was almost one of disdain. The flood of feeling that was on the point of breaking forth remained painfully suppressed within my breast. I did not even dare to provoke an explanation. But the explanation came of its own accord.

Calvi had been informed by a person "worthy of trust" that I, having left Venice upon his orders, instead of making for Chioggia, had gone over, with arms and baggage, to the Austrians. He even cited the name of one who had sworn to have seen me, behind the trenches, firing upon my own countrymen.

Slander! — what man has not been, to one degree or another, its victim? Little by little, the breast hardens itself against its blows, so that in time they no longer avail to stir any storm within me. I have known bold men who, by their very bearing, seem almost to provoke it of set purpose, for the sharp pleasure of showing themselves superior to it. But that was the first time I had encountered it face to face, and its bite reached me with extreme cruelty.

Nevertheless, it proved easy to demonstrate the absurdity of the charge.

Fortunato Calvi, who had no less desire to find me innocent than I had to prove it to him, extended his right hand to me with a smile, and I grasped it with feverish warmth. Meanwhile Don Bastiano, silent witness to that scene and a man of exceptionally sensitive feeling, turned upon his heel to conceal a tear.

The consequences of that conversation were, as far as I was concerned, of no slight moment. Giuseppe Mazzini, Luigi Kossuth, and Ledru Rollin had openly founded in London a European Revolutionary Committee, to which innumerable branches were soon attached. Each People that, having overcome the fatal apathy — that cryptogam of the soul — understood how regeneration springs from action and sacrifice, organised its own branch. Italy was a hive of them, and by force of circumstance the Committee of Turin held predominance over all the others. Upon it fell, above all, the task of fanning the spirit of rebellion that was spreading through the Peninsula and of co-ordinating its movements. Its head in 1852 was Count Grillanzoni; but the command of any eventual military operations fell to Fortunato Calvi.

From Calvi and Count Grillanzoni I received the commission of travelling, as special envoy, through the principal cities of Lombardy-Venetia; first to distribute subversive pamphlets and bonds of the Mazzinian national loan; then, on a subsequent journey, to inspect the various garrisons and report precise particulars of their strength, their nationality, and their temper.

The system adopted was simple. It was decided to procure for me a passport in the name of Carlo Moretti, a merchant in silks. All I needed to write was: In such-and-such a town I have sold two, three, four bales of white silk, or green, or yellow. The number of bales corresponded to the number of Regiments; the colour indicated their uniform and arm of service. A studied allusion to the particular quality of the goods, or to the character of the customer, sufficed to convey whatever other circumstances I considered it necessary to bring to notice.

I recall the admonition given me by Calvi on the very day of my departure. I was concealing in the stem of my pipe the list of the various Sub-Committees, and I was smiling. Calvi suspected that smile to be the fruit of levity, and said sternly: "Do not deceive yourself, young man! A slight imprudence, a simple chance incident, and you would be hanged!" I did not fall into the gallows' hands, but I more than once passed within very close reach of them.

My first halt was at Stradella, where I shook hands with Nino Bixio — who had survived the wound received at Villa Corsini — and with that Winkler who had been, at Venice, the commander of a Hungarian legion, and who was to close, a few years later, his heroic existence fighting on the banks of the Volturno. Contact with men of such mettle was a source of pleasant excitement to me. But having crossed the blue Ticino with the help of a smuggler and ventured but a little way into the domains of Austria, I felt as though an iron hand had closed about my throat. The oppressor had, after 1849, sharpened his sluggish wits in order to transform those lands into a pit of hell.

As a general rule, Austria regarded the Provinces subdued by her own arms, and the others reconquered by the allied arms of Russia, as a den of brigands. To vent upon them a barbaric hatred, to indulge in the most atrocious reprisals — this appeared, in courtly spheres, the height of political wisdom. Persuaded that he was carrying out a mission entrusted to him by God, Francesco Giuseppe did not even consider himself bound by his own word or by the capitulations he had stipulated. General Klapka, the heroic defender of Komorn, had obtained excellent terms of surrender for his garrison. Every one of them was violated. A code of honour exists even among thieves; but under the pretext of *ragion di Stato* — reasons of state — none such existed for Francesco Giuseppe.

Of the thirty-four Hungarian generals — chosen minds, heroic fibres — who had repeatedly compelled the Imperial troops to show their backs, three were condemned to perpetual imprisonment, twelve were with Christian charity shot or hanged. And the carnage would have been greater, had the others not saved themselves by flight and exile. For several months a swarm of executioners, clad — as though in crowning mockery — in the colour of hope, busied themselves in Hungary heaping up the bodies of patriots. All this seems now to have been blotted from the memory of those who shout in the streets their cheers for the contemptible old man. Yet people never cease to heap curses upon the generous souls who, having taken into their own hearts the anguish of the victims, hazard their lives in order to administer to despots a measure of their own medicine.

In Italy, it is true, Francesco Giuseppe had his talons blunted by Vittorio Emanuele II, who, having ascended the throne amid the glacial distrust of his subjects, knew how to bow to the terrible necessities pressing upon him without compromising the future. He refused to sign the peace treaty with Austria unless she undertook the obligation of granting full amnesty to the hundred thousand Italians who, from Lombardy-Venetia, had taken refuge in Piedmont, whether to make war upon her or to escape from her persecutions.

Vittorio Emanuele II disposed, even in defeat, of a force not to be lightly reckoned with, and the Emperor kept the pact. Nevertheless, his "pardon" was tacitly conditional upon complete submission to the Austrian yoke. Pretexts were never lacking; sequestrations, arbitrary impositions, chains and the gallows were not doled out sparingly by the paternal solicitude of Francesco Giuseppe, and the rod was applied to the backs even of those men or women who, without rebelling, gave expression to manly sentiments.

Poor young Italian women, your souls open to every fair and gentle thing; and you mothers, aristocratic or of the people, who were made to pass, with commanded slowness, between two rows of hirelings bringing down their cudgels with full force upon your bare limbs — lend me, you, the voice sufficient for a worthy apostrophe to the apostolic beast!

And I name Francesco Giuseppe deliberately, and not Austria; because when the sum of power is concentrated in one man, upon his head must be concentrated, by equal right of justice, the sum of hatred and of infamy.

Mingled among the Italians, I encountered in my wanderings a good number of Hungarian officers who had been degraded because they had loved their Fatherland and defended it. Harassed with ferocious persistence, they had none the less the courage to rebel against the odious commands issued to the detriment of a People bound to them by common misfortune. And there were among them those who, despairing of better times, put an end by suicide to a life that had become unendurable.

Fortunately, as though to confound the oppressors, Nature has so disposed her affairs that death itself is the great sower of life, and tyranny itself the great fecundator of every liberty.

Despite the defeats, and the bloodletting, and the persecutions, life pulsed in our Fatherland — underground, but intense. Italians who were organising, conspiring, or who betrayed the feverish impatience to do so, I found in abundance in every centre. The logical conclusion drawn from the dreadful situation was that one must begin again from the beginning, do better, and conquer at any cost.
