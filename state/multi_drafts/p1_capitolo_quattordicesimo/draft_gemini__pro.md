THE EXCITEMENT of the moment did not prevent me from thinking upon the strangeness of the case. I, a patriot, and not of the lowliest (if the intensity of affections counts for something), was driven out, almost as an enemy, from the only free strip of Italian land, and yearned feverishly to cross the median line of the lake to return, as they say, to the shadow of the yellow and black... the object of our execration suddenly become the symbol of my salvation. I reached it and breathed more freely. But it was a temporary salvation, and destiny disposed that I should not be able to reach the Swiss border without grave hardship.

The weather, which had shown itself threatening since the morning, turned for the worse, and from the Alpi, towards which we had turned our prow, there proceeded not so much the wind as the tempest. The waters, which foresaw it, acquired a formidable undulation, and our fragile vessel bobbed about madly. I soon noticed that we were no longer advancing and, seizing the reserve oars, I made a third in that desperate struggle between human will and the tyranny of the storm. But deeming it imprudent to turn my back on the two boatmen, I had to resign myself to keeping my face turned towards the Alpi. And then a frozen sleet came hurtling down slantwise, or rather a whirlwind of icy needles that grazed the skin, that tormented my eyes, that took my breath away. And the waves, foaming at their crests, curled by the wind like wood-shavings, threatened to capsize our little boat. No doubt! The elements had declared for Austria and, more than once, I thought I had escaped so many dangers only to end miserably in that pond of shads.

Stiffness of the limbs set in, and then night fell. It was a necessity, it was our salvation to give up on Locarno, and make for the nearest republican land, Brissago. We arrived there with moustaches encrusted with ice and our bones aching. Nor was I given the chance to show my saviours all my gratitude; because one of them, the same one to whom I had counted out the established price and a relatively generous tip, brought his hand down harshly upon my shoulder and said to me: "Whatever happens, we served you because that two-mouthed implement of yours admitted no reply. Go about your business; we shall mind our own. Good night and good fortune!"

Half an hour later I was between the sheets. Scarcely had I laid down, when Sander and Radonitz, the captain of the Arno and Fortunato Calvi, the lake and all of Italy vanished into the darkness.

The following morning, I rediscovered my worries upon the pillow. I persuaded myself after long reflection that my failure was entirely personal. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, having remained free, had surely known how to organize everything, even without my assistance. It was the 4th of February... therefore, the useful time had not yet passed... an initiative delayed, not lost.

I decided to make my way on foot as far as Locarno and, without further ado, I set off. The wind had ceased, but the snow was falling in large flakes... the mountain and the valley were white with it. In that fresh snow, my legs sank up to the calf, and a stubborn clog formed under my shoes that treacherously robbed me of my road and my energy. But when I arrived before the Olivieri house and saw light filtering through the grilles, and deduced from it that they were awake, that they were at work, my fatigue vanished as if by magic.

The prearranged signal opened the door to me. I was led into a room where, seated at a small table, their faces illuminated by an oil lamp, were Sanders and Radonitz. Standing, with arms crossed, I found a third person there: Pietro Fortunato Calvi.

Greetings and felicitations! I cut them short to ask for news of the situation: "What have you arranged? What hopes are left to us?"

Sanders and Radonitz shrugged their shoulders. They were waiting for the Police to remove their ambushes and fall back asleep. My mishap, recounted by Calvi, had not only confirmed them in their plan to beat about the bush, but had also persuaded them that the Austro-Hungarian authorities, having grown suspicious, had stirred up those of the Republic. "We are no longer safe here!" said Radonitz. "By the way, how are you for funds?"

And, without awaiting my reply, he reached his hand towards some rolls of money that, in my initial preoccupation, I had not even noticed.

It was as if a veil had fallen from my eyes. I thought, "Late... but in time!" And turning to Radonitz, who had unrolled the money: "Since you consider our attempt to have failed, return the sum that I brought you. It was intended for the purchase of arms and not for your benefit. It was paid out to me in the name of Mazzini; to Mazzini I shall deliver it."

The expression Radonitz assumed at that moment is among my most comical memories. He became haughty again: "I issued you a proper receipt; your own responsibility is covered. Do not concern yourself with what does not regard you."

With hardship and with sweat, the men who groaned beneath the lash had scraped together that gold... bread taken from their own mouths, from the mouths of their children. I insisted. And Radonitz, pale as wax: "Young man, you are a subordinate here; take care not to forget it."

Sanders, who had remained seated, with his elbows on the table and his face in his fists, thought it opportune to intervene:

"How do you expect us to get to safety, to preserve our work for better times, if we lack the means to do so?"

I replied: "I do not believe that the little chain and the trinkets that hang upon your chest, and your gold watch, are truly indispensable to you. You have sworn to give to the Fatherland your time and blood and soul and everything... would you now deny her four trinkets?"

I turned my gaze to the man who had not yet spoken, who had remained standing in the half-light, to Fortunato Calvi. He gave me, with his head, a brief nod of approval. Then with a leap I was at the door; I locked it and put the key in my pocket. Then, drawing that twin-barreled implement that had already rendered me such signal service at Intra, I leveled it, crying: "Enough talk! Either you return the money, or no one leaves this room!"

I put the gold back in my money belt and shook Fortunato Calvi's hand. To the others I gave a military salute... not without a touch of irony... Then I departed.
