CHAPTER THIRTEEN

I KNOW of no Italian insurrectionary movement that brought Giuseppe Mazzini so many slanders, so much bitterness, and so many defections as that of 1853.

The so-called practical men — possessed of that short-sighted positivism which consists in letting the world run undisturbed, even to its own ruin — maintained that a people recently bled dry and broken could not respond with spirit to a call to arms, nor willingly submit to fresh sacrifices. And they pronounced, with perfect gravity, that revolutions come about when they ripen of their own accord, not when one wills them to.

A singular maxim which, carried to its conclusion, would amount to this: "For sons ill-disposed to win their own independence, to reclaim their own rights, nothing is better than to recline in an armchair, leaving to the Sun the care of ripening events, as it ripens gourds."

The movements organised by Giuseppe Mazzini were never true revolution, nor outright war; but they undoubtedly prepared the way for both, and rendered both necessary.

Moreover, the events of February 1853 were not set in motion by the great agitator himself, but by that very People who, if one were to credit the serious-minded men, would have had no thought beyond making themselves comfortable amid accomplished facts and their own misery.

And Mazzini said as much, and wrote it, and proved it. But even today there is no shortage of supermen, the lustre and ornament of our Italy, who, being unable to refute the declarations of that great man who never lied, leap nimbly over them with both feet and continue to extol the portents of the armchair, persisting in the old slanders.

The *Fratellanza* — a secret society bent on insurrection — had already been strongly organised in Milan, three years after every hope for Italy seemed to have withered. Composed of some thousands of workmen, it was divided into various sections distinguished by the letters of the alphabet. Its existence was entirely unknown to the Austrian Police, to Mazzini, to the intellectuals, and to the aristocratic class, which nevertheless shared with it a common hatred of the foreign yoke.

Its principal organiser, the dyer Assi, entered into communication with Mazzini when he judged that the *Fratellanza* had sufficient strength to act. Assi requested arms, money, and leaders capable of bringing a plan of attack to a successful conclusion.

Mazzini received the news as proof that the propagation of his ideas had sown good seed even among the popular multitude; but he did not place full faith in Assi's assurances regarding the numerical strength and seriousness of purpose of the new organisation, until Major Eugenio Brizzi of Jesi and other informants, whom he had secretly dispatched to Milan, confirmed them in every particular.

Nevertheless, he counselled delay, when in December of 1852 Austria, giving fresh vent to its own ferocity, crowned the ramparts of Belfiore with gibbets, and having chosen a victim from every class of citizens, hanged them there.

Confronted with this savage provocation, the workmen of the *Fratellanza* felt it a crime to deliberate further. They notified Mazzini that they had resolved to act, even without his assistance and without his consent. Mazzini's inescapable duty became that of supporting the inevitable, of striving to ensure that the generous initiative, should it proceed, did not remain isolated and barren.

In the last days of December 1852, acting on a commission received directly from Count Grillanzoni, I departed for Milan, and on the appointed day, after a brief wait beneath the arcade of the Piazza Mercanti, I delivered a packet of instructions to a man who, emerging from the shadows, had given me the agreed signal.

The final plan thus reached the hands of Major Brizzi himself, charged with overseeing its execution. Its principal lines had been drawn by Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Its fundamental premise was that arms must be seized by surprise from the enemy, first disorganised by the killing of its officers.

By a provision of military regulation, these officers slept, as a rule, not in barracks but in private lodgings. Their addresses had been noted down, and equally well known were the haunts where they were accustomed to gather.

The Austrian military establishment was, among other positions, quartered within the walls of Milan at the Palace of the General Command, at the Palace of the Grand Guard, and at the Castello.

The first was guarded — or rather watched over — by a picket of twenty-five soldiers. Their arms stood aligned in the rack beneath the entrance archway, before which a vigilant sentinel paced without cease. The remaining soldiers whiled away the hours stretching their limbs in the courtyard or smoking in the barracks rooms. At five in the afternoon, the Governor, the Generals, and the General Staff dined in that palace.

To render the sentinel powerless by a sudden stroke was a surprise attack of no great difficulty.

Something similar, but requiring more formidable means, was to be attempted at the Grand Guard, which housed some hundred men-at-arms; and especially at the Castello, which contained in its armoury twelve thousand rifles. The two armourers who inspected them daily were of our number. They undertook to leave the doors ajar, so that forcing them should not rob us of precious time.

Within the premises of the Grand Guard, the insurgents were to entrench themselves and establish their headquarters, so as to give the revolution a centre. And from that centre, clad in the uniforms stripped from the dead and the prisoners, small patrols were to fan out, beating the drum, inducing officers to emerge from their dwellings. On the threshold they would find those appointed to seal their lips — and for ever.

The sixth of February was chosen for the assault upon the enemy — a day of carnival revelry and of soldiers' pay. Their quarters would be left all but deserted. The surprise would thus stand a greater chance of success; and our men would have greater ease in rushing the cafés and taverns, to renew — for a more sublime purpose — the blood-baptism of the Vespers.

To smooth the path to victory, Giuseppe Mazzini had solicited and obtained from Kossuth, who was on the point of departing for the United States of America, a revolutionary proclamation addressed to his countrymen quartered chiefly in the barracks of San Francesco; and there were already assurances that at the first sign of serious insurrection, the ill-tamed sons of valiant Hungary would make common cause with their oppressed Italian brothers.

As the crowning work: the barricades! The contractor for the city's illumination had undertaken, in order to protect those who raised them, to plunge Milan into darkness. And to defend them, Giuseppe Mazzini had caused to be smuggled into the city certain projectiles of new invention, to be hurled upon the assailants in the manner of grenades.

It should be added that the students of Pavia, secretly consulted, had pledged themselves to hasten to the aid of the capital; the agreed signal: a sheet of flame upon the spire of the Duomo. They were to be commanded by Acerbi — and they asked nothing more than arms. Mazzini had provided them by way of the Po.

Nor did the preparation of simultaneous movements beyond the walls end there. In Piedmont, the throng of political refugees watched and waited, impatient for action. It was necessary to open for them a broad and easy road by which they might descend in force upon the enemy. Giuseppe Mazzini entrusted this charge to Fortunato Calvi, who conceived the bold plan of seizing Lake Maggiore — a plan I shall explain presently.

And Aurelio Saffi, having knocked at the doors of the wealthier patriots — on which occasion Agostino Depretis, the future President of the Council, donated twenty-five thousand lire — then went, defying death, to Bologna, where he fomented with good hopes of success the insurrection of that city and of the Romagna.

In brief, Giuseppe Mazzini had secured the cooperation of those centres which might have interposed a barrier between the garrison of Milan and the various Austrian nuclei scattered throughout the Peninsula. Fortunato Calvi confided to me later, in Zürich, that he flattered himself he might outflank, in a fortnight or so, with the revolutionary forces, the Austrian army of Italy, and cut it off at the Alps from its base of reinforcement. It was enough, to give Lombardy heart and time to rise, that Milan should hold firm and fight in its streets for even a few days. And Italy would have won her independence by her own strength, as Greece had done, as the United States had done, avoiding the fatal train of consequences left by hybrid alliances, murky intrigues, and the compromises that have dimmed her greatness, and whose effects, alas, will be felt for many years to come.

But here I do not argue — I narrate.

The workmen of the *Fratellanza*, excepting the leaders, were ignorant of the particulars of the insurrectionary plan; but they knew that on the sixth of February the action would begin. They had unearthed some hundred pistols and, on Mazzini's advice, set themselves to fashioning daggers and knives. Even the nails of their canal barges were wrenched out and fixed upon poles... All things serve the desperation of a People!

And I, seized by the general fever, was already weighing, so as to forestall them, all the sinister eventualities foreseeable in the assault upon the Grand Guard — to which Brizzi had appointed me — when I received from Count Grillanzoni the order to proceed immediately to Genoa and to await his instructions there.

These instructions were: to meet a certain affiliated member, to have counted out in my name, on behalf of Mazzini, four thousand lire in gold; to carry them to Sander and Radonitz, who were awaiting me at Locarno. To keep, on the journey, to the road along Lake Maggiore, and to watch whether anything suggested that Calvi's plans had been discovered, or that some circumstance requiring their modification had arisen. To meet, on my return from Locarno, with Calvi himself, who would be at Intra, and to report to him faithfully everything I had seen and heard.

Austria maintained upon the Verbano a flotilla of gunboats, which ordinarily lay at anchor off Luino. The surveillance of the shores and the passenger service were entrusted to the steamship *Radetzky*, which plied the lake daily and always carried aboard a company of soldiers. These, during the crossing, remained below decks, leaving their rifles aligned by the hatchway under the guard of a sentinel. With the sentinel disposed of, it seemed more than probable that one could seize the arms and lock the remaining soldiers in the hold. Sander and Radonitz, who had formerly served as officers in the Austrian navy before passing into the service of the Venetian Republic, would have taken command of the steamship and disposed of it as an Italian vessel.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi had arranged that the *Radetzky*, putting in at Luino late in the evening, should continue to fly the Austrian flag — I and a handful of our men on board, dressed in the hated uniform.

It would be necessary to approach as friends the gunboats unprepared for action; it would be necessary to seize them by surprise. Then, having towed them into open water, to turn their murderous mouths upon the small garrison. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, having organised a solid body of patriots, would have assaulted Luino from the landward side. With the Italian flag hoisted over that strategic point, and the lake in our hands, the munitions, the arms, and the armed men coming from Piedmont would have found a relatively easy road all the way beneath the walls of Milan.

When I arrived at Arona there remained some hours before the departure of the *Radetzky*. I spent them walking along the shore of the lake. I recall that the magnificence of the landscape, all green and bright despite the winter, made upon my spirit an impression of such sweetness that for a moment it nearly unmanned me. And I marvelled that men, being able to enjoy fraternally so much of paradise, should spoil it by grappling perpetually with one another, as though in their breasts nothing could take root but base and ferocious passions.

But no sooner had the bell of the *Radetzky* announced the imminent departure, than my rebellious spirit regained the upper hand. I thought: "Beautiful is our homeland. It is for that very reason they put the knife to our throats and attempt to make it their own. So much more holy, then, is the fury that drives us to defend it — and the fiercer that fury, the holier the cause. Let them recross the Alps and we shall be brothers again."

The steamship put out upon the lake. I lit my pipe, and walking with slow steps, now here, now there, endeavoured to assume the attitude of one who gazes in rapt contemplation at the landscape passing before him. But my mind was fixed upon my secret purpose. I saw the rifles aligned by the hatchway; I scrutinised — almost weighed — the sentinel who, with German rigidity, with the regularity of a pendulum, trod the planking back and forth; I heard the exotic voices rising from deep within the vessel and understood them... they spoke, with lascivious frankness, of hips and haunches, punctuating their talk with coarse laughter. Nor throughout the entire voyage did the pomaded whiskers of a single *kaiserlich* emerge from that dark den. Faithful to their orders, those geometric heads!

The *coup de main* seemed to me so certain of success that more than once I regretted being alone and unprepared to attempt it.

But at Locarno I found spirits less willing than my own. Sander and Radonitz received me, not without a certain stiffness, at the house of one Olivieri, a dealer in sundry goods and also in arms. They took and carefully counted the gold which I drew from my money-belt. But when it came to settling the preliminaries of the action, the ifs and buts grew as thick and solid as a wall.

A few days earlier — I was told — two Hungarians with whom secret negotiations had been under way had been thrown into prison; two of our men. "The Authorities held them in complete isolation and nothing had leaked out regarding the grounds of their arrest. Why such precautions, such mystery? There was cause for misgiving at far less."

Radonitz was firmly persuaded that the Police had some inkling of what was being plotted. To venture something new would be to plunge headlong into the net. He urged that courage should not be confused with rashness. The lives of so many patriots were at stake — lives which were sacred to him. What would be lost by waiting; by ascertaining, with due caution, the temper of an enemy so powerful and so cruel? Wisdom did not consist in haste, but in acting in such a manner that victory should be certain and as it were inevitable.

To all of this I replied that if the arrest of one brother were sufficient to turn Italians from their purpose, they might as well renounce action for ever. Since '49 there had not passed a day without the Austrian cudgel at work, without some chain being riveted anew. The Police could not suspect a movement as vast as ours without taking extraordinary precautions; and such precautions could not have escaped the notice of the brothers dispersed in every quarter. No alarm had come from any direction whatsoever. I myself had traversed the full length of the lake, aboard an Austrian vessel, with eyes alert, and had observed nothing — absolutely nothing — out of the ordinary. Moreover, the chances of discovery were in proportion to the time spent in organising the conspiracy and to the number of those taking part in it. All the greater, therefore, appeared the necessity for swift and almost lightning action. Fortunato Calvi must already have left Turin; he would be at Intra the following day. It was for him alone to decide upon the greater or lesser expediency of delay. Our inaction would have robbed him of the power of choice and would have amounted to a defection. In any case, I had come to Locarno not to debate, but to act. My orders were to report to Intra everything I had seen and heard. I would depart alone if need be, should it cost me my life.

Olivieri's wife was present while I spoke. She left the room without a word and returned shortly, carrying a packet of revolutionary manifestos and a bundle of daggers. With her own hands she helped me to conceal both upon my person, and with that all discussion was cut short. The following morning I embarked upon the *Arno*, a Piedmontese steamship, and descended the Verbano at speed. Sander and Radonitz had stayed ashore.

What a profound change in those few hours! The overcast sky heralded snow; the lake grey, almost ashen. From the Alps blew an icy wind that seemed to have swept away all festive life from the shores and the hillsides.

I found on the *Arno* muffled figures wearing the selfishly preoccupied air of men absorbed in their own affairs; common faces that betrayed the anxieties of commerce. My attention eventually came to settle upon the captain, a bony man with a phenomenally large head. He was deep in the reading of a newspaper and cast only the occasional glance at the course. Who was he? What sentiments animated him? Could the name and sufferings of the homeland move his heart?

I answered myself: yes — and committed a grave error. He had risen abruptly and, as though following the impulse of a passing thought, gone off with hurried steps elsewhere. He had tossed his newspaper onto a seat. I drew out a revolutionary proclamation, concealed it within the folds of that newspaper, and returned running to my place.

I had not long to wait. A face painfully startled, a knitting of brows, a waggling of the heavy head. He fixed me with a pair of eyes I have never forgotten. And perhaps they were set in the face of the most unblemished Aristide in all Piedmont; but to me they seemed steeped — almost drunk — with idiotic commiseration and bestial menace. I dissembled by whistling a little cabaletta of Verdi, against the wind roaring around me.

I am at Intra. Among the cluster of idlers gathered to watch the steamship arrive, I catch, as one might say in passing, a well-known signal. I followed at a distance the person who had made it and was led to a solitary villa. The unknown brother remained outside and I entered. I found myself face to face with Fortunato Calvi.

What a flood of feeling, and how many things I should have wished to say to him; how many others, I imagine, he would have wished to confide to me! But we had to content ourselves with clasping each other's right hand for a long moment. Just then, neither he nor I was able to speak a word. When I at last found my voice, I said: "Are the sentinels at their posts?"

Having received the assurance, I drew from my person the proclamations and every one of the daggers save one, and laid them on a table. I continued:

— All of them here and in the surrounding country. They await arms and the signal.

— I received word from Milan. (1) It was an imprudence; but his shrewdness served him well. He is now at Lugano. Let the arms do their duty — the brain is watchful and will do its own.

I then gave him an account of my journey, and did not conceal from him the strange hesitation of Sander and Radonitz, who had failed to appear at the rendezvous. I had not yet finished when a window opened and in the gap appeared the face of the same affiliated member who had guided me there. He said to me, in an agitated voice: "Run, run! The carabinieri are at the gate. They are looking for you!"

In a single bound I was in the garden, crossed it at a run, taking care that the view of the gentlemen at the gate should remain blocked by the house. I climbed a tree and scaled the boundary wall. Four strides and I was at the lakeside. On the shore, chained to a landing-stage, several small boats lay in a row. One only, propelled by two oarsmen, was gliding close inshore, as though seeking a place to ground. Before the oarsmen, stupefied, had time to open their mouths, I had taken the leap and was finding my balance in that little shell. "Twenty francs for you! Quickly, to Locarno!" They understood and bent to the oars with a will.

And there they were — followed by a swarm of children — two gendarmes arriving on the shore with wild gesticulations. One jumped onto the shingle and busied himself, but in vain, with the boats. They were, I believe, secured with padlocks. The other, flailing his arms like a Dutch windmill, shouted: "Stop, stop!" I judged it prudent not to entrust my safety to another's commiseration, and drawing my double-barrelled pistol: "Either Locarno or the next world!"

From the shore rang out laughter and applause. The two carabinieri scanned the surface of the lake — no hope of assistance from that quarter. They vented their vexation by shaking their fists at the horizon.

(1) — That Mazzini was in Milan in 1853 was never stated either by him or by anyone else, so far as I know. But the Count di Rudio insists that the assertion of Fortunato Calvi admits of no ambiguity. C. C.
