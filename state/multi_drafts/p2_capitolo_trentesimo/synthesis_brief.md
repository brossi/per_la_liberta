ANNO stesso del mio arrivo a Londra, la Polizia francese fece, per quanto mi riguarda, il suo ultimo tentativo di corruzione. Le mie finanze volgevano allora al peggio. Sfumate le cinque sterline dell'Allsop; i pochi risparmi effettuati nel mio giro di conferenze, sfumati! L'Elisa, in procinto di diventare madre una seconda volta, richiedeva di grandi cure. Fortunatamente l'aiuto che, per un senso d'orgoglio, più non sollecitavo dai miei connazionali, ebbi dal Le Houx, l'ex galeotto francese. Il quale, trovata occupazione in una segheria di marmi, aveva indotto il suo principale ad impiegarmi per levigar lastre. Ma la mia imperizia assottigliava la mercede. Fatto in simili momenti, il passo della Polizia francese non mancava di tattica. L'Ispettore che entrò nella mia modesta dimora, procedette all'attacco senza alcun giro di parole, a carte scoperte, con una ruvida franchezza degna di miglior causa. Egli insistette sul mio isolamento, sulla ingratitudine delle persone a cui "istigazione" avevo affrontato il patibolo, sull'indigenza rivelata dalla mia abitazione e dal mio aspetto. Valeva la pena di sacrificarmi per gente che, nella migliore delle ipotesi, se ne infischiava de' miei sacrifici? Egli comprendeva benissimo come potessi nutrire male prevenzioni contro il Governo di Napoleone III; ma quel Governo non ne nutriva punto a mio riguardo. Una sola parola, ed ogni mia miseria sarebbe finita e avrei potuto rimettere mia moglie nell'ambiente agiato che si meritava, che le spettava di diritto.

E diede maggior peso alle sue parole, cavando dalla tasca una manata di sterline, che si sarebbe — disse — periodicamente rinnovata.

Ma l'astuzia non aveva fatto entrare ne' suoi calcoli un coefficiente sottile e prepotente: l'onesta coscienza che snatura i patimenti affrontati per causa buona e li trasforma in profonde soddisfazioni; che snatura le soddisfazioni scaturite dalle opere malvagie e le trasforma in indicibili torture.

La mia Elisa? Avrebbe indubbiamente sdegnata l'agiatezza offertale a prezzo della mia onestà. Che diventava un pugno d'oro, di fronte all'amor suo? Fortunato l'uomo che può, nei momenti difficili, specchiarsi nell'occhio sereno di una donna degna di tal nome! Egli passa sul fango sociale come il Cristo della leggenda sull'acque, a piedi incontaminati ed asciutti!

Cessata la pressione importuna della Polizia, venne quella più nauseante del prete. Un giorno che ero affaccendato al mio scrittoio, me ne trovai uno tra i piedi, affabile, insinuante, maestro nel dissimulare lo stato dell'animo col sorriso... quel sorriso mi irritò sin dal suo primo apparire. Ond'io, troncandogli a mezzo le frasi dell'esordio che esprimevano il suo compiacimento nello stringere la mia personale conoscenza, gli domandai, secco secco, in che cosa potessi servirlo.

— I miei parrocchiani sono tutti un po' miei figli. Ho lasciato le novantanove pecorelle al sicuro sul monte...

Nuova interruzione da parte mia, per ben stabilire una necessaria premessa. Di tutti i quadrupedi a cui, con licenza non soverchiamente di buon genere, avrebbe potuto paragonarmi, la pecora mi riusciva il più detestabile.

— Carità — disse — non facciamo questione di semplici parole. Le ragioni che mi spinsero a venir qui, sono basate sul sentimento del dovere ed in nome del dovere posso ben chiedere qualche minuto d'attenzione, anche a costo di riuscire importuno.

Ad un mio cenno, sedette e protestando di voler subito entrare nel cuore dell'argomento, mi rammentò la mia qualità d'italiano, che egli riteneva inseparabile da quella di cattolico. Non per smania d'intromettersi ne' fatti altrui, ma pel coscienzioso disimpegno del proprio ministero, era venuto a domandarmi il permesso di battezzare mio figlio e lo sorreggeva la speranza che io non mi sarei rifiutato di rendere quel doveroso omaggio alla religione di mio padre.

— Mio padre non crede ai preti... non è perciò questa la chiave che possa serrarmi e disserrarmi il cuore. Ma quand'anche... se ai figli corresse l'obbligo di praticare la fede degli avi, noi getteremmo ancora, in pieno secolo decimonono, vittime umane nel ventre infocato di Moloc; noi adoreremmo ancora le cipolle!

Con quella risposta avevo offerto l'addentellato ad una discussione che era mia intenzione di evitare. Il prete colse l'opportunità al volo e, sempre sorridendo, mi fece osservare che gli antichi antenati credevano nell'errore e praticavano la menzogna. Allora ai figli correva l'obbligo di ricercare, sia pure brancolando, la verità. Ma dal momento che a noi la verità era stata rivelata da Tale che è l'essenza stessa del vero, perchè dipartirsene, perchè chiudere ostinatamente gli occhi alla luce? E se anche, per un orgoglio malinteso, volevo dare a testa bassa nel male, in forza di quale diritto negavo la redenzione ad una creatura innocente!

Ed io: Se mio figlio è innocente, perchè il vostro Dio vorrebbe tenerlo responsabile di colpe non sue?

— Questi sono misteri della nostra santa religione; né a mente umana è dato lo scrutarli...

— Neppure agli uomini prescelti da "santa" vocazione ed unti dal Signore?

— Sarebbe troppo presumere dalla povera creta!

— Dal che debbo arguire che Ella si fa banditore e maestro di cose che non conosce; che, per sua stessa confessione, non sarà mai in grado di conoscere. Che direbbe di me se pretendessi d'insegnare il greco, quando ne ignori persino l'alfabeto?

— La fede, signor conte, la fede!...

— Se è merito il credere l'assurdo, non vedo quale differenza potrebbe correre tra un vescovo ed il selvaggio che adora l'aerolita piovuto dal cielo.

E qui, per mia dabbenaggine, mi lasciai trascinare a decifrargli l'essenza delle leggende braminiche, la matrice feconda da cui scaturirono tante altre leggende religiose, quella cristiana compresa. Anzi, procedendo più addentro nella serie dei secoli, gli provai — facile assunto! — che tutte le religioni le quali annebbiano l'umano intelletto provengono, in ultima analisi, dalle nozioni astronomiche possedute dagli antichi e che sulla 'scienza del Sole' è basata appunto ogni allegoria sacra, ogni preteso sacro mistero. Egli veniva perciò, inconsciamente forse, ma indubbiamente, a propormi di piegare le ginocchia, di curvare l'animo dinanzi alla maestà di Apollo, di Bacco, di Mitra,... del Sole!

Mi lasciò dire, procurando di assumere, qua e là, l'espressione di un sapiente che ascolti, per benevole condiscendenza, le fiabe create dall'esuberante e viziata fantasia di un bimbo. Ma all'ultima frase, le sue labbra sottili acquistarono la durezza del sarcasmo e parve volessero spingersi sino a toccare, coi propri angoli, le orecchie.

Era quel sarcasmo sincero? Forse!... Nella Chiesa succede come in un esercito: lo Stato Maggiore conosce i piani reconditi; ma la moltitudine dei subordinati opera al buio.

Egli disse: Signor conte, mi permetta di usare, a difesa della verità, che non ne avrebbe del resto alcun bisogno, l'identica franchezza che Ella adopera nel difendere l'eresia. La fantasia umana si è sbizzarrita in ben altri voli che non sia codesto suo. Ma nel campo dei fatti positivi, la Chiesa Cattolica, Apostolica Romana ha diciannove secoli di bene che né Ella, né alcun altro spirito sfrenato, saprebbe distruggere.

Ed io di rimando: "Il bene esisteva in teoria ed in pratica, anche quando l'umanità non sognava neppure che, in una piccola e pittoresca penisola del selvaggio Occidente, sarebbe sorta una Roma e in Roma, dopo una lunga sequela di raggiri e di stupri, una coalizione cattolica di superstizioni e di cupidigia che avrebbe presa la morale per insegna o, meglio, a guisa di grimaldello. Ed io sostengo che si continuerà a praticare il bene anche quando della Roma dei Papi non resterà, pallido ricordo, altro che il nome!

"Individualmente, malgrado l'abito che porta, anche un sacerdote può praticare la virtù, perchè la bontà è in germe nell'umana natura e verrà giorno che, spezzati gli inciampi, finirà per trionfare sulla Terra. Ma il male che la Chiesa cattolica, considerata nella sua essenza esclusiva, ha operato e persiste ad operare, oh questo sì che non si eclissa con quattro frasi! Vuole proprio che glie lo rammenti, rievocando diciannove secoli di oscurantismo, di inganni, di prepotenze, di torture, di roghi, di lagrime e di sangue?"

Abbassò gli occhi e parve riflettere. Poi venne il colpo della coda velenosa! "Lasciamo per un momento da parte — disse — le opinioni che, tanto, poco o nulla possono contro il fatale andare delle cose... Non si potrebbe, con un po' di buona volontà, trovare un terreno sul quale due galantuomini possano intendersi, malgrado la disparità delle idee?"

Riabbassò gli occhi e, con voce sommessa: "Mi permetta, non come sacerdote, ma come uomo di cuore che potrebbe, per l'età esserle padre... Se Ella mi autorizzasse a battezzare suo figlio, io mi impegnerei a procurarle — parola d'onore! — un impiego lucroso e tale da rimetterla in piedi nel consorzio sociale. Io scorgo a me d'attorno segni di povertà anche troppo manifesti. Scusi!... da uomini seri, da coscienze integre, ma che sanno ponderare le esigenze della vita. Perchè sacrificare la realtà alle semplici astrazioni? Non le sembra che il massimo tra i nostri doveri sia appunto quello di provvedere decentemente ai nostri cari? E quale maggiore soddisfazione per un padre di famiglia?"

Era il capestro del bisogno gettato attorno al mio collo! Stato Maggiore o subordinati, l'indelebile carattere pretesco non si smentisce mai.

Mi alzai di scatto e, con una voce piena d'ira: "Il massimo fra i miei doveri è quello di lasciare a mio figlio un'eredità di esempi virili. Ad ogni modo io non son prete e non traffico sulle mie convinzioni!" E gli additai la porta.

Quell'anno stesso, dopo un'esistenza di brevi settimane cessava di vivere il mio secondogenito. Era la vigilia del Natale... l'anniversario del giorno in cui mi ero imbarcato per navigare a gonfie vele, verso il consorzio dei liberi... e non avevo in casa tanto che mi concedesse di disporre per la sua sepoltura. Osservavo con occhi aridi il suo visino bianco come il guanciale su cui posava e, tentando d'asciugare le lagrime d'Elisa, dicevo:

"O tosto o tardi, rotti gli inciampi, il bene trionferà sulla Terra. E sarà, in gran parte, merito di chi, conoscendo la verità, non si perita di praticarla. Ma ora... Taci, taci! Coscienza integra non aspetta compenso dagli uomini. E forse questo squallore... è l'unica, vera gloria nostra!"

CHAPTER THE THIRTIETH

IN that same year of my arrival in London, the French Police made, so far as I was concerned, its last attempt at corruption. My finances were then at their worst. The five pounds from Allsop had vanished; the small savings gathered in the course of my lecture tour, vanished likewise! Elisa, on the point of becoming a mother for the second time, required great care. Fortunately, the help which, out of a sense of pride, I no longer solicited from my fellow-countrymen, I received from Le Houx, the former French convict. Having found employment in a marble saw-mill, he had induced his employer to take me on for polishing slabs. But my inexperience wore thin my wages. The move made by the French Police at such a moment was not wanting in tactics. The Inspector who entered my modest dwelling launched his attack without any beating about the bush, cards on the table, with a rough frankness worthy of a better cause. He dwelt upon my isolation, upon the ingratitude of those persons at whose *istigazione* — instigation — I had faced the scaffold, upon the destitution revealed by my lodgings and my appearance. Was it worth sacrificing myself for people who, in the best of cases, cared not a jot for my sacrifices? He understood perfectly well how I might harbour ill feelings against the Government of Napoleon III; but that Government harboured none whatever towards me. One single word, and all my misery would be ended, and I should be able to restore my wife to the comfortable station she deserved, which was hers by right.

And he lent greater weight to his words by drawing from his pocket a handful of pounds sterling, which would — he said — be periodically renewed.

But cunning had failed to reckon into its calculations one subtle and imperious coefficient: the honest conscience that transforms the sufferings endured for a worthy cause and converts them into deep satisfactions; that transforms the satisfactions born of wicked deeds and converts them into unspeakable torments.

My Elisa? She would without question have scorned the comfort offered at the price of my honour. What was a fistful of gold, set against her love? Happy the man who, in difficult moments, may see himself mirrored in the serene eye of a woman worthy of that name! He passes over the social mire as the Christ of legend upon the waters, with feet uncontaminated and dry!

Once the importunate pressure of the Police had ceased, there came that yet more nauseating pressure of the priest. One day when I was busy at my writing-desk, I found one of them at my door — affable, insinuating, a master in concealing the state of his soul beneath a smile... that smile irritated me from its very first appearance. Whereupon I, cutting short the opening phrases in which he expressed his gratification at making my personal acquaintance, asked him point-blank in what way I might serve him.

— My parishioners are all something of my children. I have left the ninety-nine sheep safe upon the mountain...

Another interruption on my part, to establish firmly a necessary premise. Of all the four-footed creatures to which, with a comparison of no very distinguished kind, he might have seen fit to liken me, the sheep was the one I found most detestable.

— Come now, — he said — let us not quarrel over mere words. The reasons which moved me to come here are grounded in the sentiment of duty, and in the name of duty I may well ask for a few minutes' attention, even at the risk of proving importunate.

At a sign from me, he sat down, and protesting that he wished to come at once to the heart of the matter, he reminded me of my quality as an Italian, which he held to be inseparable from that of a Catholic. Not from any eagerness to meddle in the affairs of others, but for the conscientious discharge of his ministry, he had come to ask my permission to baptise my child, and he was sustained by the hope that I would not refuse to render that dutiful homage to the religion of my father.

— My father has no faith in priests... that is therefore not the key which can lock or unlock my heart. But even if it were... if children were under any obligation to practise the faith of their forebears, we should still be throwing, in the full light of the nineteenth century, human victims into the fiery belly of Moloch; we should still be worshipping onions!

With that reply I had furnished the foothold for a discussion which it was my intention to avoid. The priest seized the opportunity on the wing and, ever smiling, pointed out to me that our ancient ancestors believed in error and practised falsehood. In that case, children were under the obligation of seeking out the truth, however much they might grope in doing so. But from the moment that truth had been revealed to us by One Who is the very essence of truth, why depart from it, why obstinately close one's eyes to the light? And if even, through a misguided pride, I chose to plunge headlong into evil, by what right did I deny redemption to an innocent creature?

And I: If my son is innocent, why would your God hold him answerable for sins not his own?

— These are mysteries of our holy religion; nor is it given to human understanding to fathom them...

— Not even to men chosen by a "holy" vocation and anointed by the Lord?

— It would be too much to presume from poor clay!

— From which I must infer that you set yourself up as herald and teacher of things you do not know; which, by your own confession, you will never be in a condition to know. What would you say of me if I claimed to teach Greek while ignorant of even its alphabet?

— Faith, my dear Count, faith!...

— If it is a virtue to believe the absurd, I fail to see what distinction could be drawn between a bishop and the savage who worships the meteorite fallen from the sky.

And here, by my own foolishness, I let myself be drawn into expounding for him the essence of the Brahmanic legends, the fertile matrix from which so many other religious legends sprang, the Christian among them. Indeed, pressing further back through the succession of centuries, I proved to him — an easy enough undertaking! — that all those religions which cloud the human intellect derive, in the last analysis, from the astronomical knowledge possessed by the ancients, and that upon the 'science of the Sun' is founded precisely every sacred allegory, every pretended sacred mystery. He came to me, therefore, unknowingly perhaps, but indubitably, to propose that I bend the knee, that I bow the soul before the majesty of Apollo, of Bacchus, of Mithras,... of the Sun!

He let me speak, contriving to assume here and there the expression of a sage who listens, out of benevolent condescension, to the fables created by the exuberant and wayward fancy of a child. But at the final phrase, his thin lips acquired the set of sarcasm and seemed on the point of stretching, at their very corners, as far as his ears.

Was that sarcasm sincere? Perhaps!... In the Church things proceed as in an army: the General Staff knows the secret plans; but the multitude of subordinates operates in the dark.

He said: My dear Count, permit me to employ, in defence of the truth — which would, besides, have no need of any such defence — the selfsame frankness which you employ in defending heresy. Human fancy has indulged itself in flights of fancy far bolder than this one of yours. But in the field of positive facts, the Holy Catholic, Apostolic and Roman Church has nineteen centuries of good to her name which neither you, nor any other unbridled spirit, could destroy.

And I in return: Good existed in theory and in practice even when mankind had not so much as dreamt that, in a small and picturesque peninsula of the wild Occident, a Rome would arise, and in Rome, after a long succession of intrigues and outrages, a Catholic coalition of superstitions and cupidity which would take morality for its signboard or, more precisely, for a kind of skeleton-key. And I maintain that good will continue to be practised even when of the Rome of the Popes nothing remains, a pale memory, save the name!

Individually, notwithstanding the habit he wears, even a priest may practise virtue, for goodness lies in germ in human nature and the day will come when, its obstacles shattered, it will at last triumph upon this Earth. But the evil which the Catholic Church, considered in its exclusive essence, has wrought and persists in working — ah, that is not eclipsed by a handful of phrases! Would you truly have me call it to mind, by evoking nineteen centuries of obscurantism, of deceit, of tyranny, of torture, of the stake, of tears and of blood?

He lowered his eyes and appeared to reflect. Then came the stroke of the venomous tail! Let us set aside for a moment, — he said — opinions which, in any case, can do little or nothing against the fatal march of events... Could one not, with a little goodwill, find a ground upon which two men of honour might reach an understanding, despite the disparity of their views?

He lowered his eyes again, and in a subdued voice: Allow me, not as a priest, but as a man of feeling who might, by his age, be your father... Were you to authorise me to baptise your son, I would undertake to procure for you — on my honour! — a lucrative position such as would restore you to your proper standing in society. I perceive around me signs of poverty all too manifest. Forgive me!... Let us speak as serious men, as upright consciences, who yet know how to weigh the exigencies of life. Why sacrifice reality to mere abstractions? Does it not seem to you that the greatest among our duties is precisely that of providing decently for those dear to us? And what greater satisfaction for a father?

It was the noose of need cast about my neck! General Staff or common soldier, the indelible priestly character never belies itself.

I sprang to my feet and, in a voice full of anger: The greatest of my duties is to leave my son an inheritance of manly example. In any case I am not a priest, and I do not traffic in my convictions! And I showed him the door.

That same year, after a life of brief weeks, my second-born ceased to live. It was the eve of Christmas... the anniversary of the day on which I had set sail to navigate at full canvas toward the fellowship of free men... and I had not so much in the house as would allow me to arrange for his burial. I gazed with dry eyes upon his little face, white as the pillow on which it rested, and, endeavouring to dry Elisa's tears, I said:

Sooner or later, its obstacles shattered, good will triumph upon this Earth. And it will be, in great part, the merit of those who, knowing the truth, do not shrink from practising it. But now... Hush, hush! An upright conscience does not look for reward from men. And perhaps this desolation... is our one, true glory!

But at the last phrase, his thin lips acquired the hardness of sarcasm and seemed to want to stretch so as to touch his ears with their corners.

Was that sarcasm sincere? Perhaps!… In the Church, it is as in an army: the General Staff knows the hidden plans; but the multitude of subordinates operates in the dark.

He said: Count, permit me to use, in defence of the truth, which for that matter has no need of it, the identical frankness that you employ in defending heresy. The human imagination has indulged in far greater flights than this of yours. But in the field of positive facts, the Catholic, Apostolic, Roman Church has nineteen centuries of good that neither you, nor any other unbridled spirit, could ever destroy.

And I retorted: "Good existed in theory and in practice even when humanity did not so much as dream that, in a small and picturesque peninsula of the savage West, a Rome would arise, and in Rome, after a long series of deceptions and violations, a Catholic coalition of superstitions and greed that would take morality for its banner or, rather, in the guise of a picklock. And I maintain that good will continue to be practiced even when of the Rome of the Popes nothing remains, a pale memory, but the name!

"Individually, despite the cloth he wears, even a priest can practice virtue, because goodness is nascent in human nature and a day will come when, the obstacles broken, it will finally triumph on Earth. But the evil that the Catholic Church, considered in its exclusive essence, has wrought and persists in wreaking, oh, that is not eclipsed with a few phrases! Do you really wish me to remind you of it, by evoking nineteen centuries of obscurantism, of deceit, of arrogance, of torture, of pyres, of tears and of blood?"

He lowered his eyes and seemed to reflect. Then came the strike of the venomous tail! "Let us leave aside for a moment," he said, "opinions which, in any case, can do little or nothing against the fatal course of things… Could we not, with a little good will, find a ground upon which two gentlemen might come to an understanding, despite the disparity of their ideas?"

He lowered his eyes again and, in a soft voice: "Allow me, not as a priest, but as a man of heart who could, by age, be your father… If you were to authorize me to baptize your son, I would undertake to procure for you—word of honour!—a lucrative post, such as to put you back on your feet in social fellowship. I see around me signs of poverty that are all too manifest. Forgive me!… we are serious men, with upright consciences, but who know how to weigh the demands of life. Why sacrifice reality to mere abstractions? Does it not seem to you that the greatest of our duties is precisely to provide decently for our loved ones? And what greater satisfaction for a father of a family?"

It was the halter of need thrown around my neck! General Staff or subordinates, the indelible priestly character is never belied.

I rose with a start and, in a voice full of anger: "The greatest of my duties is to leave my son a legacy of virile examples. In any case, I am not a priest, and I do not traffic in my convictions!" And I pointed him to the door.

That same year, after an existence of a few short weeks, my second-born son ceased to live. It was Christmas Eve… the anniversary of the day I had embarked to sail with swelling sails, towards the fellowship of the free… and I did not have enough in the house to allow me to arrange for his burial. I observed with dry eyes his little face, as white as the pillow on which it lay, and, trying to dry Elisa's tears, I said:

"Sooner or later, the obstacles broken, good will triumph on Earth. And it will be, in large part, thanks to those who, knowing the truth, do not hesitate to practice it. But now… Hush, hush! An upright conscience expects no compensation from men. And perhaps this squalor… is our only, true glory!"

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-NINTH

HOWEVER vast my desires, for the moment I could do nothing other than seek a way to establish myself decently in London. Soon the arts of Bernard put me in such a position that, far from combating the enemies of Italian integrity, I was compelled to provide, with all urgency, for the defence of my own personal integrity.

The calumnies of which I had received, from Rossi, the first inkling, were at once confirmed to me anew, with a greater abundance of particulars, by various other persons. Doctor Bernard not only persisted, in private, in the assertions he had launched when he believed me already beneath the earth; but he attempted to lend them credence by maintaining that I had regained my liberty, not through a premeditated and boldly accomplished escape, but because the French Authorities, in recompense for my denunciations, had, so to speak, thrown wide the doors of the penal colony.

Elisa informed me furthermore that the gentlemen of the Times and others — the very same who had initiated the petition for my pardon — had crowned their work by collecting, in her favour, a small capital of one thousand one hundred pounds sterling. From such generosity, and from the sort of allowance settled upon her on the eve of my departure for Paris, she had drawn no relief whatsoever. During my long absence, Bernard had delivered to her a single, solitary pound sterling.

Upon whom was the blame to be laid? Was I to consider Bernard's conduct towards me as inspired by the terror of the guilty? Did he slander me because he found himself incapable of defending himself honestly? To what end did his cowardly manoeuvres tend?

I wrote, on this matter, to several conspirators of the English Committee; but not one deigned to answer me — whether because Bernard had forestalled me; or because secret ties induced them to treat the question exclusively in secret assembly; or because human selfishness counselled them to imitate the worthy Pilate, of traditional memory.

I must make a half-exception for Tomaso Allsop, the man who had lent his own name to Felice Orsini. And I say half, because he sent me a letter containing very courteous phrases and — a banknote of five pounds sterling; but not the slightest allusion to the pressing questions I had put to him.

Having weighed my situation thoroughly, I concluded that to insist upon the question of the one thousand one hundred pounds might give the impression that my agitation was intended to satisfy venal instincts. I therefore resolved not to raise it again, and to combat Bernard openly, solely on the other question. As judge between his assertions and mine, I would call upon the English public.

Rossi, who did not cease to take an interest in my fortunes, introduced me to a certain Kinaer, a journalist already reduced to extremity by tuberculosis; but, intellectually, still full of vigour. He, guided by the information I furnished, wrote a succinct account of the Orsini trial and of my escape. A German publisher undertook to send a printed copy to the most widely read English newspapers. This preliminary accomplished, I embarked with Kinaer upon a tour of lectures, beginning with Nottingham, my wife's city, and proceeding thence through Derby, Birmingham, Manchester, Lee, and other manufacturing centres. Kinaer attended, with great dexterity, to the financial side of the enterprise. I had reserved to myself the task of setting forth to the audience the history of my affairs, and of answering whatever questions might be put to me when I had done speaking.

I never failed to make it plain that I had resolved to speak in public, not to make money, but to provoke a discussion from which I had nothing to fear. And I challenged my accusers to come forward and oppose, to my defence, their alleged documents and their arguments.

Did this ever come to pass? A certain Sherman, who had attended one of my lectures, spoke of it at a Club of Freethinkers to which he belonged, observing how contrary it was to the innate sense of justice of the English people to witness the spectacle of a man struggling under the weight of an infamous accusation, and not to lend him a strong hand so that he might find himself face to face with his detractors.

The *Reasoner*, the organ of that club, then directed by Hollyoak, reproduced that sentiment in one of its issues which came under my eyes at Birmingham. Immediately, I wrote to Hollyoak declaring myself most willing to submit to the judgement of whatever Committee he might be pleased to appoint. At the same time, I expressed the wish that Giuseppe Mazzini might be included as a member of that same Committee — he who, knowing thoroughly the inner workings of our revolutionary endeavours, and also, in some measure, my own public career, would greatly have facilitated the rendering of a dispassionate and impartial verdict.

Three events, in rapid succession, led me to consider the affair fully resolved, notwithstanding the well-known proverb: “Calumniate, calumniate, calumniate, etc.”

Among the convicts I had known on the Île Royale du Salut, there was a Le Roux, a Frenchman who, shortly after my escape, had been transferred to the penitentiary of Corù. From that latter place he had contrived to escape and take refuge in England. Having heard of the rumours circulating on my account, he had, of his own accord, set about confuting them, declaring himself an eye-witness of my “coup d’état,” as he called it. Narrating its particulars, he added those of the sequel it had upon the life and the rigours of the penal colony.

Now, however absurd it may appear, the faith that had not been accorded to the words of an Italian patriot was freely given to those of a French convict. It is somewhat humiliating; but I shall not complain of it — the strangeness redounded to my advantage and to that of the truth. And thus the little tale of “the doors thrown wide” collapsed.

The second incident had its origin in Giuseppe Mazzini. The editor of the *Reasoner*, having conferred with him, wrote to me and published that a jury of honour was superfluous. A dispassionate examination of the circumstances, together with the attestations of the Maestro, had placed my innocence beyond all dispute.

The third was brought about, unexpectedly, by Simon Bernard himself. “He who is born of a hen must needs scratch.” The funds entrusted to his care by a certain secret society vanished in a manner considered not in the least mysterious. He was abandoned by his very own supporters, lost his reason, and — *si vera sunt exposita* — died in a madhouse. This reticence is rendered necessary by the fact that I never troubled to verify, in person, the death certificate relating to him...

Let us refresh ourselves in the contemplation of a very different conspirator. During the wanderings to which I have alluded, I made the personal acquaintance of Baron Egassy di Torocfalda, the inventor of the Orsini bombs, the technical mind of our conspiracy. If the martyr, in his trial, attributed the original idea to himself, the reason is to be sought in his honest circumspection. I have already remarked that he never named any person not already compromised.

Baron Egassy was a native of the Carpathian mountains and had been a lieutenant-colonel of Austrian artillery. A great liberal and a man of action, but rough, soldierly to the extreme, he detested all those who pretended to vindicate their rights by clamouring in the public squares, or to foment revolutions by preaching the postulates of philosophy. His muscular strength surpassed the bounds of credibility.

He maintained that the best way of disposing of a despot is to approach him on some plausible pretext; to place, with lightning swiftness, one hand upon his shoulder, the other upon his head, and to snap his vertebrae with a single sharp blow. Unfortunately it is exceedingly difficult to bring Brutus back to life and to endow him, into the bargain, with the muscles of an Egassy di Torocfalda.

He possessed, among his other gifts, that of being a chemist of no small distinction. Not only did he teach Felice Orsini the method of manufacturing mercury fulminate, but he had also invented a mixture which, on exploding, would asphyxiate all those within a circuit of five hundred feet in diameter. I believe it was something akin to modern lyddite. He was unwilling to reveal its secret to Governments, because they would have used it — he said — to perpetuate the oppression of Peoples.

One day, stigmatising with wrathful phrases the sheepishness of those petty politicians who strain so mightily to vindicate the right of “free speech,” while remaining deaf and dumb and blind to the far more vital question of the liberty of action, he ended by promising me that, sooner or later, he would confide to me the formula of his explosive.

Uttered in a moment of expansive agitation, I attached no great weight to that promise. A few days later we parted, and I thought no more of it. To my surprise, in 1862 I received a letter from him. He begged me to hasten without delay to the Hospital at Birmingham. He lay there in a grave condition and wished to keep his promise — to confide to me “the secret.” I departed by the first train; but scarcely had I entered that house of sorrow when my eagerness was unexpectedly arrested by an attendant. “Baron di Torocfalda? He died this morning.”

I wished to see him. The vigour, the portentous strength, more than extinguished and destroyed by the onset of an overpowering malady, seemed rather to have evaporated — the yellowish skin clung to the bones as though glued fast. Observing him, I could discern the slow work of hunger; though the physicians, taking their pretext from the final collapse, had attributed it to an entirely different cause, distinguished by some name drawn from the Greek which I no longer recall.

And yet that man possessed the secret of a formula which might have restored him to his former wealth. To follow the current, to confide — who could have blamed him? For centuries, every great invention has been turned, principally, to profit and to subjugation. Progress the exclusive possession of a few; science a prostitute, like religion, like human Justice. It seems fated!... But not with impunity did the poor Baron enclose within his heart a spark of greatness!

He had preferred to die of want among the destitute; forgotten, without glory — if glory consists in the applause of men!

To tear myself away from that corpse, already well-nigh in dissolution, I was forced to make an effort. His eyes were wide open, as though he had died with his thoughts fixed upon my arrival and were waiting for me still. I wished to close them and was seized with a shudder — it seemed to me that, in drawing down the thin veil of his eyelids, I had laid the last touches upon the handiwork of death; that with my own fingers I had thrust that exceptional man into eternity!
