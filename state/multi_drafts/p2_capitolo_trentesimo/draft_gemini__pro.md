IN THE very year of my arrival in London, the French Police made, as far as I was concerned, their last attempt at corruption. My finances were then turning for the worse. The five pounds from Allsop had vanished; the few savings made during my round of lectures, vanished! Elisa, on the point of becoming a mother a second time, required great care. Fortunately, the help that, out of a sense of pride, I no longer solicited from my compatriots, I received from Le Houx, the former French convict. Having found employment in a marble sawmill, he had induced his principal to employ me to polish slabs. But my inexperience thinned my wages. Made at such a moment, the French Police's move was not without its tactics. The Inspector who entered my modest dwelling proceeded to the attack without any beating around the bush, with his cards on the table, with a rough frankness worthy of a better cause. He insisted on my isolation, on the ingratitude of the persons at whose "instigation" I had faced the scaffold, on the indigence revealed by my home and my appearance. Was it worth sacrificing myself for people who, in the best of cases, cared nothing for my sacrifices? He understood perfectly well how I might harbour ill feelings against the Government of Napoleon III; but that Government harboured none at all towards me. A single word, and all my misery would be over, and I could restore my wife to the comfortable environment she deserved, that was hers by right.

And he gave greater weight to his words by drawing from his pocket a handful of pounds sterling, which would be—he said—periodically renewed.

But this cunning had failed to factor into its calculations a subtle and powerful coefficient: the honest conscience that changes the nature of sufferings endured for a good cause and transforms them into profound satisfactions; that changes the nature of satisfactions born of wicked works and transforms them into unspeakable tortures.

My Elisa? She would undoubtedly have disdained the comfort offered to her at the price of my honesty. What was a fistful of gold compared to her love? Fortunate is the man who can, in difficult moments, see his reflection in the serene eye of a woman worthy of the name! He passes over the social mire as the Christ of legend upon the waters, with feet uncontaminated and dry!

Once the importunate pressure from the Police ceased, came that of the priest, which was more nauseating. One day, as I was busy at my desk, I found one underfoot, affable, insinuating, a master at dissembling his state of mind with a smile… that smile irritated me from its very first appearance. Whereupon I, cutting him off mid-sentence in his opening remarks which expressed his pleasure in making my personal acquaintance, asked him, quite bluntly, how I might be of service to him.

— My parishioners are all, in a way, my children. I have left the ninety-nine sheep safe on the mountain…

A new interruption on my part, to establish a necessary premise. Of all the quadrupeds to which, with a liberty not of the best taste, he could have compared me, the sheep was to me the most detestable.

— Charity — he said — let us not quibble over mere words. The reasons that prompted me to come here are based on a sense of duty, and in the name of duty I may well ask for a few minutes of your attention, even at the cost of being importunate.

At a nod from me, he sat down and, protesting that he wished to get straight to the heart of the matter, reminded me of my quality as an Italian, which he considered inseparable from that of a Catholic. Not from a desire to meddle in the affairs of others, but for the conscientious discharge of his own ministry, he had come to ask my permission to baptize my son, and he was sustained by the hope that I would not refuse to render that dutiful homage to the religion of my father.

— My father does not believe in priests… that is not, therefore, the key that can lock and unlock my heart. But even if it were… if the obligation fell to children to practice the faith of their ancestors, we would still be throwing, in the middle of the nineteenth century, human victims into the fiery belly of Moloch; we would still be adoring onions!

With that reply I had offered a foothold for a discussion that it was my intention to avoid. The priest seized the opportunity on the wing and, still smiling, observed to me that our ancient ancestors believed in error and practiced falsehood. For their children, then, the obligation was to seek the truth, even if by groping in the dark. But from the moment that the truth had been revealed to us by One who is the very essence of truth, why depart from it, why stubbornly close one's eyes to the light? And even if, out of a misguided pride, I wished to run headlong into evil, by what right did I deny redemption to an innocent creature!

And I: If my son is innocent, why would your God wish to hold him responsible for faults not his own?

— These are mysteries of our holy religion; nor is it given to the human mind to scrutinize them…

— Not even to men chosen by a "holy" vocation and anointed by the Lord?

— It would be too much to presume from poor clay!

— From which I must deduce that you make yourself a herald and master of things you do not know; that, by your own confession, you will never be in a position to know. What would you say of me if I claimed to teach Greek, when I do not even know its alphabet?

— Faith, Count, faith!…

— If there is merit in believing the absurd, I see no difference that might run between a bishop and the savage who adores the aerolite fallen from the sky.

And here, through my own foolishness, I let myself be drawn into deciphering for him the essence of the Brahmanic legends, the fertile matrix from which so many other religious legends sprang, the Christian one included. Indeed, proceeding deeper into the series of centuries, I proved to him—an easy task!—that all the religions which cloud the human intellect derive, in the final analysis, from the astronomical notions possessed by the ancients, and that it is precisely upon the ‘science of the Sun’ that every sacred allegory, every supposed sacred mystery, is based. He was therefore coming, unconsciously perhaps, but undoubtedly, to propose that I bend my knees, that I bow my soul before the majesty of Apollo, of Bacchus, of Mithras,… of the Sun!

He let me speak, contriving to assume, here and there, the expression of a learned man who listens, out of benevolent condescension, to the fables created by the exuberant and spoiled imagination of a child. But at the last phrase, his thin lips acquired the hardness of sarcasm and seemed to want to stretch so as to touch his ears with their corners.

Was that sarcasm sincere? Perhaps!… In the Church, it is as in an army: the General Staff knows the hidden plans; but the multitude of subordinates operates in the dark.

He said: Count, permit me to use, in defence of the truth, which for that matter has no need of it, the identical frankness that you employ in defending heresy. The human imagination has indulged in far greater flights than this of yours. But in the field of positive facts, the Catholic, Apostolic, Roman Church has nineteen centuries of good that neither you, nor any other unbridled spirit, could ever destroy.

And I retorted: "Good existed in theory and in practice even when humanity did not so much as dream that, in a small and picturesque peninsula of the savage West, a Rome would arise, and in Rome, after a long series of deceptions and violations, a Catholic coalition of superstitions and greed that would take morality for its banner or, rather, in the guise of a picklock. And I maintain that good will continue to be practiced even when of the Rome of the Popes nothing remains, a pale memory, but the name!

"Individually, despite the cloth he wears, even a priest can practice virtue, because goodness is nascent in human nature and a day will come when, the obstacles broken, it will finally triumph on Earth. But the evil that the Catholic Church, considered in its exclusive essence, has wrought and persists in wreaking, oh, that is not eclipsed with a few phrases! Do you really wish me to remind you of it, by evoking nineteen centuries of obscurantism, of deceit, of arrogance, of torture, of pyres, of tears and of blood?"

He lowered his eyes and seemed to reflect. Then came the strike of the venomous tail! "Let us leave aside for a moment," he said, "opinions which, in any case, can do little or nothing against the fatal course of things… Could we not, with a little good will, find a ground upon which two gentlemen might come to an understanding, despite the disparity of their ideas?"

He lowered his eyes again and, in a soft voice: "Allow me, not as a priest, but as a man of heart who could, by age, be your father… If you were to authorize me to baptize your son, I would undertake to procure for you—word of honour!—a lucrative post, such as to put you back on your feet in social fellowship. I see around me signs of poverty that are all too manifest. Forgive me!… we are serious men, with upright consciences, but who know how to weigh the demands of life. Why sacrifice reality to mere abstractions? Does it not seem to you that the greatest of our duties is precisely to provide decently for our loved ones? And what greater satisfaction for a father of a family?"

It was the halter of need thrown around my neck! General Staff or subordinates, the indelible priestly character is never belied.

I rose with a start and, in a voice full of anger: "The greatest of my duties is to leave my son a legacy of virile examples. In any case, I am not a priest, and I do not traffic in my convictions!" And I pointed him to the door.

That same year, after an existence of a few short weeks, my second-born son ceased to live. It was Christmas Eve… the anniversary of the day I had embarked to sail with swelling sails, towards the fellowship of the free… and I did not have enough in the house to allow me to arrange for his burial. I observed with dry eyes his little face, as white as the pillow on which it lay, and, trying to dry Elisa's tears, I said:

"Sooner or later, the obstacles broken, good will triumph on Earth. And it will be, in large part, thanks to those who, knowing the truth, do not hesitate to practice it. But now… Hush, hush! An upright conscience expects no compensation from men. And perhaps this squalor… is our only, true glory!"
