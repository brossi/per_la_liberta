BETWEEN one and another incident of my private life, the History of my Country sends forth gusts laden with renewed hopes and enthusiasm, with bitterness and with anger.

My predictions concerning the fatal consequences of the alliance concluded between the King of Piedmont and Napoleon III, though they had formed themselves in my mind at so great a distance from the theatre of events and whilst I found myself as though shut off from the rest of humanity, tallied with those expressed by Giuseppe Mazzini. Those predictions had come to pass.

Having undertaken to drive the Austrians out from the Ticino to the Adriatic in exchange for a compensation which, wounding our national sentiment, placed in his hands the keys to the western gateways of Italy, Napoleon had unexpectedly halted his victorious army and concluded the pretty Peace of Villafranca, which left to the enemy the Veneto, the Quadrilateral, and part of Lombardy itself.

Thus the pact of alliance was torn up in the very face of Vittorio Emanuele II, who, by greater insult still, was not accorded, in the discussion of the armistice and the peace that followed, so much as a *pro forma* consultative vote.

That reversal of front, that breach of faith, that sudden manifestation of imperial spite were owing to the turn taken by Napoleon's manœuvres, in part confessed, in part only too manifest. Napoleon had descended into the valley of the Po to substitute his own influence for that of Austria. A Piedmont enlarged but weak, defenceless against imperial overbearance; a central remnant governed by Plon-plon and vassalised to France; a Murat at Naples; an Italy still in fragments, under the Presidency of Pius IX; the Pope protected and made the instrument of the man to whom he owed his sceptre — this was, in a more or less veiled but nonetheless substantial fashion, the conquest of the entire Peninsula: the most sorrowful pages of our History reactivated in the full nineteenth century!

The unitary patriotism of the Tuscans and the sagacity of Bettino Ricasoli, who was its supreme exponent, made a mockery of the trafficking which had made a pawn of the King through the vulgar Plon-plon, and by persuading the latter of the futility of any attempt on his part, lifted from the minds of the patriots the newest burden of dread.

Following the example of Tuscany, Umbria, Modena, Parma, Piacenza, Bologna and the Romagne had risen up, had dismissed their petty tyrants — not to bow before foreign sovereigns, but to attest their solidarity with the rest of the Italian family. The long propaganda of Giuseppe Mazzini was bearing its fruits.

Had the Empire possessed a popular foundation; had the ill-suppressed apotheosis of Felice Orsini, made by Napoleon's own subjects, not robbed Luigi Napoleone of every illusion on that score; had the general conditions of Europe permitted it, he would have unquestionably assumed the posture of an absolute master, dictating his own will to the peoples of Italy as supreme law. And the Gallic armies would have drunk the waters of the Po for an indefinite time. He lacked the audacity, and, gravely misjudging our popular forces — forces destined henceforth to expand "with, without, or against" the high foreign and domestic influences — trusting, on the other hand, too greatly in the subtle arts of his diplomacy, he preferred to spy upon events, playing for time.

And he announced it clearly enough with the celebrated phrase: "Now we shall see what the Italians can do on their own!" Which was a warning, was a threat; which, broken down into its component parts and glossed in plain language, signified: "Engaged in the absorbing concerns of a war, you would slip entirely from my grasp. Nor do I stake my crown for the platonic love of your fair skies. Free from excessive cares, at the head of an army strengthened by the prestige of victory, I shall be better able to dominate the situation. And let the Italians take care not to go too far beyond the limit willed by my interests!"

Vittorio Emanuele II — with Austria thirsting for vengeance, well entrenched in Italian soil; with the spectre of revolution seething in every part of the Peninsula; fearful of losing even the little he had gained; constrained to subordinate the unity of the Fatherland to the fortune of his own dynasty (he and Cavour deemed it a dream of exalted minds) — could no longer flatter himself with the hope of attempting anything new, of taking a single step, without the blessing of the "magnanimous ally." In other words, that ally had reduced the great King to the condition of a simple vassal of the Empire, perhaps more, certainly no less than the Pope. And we see that, precisely from the Peace of Villafranca, the Government of the King inaugurates that policy of phenomenal pliancy to the caprices of the fateful man; a policy which may seem practical, and perhaps even necessary, but formative of character, but Italian — never!

And Vittorio Emanuele II, glimpsing this, cried out in bitterness: "Poor Italy!" But here was, at bottom, another great intellect deceiving itself. The unitary patriotism of Tuscany not only added the true cornerstone to our national edifice, but, by forcing Napoleon III to throw off his mask, was destined also to render his much-lamented defection an auspicious event in the annals of Italy. The sublime boldness of the *partito d'azione* (the party of action), impatient of delay, redoubled in intensity and precipitated History!

Two months after my arrival in London, there spread, swift as lightning, the news that Giuseppe Garibaldi had landed at Marsala; then, amid general trepidation, came the most contradictory reports, and even that report claiming he had been captured by the Bourbons and summarily hanged from a tree. To dispel that sinister vision came the announcement of the victory of Calatafimi and of the rapid triumphs that followed. England remained as though dazzled by those bursts of intermittent light, and such and so intense was her enthusiasm as to leave one almost supposing that the legionaries of Garibaldi, more than fighting battles for the redemption of Italy, were fighting for the greatness of the English People.

The entry of Garibaldi into Palermo was met, unhappily, by Parliament's sanction of the cession of Nizza and Savoy; the full price paid for a service not fully rendered. Thus, while the Revolution was sacrificing the blood of heroes to add new provinces to the resurgent Nation, to make her whole, the Savoyard Monarchy was operating in reverse and amputating two of her ancient provinces.

Then suddenly, the superior intellect governing the fortunes of that Monarchy, with a master-stroke, turned from devil to friar. I mean that Camillo Benso di Cavour, for all that he deprecated the arts of the revolutionaries as the supreme calamity of Italy, became in his turn a provoker of revolutions. The Marche, having risen, were traversed and re-traversed by his emissaries, who encouraged the wavering, gathered up the scattered threads, distributed arms, redoubled boldness and enthusiasm by promising the imminent intervention of the royal army. And General Cialdini, charged with making good that promise, invaded the Marche to crush the mercenaries of reaction. This took place on the very same day that Garibaldi entered Naples in triumph.

Naturally, even for his new enterprise, the King's Minister had sought the consent of Napoleon III, to whom he demonstrated the necessity of drawing a cordon of defence about the Papal States, menaced by the red shirts. But the supreme purpose was to cancel, in part, the shame of the cession of Nizza, to which Napoleon himself had shown himself disposed to resign. To cancel that shame and to paralyse the prestige of the *partito d'azione* (which had become almost irresistible after the conquest of the Two Sicilies) — whose republican tendencies he vastly overrated. Monarchist to the point of fanaticism, he could not have desired the unity of Italy when attempting it would have compromised the Monarchy; he had become a unitarian when the Monarchy would have been compromised by inaction. The dynamic force of republican thought had thus, indirectly, drawn even the Marche into the bosom of the Italian family.

On the thirtieth of November of that year, I received from the English newspapers the news that Giuseppe Garibaldi, a new Cincinnatus, had retired, as a private citizen, to the solitude of Caprera. With scanty troops, poorly armed, in a few months and as if by a single impulse, he had conquered a Kingdom. Having been proclaimed its Dictator, from that height which permitted him to treat as an equal with the great King, to dictate terms, he held out to him, that he might gird it upon his brow, the crown of the Bourbons. A gesture entirely new in History! — but not new the bad faith with which he was repaid by the very one who had benefited by him. Vittorio Emanuele II assumed the posture of a man who had known how to forestall usurpation; who recovered, by his own virtue, that which was his by right. The debt of gratitude he would see to paying — later!

The glorious enterprise of the Thousand had been organised by Giuseppe Mazzini, by Rosalino Pilo, who left his life in it, by Agostino Bertani, by Nino Bixio, and by many and many another, with those means and with those arts which were so frequently reproached to the Master as a crime. The last at the time of a series of misunderstood and sublime endeavours which had created an "Italian question," which had compelled the Potentates of Europe to concern themselves with it, it was the most fortunate, but not the most exuberant in boldness and spirit of sacrifice. To it the glory of triumph; to the others, that of having prepared and made it possible. And I shall recall that Francesco Crispi, who had not yet renounced embodying within himself the genius of rebellion, brought to it his own contribution — among other things, with an impressive supply of... Orsini bombs!

The enterprise itself gave wider scope to the various tendencies to define themselves. Napoleon III accentuated ever more openly his Italophobe designs, and Vittorio Emanuele II appeared ever more clearly in his equivocal light: champion of the Italian cause, he conspired with the enemies of Italy; liberator, he opposed liberty. Intent upon obstructing the triumphal advance of Garibaldi, he did not blush to open negotiations with the Bourbon himself. And with the Emperor of the French, he came openly to an understanding to spread, along the Strait of Messina, a snare of iron and lead, to bar the bold legionaries of redemption from the road to the Continent. However, the English Cabinet, sounded on the matter from Paris, responded that the Neapolitans had the right to determine their own destinies, and imposed upon Napoleon the policy of non-intervention.

The greatness of the republican soul shines all the more brilliantly in the light of these comparisons. Let it bear repeating: the monarchical entity never throws itself into the fray without keeping an eye to its own interest, and subordinates the unity of Italy to privileges which it proclaims and will have inviolable; the republican entity, having written its own epic in characters of blood, sacrifices freely, upon the altar of the Fatherland, both itself and its serene convictions.

True! — Vittorio Emanuele II was regarded at that time by Garibaldi as the *Dux* foretold by Dante, as the Prince schooled by the powerful mind of Machiavelli. Giuseppe Mazzini, more sparing in his judgements, contented himself with considering him better than his Ministers.

With the swiftness of lightning came the days of disenchantment. Cavour, overwhelmed by immoderate labours, succumbed before his time to the common fate. In England he was mourned sincerely; though, to shake his popularity, something had already leaked out of those secret pacts, so greatly resented by English liberalism, which, later given concrete form in the ill-omened "Convention of September," led the first King of Italy to the renunciation of Rome and to the defence — against the Italians themselves — of the greatest among the enemies of our Country. The *partito d'azione* hoped instead that, with the disappearance of Cavour, it might be possible to draw the King free from the baneful influence of Napoleon.

To the Piedmontese statesman there had succeeded a most thoroughly Italian figure endowed with wide views, one who desired unity and independence not as an end but as the means of ensuring the political regeneration and the greatness of the People — the Baron Bettino Ricasoli! The ship of State changed its course at once. The edict perpetuating the exile of Giuseppe Mazzini was abrogated; constitutional guarantees were respected; the extreme parties were encouraged to draw together, to give concrete form, with their accustomed boldness, to the good of the Fatherland: a renewed current of enthusiasm, a powerful tremor, a feverish activity shook the entire Peninsula. But when, working in concert with Garibaldi, the proud Baron set himself to making of the Italian collectivity not an inert mass at the mercy of its own governors, but a formidable armed force, capable of imposing upon Privilege the respect for popular rights, the retrograde party — already livid with suppressed rage — took fright and decided to make an end of it. One of those parliamentary crises that cover, beneath the cloak of legality, the odious interest of caste, stripped him unexpectedly of the reins of public affairs. And the antiquated and mendacious Diplomacy, under the auspices of Urbano Rattazzi, sharpened oppression and violence the better to corrupt young Italy.

Rattazzi concealed himself at first beneath the skin of the just man; he assumed the posture of a continuator of his predecessor's work: he promised, encouraged, spurred the *partito d'azione* toward the conquest of the Trentino and of the Alps. At the tactical moment, the news broke of the seizure of arms and the arrest of patriots. Brescia, whose horrible wounds inflicted by the beast Haynau were not yet healed, was made to writhe under those opened in her by Piedmontese bullets. In place of foreign tyranny, domestic tyranny had been installed. And Garibaldi, in a burst of righteous indignation, flung his sarcasm in the faces of these fierce men, proposing a national subscription intended to offer a sword of honour to the Russian Popov who, at Warsaw, had broken his own sword rather than drench it in the patriotic blood of defenceless Poles.

Nor was the measure of the affront yet full. The possibility of acting in the North having been cut off, Giuseppe Garibaldi gathered in Sicily a band of volunteers, crossed the Strait once more, and marched up through Calabria, resolved to give the Fatherland her glorious capital. He had made his own the battle cry launched by the patriotic population of Marsala: "O Roma, o morte! — Rome or death!"

"You shall have death, not Rome!" was the cry from Paris. And Napoleon imposed upon Vittorio Emanuele, who accepted, the rôle of the modern Cain.

We are come to the infamy of Aspromonte. When the Garibaldian Legion and the soldiers of the King encountered one another on the 28th of August 1862, the leader of the Thousand responded to the assault of the Bersaglieri by ordering his men to stand firm without opening fire. This alone should have sufficed to avert the fratricide, had not those men who had labelled the attempt of Orsini an abomination sought to eliminate for ever from the stage of the world the foremost and most beloved of the combatants for the cause of Italy. And perhaps it is to the conscience of Sergeant Cavalli of Ivrea that we owe the failure of that conspiracy, truly abominable in its nature. Stepping out from the ranks and advancing three hundred paces beyond the line of fire, he, a most expert marksman, fired a round into the air. Reprimanded, covered with abuse, he took aim once more —

"Viva l'Italia!" cried the leader of the Thousand, and fell at the foot of a tree which resurgent Italians ought to hold sacred as the wood of Calvary. The ball which had wounded Garibaldi in the ankle of his right foot struck the Fatherland to the heart. Vittorio Emanuele II paid the debt of gratitude in that regal fashion — and the courtiers had all the more reason to bequeath to posterity his name with the epithet of *Galantuomo* — the Man of Honour!

Major De Villata shortly afterwards summarily shot, at Fantina, the legionaries who, wandering in the environs of Messina, had attempted to reach Garibaldi. And the insult of that modern Maramaldo, who presumed to brand their unsullied brows with the mark of the brigand, reached them as something more bitter than death itself. In that lust for reaction, Alfonso Lamarmora — the narrow Lamarmora, the same man who in 1849, to the offers of fraternal assistance from the Triumvirs of Rome, opposed, and boasted of it, the cynical response of the bombardment of Genoa; the same who was to lead us to the humiliation of Custoza — arrested, notwithstanding parliamentary guarantees, three Deputies, three representatives of the Nation — and would have had them shot. But Urbano Rattazzi, frightened by the possible consequences, paralysed by the indignant reprobation which, on the morrow of Aspromonte, rose like a tide in every part of the civilised world, compelled him to release them and to make his apologies.

"The Monarchy," cried Giuseppe Mazzini in those days, "inveighs in Lombardy against patriots, in favour of Austria; in Southern Italy it shoots patriots, in favour of the Pope. The equivocation is no longer possible!" And he broke openly, in full view of all, with the Monarchy.

Too late! No longer the interpreter, even in outward appearance, of the popular will, Vittorio Emanuele II felt himself from that time secure in his position, and boldly proclaimed himself the sole arbiter of the destiny of the annexed Provinces. He cast, like a leaden mantle, over the rest of Italy the pedantic administrative systems which had ill enough suited the small Piedmont.
