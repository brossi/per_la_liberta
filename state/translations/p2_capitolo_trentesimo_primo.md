CHAPTER THE THIRTY-FIRST

BETWEEN one and another incident of my private life, the History of my Country sends forth gusts laden with renewed hopes and enthusiasm, with bitterness and wrath.

My predictions concerning the fatal consequences of the alliance forged between the King of Piedmont and Napoleon III, though they had come to my mind at so great a distance from the theater of events and while I found myself as though excluded from the rest of humanity, were in perfect accord with those expressed by Giuseppe Mazzini. Those predictions had come to pass.

Having undertaken the expulsion of the Austrians from the Ticino to the Adriatic in exchange for a compensation which, wounding our national sentiment, placed in his hands the keys to the western gates of Italy, Napoleon had unexpectedly halted his victorious army and concluded the treacherous peace of Villafranca, which left to the enemy the Veneto, the quadrilateral, and part of Lombardy itself.

Thus the pact of alliance was torn asunder in the very face of Vittorio Emanuele II who, as a further insult, was not granted, in the discussion of the armistice and of the peace that followed, so much as a single consultative, *pro forma* vote.
That reversal of front, that broken faith, that sudden manifestation of imperial displeasure were owing to full knowledge of Napoleon’s manœuvres — in part confessed, in part too plainly manifest of themselves. Napoleon had descended into the valley of the Po in order to substitute his own influence for that of Austria. A Piedmont enlarged yet weak, yet defenseless against imperial tyranny; a central remnant governed by Plon-plon and vassal to France; a Murat at Naples; an Italy still in fragments, under the Presidency of Pius IX; the Pope protected and made the instrument of the man to whom he owed his sceptre... this was, in a form more or less veiled yet always substantial, the conquest of the entire Peninsula — the saddest pages of our History revived in the full light of the nineteenth century!

The unitary patriotism of the Tuscans, and the shrewdness of Bettino Ricasoli, who was its supreme embodiment, made a mockery of the trafficking by which the vulgar Plon-plon had been made the King’s son-in-law, and by persuading the latter of the futility of any attempt to deceive the Italians, lifted from the hearts of the patriots that newest oppression.

Following the example of Tuscany, Umbria, Modena, Parma, Piacenza, Bologna and the Romagne had risen, had dismissed their petty tyrants, not to bow before foreign sovereigns, but to attest their solidarity with the rest of the Italian family. The long propaganda of Giuseppe Mazzini was bearing its fruit.

Had the Empire possessed a popular foundation; had the ill-suppressed apotheosis of Felice Orsini, enacted by his very own subjects, not stripped Louis Napoleon of every illusion in the matter; had the general conditions of the age permitted it, he would without doubt have assumed the bearing of absolute master, dictating to the peoples of Italy his own will as supreme law, and the Gallic herds would have drunk the waters of the Po for an indefinite time. He lacked the audacity, and, ill-appreciating our popular forces — destined henceforth to expand “with, without, or against” the higher foreign and domestic influences — and trusting moreover too greatly in the devious arts of his diplomacy, he preferred to watch events, playing for time.

And he announced this plainly with the celebrated phrase: “Now we shall see what the Italians know how to do on their own!” Which was admonition, was threat; which, broken into its component parts and glossed in plain speech, signified: “Engaged in the absorbing concerns of a war, you would slip entirely from my grasp. Nor do I risk my crown for the platonic love of your fair sky. Free from excessive cares, at the head of an army strengthened by the prestige of victory, I shall be better able to dominate the situation. And let the Italians take care not to go too far beyond the limit required by my interests.”

Vittorio Emanuele II — he had the Austrians, thirsting for vengeance and well entrenched on Italian soil; he had the specter of revolution seething in every corner of the Peninsula; he was fearful of losing even the little he had gained; he was compelled to subordinate the unity of the Fatherland to the fortunes of his own dynasty (he and Cavour regarded it as a dream of exalted minds) — he could no longer flatter himself that he might attempt new ventures, might take a step, without the good pleasure of the “magnanimous ally.”

In other words, the latter had reduced the great King to the condition of a simple vassal of the Empire, perhaps more so, certainly no less so than the Pope himself. And we observe that, precisely from the peace of Villafranca, the Government of the King initiates that policy of phenomenal pliability to the caprices of the man of destiny — a policy which may appear practical, and perhaps even necessary, but which is character-forming, but Italian, never.

And Vittorio Emanuele II exclaimed: “Poor Italy!” But at bottom it was yet another great intellect deceiving itself. Tuscan unitary patriotism not only added the true cornerstone to our national edifice; it also, by compelling Napoleon III to throw off his mask, was to render his much-lamented defection a propitious event in the annals of Italy. The sublime boldness of the party of action, intolerant of delay, redoubled in intensity and precipitated History.

Two months after my arrival in London, there spread, swift as lightning, the news that Giuseppe Garibaldi had landed at Marsala; then, amid universal trepidation, the most contradictory rumors — and even that he had been captured by the Bourbonists and summarily hanged from a tree. To dispel that dire report came the announcement of the victory of Calatafimi and of the rapid triumphs that followed. England remained as though dazzled by those bursts of intermittent light, and such and so intense was her enthusiasm as to leave one almost supposing that the legionaries of Garibaldi, rather than fighting battles for the redemption of Italy, were fighting for the greatness of the English People.

Garibaldi’s entry into Palermo was matched, unhappily, by the sanction of Parliament for the cession of Nice and Savoy — the full price paid for a service only partially rendered. Thus, while the Revolution sacrificed the blood of heroes to add new Provinces to the resurgent Nation, to make it whole, the Savoyard Monarchy worked in reverse and severed from it two of its ancient Provinces.

All at once the superior intellect that governed the fortunes of that Monarchy, with a master stroke, turned from devil to friar. I mean that Camillo Benso di Cavour, while deploring as the supreme affliction of Italy the arts of the revolutionaries, himself became, in his turn, a provoker of revolutions. The Marches, stirred to revolt, were traversed back and forth by his emissaries who encouraged the wavering, gathered together the scattered threads, distributed arms, redoubled boldness and enthusiasm by promising the imminent intervention of the royal army. And General Cialdini, charged with making good that promise, invaded the Marches to vanquish the mercenaries of reaction. This took place on the very day that Garibaldi entered Naples in triumph.

Naturally, for this new enterprise too, the King’s Minister had sought the consent of Napoleon III, to whom he demonstrated the necessity of drawing a cordon of defense around the Pontifical States, threatened by the red-shirts. But the supreme aim was to efface, in part, the shame of the cession of Nice — to which Napoleon himself had shown himself disposed to renounce — to efface that shame, and to neutralize the prestige of the party of action (grown almost irresistible after the conquest of the Two Sicilies), whose republican tendencies he feared to excess.

Monarchist to the point of fanaticism, he could not desire the unity of Italy when the attempt would have compromised the Monarchy; he had become a unitarian when the Monarchy would have been compromised by inaction. The dynamic force of republican thought had thus, indirectly, thrust the Marches too into the bosom of the Italian family.

On the 30th of November of that year, I received from the English newspapers the news that Giuseppe Garibaldi, a new Cincinnatus, had retired, as a private citizen, into the solitude of Caprera. With scanty troops, poorly armed, in a few months and as though by a single bound, he had conquered a Kingdom. Having been proclaimed its Dictator, from that height which allowed him to treat on equal terms with the great King, to dictate conditions, he proffered him — that he might bind it about his brow — the crown of the Bourbons. A gesture wholly new in History! — but not new the bad faith with which he who was the beneficiary repaid him. Vittorio Emanuele II assumed the bearing of a man who had known how to forestall usurpation; who by his own virtue recovered what was his by right. The debt of gratitude he would attend to repaying later.

The glorious enterprise of the Thousand had been organized by Giuseppe Mazzini, by Rosalino Pilo — who gave his life in it — by Agostino Bertani, by Nino Bixio, and by many and many others, with those means and those arts which were so frequently reproached to the Master as a crime. The last in a series of ill-understood and sublime attempts that had created an “Italian question,” that had compelled the Potentates of Europe to occupy themselves with it, it was the most fortunate, not the most overflowing with boldness and spirit of sacrifice. To it the glory of triumph; to the others that of having prepared it and rendered it possible. And I shall note that Francesco Crispi, who had not yet renounced the idea of embodying in himself the spirit of rebellion, brought his contribution, among other things, with a conspicuous supply — of Orsini bombs!

The enterprise itself gave the various tendencies wider scope to define themselves. Napoleon III accentuated ever more his Italophobe designs, and Vittorio Emanuele II appeared ever more in his equivocal light: a champion of the Italian cause, he conspired with the enemies of Italy; a liberator, he opposed liberty. Intent on impeding the triumphal advance of Garibaldi, he did not blush to enter into negotiations with the Bourbon himself. And with the Emperor of the French he came to an open understanding to stretch, along the Strait of Messina, an ambush of iron and lead, to bar to the bold legionaries of redemption the road to the Continent. But the English Cabinet, sounded on the matter by Paris, replied that the Neapolitans had the right to dispose of their own destinies, and imposed upon Napoleon the policy of non-intervention.

The greatness of the republican soul shines all the more brilliantly from these contrasts. To repeat is of use: The monarchical entity never throws itself into the fray without an eye to its own interest and subordinates the unity of Italy to privileges which it proclaims inviolable; the republican entity, having inscribed its own epic in characters of blood, freely sacrifices upon the altar of the Fatherland both itself and its serene convictions.

A true statesman, Vittorio Emanuele II was regarded by Garibaldi at that time as an end, yet foreshadowed by Dante as the Prince schooled by the powerful mind of Machiavelli. Giuseppe Mazzini, more sparing in judgment, contented himself with holding him better than his Ministers.

The days of disillusionment succeeded one another like lightning. Cavour, overwhelmed by unrelenting toil, succumbed before his time to the common fate. In England he was sincerely mourned; though, to shake his popularity, something of those secret pacts had already transpired — pacts very unwelcome to English liberalism — which, in the ill-fated “September Convention,” were to lead the first King of Italy to the renunciation of Rome and to the defense, against the Italians themselves, of the greatest among the enemies of our Country. The party of action hoped instead that, with the disappearance of Cavour, it would be given to it to draw the King from the baneful influence of Napoleon.

To the Piedmontese statesman had succeeded a man of the most Italian spirit, endowed with broad vision, who desired unity and independence not as an end, but as a means of ensuring the political regeneration and the greatness of the People — the Baron Bettino Ricasoli.

The ship of State changed course abruptly. The edict perpetuating the exile of Giuseppe Mazzini was undermined; constitutional guarantees were respected; the extreme parties were encouraged to come together, to give concrete form, with their accustomed boldness, to the good of the Fatherland — a renewed current of enthusiasm, a powerful tremor, a feverish activity shook the entire Peninsula. But when, coming to an understanding with Garibaldi, the proud baron set himself to making of the Italian collectivity not an inert mass at the mercy of its own governors, but a formidable armed force capable of imposing upon Privilege respect for popular rights, the retrograde party, already livid with suppressed fury, took fright and resolved to have done with him. One of those parliamentary crises which conceal, beneath the mantle of legality, the odious interest of caste, unexpectedly wrested from him the reins of government. And the antiquated and mendacious Diplomacy, under the auspices of Urbano Rattazzi, sharpened abuse and violence the better to corrupt young Italy.

Rattazzi concealed himself, at first, beneath the skin of the just man; he assumed the bearing of a continuator of his predecessor’s work — he promised, encouraged, and drove the party of action toward the conquest of the Trentino and the Alps. At the tactical moment, news burst forth of the seizure of arms and the arrest of patriots. Brescia, her horrible wounds inflicted by the beast Haynau not yet healed, had to writhe beneath those opened in her by Piedmontese lead. A domestic tyranny had taken the place of the foreign one. And Garibaldi, in a burst of indignation, flung his own sarcasm in the faces of the fierce, proposing a national subscription intended to offer a sword of honor to the Russian Popov who, at Warsaw, had broken his own sword rather than stain it with the patriotic blood of defenseless Poles.

Nor was the measure yet full. The possibility of action in the North having been cut off, Giuseppe Garibaldi assembled in Sicily a band of volunteers, recrossed the strait, and marched up through Calabria, resolved to give to the Fatherland its glorious capital. He had made his own the battle-cry launched by the patriotic population of Marsala: “Rome or death!”

“You shall have death, not Rome!” was the cry at Paris. And Napoleon imposed upon Vittorio Emanuele, who accepted, the part of the modern Cain.

We are arrived at the infamy of Aspromonte. When the Garibaldian Legion and the King’s soldiers met, on the 28th of August 1862, the leader of the Thousand replied to the assault of the Bersaglieri by ordering his men to stand their ground without firing. That alone ought to have sufficed to avert fratricide — had not those men who had passed off the Orsini outrage as execrable aimed in truth at removing forever from the stage of the world the first, the most beloved among the fighters for the cause of Italy. And perhaps it is owing to the conscience of Sergeant Cavalli of Ivrea that the conspiracy, truly execrable, miscarried. Stepping from the ranks and advancing some three hundred paces beyond the line of fire, he — an expert marksman — discharged a shot that went wide. Reprimanded, covered with abuse, he took aim once more —

“Long live Italy!” cries the leader of the Thousand, and falls at the foot of a tree which resurgent Italians ought to regard as sacred as the wood of Calvary. The shot that had wounded Garibaldi in the ankle of his right foot struck the Fatherland to the heart. Vittorio Emanuele II repaid in that kingly fashion the debt of gratitude — and the courtiers had one more reason to hand down his name to posterity with the epithet of Galantuomo.

Major De Villata shot by summary execution, shortly after, at Fantina, the legionaries who, wandering in the vicinity of Messina, had attempted to reach Garibaldi. And the insult of that modern Maramaldo — who presumed to brand their unsullied brows with the mark of the brigand — reached them certainly more bitter than death itself. In that lust for reaction, Alfonso Lamarmora, the narrow-minded Lamarmora — the same man who in 1849, in response to offers of fraternal assistance from the Roman Triumvirs, had counter-proposed, and boasted of it, the cynical response of the bombardment of Genoa; the same man who was to lead us to the humiliation of Custoza — arrested, in defiance of parliamentary guarantees, three Deputies, three representatives of the Nation, and wished to have them shot. But Urbano Rattazzi, frightened by the possible consequences, paralyzed by the indignant reprobation that, on the morrow of Aspromonte, rose like a tide in every part of the civilized world, compelled him to set them at liberty and to make his apologies.

“The Monarchy!” exclaimed Giuseppe Mazzini in those days — “rails in Lombardy against the patriots, in favor of Austria; in southern Italy shoots the patriots, in favor of the Pope. Equivocation is no longer possible!” And he broke openly, in full view of all, from the Monarchy.

Too late! No longer the interpreter, even in appearance, of the popular will, Vittorio Emanuele II now felt secure in his own course and declared himself boldly the sole arbiter of the destiny of the annexed Provinces. He cast over the rest of Italy, like a leaden pall, the pedantic administrative systems which were suited with difficulty even to little Piedmont.

And, what is worse, he lavished medals and rewarded with promotion the dishonest and the deluded who had served as his hired assassins — thus inaugurating that policy, continued by his successors, which, aiming almost exclusively at the augmentation of the army and honoring ferocious repressions, made it possible to renew them, and by means of them, to survive. Nor are there ever wanting, at every shameful act, the great poppies of the Ministry to smother the solitary voice of some Deputy, exalting to the skies the spirit of self-abnegation, the profound sense of duty with which the army shows itself animated — as though Italy had undergone and still undergoes so many sacrifices to impose upon the flower of its youth the lofty ideal of incruelling itself against hungry and defenseless brothers, and of being cowardly!
