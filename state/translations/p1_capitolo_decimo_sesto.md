CHAPTER SIXTEEN

HERE is the painful page: The secret of the vast conspiracy, jealously guarded; the Austrian Authorities utterly unprepared; the distribution of soldiers’ pay already accomplished; the targeted positions stripped of their defenders; the garrison dispersed and carousing. Eugenio Brizzi himself, on the very eve of the meditated assault, penetrated into the inner courtyard of the Castello and conferred with the two armorers. He found them steadfast to their pledged word; nor had any coup de main ever set forth under fairer auspices.

But at the moment of action, Assi and several other chiefs, instead of mustering their squads, vanished. The few bold men stationed at their posts, seeing themselves too small in number, waited for a time, chafing with rage; then, suspecting treachery, or believing that everything had been deferred to another day, they dispersed.

Of Fortunato Calvi’s plan, only so much succeeded as suffices to show that it was not built upon clouds, and that it would have had a very different outcome had there not been in Milan certain individuals of great influence who, though they desired the expulsion of the foreigner, kept their eyes fixed — under the color of prudence — upon the interests of their own caste.

The assault upon the Gran Guardia, led by a simple charcoal-burner, ended in success. The insurrection would have had its center, and two howitzers to defend it. Yet the directing minds were conspicuous by their absence, and the popular impetus, impatient of delay, cast aside the instructions it had received. Instead of barricading themselves within the palace, those few brave men poured out tumultuously into the streets. They killed several officers, then fell back upon Porta Romana, where the innkeeper Giuseppe Monti organized a defense.

To suppress it, an entire battalion was despatched. And while a handful of common folk generously exposed their lives, in the hope of holding out until the middle and upper classes should resolve to descend armed into the revolutionary vortex… the wealthy, the right-thinking, closed their street-doors, closed their windows, and gave four-fifths of the capital the aspect of a deserted and silent city.

The insurrection failed because the Milanese intellectuals lacked the ardor, the heart, the spirit of sacrifice, the faith of the People. Already in the preliminary negotiations conducted between themselves and Giuseppe Mazzini, Emilio Visconti Venosta — the future Minister of the monarchy — and his kind had employed all the keenness of their wit in raising objections. Compelled by argument to yield one after another, they ended by declaring that, before resolving to lend their support, they intended to seek the opinion of a competent military man. And they turned to Giacomo Medici, who was then residing in Genova.

The hero of the Vascello, future general of Vittorio Emanuele II, entertained the selfsame thought that had informed the conduct of Giuseppe Mazzini: “To counsel against the rising; but to support it strenuously once it proved inevitable.”

This did not please them, and they did not follow him. I believe they had hoped to hear from Medici’s lips the very phrase that would lend respectability to their timid egotism. The conspiracy took shape and began its fateful course; yet they gave neither a soldo, nor a man, nor a musket. And they possessed all of these! — but with an expression already heralding the short-sighted and corrosive opportunism of times all too vivid in the memory of Italians, they declared “that they were reserving these for good use on the second day!”

The flight of Assi and the others, however blameworthy, had no other motive than these underhand equivocations. The Milanese working class was not yet accustomed to the present-day, subtle distinctions between proletariat and capitalism; it had not yet learned to stand erect upon its own spine. It had heard and read so many times that all Italians are brothers and, in its artless good faith, it held them as such. The first doubts are always the most bitter. Why should the *marsine* now refuse to make common cause with the *blouse*? Were not the sons of the People risking their lives for a holy common cause? Why should all the risks of initiative fall to them alone?

I came to know Assi in exile. He was expiating, in muffled anguish, his own error and those which were attributed to him unjustly. And he defended himself, saying: “They wished to drive us alone to the slaughter, so that they might disown us if we were beaten. Had victory smiled upon us — at no risk to themselves — they would have robbed us of its fruit!”

The bourgeois and aristocratic inaction of 1853 had recalled to mind that in 1848 the direction of public affairs had been usurped by those who had not made the revolution. Deplorable logic, that of Assi — but logic nonetheless!

Giuseppe Monti and his men fought on, from house to house, until not a scrap of powder remained to them. And so effective was their resistance that a full hundred and fifty of the enemy lay upon the cobblestones and ceased to lacerate Italy.

Then came the martyrdom! A military tribunal, hastily constituted, hastily condemned to the gallows Giuseppe Monti and fifteen other brave men. *(1)*

They faced their punishment with a serene spirit, teaching the slothful how to die, as they had already taught them how to rise in arms.

Then came the arts of Tartuffe. It was necessary to diminish the heroism of the good in order to give to their own desertion the character of an enlightened decision worthy of supermen. There is no lack of connoisseurs who would dismiss the verses of Dante as insipid, were they submitted to their judgment over an unknown signature. The plans of Fortunato Calvi, attributed to Mazzini — an intellect held to be refractory to all military discipline — were declared a foolishness more than puerile. And men were not ashamed, in order to prove it, to borrow with a smug air of triumph the Croatian phrases belched forth during the heroic hours spent at the tavern: “Had the Milanese killed our officers, we should have put the Milanese to the sword — men, women, and children alike!”

And the people of the Five Days, to please the Croatians and the Visconti Venosta, would have resigned themselves to being skewered like a dish of flaccid gnocchi!

Then came the infamy! — To vilify the honest; to reduce, if possible, the stature of the giant, so that the People, deceived and deluded, might resolve to gravitate toward the will-o’-the-wisps. The tactic is ancient.

And it was insinuated and written and given for certain that Giuseppe Mazzini was a spy in the pay of Austria. He provoked excesses, remaining himself prudently at a distance, in order to furnish the oppressor with the pretext for terrorizing the timid by strangling the bold.

Then came the nauseating cynicism!

To stupefy a conscience revived at intervals by the tormenting thought of one’s own guilt; to attempt to deceive oneself and others by disowning even one’s own martyrs. To this they descended: they circulated a protest in which, having inverted all the parts, Giuseppe Monti and the other unfortunates were portrayed as assassins, and as martyrs the assassins righteously cut down in that desperate, glorious attempt to restore to us a Fatherland! Never had creatures born and raised beneath the fair sky of Italy prostrated themselves so low!

I repeat: The failure of the rising of the 6th of February 1853 is due principally to the fact that a handful of young men — wealthy, privileged, and influential — recoiled in horror before the possibility of a popular Government, and already preferred the not-making of Italy to making it otherwise than conservative and monarchical.

Yet those risings were not without fruit. They hardened Austria and, rendering her ever more blind to her true interests, drove her back toward reckless sequestrations, toward fresh persecutions; toward remedying our errors with the force of her own errors, greater and irreparable.

Giuseppe Monti and his companions in martyrdom thus indirectly rendered any accord between the oppressed and the oppressors impossible. The holy work of redemption hastened its course and became inevitable!
