# Chapter Twelve

Of my excursions, which extended, on two separate occasions, as far as remote Udine, I shall recount, in brief, some episodes.

Having eluded the police at Milan, at Como, at Bergamo, at Brescia, I was approaching Mantova with a great confidence of spirit — I was sustained by the certainty that not a living soul there could know me. But at the very moment I was passing through the toll-gate, violent as a blow to the chest, my full name rang out.

In that instant, I believed myself discovered and lost. But the man who had called to me from above wore a smiling face, and his right hand reached out to welcome mine. It was the very chief of the guards. How he knew me I cannot now recall; I believe he was a patriot well versed in matters of the secret society.

When he learned that I was come to Mantova to take the air, his face clouded over. Only the day before, the police had carried out several arrests, and the reason for them remained unknown; he perceived a connection between those unexpected severities and my arrival. He counselled me to take refuge immediately in his house and to remain there shut in. He would endeavour to sound out the ground, and in the evening, over a bite to eat, we would deliberate on what was to be done.

Provoked by the conspiracy in which the gentle figure of the priest Tazzoli held a foremost place, there was beginning at that very time, in Mantova, the series of refined tortures that drove to an inhuman death the glorious little band of the martyrs of Belfiore. But on his return late that evening, my host could bring me nothing save vague tidings — along with them, however, came a breath of the terror that hung over the city. He had not encountered a single brother who felt sure of being able to pass the night in his own bed.

I lay down in the attic as best I could, without undressing — prepared, at the slightest alarm, to attempt escape across the rooftops.

Early the following morning, after a long conversation with my host, I learned that among the garrison there was an officer who had been trained at the Military Academy of Milan and had been my fellow pupil there: the bonds of early youth do not crumple away with the passing of the years. Half an hour later I was in the Castello, exchanging a brotherly embrace, or thereabouts. I was under the protection of the double-headed eagle.

To account for my presence, I gave it out that I was making for Belluno to attend my father, who was failing with age — I had made a detour as far as Mantova expressly to see my old friend again; to evoke, in his company, the years of happy carelessness. As if by tacit agreement, we were both careful to avoid any subject of a political nature; but amid the modest festivities organised in my honour, I never lost sight of the purpose of my journey, and gathered, in snatches and by degrees, all the information I was seeking. Two hours before sunset the officer himself courteously offered to accompany me by carriage beyond the city walls. And under his aegis I passed through the toll-gate, unmolested, saluted with a military greeting.

Having escaped one danger that was in all probability imaginary, however, I was compelled to face a danger all too real, at Verona.

I had taken lodging at the *Scudo di Francia*, and no sooner had I shut myself inside my room than I made, by instinct, a brief survey of it. It was situated on the third floor; the single window gave onto a courtyard. Directly opposite stood the rear wall of another hotel, that of the *Regina d'Inghilterra*. Craning my face downward, I made out a broad sloping roof that declined toward the centre of the yard. A smell of stables rose up to that height; I heard the stamping of a horse.

I had already made an appointment with Don Bastiano Barozzi for nine o'clock at the café near the Pantheon. The good priest entered it pale and nearly beside himself. His short-sightedness prevented him from noticing me at first, and the fireplace was burning brightly. He was on the point of exclaiming: "Ah, what a calamity! What will my—" when measured footsteps rang out in the corridor. I blew out the candle and cast my letter into the fire; it carbonised and flew up through the flue.

There came a knock at the door, and an obsequious voice — that of a servant: "Signor Moretti—" Then, after a brief silence, another voice, gruff and imperious: "In the name of the Law, open!"

I had climbed over the parapet and was hanging there with arms fully extended, to diminish the distance that separated me from the roof below. The gendarmes began to batter the door with their rifle-butts. I released my grip and let myself drop, scraping the wall. The impact was rough, but the damage fell to the tiles. I slid along them, and reaching the edge, made use of a projecting beam and of what gymnastics my bruised limbs would allow, to put the gutter between myself and the constables. Crouching beneath it, I heard the door of my room give way with a crash — then a rush of hurried footsteps — then silence — then a ringing voice: "Sergeant, I'll wager he jumped from the window!" And the sergeant, who flattered himself he knew better: "Blockhead! One does not jump from a third floor!"

I believe I lay very still at the window-ledge for some time. The courtyard was deserted. Through the half-open door of the stable, a strip of light fell across the ground. It revealed to me a heap of manure. I jumped into it.

A moment later, I walked boldly into the kitchen of the *Regina d'Inghilterra*. Scullions and servants, busy at their work, did not so much as lift their heads. A cook, his great belly swathed in a white apron, the white cap upon his head, was at that moment blowing on his ladle — he stared at me in astonishment; but before he had recovered from his surprise I was already passing through the door of the dining room. The waiters presumably took me for some scullion's acquaintance. Within seconds I found myself in the open air.

Where to pass the night? The police, rendered all the more suspicious by my disappearance, would not have allowed me to find rest in any hotel. I drifted aimlessly about until I found myself facing the Post. Several diligences stood there in a row with their doors thrown open — into one of them I flung myself at full length and lay there huddled until the dawn came. Several times, between sleep and waking, I heard the night-watch pass at my feet. Had I snored, in all probability I should not be here to recount that adventure of mine.

An even more devilish danger I brought upon myself, through excess of zeal, on my return to Milan. On the advice of a certain Valentini, a student of chemistry, I was lodging, during my stay in the Lombard capital, at the house of his sweetheart, a woman who made her living from a dairy shop she kept and from taking in lodgers. At her table there dined several customs guards and a Hungarian sergeant who was an obstinate tormentor of the pianoforte. I felt as though I were entrenched within the enemy's own camp.

The dairywoman, a fleshy creature with the face of a fresh *panettone*, was of the most Italian sympathies — so much so that, on my previous visit, I had entrusted to her, for destruction, a packet of revolutionary pamphlets that had become superfluous. Now she informed me that she had kept it, intact, at the bottom of a chest of drawers. I was glad of it: before returning to Piedmont, I might, by a thorough posting-up, give the finishing touch to my work.

I spoke of it to the Revolutionary Committee which, highly irritable in those days on account of the wretched news arriving from Mantova, approved my intention. And among those patriots I found one who, of his own accord, offered to assist me — a stocky workman with a broad scar upon his face. He did not tell me his name; he was an upholsterer, like the poor Antonio Sciesa.

A cold, rainy night — the streets deserted. We began at the locality known as the Carrobbio; I on one pavement, he on the other. We were pasting up our proclamations with those little wafers which the Milanese call *obiaditt*. We were making our way toward the Duomo, and already I could see the façade of that magnificent temple gleaming white, when from the other side of the piazza — precisely beneath the *Coperchio dei Figini* — there rang out in the silence that terrible warning: "*Scappa, scappa!*" — Run, run!

I saw the stocky figure of the upholsterer take to his heels toward the piazza Mercanti, with a police patrol that had emerged from some dark passageway in hot pursuit, shouting after him. Had it not been for his brotherly solicitude, I might perhaps have slipped away without drawing attention; but by hurling that cry at me he had also alerted the constables, a good number of whom advanced at a run in my direction.

There was no alternative. I flung down the pamphlets and fled headlong through a labyrinth of narrow streets, until, breathless, I found the line of the city walls rising sheer before me. I climbed the ramp on all fours; but with my pursuers at my heels and a toll-gate, with its respective guard-post, to right and to left, I could consider myself in a trap. I took temporary shelter behind one of the great horse-chestnuts, and for a moment I flattered myself that, uncertain of the direction I had taken, the wretches would turn back discomfited, or that, mounting the rampart, they would all bear to the right, leaving me free to slip away silently to the left. But they were not so simple.

Upon reaching the top they divided into two groups which, turning their backs on each other, advanced simultaneously from both sides, peering carefully behind every tree. As at Verona, I found myself faced with the terrible alternative: either to submit, at some later time, to that little leap which, by virtue of a halter, admits of no remedy — or to confront the immediate perils of a great leap that at least left me some percentage of escape. I resolved upon the great leap.

How long I lay stunned at the bottom I was never, in truth, able to say. Fortunately I had fallen on soft ground, and perhaps it was the rain — which fell as if being poured directly upon me — that helped me to my feet again. I set out, painfully and soaked to the bone, toward Pavia.

In what state I arrived there is not difficult to imagine. Three days under the bedclothes and I was in good order again. The brother of the *Giovine Italia* who had tended me with such affection would have wished me to stay longer; but the land of safety was too near for me not to feel a fever of longing to set foot on it once more. He accompanied me as far as the bridge that crosses the Ticino and joins the Lombard bank to the Piedmontese, and there we parted.

And then — two, three, a whole crowd of frantic figures came running noisily to the mouth of the bridge. From the phrases they exchanged in loud voices, I understood that an unknown man — some smuggler or hot-head — pursued by a patrol, had thrown himself from the parapet. They had hit him in flight with a gunshot in the back, and were now endeavouring to fish him out.

I gave the place a wide berth and, passing myself off as a deserter, found the means to get myself ferried across, arriving at Stradella by nightfall. Casa Winkler was still lit up. I entered and found before me a group of patriots who received me as though I had dropped from the clouds. A secret message arrived from Pavia had brought the news that I had been discovered, pursued, and mortally wounded while seeking escape by leaping into the Ticino. Fortunato Calvi had already been informed by telegraph.

I set out immediately and found Calvi still in bed. No sooner had he recovered from his astonishment than his first thought was for Don Bastiano Barozzi, with whom he had already made an appointment for nine o'clock at the Caffè del Pantheon. The good priest entered it pale and nearly beside himself. His short-sightedness prevented him from seeing me, and Calvi went to meet him. At the sight of him, the priest exclaimed: "Ah, what a calamity! What will my benefactor say when he learns that I repaid him by driving his son to ruin?"

— And what would you say, if Carlo were to walk in here, hale and smiling?

The face of Barozzi was convulsed. All at once the knot that choked him loosened, and he burst into tears.

Then the atheist flung his arms around the neck of the priest. Which goes to show that the hypothesis of God is not necessary for men to love one another as brothers.
