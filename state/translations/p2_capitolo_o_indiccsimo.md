WHEN the door of the cell closed behind me, it was as though the turbid passions that had agitated me remained on the other side of the threshold. Saturno and the other questioned me with their expressions. "It is all over! Every aspiration toward liberty has been cut off for me from this day forward. One must prepare to die." I did not say this aloud, but within myself I felt that I should be sustained by the pride of having sacrificed myself for my Country.

No sooner had I finished my account than they endeavoured, as though by tacit agreement, to drag me back from my convictions. They put forward, among other things, the sympathies of the population and the favourable dispositions which were said to animate the imperial Court. To this I replied by wagging my head and smiling. A curious dispute arose from this, and we ended by laying a wager. If the Tribunal pronounced a sentence of death, they would undertake to pay me a bottle of champagne — and of the finest. If not, I would pay for it myself. We sealed the pact with a vigorous handshake.

Mathieu de la Drôme paid me his first visit that very day. He was a dignified man, already somewhat advanced in years, grey at the temples, placid in bearing and in speech. For several days he had spontaneously offered to act on my behalf, but it had not been permitted until the matter was sufficiently certain. He drew near, so to speak, to my soul with the delicacy of a physician who knows he must touch limbs that are sensitive and sore. And I told him everything at once, without reserve — all that was my past.

When I had finished, he lowered his eyes and withdrew into his reflections for a moment. Then, with a paternal affability: "The damaging admissions might be retracted. It is within your prerogative. But to what extent it would benefit you, I cannot say. You are accustomed to facing danger, and I prefer to speak plainly. The direct evidence gathered by the Police renders an acquittal unattainable. Were it only a matter of your own person and of your bearing before your compatriots, I should say to you: go to meet death majestically, all of a piece, blessing the Fatherland, cursing its oppressors. The People will accord you, in their great heart, the place that is your due. But the wise man—"

Here he drew away and explained at length what, in his view, a wise man would have done in my position. To rush headlong toward the scaffold was not to serve the Fatherland; nor would my sacrifice improve the situation of my fellow conspirators. And how would my aged parents, who had already suffered so greatly, endure a blow past all remedy? A young wife trembled for me, and I had made her a mother. Why not attempt every honourable means to live, when, by cheating the executioner, I should spare untold grief to those unhappy souls?

He went on at length, explaining what a wise man would have done in my position. To rush headlong toward the scaffold was not to serve the Fatherland; nor would my sacrifice improve the situation of my accomplices. I asked him for an explicit clarification of what he required of me. "You are free," he replied, "to attempt the means that might save you, and I ask of you only a formal promise to moderate your impulses, whatever the part I should judge it fitting to have you assume."

And this explains why, in the records of the proceedings, I emerge colourless, almost without a will of my own, easily swayed, driven to the terrible act by the iron mind of the leaders and by the morbid preoccupation of not appearing pusillanimous, of not being confused with the Rossi from Mantua, of not being taken for a spy.

I have never had, nor do I have, toward Mathieu de la Drôme anything but sentiments of filial gratitude. Unhappily, his noble efforts to "cheat the executioner" subsequently served as a convenient instrument of denigration for those ill-disposed toward me, blinded as they were by personal rancour or partisan rancour — not the least among the reasons which, for fifty years, kept me locked in a disdainful silence.

The evidence gathered by the Police was of itself sufficient to secure a conviction under the letter of the Law. To reinforce it with the admissions of the accused would have lent greater emphasis, greater theatrical effect, to the cleverness of the Police.

The better to achieve its end, it had begun its subtle work upon me, as I was the most jovial; then came the turn of Antonio Gomez, who was some years my senior. Before him, the examining magistrate triumphantly laid a letter of introduction obtained from Pieri, addressed to Orsini. Worse still, a letter written in his own hand to Orsini himself. In it he laboured to dispel certain suspicions that had insinuated themselves into the mistrustful mind of the leader, and, pressing for confirmation of the mission that had been promised him, he declared that he wished to take part in the conspiracy, moved solely by the love he had always borne and still bore to the common Fatherland.

Upon Italians, risen again to nationhood, falls the moral obligation of counting such an appeal and the sentiments that inspired it to the credit of Gomez — but before the French Police, could there have been a more compromising document?

As though this were not enough, Gomez was seen to set out, step by step, his every movement from the 6th of January, when he departed with Pieri from Birmingham, pausing first in London at the house of Miss Cheney, then in Lille, awaiting Pieri who made a foray to Brussels — and so on, up to the moment when he set foot for the first time in Orsini's furnished apartment at No. 10, rue Monthabor.

And here, as the final stroke, came the artful insinuation: "his accomplices had confessed their own part and his. What purpose was there in persisting with denial?"

After various reticences and various moves too desperate to pass for sound tactics, upon being told plainly that I, di Rudio, having admitted to throwing the second bomb, had revealed everything else, he ended by confessing to having thrown the first. But even in such extremity he was not abandoned by an instinct widespread among the population of southern Italy: that which leads a man to draw from his pocket the bag of *dabbenaggine* — simple-mindedness — and to smear it upon his face. He, who had been a soldier, tried to make it believed that he had thrown his projectile as a faithful servant, without having formed any precise notion of its deadly effects. It was upon this supposed simple-mindedness that his defence was founded.

A more arduous struggle it proved to be, in like straits, for Giuseppe Pieri. The police had placed before him several revealing letters; they had recounted, with indisputable precision, step by step, his movements before the attempt; his relations with Orsini, with Gomez, with Simon Bernard, with myself; his complicity in importing the unfilled projectiles into France, in the purchase of certain revolvers found near the scene of the attempt. Elisa Cheney pointing him out, and Hartmann denouncing him, had with their personal testimonies corroborated the written evidence. The arrest carried out within minutes and within steps of the attempt, the charged bomb in the pocket of his overcoat, the boasts that had escaped him after his arrest — could there have been a more encompassing net, a more complete story?

He added nothing to it, for there was nothing left to add. He attempted instead to embellish the facts with outlandish inventions and to draw from them deductions tending to distort their import.

"He had come to Paris solely to settle some family business and intended to go to Italy, where a revolution was imminent. He had discussed bombs with Orsini, but solely in order to use them in the Fatherland, on the barricades. And since he had expressed some doubt as to their efficacy, Orsini had handed him one; but with the understanding that they would set it off, by way of experiment, at the Barrière des Martyrs. Only Pieri had kept the appointment; and so the bomb had remained in his pocket and been found there upon his person. If on the evening of the attempt he had gone to the rue Le Pelletier, it was simply because he had been drawn there by the extraordinary illumination."

A shield assembled with sufficient cunning — but wasted effort! A few of his own written phrases and a little analysis were enough to breach it. He had the merit of perceiving this in time. Thereupon, citing as his pretext "the inquisitor's methods of the examining magistrate," he declared that he would answer no further questions; that he would refute the charges only during the actual trial, before those he regarded as his natural judges.

By this means he conjured away the danger of compromising himself still further. But was what awaited him truly the worst possible?

Nugent de Saint Laurent, who subsequently gave his full approval to Pieri's tactics, attempted to lend them greater weight by laying great emphasis on the animosity existing between Pieri himself and Orsini, as though to conclude from this that a conspiracy, and the fraternal relations it entails, were impossible between two men who cordially hated each other. Unhappily, the reason for the ill-feeling was not made clear; what was made quite clear, however, was that it was of too recent a date to have had any dissolving force upon the conspiracy.
