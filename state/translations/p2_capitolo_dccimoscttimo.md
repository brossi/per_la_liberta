THE twenty-fifth of February arrived, the day appointed for the conduct of the trial. I was brought to the Palais de Justice and, for the first time since the evening of the attempt, I saw my companions in misfortune fully before me.

The courtroom was thronged with men and ladies. There was a splendour of attire... people had come to the trial as to the theatre. Yet, through the perception of some mysterious and indefinable sense, I understood at once that the manifold spirit of that audience was not hostile to us. Hostile, on the contrary — deliberately hostile — were the individuals seated upon the bench and the handful of hireling attendants that surrounded them. These men perhaps imagined themselves to be the Empire and its power, and regarded our attempt as one regards a personal offence.

The trial was, at its core, a repetition of the preliminary inquiry — revised and corrected. The right of defence exists for the virtuous and exists for the wicked, and I should be a fool were I to reproach the French judicial authorities for not having pronounced a eulogy upon the accused; but equally foolish would be he who, blinded by the prestige inherent in social position and possessed of the instincts of a fetish-worshipper, were to take the opinions expressed by those same authorities as the unit of measure by which to judge us.

From the first unfolding of History down to our own days, privilege has been compelled, in order to subsist, to reinforce tyranny with artifice. Tyranny is, by its very nature, intermittent and does not reach everywhere. It makes good its deficiency through a discipline refined across the centuries, which manifests itself in various ways according to time and custom; but which aims constantly to portray the rulers of a State or a religion — at least so long as they live — as arks of genius, arks of sanctity; which aims, at the same time, to portray the refractory who refuse to enter the orbit of those great ones, those saints, as fanatics, as degenerate and wicked creatures.

This discipline creates the insubstantial larvae, rosy and phosphorescent, that draw the multitudes along tortuous paths; it creates the terrifying phantoms that drive them back, with senseless fears, from the straight roads. It was this discipline that rendered the Grand Lama inaccessible, lest the eye that sees, analyses, and judges should discern, in his flesh and in his spirit, the defects inseparable from human nature. It induced the Sultans to cause themselves to be preceded and followed by a pack of assassins whirling their scimitars. The bold ones who dared raise inquiring eyes to the demigod provoked their own instantaneous death.

It has taught, on the other hand, that the rancour against rebellious souls must continue even after execution. To sever the heads that refused to bow is not enough; they must be exposed, bleeding, to the mockery of the unthinking. To burn the thinker at the stake is not enough; a fierce malediction must be pronounced over his ashes, and those ashes must be scattered to the winds.

Austria would have been naïve had she contented herself with hanging the Italian patriots. She buried them without funeral rites and without coffin, in out-of-the-way pits, as was the practice with the carcasses of four-footed beasts.

It is that baleful discipline, modified by the times, which brands and persecutes as a crime the honest expression of an honest thought; and where certain extremes are no longer possible, the livid war against reputation and memory is still very much in vogue. Which war, if it be useful to privilege more or less legally constituted, becomes an indispensable necessity for privilege that has attained its station through violence and crime.

At my age one records facts; one does not vent rancours. In the trial mounted against the authors of the attempt of the fourteenth of January, the denigration aimed at our detriment was all the more necessary inasmuch as the very origin of the Empire was, more or less openly, under discussion. We sat in the dock facing Napoleon; but Napoleon sat in the dock facing France and the civilised world. Every nobility of spirit on his part, every loftiness of purpose, became an aggravation of our guilt; every loftiness of purpose on our part tended to diminish him — it was a condemnation of him.

Nor must it go unsaid that violent action sometimes gives rise to incidents which demonstrate it more efficacious than any other form of propaganda. To blacken us, to make us pass for malefactors of the lowest order, to diminish the likelihood of finding imitators — this, therefore, had to be the undeclared yet essential purpose of the trial. I acknowledge that, from their own point of view, the French judicial authorities acted wisely in attempting to decapitate, along with our bodies, our reputations. I acknowledge, at the same time, that they flung themselves into the enterprise with all the sinister dexterity of the worms of the Second Empire.

The bill of indictment, formulated and read by the Imperial Procurator Chaix-d'Est-Ange, slid deftly over the initial uncertainties and the police machinations; it portrays the examining authorities with the bearing of genius that surveys, dissects, and conquers. It suppresses, on the other hand, every impulse of ours that might reveal a character above the common, and, by insisting upon the most readily censurable aspects of our conduct and giving emphasis to our weaknesses, it burdens us with commentary — often wholly fantastical — that savours of cowardice.

The President of the Tribunal, Monsieur Delangle, for his part, laboured with viperous persistence to bestow upon the Procurator's semi-literary creation its proper embellishments, and concealed its improbabilities by introducing incidents which, even had they been true, would have had no connection whatsoever with our conspiracy. Worse still: he cast "charitably" over the threadbare garment of disinterestedness the broad mantle of the hired assassin; he stamped the brand of the charlatan upon a dying virtue.

An easy task, after all! We lived in times of intense passions; times in which the attractions and repulsions of souls succeeded one another with the violence of the tempest. What man who took any active part in those times, however great or small, has not found himself treated — to his own amazement — now worse than the devil, now better than a saint; the object, often simultaneously, of profound veneration and immeasurable hatred? It is readily understood that the Police could not have been wanting in mud to rake through.

Thus upon Giuseppe Pieri the President Delangle poured, in Paris in the year 1858, the hatred of the Tuscan reactionaries which had pursued him since 1830; the hatred which, by painting him as a beast of liberalism, had succeeded in having him stripped of his colonelcy; the hatred which had seized upon a prank among *scapigliati* — free-spirited rogues who had relieved another such rogue of a watch worth a few *baiocchi* — as a pretext for his infamy.

The generosity of spirit, the pride, the indignant impulses of Felice Orsini were branded as "ridiculous ostentation." And when Orsini persisted in maintaining that the third bomb had not been thrown by him, but by the Italian to whom he had entrusted it a few minutes before the attempt, Delangle demonstrated his serene impartiality in the search for truth by breaking forth and portraying him as a liar who was anxious to make it believed that his hands were free of the blood of the children, the women, the old men wounded and killed; "because the smell of that blood rose to him and went to his head."

To complete his meritorious work in the service of lofty and moral principles, President Delangle then lent a complaisant ear to the whispers of the sacristy, and Felice Orsini — Commissioner of the Roman Republic in a distant province, who, surrounded by foreign enemies and weakened by enemies within, maintained his phalanx through requisitioning — was portrayed to the jurors as a common thief who had subsequently escaped, by flight, the delights of the pontifical *ergastolo* — life imprisonment.

As concerns myself personally, Delangle had not the shadow of a pretext for reproaching me with either disreputable family connections or misappropriations real or apparent; but this was not to render me immune to his venom. I had revealed how and why, on the evening of the fourteenth of January, Felice Orsini had handed us the sum of two hundred francs. My disclosure was intended to make our judges understand that venality was not in our nature. But I was to discover that I had not reckoned with the refined malice of my judges. That slender drop, in the midst of such a crisis, sufficed for the incorruptible Magistrate — assuming an air of indignant surprise — to accuse me of having, I who belonged to an honourable family, abandoned school, abhorred honest labour, and so degraded myself by degrees as to become a mercenary assassin for two hundred francs.

A word on the records of the proceedings. The tactic of denigration could not arrest itself at the margin of the written documents, as our fabled progenitors halted at the threshold of paradise lost. The records were retouched and mutilated and distorted, like everything else, at the pleasure of His Excellency.

An example: when President Delangle asked whether we wished to adduce anything further before the imminent application of the sentence, Gomez and Orsini replied: "Nothing!" Pieri observed that he had shed no blood. I pointed out Kim, the municipal street-sweeper who, filing past with the other witnesses, had delivered his false testimony. And I denounced him aloud, protesting that in pronouncing sentence, justice required that no weight be given to a perjured deposition.

The official record states that, contrary to my three companions — unanimous in pronouncing their "nothing" — I had implored "the clemency of my judges." The meeting of Francesco Crispi with Orsini? I should be lying were I to say that I did not deny it. But one must remember that Orsini himself had, a few seconds before, declared that the Italian of the third bomb was not known to any of his accomplices, not even to Pieri; that he had revealed his existence to no one; that none of us had so much as laid eyes upon him.

Was this not almost openly suggesting to me my line of conduct? And was not, moreover, that denial of mine the most expeditious means of avoiding insidious questions and stratagems that might have compromised a man who remained unsuspected and at liberty? Had the charlatans of the Empire not felt in their souls the terror of the truth, they would not have imposed upon the newspapers the strict requirement of adhering, in their reports, solely to what was published in *Le Droit* and the *Gazette des Tribunaux*. A halved truth is no longer truth at all, and a raison d'État that does not scruple to adulterate truth by paring it down, will not hold itself back from scruple when it serves to deck it out like a strumpet, or to line its skirts with a few tendentious little falsehoods. Let the man who swears by the infallible sanctity of the powerful believe blindly in official records — with that simplicity of soul which leads others to believe in the infallibility of the hypocrite into whose ear a dove would coo the truth. Infinite and manifold is human folly!

But the prudent man who does not allow any broth to flow into his intellect without first filtering it will certainly not fail to reflect that we find ourselves, at the very least, in the unfavourable light of those whose history has been written by their own enemies, at a time and in circumstances that led those enemies to believe our every refutation impossible.

The sentence was pronounced on the night of the twenty-sixth, and almost as though to heighten the theatrical quality of that denouement, as the Court — its deliberations concluded — re-entered the great hall of the audience chamber, the clock of the Palais de Justice struck its chimes. To Gomez, life at the galleys; to Pieri, to myself, to Felice Orsini, the penalty reserved for parricides. Luigi Napoleon had become, after having been cannonaded by him, the father of his people... History is full of these sanguinary and yet clumsy ironies! And lest any doubt remain as to the nature of that penalty, its description was there and then read aloud: "led to the scaffold, in a shirt, barefoot, the head covered by a black veil; then the reading of the sentence... then decapitation."

I looked into the faces of my companions in faith and in misfortune. Gomez, deathly pale, maintained a marble immobility... the grief of the condemnation and the natural satisfaction of having escaped the worst seemed to cancel one another out in his heart. Pieri swayed his head and kept his eye fixed upon the President with an indefinable mingling of contempt and commiseration. I noticed that his lips were moving, and I had the impression that he was rehearsing, in that moment, a certain phrase he had addressed to Delangle himself during the interrogation: "I knew well enough that nothing good would ever come of dealing with that obstinate fellow!"

Felice Orsini turned his eyes slowly from one judge to another. He seemed to say, without the slightest trace of bravado: "Your meanness does not touch me!"

As for my own state of mind... what am I to say? I had long foreseen the condemnation; now that the foresight had been fulfilled, I struggled to possess its full consciousness, to grasp its full import. It was to assure myself that I was indeed awake that I had looked into the faces of my companions. All at once there rose vividly before my mind the image of Elisa — not fresh and smiling, but as I had kissed her for the last time — slumped in her chair, eyes vacant, bereft of her senses, as though the tribunal of the Seine had pronounced her condemnation and not mine.

That image fixed itself in my mind so relentlessly that it left me no room to see or think of anything else. As we passed through the underground passage that led us back to the Conciergerie, I roused myself and, turning to Orsini, who followed a few paces behind me, I said: "You who have means — remember them!" He understood, and murmured: "I promise you." The prison door had scarcely closed behind me when I experienced a strange sensation, as though I were being plunged into an almost familiar quietude. Saturno and the other guard already knew everything... they had lost their wager, and they had prepared champagne. But they wished for the chance of a return match. If the sentence were not quashed by the Supreme Court, or by a decree of clemency, they would pay another bottle; otherwise I should pay. Never did a wagerer so ardently desire to lose.

And so, by a caprice of fate, conversing gaily and raising our glasses, I ended the night on which I had been condemned to the guillotine. How often have I wished to know how my Judges, Napoleon III, the street-sweeper Kim, and the others who had contributed to saving the social order and the usurped Empire all ended their days... but my desire has always remained unsatisfied.
