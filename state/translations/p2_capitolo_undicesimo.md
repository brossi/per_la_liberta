CHAPTER ELEVEN

SCARCELY had I found myself out of danger, when my first thought turned to Elisa. I had made my way toward the Boulevard des Italiens… and entered a café. I must relate things that will seem trifling; yet they carried their own weight. The café was empty. The proprietress, behind the counter, was speaking animatedly with a customer who stood facing her. She did not see me enter, nor notice my presence, until I asked her, if she would be so kind, for paper and an inkwell. I wrote briefly: "Elisa, all is well. I shall depart tomorrow for Oporto." Having sealed the letter, I returned the inkwell to the counter and asked for an eight-sou stamp. The lady rummaged in a drawer and asked, handing them to me, whether four two-sou stamps would not serve as well.

I made my way to the Place de la Concorde. It was deserted. I stood my watch upon the bridge for a long while. The thousand sounds of the city reached me in a confused murmur, the hum of an immense hive. The Seine, grown darker by the restless reflection of the lanterns, flowed toward the sea with an air of mystery, as befitted the hour that hung over us. Not one of the conspirators appeared. When to wait any longer would have been madness, I rid myself of the revolver by casting it into the river, and set off toward the Boulevard, resolved to reconnoitre the ground.

During that journey, every faint breath of conversation caught in passing struck within my breast like a gust of tempest. The noble audacities, the boundless hopes, the stubborn insistence on glimpsing a thread of blue in the darkest of situations; the instinctive faith in the slow yet certain justice of things — all of it remained as if uprooted, reduced to dust, and scattered into nothingness. Napoleon III was alive!…

Sheltered behind the iron plates of his carriage, he had remained there as though paralysed until the explosion of the third bomb… Then, all at once, he appeared at the theatre, erect in the imperial box, ashen as a dead man. At his side stood Eugenia Montijo, exceedingly pale but beautiful still, with a few drops of blood tracing crimson lines across her brow. At the frantic applause that broke out on every side, she had let herself fall back into her chair, her breast heaving as though she had, at that very instant, recovered the only atmosphere capable of breathing life into her. But Napoleon had remained standing. The sense of being once more Emperor had restored colour to that pulsing cadaver, who now bowed with studied grace to right and left.

Outside, meanwhile, the Police and the most trusted battalions were securing his road back to the Palace.

Thus fell the intended revolution; thus the Empire remained, in all its integrity, stronger perhaps than before. Napoleon III clinging to France more than ever, like a vampire; France interposed more than ever, in my understanding, between Italy and her independence and her greatness. And I asked myself, trembling, whether it were an inexorable law of Nature that the vulgarity and the treachery and the corruption and the greed of the few must hold eternal dominion in Europe; whether noble aspirations derided, persecution, misery, and the hangman's noose must be, for ever and ever, the only inheritance of the honest.

And yet — yet not as one who, paralysed by fate, does no more than bite his own hands. Not a single fibre was there within me, in those moments, that was not swollen with turbulent spirit, that was not all one impulse of rebellion. Had occasion offered, I feel I could have killed even without the highest of aims; simply as a human protest; to die, perhaps, but in vindicating the humble and the oppressed…

I resolved to return to the Hôtel de France et Champagne. On the way there I had occasion to convince myself that the situation was growing murky… *Pardon*, public conscience, as it manifested itself in the clusters of people gathered here and there, was already attributing the attempt to foreigners. It was no longer merely a question of resuming the unequal struggle; one had also to think of saving one's neck. And perhaps from that very moment I might have seen to my own safety, had it not seemed to me an act of cowardice to depart without ascertaining at least the fate of my room-mate.

Nothing out of the ordinary at the hotel. Pieri had not returned… the room perfectly in the order in which we had left it. I prepared my valise, the one quilted with the tiger pattern; went down to the office and asked for my bill. From that moment events precipitated.

While the proprietress was verifying the date of my arrival in the register, I settled myself at a small table and began to leaf through an illustrated newspaper. Just then, several individuals entered noisily — plainclothes policemen; the uniform cut of their hair was enough to reveal them to me. They requested leave to inspect the register. One of them, the apparent chief, bending toward the lady, murmured something. The lady indicated with the tip of her finger a point in the register. A dozen eyes fixed themselves upon me with such intensity that I felt their gaze driven into my skin, and the *Maître d'Hôtel* came forward all deference and asked for my passport. I drew it out and unfolded it… the policemen took possession of it, glanced it over, exchanged looks. "*Pardon, monsieur!*" And they departed.

They departed… but from my position, from the corner of my eye, I saw two hounds remain on guard at the entrance. Was I to consider myself a prisoner within that hotel? The proprietress came with the bill and I was compelled to sustain the conversation in a gallant vein which she was pleased to strike up. All at once I sighed; though not for the reason perhaps imagined by my charming temptress. Glancing beyond the window, I had seen the policemen moving away. The way was clear!

And I took advantage of it. Slowly at first, so as not to arouse suspicion; then, once I was quite certain I was not being watched, away at nearly a forced march.

By now I was persuaded that Pieri had made arrangements to quit Paris. I come upon a public cab and engage it: "Station of Orléans… a handsome tip if you arrive in time!" The coachman whips up with such zeal that it seems he means to flay the hide from his nag. We arrive… it is eleven o'clock… four minutes late!… no train for Bordeaux until four in the morning!

What was to be done? Doze at the station? Seek hospitality at the Zappa household, or with some other trusted friend? I decided to return to the hotel. The Police had already poked their nose in there… a circumstance which seemed to me sufficient, in itself, to render it an inviolable sanctuary.

And so I set off on foot, my valise hanging from my arm. I had covered some five hundred paces, more or less, when there came at a full gallop a squadron of municipal guards. It swept past me like a whirlwind, slowed at the station, surrounded it, and enclosed the passengers within a ring of iron. I thought: "If the bird of the wood was slow in taking flight, the hunters are equally slow in springing their trap." At midnight I was in my room. I had left orders to be woken in good time; I had turned off the gas; I had stretched out, as was my custom in such circumstances, without undressing in the least. And already I was promising myself to draw sound counsel from the night, when I heard the bell at the entrance ring. The window of my room gave on to the courtyard. I leap to it, and down below, deep in the shadows, I made out a dark knot of figures about which I could harbour no illusions whatsoever. In the silence of the night, there reached my ear the words: "We wish to see the foreigner in number 53!" The number of my room.

In haste I undressed and burrowed beneath the bedclothes. The better to display a tranquil spirit, I had left the door ajar. The guards arrived accompanied by the *Maître d'Hôtel*; they entered by the thin light of a candle, lit the gas… I felt a gentle touch upon one shoulder. At that I ceased to snore. "*Excusez-nous! — nous devons faire notre devoir.* Your passport, if you please?" They examined it minutely, then looked at the *Maître d'Hôtel*: "But is this not the gentleman with whom we spoke an hour ago, in your office?" A night for misunderstandings, truly! "*Pardon, monsieur!*" I heard their footsteps recede along the corridor and fade away down the stairs.

But it was fated that I should not rest in my bed. Half an hour later they were back, and this time they stood upon no ceremony. "A certain Monsieur Pierey," they said, "who lodged in this very room, declares himself to be a friend of yours. He is at the Prefecture, in safe keeping. You must resign yourself to keeping him company. We have orders to arrest you!"

While I dressed, two policemen stood at my sides, at attention; others busied themselves forcing open our wardrobe. From it they drew the revolver case, which Pieri had tossed there, and some bullets. "What is this?"

— I don't know! The wardrobe is for the exclusive use of the gentleman who lodged in this room. Of my own I have nothing but this bag.

They felt it, rummaged through it, drew out, item by item, the few things it contained; but the dagger concealed between the inner lath and the false bottom escaped their scrutiny.

On arriving at the Prefecture, yet another blow awaited me. I had been conveyed there in a cab, with two guardian angels inside with me in white, a third seated on the box beside the coachman, and a fourth behind the carriage. I was about to alight and had not yet set foot on the step, when another heavily loaded carriage entered the courtyard at a trot. I made for the staircase and, seized by a grim presentiment, turned my head. A few paces away, preceded and followed by a guard, came a man who at that moment surely carried within his breast all the sorrows of his Italy. He was pale of face, with dark side-whiskers, his head swathed in bandages… Felice Orsini!
