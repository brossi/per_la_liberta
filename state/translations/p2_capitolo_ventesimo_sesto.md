## Revised Translation — Chapter Twenty-Six

CHAPTER TWENTY-SIX

I COULD not recount, in perfect chronological order and as though reading from a ship's log, the manifold vicissitudes of our arduous trial. I shall say briefly of those which most properly determined its course.

The storms, invoked by Cousin, came — and came with such fury as to imperil vessels far sturdier than our own. It was not the sea alone we had to contend with; it was sea and sky in league together, battering us with a persistence that seemed almost to betray some deliberate intent.

To comprehend the full intensity of our danger, one would need to be seasoned in the art of navigation. For those who are, no description is needed; for those who are not, no description suffices. For two days and three nights we found ourselves in worse straits than those unhappy souls condemned to labour in those subterranean dungeons where water enters in cunningly measured jets. Either work the pump without respite, or drown.

It was then that I understood in all its weight the phrase: "And the floodgates of heaven were opened." Whoever was not keeping every fibre, every atom of himself grappled to the oars that held us in balance, toiled feverishly to bail out the boat. For this purpose our palmetto hats served us well; but the storm had left several of us bare-headed — these resorted to the only implement still within their reach: their wooden clogs. In such a predicament, no resource, however slight, was to be neglected.

The driving rain, the waves that rendered any distant view impossible, and above all the profound darkness, had yet another grave and unexpected peril in store for us on the third night. Blacker than the night itself, and like a phantom rising up threateningly from the abyss, we found ourselves face to face with a great sailing vessel. Cousin had barely time to wrench the tiller; but the surge was so violent that for an instant we believed ourselves swamped and finished. The fearsome apparition swept past us, vast and swift; then it vanished, as though it had dissolved into the darkness.

Not even in this circumstance did Cousin lose his composure, and after noting that in all probability it had been a Dutch vessel, he added: "Courage, lads! In this darkness, I'll wager I can slip between the legs of Death without letting Him catch hold of us!"

I reflected that we owed our salvation to the fever that had confined Morelli to the hospital — had the little craft been but slightly heavier, no human strength would have sufficed to keep her afloat. After that encounter, my companions saw a phantom on the crest of every wave.

The following day, the storm abated. Hoisting the sail once more, we were at last able to think of restoring our exhausted strength. Through the long hours of danger we had barely swallowed the odd sip of rum, and our vigour had been sustained, such as it was, by the peril itself. Now that the tempest had subsided on every side, a tempestuous hunger rose in its stead. But a terrible disillusionment awaited us: the salt water had partly swept away, and partly ruined, our provisions, rendering them all but useless.

The horrors of cannibalism then darkly flashed across our imaginations, and into the air burst imprecations fit to shame those attributed to the damned of hell. Cousin judged it not merely opportune but urgent to change course, and he turned the prow to the south-west — our natural heading.

That very day we sighted a long streak of dense smoke in the distance, a few spans above the surface of the water — the *Abeille*, perhaps, hastening westward in pursuit of man. We lowered the sail and for an hour crouched in great anxiety at the bottom of the dinghy. At dusk, the evil streak disappeared below the horizon. Two days later we were coasting the Continent.

At what point? Did our anxious eyes scan a hospitable shore, or did it hold greater perils than those we had overcome with such effort?

For a long stretch we saw no trace of human life; then suddenly, rounding a headland, there opened before us a small, tranquil cove, and on the beach a little hut, and around the hut several men — some labouring to move baskets and barrels, others spreading nets. Still others, seated on the sand, were mending meshes.

We bent to the oars and came ashore. While we were hauling the boat up onto dry land, a dozen Black men gathered around us, their bearing not openly hostile, yet that of people who harbour some suspicion. Among them was a white man, dressed in European fashion and armed with a rifle.

I understood at once that I was among slaves set to catching fish and drying them, working under the lash of the white man — of the civilised man, the believer in Christ.

I asked him whether, for payment, it was possible to obtain some provisions. He knew French and understood me. But his entire reply was: "Where is your money?"

I made my few coins clink and, pressing the first question, added: "What land is this?"

— Dutch Guiana! The land that had driven Pianori's associates back to certain death. My companions felt hunger no more, felt weariness no more. They ran to the dinghy and leapt in, ready to face afresh whatever trials might come — so potent, even in them, was the love of liberty!

We sailed westward again: the wind that blew was laden with the fragrances of the Continent. We reckoned that the *Abeille* had already passed that way and was leaving us ever further behind with every stroke of her piston. Cousin calculated moreover that British Guiana was not far distant, and our dinghy ran so swiftly that it seemed more than reasonable to count the end of our torments as near at hand.

Then suddenly the favourable wind dropped; the boat slowed, dipped her prow, and moved no more — as though some mysterious hand, having seized her by the keel, had held her forcibly back. I looked instinctively at Cousin. He was pale, cadaverous. To my silent interrogation he answered: "We are lost!" A simple and terrible thing had occurred — we were caught in a *vase*.

But we were not men to submit to our fate without first fighting with every ounce of our strength. And Cousin himself, after that first moment of dismay, having recovered all his composure, gave us the order to jump into the water and range ourselves around the dinghy to support it with our shoulders. "If the tide goes out," he said, "commend your souls to God — if you believe you have a soul, and if you believe in God. If the tide comes in — perhaps — well, not all hope would be lost!"

For a couple of hours we remained in the tenacious mud; up to our knees at first; then, little by little, up to our waists, as the water alternately rose and fell. The mosquitoes, fortunately, did not swarm; but the crabs were not slow to torment us with their pincers. There were moments of impotent fury bordering on delirium; moments of grim phantoms barely dispelled by my hardy philosophy. To be eaten alive, in small morsels, or to be devoured one by one by the thousand ravening monsters. What became, in that unequal struggle, of human pride? The longer the minutes passed, the more closely the invisible enemy pressed upon us. I had found myself exposed to many and grave dangers, both before and after; but it was reserved for the dagger of a compatriot and for the loathsome crustaceans of Guiana to pierce my flesh so as to leave thereon indelible scars.

It was our good fortune that the tide rose. The boat we had been supporting became at last our support. We clambered in — filthy with mud, bleeding, spent!
