CHAPTER SEVENTEEN

IN LOCARNO, where I had returned to deliver a missive from Mazzini to Calvi, I was arrested and released a few hours later, at liberty, on my word to present myself to the Federal Delegate. I was later tried for conspiracy against a friendly Power, expelled from Switzerland, and put to the alternative of choosing between America and England.

England was closer to the field of probable future enterprises and was preferred by me. I settled in London, where I found not a few of those compromised in the February uprisings, who had escaped Austrian vengeance through adventurous flights... among others, a Milanese Jean Valjean, accompanied out of the city walls in a coffin and with a solemn funeral.

Bound by our common ideal and common sufferings, we lived for some time in community, in the district called Baldwin's Garden, and precisely in the modest inn of the Milanese Tressoldi, a good-natured fellow who possessed, among his other virtues, that of turning a blind eye when we could not settle the bill.

Giuseppe Mazzini arrived in London in April. From him we learned that Pianciani, who was then dictating his "History of the Popes," tempered the fatigues of the intellect by modifying and re-modifying the garden of his villa... a probability of work for several of us. We were presented to his amanuensis, a certain Bardini from Padova, a law student, and he, in turn, presented us to Pianciani who, there and then, entrusted the material labour of those incessant modifications to the young Varisco, granting him ample faculty to avail himself of the work of as many of us as he deemed necessary. I too was immediately engaged, and thus, for some months, I drew my sustenance from the spade and the flowers.

That simple and laborious life was troubled by terrible images. Austria was growing ever more ferocious, and every newspaper, every letter brought us the painful certainty that the phalanx of the generous was being inexorably thinned.

I spoke of it on long walks with Giuseppe Mazzini, and I would fix my gaze upon that olive-hued, intellectual face, as if to say: "What is to be decided? Shall we let them be slaughtered like dogs?" The Master would quicken his pace. His expression was one of ineffable melancholy; but of the thoughts that swirled in his head... not a syllable!

One day in August, he sent for me with urgency. Why ever for! Certainly not to say "good day" or "good evening." So then?..

I planted the spade in the middle of a flowerbed: "Stay there! You are not the only noble instrument!" And off I ran.

But when I stood before the Master, I felt my arms fall to my sides, so sparing of words was his welcome. I was asked to carry a letter to Kossuth and to await the reply. Not another syllable!

Kossuth then held, near Regent's Circus, a sort of Court. At the door I found carriages and servants in livery; up the stairs, a coming and going of staid Englishmen in formal attire; Hungarians in their elegant, militaresque national costume; people with broad scars; uncertain figures, begging under the pretext of the Fatherland. Honest and dishonest, rich or poor, they rarely obtained access to the Dictator who, even beaten and in exile, exercised not only the prestige of his fame, but also that which comes from the habit of command.

While I awaited his pleasure in the antechamber, I tried to pass the time observing the paintings that hung from the walls: Kossuth in the guise of the simple citizen; Kossuth in hunting attire; Kossuth in a general's uniform — now in an armchair, now on horseback, now beside a shattered cannon; but always Kossuth, nothing but Kossuth!

Finally, from among so many simulacra, the great man appeared in the flesh. I must admit that art, in its intent to idealise him, had slandered him. The figure of the illustrious Magyar, more than affected heroism and slaughter, recalled paternal affections and the gentle emotions of the domestic hearth. It is the privilege of artists to alter, at their whim, the colours of History... for this reason Napoleon I is handed down to posterity crossing the Saint Bernard on a prancing colt, with manifest injustice to the poor donkey that lent its back to so great a weight.

The welcome reserved for me was not what the Count di Rudio had expected — nor the Italian patriot, nor the trusted envoy of Giuseppe Mazzini. Kossuth read the letter I handed him while standing on the threshold and, casting it aside, drew a memorandum from his pocket, wrote a few lines, and ordered me, curtly, curtly, to carry them to their destination. Not a word of greeting! And I, descending the stairs, asked myself whether the Cossacks of the Czar and the Croats of Jellacich, by lending their strength to Austria, had not, in substance, spared the brave Hungarians the vexations attendant upon a change of dynasty.

The few lines consigned to me by Kossuth were addressed to a high officer whose name I am now seeking in the recesses of memory. From him I received several rolls of sterling... "The same," he told me, "that we received from Mazzini for our participation in the movements of 1853. May they serve your great, unfortunate country!"

Kossuth, on the morrow of that failure, had publicly denied all complicity in those movements!

Upon my return, the Master opened his soul to me. He began by asking whether I felt disposed to place my life once more in jeopardy for the cause of the Fatherland and, having received the answer from my very bearing, he pointed me to a chair, and we conversed in low voices, the one opposite the other, for over an hour.

He explained to me that, on the counsel of Fortunato Calvi, the future insurrectionary attempts were to unfold in the region of the Alps. The reasons he adduced are the following: Our country is rich in latent forces, ready to burst forth; but whoever takes an initiative must know how to give assurance of serious resistance. The mountain is the natural bulwark of the weak and the oppressed. There it becomes possible for the few to hold their ground against the many; possible to escape pursuit; to descend with the swiftness of the eagle upon the plain; to surprise, to intercept. For the pitched battle, which was denied to us, it was necessary to substitute guerrilla warfare, which is necessarily the daughter of the mountains. If the banner of redemption could be kept upright and flying for three, for two months, there would not be a patriot without fever in his blood, not a corner of Italy where the flame of action would not blaze.

This last thought seemed to lead him into another order of reflection. A shadow spread across his face; then he said, almost in these very words: "I know! Cowardice, masked as prudence, accuses me of driving spirited youth to the gallows, of provoking desperate attempts that offer Austrian tyranny, and that which is home-bred, the opportunity to intensify their cruelties. But they do not reflect that the audacity and sacrifice of the few always end by shaking the torpid from their lethargy and driving the many to action. Nothing great without audacity! Napoleon III would in all probability never have smoothed his path to the Empire without the failures of Strasbourg and of Boulogne. No King of Italy will show spirit so great as to champion the redemption of our Fatherland, so long as Italians show themselves to be basking, in peace, in slavery. Would Carlo Alberto have crossed the Ticino were it not for the magnanimous self-abnegation of the Milanese! It is an imperative necessity, this keeping the great Italian question open and pressing. Europe cannot have peace if our agony endures. She will intervene, sooner or later, in our favour, and the sooner the more we show ourselves intolerant of the yoke and menacing. And what use are sound ideas, vigorous intentions, when want of initiative relegates them to the realm of mere abstractions! Action cleanses our centuries-old faults. Between us and a free Italy lies this baptism of blood. Should we hold back for the slanders of cowards!"

That very evening I procured a passport under an assumed name and set out for Switzerland. Fortunato Calvi was already awaiting me in Zurich. "The plan for the new movements is his," Mazzini had told me. "It falls to him to see to its execution. You will receive the necessary instructions from his own mouth."

In Brussels, where I made a brief halt, I delivered a letter from the Master to dall'Ungaro, the Venetian Catullus. "Where is the Master, what is he doing?" he asked me, almost feverishly. And I, in his own verses:

*"Mazzini è in sul loco ove si trema*
*"Che giunga a' traditor l'ora suprema;*
*"Mazzini è in ogni loco ove si opera*
*"Versar il sangue per l'Italia intera!"*

["Mazzini is in the place where men tremble / lest the supreme hour come upon the traitors; / Mazzini is in every place where men labour / to shed their blood for all of Italy!"]

Having arrived in Zurich, I wished, before going to Calvi, to have my hands free and hastened to pay a visit to de Boni, to whom I likewise delivered a missive; a third I carried to Caronti of Como. Giuseppe Mazzini had charged me to be very much on my guard with this man, of whom he was highly suspicious. He did, however, contribute sums of some considerable amount to the good cause, and this restrained Mazzini from being rid of him.

The brazenly inquisitive questions that Caronti directed at me came to me, already ill-disposed as I was, as a sharp irritant, and when I set foot once more beyond the threshold of his villa, I felt a relief as if I had suddenly been freed from a nightmare.

At last I could restore myself in the embrace of an authentic patriot, of an untarnished conscience... Fortunato Calvi! What joy in seeing him again, still handsome, still proud, still exhaling that indefinable sense of serenity which breathes forth from honest souls!

The substantial part of our colloquy had a beginning which I shall recount without false modesty.

"You are not," he said to me, "and you must not be, an instrument for blindly executing orders you do not comprehend. I shall confide to you the plan I have devised and which the European Revolutionary Committee has fully approved. Thus you will know how to parry, with unity of purpose, the inevitable counterblows. If we succeed in something concrete, you will be my aide-de-camp; if we do not succeed... we shall, in all probability, be made equal in our rewards... martyrdom!" My preliminary charge was to proceed into the valley of Treviso and make contact with certain smugglers, surnamed the Burrasca brothers, so as to induce them to carry a large consignment of munitions into the Agordino. I then had to cross the border, enter Belluno clandestinely, and make contact with Don Bastiano Barozzi, who had returned thither and who was expecting me. Barozzi knew of certain arms hidden in 1848 upon the return of the oppressors. In the Cadore, rebellious spirits abounded who had covered themselves with glory in its defence; the name and exhortation of Fortunato Calvi, who had been the guiding genius of that defence, would have rekindled in their breasts the slumbering enthusiasm. It was necessary to distribute among them the old arms. A band of exiles awaited the signal to descend from the Alps... Fortunato Calvi, leading them, would direct their fortunes.

The Austrian colossus was to be struck precisely at Belluno, and with a threefold surprise: at the Finance Office, at the Gendarmerie, and at the Barracks of the regular troops.

The Finance Office, situated near the old church of Santo Stefano, a solid building erected to serve as a convent, frames a vast courtyard with porticoes. Between one column and the next extends a parapet, so low that a man, creeping along it and on all fours, cannot be spotted by a sentry who, even while keeping watch, patrols the other parts of the arcade. The main gate had no doors and was left unguarded; the custodians of the building, a small body of militia quartered at the far end of the courtyard, to the right... three men in all and a corporal.

In the dead of night, a handful of our men in ambush near the gate; one bold spirit to creep, barefoot, along the parapet and take the sentry from behind... a hushed signal... men come running!... the three militiamen sleeping are rendered powerless. A bold enterprise, not an arduous one!

It was autumn, the season of tax collection; the coffers of the Administration, according to reliable calculations, were to contain a million and a half florins. To seize them... the sinews of war!

Simultaneously, another band of patriots was to conceal themselves near the Gendarmes' Barracks. They were to simulate a brawl and ring the bell of the Gendarmerie, as if to summon assistance. The guard who opens the door is to be dispatched. The lair is to be stormed, and the thirty manaclers sleeping there compelled to pass, by force, into the category of the manacled.

The garrison of Belluno at that time consisted of one hundred and fifty soldiers. The barracks were approached, to the right and to the left, by a circular ramping road — a *tourniquet*, as the engineers call it — flanked by trees and benches, a favourite resort of nursemaids and urchins. As a general rule, when the alarm sounded, the small garrison would emerge, in straggling fashion, from the main gate and draw up on the esplanade, awaiting orders. It was necessary to conceal a body of picked marksmen along the edges of the ramping road, to sound the alarm, and to shoot down as many soldiers as should come running.

The threefold surprise accomplished, the insurgents were to take possession of the civil and ecclesiastical authorities... hostages! Then we seize the means of transport, don the Austrian uniform, and proceed in military fashion to the fortress of Osoppo in Friuli, taking prisoners and compelling all whom we encounter on the road to follow us, so as to forestall any indiscretion.

At this point Fortunato Calvi, who had been speaking throughout in a low voice, almost as though meditating upon his own phrases, grew animated and said vibratingly: "We approach the fortress preceded by the Austrian flag and beating the drum, as friends. Once across the bridge, once the foot is set beneath the archway, the scene changes — the Latin valour bursts forth. On the ramparts, twenty-two of our own, we raise our flag, and woe betide whoever assails it! The hostages remain with us, with us the arms, the money, and the audacity. Osoppo becomes our base of operations, and it is an impregnable stronghold. Through the winter we organise the guerrilla warfare in the Alps, we hold in check the troops that would descend into the Peninsula. In the spring, Lombardy, Venetia, Hungary, France perhaps as well, second the movement... the oppressed and the oppressors measure their forces. The errors of the past are our instruction; the age-old vow is realised; Italy rises again, never to fall again."

I listened with bowed head, thinking what any other man would have thought: "To what extent could we count upon the ability of the European Revolutionary Committee to extend our initiative, to transform it into an international uprising?"

Could I hold back when an entity such as Fortunato Calvi was throwing himself entirely into the breach?

Three days, and I was on my way again.
