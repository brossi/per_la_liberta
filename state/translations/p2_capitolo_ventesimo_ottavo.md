CHAPTER TWENTY-EIGHTH

IN the afternoon, I saw the old Governor once more. Inspired by the boundless democracy of the English — who are, for all that, so aristocratic in their bearing — he had felt it his duty to come down to me in person, to congratulate the political convict reborn to civil life. And having understood, from what the doctor himself had told him, that my departure for England by ordinary means was not without its perils, he offered to engage me a berth on a man-of-war that would set course for the capital of the Empire no later than March.

With that, he took his leave. But before doing so he placed in my hands, without a word of commentary, a large volume of his own, bound in black Morocco leather. It was... a Bible. The good old man did not feel he had done enough by contributing to my material liberation; he wished also to cast into my soul the seeds of a moral fermentation. Although I considered then, and consider still, that his pious wish, had it taken root and flowered, would have, so to speak, unmanned me — nevertheless I could not help but feel a sense of gratitude for the disinterested nobility of his intention. From the window I watched him until he disappeared. Many times since I have wished to see him again, to prove to him that the good he did was not turned to ill. But he was already some seventy years of age at that time, and who can say how long it has been since the virtues and follies of the world have ceased to move him! He left upon me an indelible impression — not only because with him begins a new period of my existence, but also because, in so brief a time, he revealed to me so many facets of his character. His admiration for bold deeds performed at sea; the pride that led him, without denying the merits of others, to proclaim in our presence the superiority of his own race; his humane sentiments; his conception of the nobility of labour and of the regenerating influence of thrift; his faith in the rehabilitation of the fallen; his abhorrence of falsehood; his spontaneous amends for an offence, however slight, done to one who found himself in social circumstances so far inferior to his own; the respect he showed for the rights of the oppressed; even his inclination towards mysticism — all of this was the synthesis of the virtues and the failings of a People... of a great People.

In my head, amid such a kaleidoscope of men and events, a little confusion has crept in among the names. I may be mistaken; but I believe he was called Melville...

To wait until March — a device wholly irreconcilable with my impatience! A careful examination of circumstances led us to formulate the following plan. There was still at Berbice an English brig, the *John Romelly*, Captain Kandel, which had wished to sail for London on Christmas Eve, but could not, for want of the prescribed number of sailors. The doctor would go to parley with the captain and persuade him to enter me on the crew's roster. I would remain, until departure, shut in my room on the pretext of illness and, the better to deceive the watchful eye of the French spy, the doctor would publish in his journal that I had joined a caravan of rubber-gatherers which was, precisely on the following day, to set out on an expedition into the interior.

Christmas Eve arrived. At nightfall I embraced my courteous host and, having begged leave, kissed the hand of the gentle creature who, day after day during those difficult weeks of painful confinement, had soothed my fevers, kindling in my breast a wide tide of poetry. At dawn, the *John Romelly* was far away, and I could see nothing around me but the sea!...

It was calculated that the crossing would require from twenty-two to twenty-five days. Fierce storms drove us off our course. Our daily rations were halved, and it was a piece of good fortune to have been able to signal a steamer that replenished our provisions. We arrived off the Thames after more than two months of sailing.

How many tragic events had occurred since the day I had traversed those same waters in the opposite direction, summoned by the patriotic voice of Felice Orsini! Now, as then, the elements seemed to yearn for primordial chaos, and with such fury as to mark a memorable date in the history of maritime disasters. Death and destruction had gone before us, and we saw unmistakable traces thereof in the masts rising, here and there, above the surface of the water; in the wreckage drifting madly about us.

The caravan departed. Not many days passed before the sorrowful news arrived that it had been annihilated by savages. The doctor seized upon this to publish that I too, drained of blood and flayed, had passed into the company of the departed. The French spy vanished from New Amsterdam. But the *Times* of London republished the tragic little tale, and for the second time poor Elisa was compelled to make a terrible effort not to believe the plausible, if not the true.

Towards ten o'clock in the evening of the 29th of February 1860, I at last set foot upon the docks of the immense capital, where I knew not a single person who still believed me alive. Of the momentous events crowding upon one another at that hour, especially in my own Homeland, I knew nothing. I had in my pocket a couple of shillings borrowed from the second mate of the *John Romelly*; I was wearing the trousers of the prison hulk. From so humble a beginning was to unfold what I might consider my second existence. And in my soul, alongside a confused impulse towards action and an overpowering desire to cast myself into beloved arms, there was the bitterness of having arrived alone. My thoughts tore me away from the infinite sweetness I had so long imagined, to transport me to that fatal country where two martyrs with severed heads endured, unhonoured, the vicissitudes of matter beneath the earth.

An hour later, the miseries of life and political hatreds had reclaimed me.

My first steps were directed to the offices of the *Times*, to discharge a debt of gratitude and to announce to those friends who might still be interested in the matter my resurrection. Elisa, so I supposed, must be in Nottingham with her mother, and whether I resolved to go there myself or to write asking her to come to London, a few pounds were indispensable. To procure them, I was counting on the doctor Simon Bernard. This thought led me to enter a café in the West End that I knew to be frequented by Frenchmen.

While I was consuming my order, I became aware that my attire — more than shabby — was provoking the smiles and unfavourable comments of the customers, whose astonishment must have reached its height when a young man, entering noisily, ran to my table, crying: "Oh Moretto, you here? And I who have been mourning you for dead!" Hardly were the words out before he was embracing me and kissing me.

It was that Rossi upon whom Foschini had directed the dagger-thrust that had instead passed through my own chest. From the pavement outside, looking through the glass between the green curtains out of sheer curiosity, he had seen and recognised me. The torrent of questions he put to me was ceaseless; but no less ceaseless was the torrent I put to him. From him I learned that Simon Bernard had stood trial; that my wife had appeared as a witness; that the people and the press had protested in terms so forceful against that manifest subjugation of old England to the will of Napoleon III as to provoke not only the acquittal of the accused, but also the fall of the Palmerston Ministry.

From that time, Bernard had swelled with pride and was noisily assuming the pose of a hero. He claimed for himself the principal part in the Orsini conspiracy; which, to hear him tell it, would not have failed if...

At this point Rossi broke off, then resuming the thread of his discourse: "Bernard no longer frequents this café. If you wish to speak with him, I know where to find him. But I cannot understand in what way he could be of use to you... In short, shall I speak plainly? He blames you for having sold yourself to the French Police and denounced your fellow conspirators."

"Take me to him at once."

And so it came about that I found myself before the man. He had organised a small club that met in modest premises off Leicester Square. To go up to it one had to pass through a tobacconist's shop. The proprietress, to whom I applied, looked me up and down and would not let me pass. She did, however, send word to Bernard, who did not keep me waiting long. As he came down the stairs, I noted from his surprise that he had recognised me, and I noted too that, in order to keep himself — as the saying goes — abreast of the times, he had allowed two enormous moustaches to grow, in the manner of Vittorio Emanuele II.

— Good evening, Bernard.
— With whom do I have the honour of...
— You know full well. In any case I shall tell you myself...

And seized by a surge of indignation, I grasped him with both hands by his long moustaches, crying: "This will convince you that you have before you not some ghost of myself, but Carlo di Rudio in flesh and blood!"

It will be readily understood that, after such methods of an eloquence so little suited to the bar, the discussion could not proceed decorously. But the place, and regard for the lady of the establishment, would not allow me to prolong a discussion that resembled too closely a brawl. When he had threatened me with a warning not to make a scene, if I did not wish to risk being expelled from England, and when I had answered him, with a flourish of insults, that I had no need whatsoever to make a scene, since I had already charged the *Times* to make one for me, we ended by establishing by mutual accord that on the following evening at eight o'clock we would meet at the corner of Leicester Square to settle our dispute thoroughly — and coward be he who failed to appear!

I spent the night in Rossi's room, seething and writing. In the morning, resolved to seek out Giuseppe Mazzini, I went to Leather Lane to ask Massarenti for news of him.

He greeted me with extravagant praise; but when, interrupting him, I asked for the Master's address, I received the answer that he had not seen him for a long while and did not know in what corner of the world he had betaken himself.

Then the great Italian revolution, which had flung so many of my friends, so many patriots, upon London, had by now, like a maelstrom, almost entirely absorbed him.

Evening came. In Leicester Square I discerned, through the fog, beneath a street-lamp, Simon Bernard, deep in conversation with two strangers. He saw me and invited me to follow him. "Nasty cold weather!... In a public house — he said — we shan't catch a chill and shall have ample opportunity to come to an understanding."

After walking in silence for a couple of blocks, I suddenly turned round... the two strangers were following us. We walked another couple of blocks; I turned again, and the two were still following. I stopped short, and the moment the two realised they had attracted notice, they planted themselves before a shop window and stood examining it. I resumed my walk, and the two resumed theirs.

Then I moved sharply, and drawing out a concealed weapon that the provident Rossi had slipped into my pocket, I said to Bernard: "What game is this? Who are these fellows? Take care not to trifle with an Italian... If they come closer, I will run you through."

Not content with this, I advanced with sudden resolution towards the two strangers, who had stopped this time before a stationer's shop, and thrusting myself brusquely between them, I stared each of them in turn, fixedly, in the face.

From that moment I was followed no longer, and at the first public house I said to Bernard: "Let us go in here. Now we shall be able to speak freely."

But our conversation was very different from what I had expected. Doctor Bernard — a cunning, wary, double-dealing man, one who shrank from assuming responsibility for his own actions; incapable of confronting an adversary or an equivocal situation — of his own making — with manly directness; a quality that adorns the breast even of brigands! — opposed to my vehemence diplomatic equivocations, extravagant protestations of deep sympathy, lavish offers of assistance. I should pay no heed to the foolish gossip of malicious persons; I should go to Nottingham; I should hasten to bring unexpected joy to my wife, who most certainly stood in great need of it. He would confer with friends and write to apprise me of some definitive plan in my favour.

And he contrived to slip out from under me.

But permit me to pass for a moment beyond these miseries. There stirs in my soul now a memory of a very different nature... before which vulgar passions find no place in the heart.

I had written to my good mother-in-law in Nottingham to announce my arrival. Enclosed in the envelope was a letter for Elisa. The morning after that second interview with Bernard, scarcely had I thrown off the bedclothes when I caught sight on the floor near the door, where it had been pushed underneath, of a letter addressed to me. It was the reply of my mother-in-law, who, having given vent to her own happiness at my escape, informed me that Elisa was in London, at the Mancherini household. She had forwarded my letter to her, giving her my address at the same time.

I was so moved by this that as I dressed, my fingers could not find a buttonhole. And just then, as I was drying my hands, the knocker on the street door sounded with repeated blows. I made out the landlady's step as she went to open it; then a woman's voice asking whether a young man answering such and such a description lived in that house... An harmonious voice... hers!... But why did she not pronounce my name?

Perhaps she doubts — I thought — that I, out of prudence, may have given a name not my own. In one leap I was on the threshold... and I learned that excessive happiness seizes the heart with a rough, tyrannical hand, producing sensations that resemble pain. I saw her... but dimmed and as if in a dream; I wished to cry out to her: "I promised it you... here I am!" but my voice would not come; I made to throw myself forward with outstretched arms, but my arms turned to lead and my feet remained nailed to the floor.

Elisa too remained motionless for a moment... a deathly pallor drained the colour from her face. Suddenly she moved towards me, but after a few steps her head fell upon her breast and her knees buckled beneath her... She fell into my arms insensible. And when she came to herself, as I kissed her and wept:

"Elisa, Elisa! This honest refuge — more glorious... it may not be granted us, and no matter. But ever thine; and in every fibre of my heart, a shrine for thee!"

In that instant a thought flashed through my mind. Would I yet find my Homeland one, free, and great, as I had found my love again?
