## Chapter Twenty-First

From the heights overlooking the mines of Agordo, I cast a last glance at my brother, who was growing smaller in the plain below. Then I set my feet resolutely upon the mountain path, beginning a flight that was to prove full of emotion and truly singular in character.

Having reached the zone of perpetual snows, I slackened my pace, both from weariness and the increasing difficulties of the way, but also because I felt hidden and made safe by the vastness of space and solitude. It had been forty-eight hours since I had closed my eyes, and I felt myself terribly disposed to that morbid sleep which frost and altitude can render deadly. At a certain point, I was as though seized by the vertigo of the sublime; my legs would no longer support me and I fell prostrate upon the snow.

Now let one observe how far the dream — or rather, it is the mountain that comes rushing toward me — and the real are able to intertwine and confound one another! I slept and was incapable of moving a muscle... no doubt on that score. And yet the mind, vigilant within the inert body, retained consciousness of that boundless whiteness, of that fearful solitude; I felt the frail heart beating, the icy wind blowing around me and, amid the savage images of life pressing upon one another in the brain, the idea of my danger predominated, rigid and immovable as a boulder.

For I saw myself clearly — and I run, I run with the *sbirri* at my heels. I run, I run, I run: or rather, it is the mountain that comes rushing swiftly toward me. But the constables are equally swift — faster, even... already I hear the crunch of snow beneath their boots; already the foul mouths thunder their halt! and I run, run... Several shots rang out in my head!...

A single instant and I was on my feet, awake — perfectly awake! The shots still echoed from crag to crag. I peered down into the valley and made out a line of soldiers running along the edge of the forest, rifles in hand. They stopped and formed a circle around a prone, darkly clad human figure. I noted the agitated gestures; then I saw, or thought I saw, a soldier prod the fallen man's flesh with his bayonet, as though searching it for some last remnant of life.

A smuggler, I thought — mistaken, in these days of fearful suspicion, for game of greater importance. It was the very man who, stiff and senseless, was afterwards to cause such anguish to my poor mother.

I crossed the summit with all possible speed. The sky remained splendid; the snow, greatly hardened on the northern slope, no longer gave way beneath my feet. I received more than honest welcome, and bread and milk from a mountain peasant; then at Neumark I generously dissolved my cheese with an inward libation of excellent beer. At dusk I set foot in Bolzano.

If a crowd is good for concealment, I could not have arrived at a more opportune moment. For several days the life of the entire district had been flowing into Bolzano, drawn thither by the annual fair. In those times and in those places of difficult communication, fairs took on the clamorous character of village festivals. Brokers and dour farmers in pursuit of the florin; mountain women beautiful with that rustic beauty derived from open air and sun — having obtained their plenary indulgence, they departed in great throngs, singing in chorus. There was a reek of barrels issuing in gusts from the taverns; there were herds of heifers and oxen that opened a solemn passage for themselves through the human herds; mountebanks who drew the public with the grimaces of their painted faces, to the sound of the bass drum; sacks heaped in piles and ironware spread upon the flagstones, and baskets heaped with fruit and boards of sweetmeats sheltered beneath enormous umbrellas; and a press and crush of people and a buzzing of voices and a riot of colours... and above it all, conciliating every base human discord, a powerful wave of bells ringing out in celebration.

I arrived, not without making free use of my elbows, at the main square, entirely encumbered with stalls and vendors. At one corner of the square stood the inn of the piazza... along one of its sides it flanked the principal street, and it was upon this side that the main entrance opened.

I entered, went up to the first floor, and found myself face to face with a *chellerina* — a barmaid — with a rosy little face and a white apron... only to be told that all the rooms were taken, that I must content myself with sharing a bed with some other traveller — and good luck to me in finding an obliging companion for the bargain!

"Very well! In any case I can begin with a good supper."

I was shown into the dining room. By instinct I took the first table on the left, near the entrance. I was alone. The spacious room was dimly illuminated by a lamp. In its reflection I recognised upon one wall the effigy of Ferdinand, the emperor who remembered for the last time that he was a despot when he demanded, upon his deathbed, a dish of gnocchi with butter. I looked, but in vain, for that of Francesco Giuseppe... he had perhaps not yet found the means of making his way so far.

But I found something worse. Beside the door was pasted a large notice upon which the double-headed eagle appeared conspicuously. Amid the dark and crowded lines, standing out in larger characters, was a name well known to me — that of... Carlo Camillo di Rudio. The devil! What is this? I sprang to my feet and read. Austria was placing a bounty of six thousand florins upon my head. To take me dead or alive... to denounce me!... to denounce me!... a trifling endeavour, the duty of an honest citizen — for any of those zealots of order and the Law. By how slender a thread my safety hung!...

I should be lying if I said I dined with a tranquil mind. If being in a crowded centre had seemed to me a short while before my salvation, now I perceived in it the greatest of dangers. How was I to escape unnoticed from that vortex? Was it possible that among so many people there was not a single soul who knew me? Who might guess the truth by some instinct?

Almost by way of answer, upon the stairs, mingling with the varied tread of footsteps, there sounded voices speaking the Bellunese dialect — voices which, I could have sworn, were not unknown to me. I hid my face in my right hand and peered anxiously through my fingers. The door flew open; several strangers entered, and behind them, one after another, four sturdy figures... the smugglers of the Trevis valley — the Burrasca brothers.

They looked at me, recognised me; but gave no sign. They sat down at the neighbouring table, and the eldest, who was at my side, touched my foot with his foot; then, without turning his face, pretending to speak to his brothers: "I am pleased to see you. Given the rumours going round..."

At that moment the *chellerina* with the rosy face and white apron entered. She lit another lamp... the letters of the notice with the double-headed eagle darkened energetically, almost doubling their eloquence.

I said: "Saved!... only to leap from the frying pan into the fire." And pointing to the notice: "What a fine prize the lot of you might claim!"

Four faces darkened. The eldest of the Burrasca, striking his fist through the air: "Morsels seasoned with shame do not pass down this throat!"

And another of the brothers, in a deep bass voice: "In the veins of the Burrasca runs no traitor's blood!"

They had occasion to prove it to me. A few minutes more and through the wide room spread the reek of gendarmerie. One brute remained upon the threshold and, his back against the doorpost, blocked the exit. Another brute stepped forward and asked me, in a dry tone, for my passport. I drew it from my wallet and was unfolding it when he, losing patience, snatched it from my hand. He scanned it, stepped closer to examine it better by the lamplight, and ended by saying: "I do not understand Italian. You must come with me to the gendarmerie."

"Hold firm!" cried one of the Burrasca to me — he who had clearly understood the perils of the fine business being proposed to me.

One instant... one stroke! I snatch the passport back from the importunate fellow and, before he can react, the smuggler's fist strikes him like a mallet beneath the chin. He fell, his head struck the brick floor, and he moved no more.

Meanwhile, another smuggler who had drawn near to the threshold, on the pretext of reading the terms of the bounty, deals his blow upon the teeth of the brute who was blocking it... blood spurted from his mouth, from his nose; he swayed and went down in a heap.

"Wings to your feet!" was the cry. But I needed no urging... already I was taking the stairs four at a time, pistol in hand — a providential inspiration! A third gendarme was on guard at the foot of the stairs. He turned, saw me, saw the weapon, understood its silent threat reinforced by an energetic oath. He let me pass without lifting a finger, and I made my way off, retreating with the weapon aimed.

But in the compliance of that constable lay no small measure of strategy... he had conjured away his own danger and had driven me into a greater trap. A fourth gendarme, having heard the tumult on the floor above, had run to summon the intervention of the patrol. Both were advancing at the double, while I turned the corner onto the square, and it was nothing short of a miracle that I did not fall straight into their arms. In front of me the patrol, behind me the gendarme on guard who had followed me and was gesticulating furiously, pointing me out.

Night had fallen. The square, encumbered with stalls and people, was lit by lanterns of many colours, in the Venetian manner. With a sidewise movement, I plunged into that maze. "Stop him, stop him! Catch him, catch him!" But in the confusion, no one thought to act. As for the soldiers, held compact by the virtue of discipline and hampered by their arms, they contented themselves with pursuing me at a run along the sides of the square... I slipped through and crossed it.

Then, either put on my trail or having been told I had turned down a certain alleyway, they divided into two detachments; one rushed at my heels, the other went round the block and ran to the far end of the same alley, to bar my way and catch me between two fires.

They ended by finding themselves face to face with one another.

In the entryway of a house, my eye to the keyhole, I watched their movements, noted the faces, heard the words. "Der Teufel! Has the earth swallowed him up?" They discussed the matter for a moment and concluded that I must be — heaven be thanked for it! — a cutpurse. The police had spread good nets throughout the whole township and, sooner or later, I would be caught in them. They reformed their ranks and moved away.

Without a special warrant, the constables could not, at that hour, violate a private dwelling. I felt myself temporarily safe.

I climbed the stairs to the third landing — the last. There were four doors: two facing each other, one to the right, one to the left. I listened... absolute silence. I therefore sat down upon the steps and, my elbows on my knees, my face in my palms, began to reflect. Imperceptibly, my thoughts darkened and I fell asleep.

When I returned to the reality of things, the first light of dawn was revealing itself through the skylight, and already beyond the door on the left one could hear voices alternating in brief, indistinct exchanges, and the clinking of plates and crockery.

I rose, brushed down my trousers, and was about to descend when the door opened and a woman of uncertain age, in a short dress, saw me and gave a cry.

"What is the matter?" demanded a gruff masculine voice from within. Before I could open my mouth, I found before me a stocky man in his shirtsleeves, an apron at his sides, an awl in his fist.

I urged them not to be alarmed. I was a cattle dealer arrived from Switzerland the previous evening at a late hour. For all the inns I had gone the round of, I had been unable to find a bed, even offering to pay through the nose for one. And rather than spend the night in the open air...

"For the love of Christ!" said the man. "From the pavement to a landing... what difference does it make? Come in, you shall warm yourself with a cup of coffee in our company."

I offered to add a drop of *acquavite* to it and drew out my purse, which to the eye appeared well stocked.

A contest in three ensued... the cobbler affecting to take offence at my thinking him capable of demanding payment for an act of kindness; the woman drawing back with gestures of mock modesty, unwilling to let my gold coin touch her forearm and hand; I rebuffing the man's protests with a succession of "But really! come now! come now!" and pressing the woman, doing her a courteous violence. I ended by triumphing, and then added: "Indeed, to speak truly, the cold has given me an appetite, such an appetite!... and, in the town, in these days, one eats... one eats wretchedly! If you would be so kind as to fetch a few morsels... whatever you like. A little bite does no harm to friendship!"

When the woman had gone down a flight or two of stairs, I raised my voice and called her back to commend to her the portentous virtue of holding one's tongue. In Bolzano I was not without friends, and if word reached their ears that I had preferred sleeping on a landing to disturbing them...

It was an imprudence — ninety-nine times in a hundred, the most effective means of making a woman gossip is to bid her hold her tongue. But my good fortune was to ensure that it was precisely from that crack that my salvation would spring.

No sooner had my hostess reached the street and the door was heard to slam, than the cobbler took me by the sleeve and, dragging me inside into the kitchen, fixed me with a long gaze without saying a word. Then, all at once: "You have been telling me stories. You, a cattle dealer! You are a deserter!"

— Hush! How did you come to know it?

By way of all reply, he twisted his lips and pointed his forefinger at his own right eye. Which, among all peoples known to man, signifies: "I have too much salt in my pumpkin to let myself be bamboozled."

With the conversation set upon that ground, I found myself, as they say, at home, nor was I short of arguments and precise details with which to entangle my host ever more deeply in his own mistaken assumption. And he, as he listened to me, showed me his growing confidence by winking and giving me certain little claps on the shoulder that were meant to carry the full air of a demonstration of brotherly affection; for the deserter usurped, in my time, among those populations, the reputation that properly belongs to heroes, and to refuse him a helping hand tasted to them of cowardice.

Had my spirit not been terribly burdened, that would have been a day of rest. My host showed a cordiality that was rough but thoroughly genuine. However, too much spoils everything, and when, solicitous for what should come after, he began to devise plans for my escape, he was, all unwittingly, the cause of no small anxiety to me. In his enthusiasm, he never ceased from constructing schemes, each one more fantastical than the last, and on the pretext of knowing the district better and being more experienced in judging the temper of men, he insisted on forcing his hand upon me — I who, in that game, was staking my skin.

I ended by persuading him to hire a carriage and drive me out of the township in time to coincide with the change of the coach bound for Marano, which took place several miles away, in open country and well into the night.

"The police have spread good nets throughout the whole township," the corporal of the patrol had said on the previous evening. But no sooner had Bolzano and its fair subsided into slumber than I passed through them, mesh by mesh, almost in triumph, reclining upon the cushions of my carriage.

An incident not worth the telling caused me some little apprehension at Marano, and no more. But at Salzburg, in the German Tyrol, I ran a danger so great that my escape from it, without excessive effort, seems to me almost like a dream.

The postilion had pulled up at the Albergo della Posta, thus indicating to me the place where I was to spend the night, which was already well advanced. I went up at once to wash my hands and face in the room assigned to me and, when I was called for supper, I took the key from the lock but, carelessly, did not shut the door. Upon reaching the foot of the stairs — to recount these trifling details is necessary — I set my bag upon a small table and hung the key upon its hook.

In the dining room I found myself alone; but it was not long before there arrived the inevitable two carabinieri — unarmed this time. They ordered a jug of wine, a pack of cards, and struck up a game of tarocco. Supper being finished, I drew near to them as though interested in the game, and, to be brief, we ended by starting a three-handed game ourselves. We passed an hour in this manner, drinking to one another's health and trading jokes, until those tender exchanges between the devil and holy water were cut short by the landlady, who came to remind us that the Law is the Law and that the prescribed hour of closing had struck some minutes since.

I asked her: "At what hour does the coach depart?"

— At seven precisely!

— In that case, be so good as to wake me at four. I must go up the mountain to see about some heifers and I should like to be back in time to continue my journey by coach. May I sleep soundly?

— Between two pillows!

I took my leave of the gendarmes with great handshakes; but, scarcely into the corridor, my dark thoughts returned to me, and I went up the stairs without retrieving the key. Go back down? The door was ajar; I could bolt it from within with the latch. I entered, threw myself upon the bed and snuffed the candle.

It had been many nights since I had lain at full length, stretched out upon a soft mattress, with a good blanket over my legs. When I awoke, the sun, already well above the horizon, was beating full upon my face.

A curse upon the landlady! I descended with a troubled spirit and ears alert... the inn seemed deserted. Whereupon I, concentrating my fury in the knuckle of my fingers, knocked upon the small table so energetically that the candlesticks upon it shook.

And out came the old landlady from the kitchen, her face framed in her cap. She was neat, dressed in her Sunday best. At the sight of me she stopped short, open-mouthed. She suffered herself to be assailed by a tempest of reproaches; then, with perfect composure: "Good Lord! And whose fault is it? Your room key was there on its hook, and from this I concluded that you had already set out for the mountain. If you do not believe me, ask your two friends from last night. They came here at just about four o'clock. They wished to speak with you. I am sorry for it. I told them you were already well on your way."

— They came to speak with me? Prepare a good breakfast for three. I shall go and find them...

And I departed with all the appearance of a man at peace with the world. But the landlady was left with her trouble and her expense for nothing.

It was a Sunday in September. The population of the village had poured into the little church, which must have been packed to the walls, for a human swarm was crowding outside upon the small churchyard. In crossing the square, I saw the plume of a gendarme rising above those dark heads, and I was glad of it. "Here is one who will not arrest me!" And I added: "So true is it that even the Mass serves some purpose!"

Once clear of the inhabited place, I left the main road and struck out among the fields, along hedged lanes; then I took to the pine wood, which served me as a screen to perfection.

The nearest settlement was Muntzen, a cluster of houses straddling the border. It had, like Janus of ancient memory, two faces: one overcast, the other smiling — Austrian the former, Swiss and republican the latter. This I knew, and I knew moreover that the border line divided, politically speaking, the square and the village inn, which thus found itself under two jurisdictions. Hanging upon a wall of the common room was a clock with a cuckoo. The gendarmes of the Empire could not pass beyond that clock without provoking an international incident. On this side: the regime of the bludgeon, the manacles, the gallows; on the other: the regime of free men, the breath of full lungs, salvation!...

I crossed the frontier at an unfrequented spot; I entered the village and the inn from the Swiss side. Having set foot in the common room, I was searching with my eyes for the cuckoo clock when my attention was drawn to a group of *Jaeger* — the militiamen whom Austria employed for the guarding of the borders. Among them, sweet sight! the uniform and well-known face of one of the guardian angels who had come early that morning with the purpose of impressing the holy stigmata upon my wrists. I could not restrain myself from laughing heartily.

"Why, my friend! Poor sport this morning. And to think that the blackbird was in the cage. Had you climbed the stairs, the six thousand florins of the bounty would have found their way into your pocket sooner or later!"

He admitted that I had had the best of it and carried his amiability so far as to offer his congratulations. "Indeed, to show you my sincerity," he said, "come and let me stand you a bottle!"

— And I a barrel, if you like. But you must be pleased to come over to this side! And I rubbed my hands.

But that gay effervescence, so natural in one who leaves the open sea behind him, was for long months the last I knew; for long months, whenever I thought back upon it, I felt something like remorse.

The failure — already manifest to me — grew more pronounced with each piece of news I gathered as I advanced further into free territory, until at last it assumed the proportions of a true disaster, and my spirit was left as though overwhelmed by it.
