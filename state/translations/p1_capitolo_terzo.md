CHAPTER THREE

On the very day of our flight, I had my first plunge into the life of intensity... and it was to prove decisive.

The Germans were filing along the bastions that run from Porta San Celso to Porta Romana, weary, dispirited, and sullen all at once. And since we were obliged to take our place at their rear, we stood our ground and watched that forced withdrawal of theirs. From the bastions, one commanded the whole city. Here and there, houses could be discerned in flames; amid the rooftops, the white cathedral towered aloft, and the Germans shook their fists at the tricolour that flew from its highest pinnacle. The bells went on ringing the tocsin.

Between the bastions and the dense mass of houses, there stretched at that time, still green, an expanse of kitchen-gardens... a strange quietude held sway there, amidst that great city swept and re-swept by the impetuous tide of passions. Solitary near the bastions stood the farmsteaders' cottage. A child, as serene as the strip of earth that had in all likelihood seen him born, came running out and, climbing the green slope, stopped in silent contemplation beneath the horse-chestnuts that overhang the walls. Then, as though drawn by the marvellous world of small things, he bent down, and his slight fingers played among the little stones that glittered at his feet.

The troops continued to file past, each Company preceded by three Croatians serving as outriders. One of these cast a sidelong glance at the child, lowered his rifle, and drawing it back for greater force, drove a thrust forward, as though it were a matter of running a wild creature onto a spit. The bayonet entered the flank and came out through the back of that innocent who, lifted bodily by the ferocious impetus, opened wide his mouth. But the cry, intercepted by death, never came.

The little one's mother was at a window of the solitary cottage and she saw it and screamed for him. She came running, clambering with effort up the green slope... heavy upon her was the belly, swollen anew and fecund. The wretched woman did not reach him in time to gather the tender remains in her arms. No sooner had she arrived than the tormentors of Italy drove in another bayonet thrust, and she fell backwards, a corpse with a corpse in her womb.

I was seized with fever, with delirium. On the point of levelling my rifle blindly at the murderers, I felt the iron hand of my brother Achille restraining me. He whispered a phrase in my ear which, though I could not make it out, I understood. Then I raised my face instinctively, as though awaiting vengeance from on high. The sky was leaden, motionless... God, if He exists, must have been absorbed in the affairs of some better world.

It was then that in my heart there began to take hold the conviction that the wretched have no salvation save in the awakening of their own rebellious energies. "To fools," I concluded, "the feeble prayer; to the strong who strongly will, action."

Our retreat from Milan to Verona was not of a nature to weaken that terrible impression. Radetzky had given orders to the garrisons posted here and there throughout the various parts of the Kingdom to contract upon the main body of the army. At the same time, in order to forestall the danger of being harassed by an enemy that might decimate his forces and slip through his fingers, elusive as a swarm of gnats, he commanded the evacuation of every town, large or small, lying in his line of march. At the slightest sign of resistance, cannonades and the horrors of sacking.

The various garrisons descended almost unmolested, arriving in time to replenish Radetzky's forces, which had been weakened by continual desertions. The confusion and panic among the Imperialists were such that, having barely reached Lodi, all discipline among them appeared ephemeral. Had Carlo Alberto, instead of wasting precious time in scheming to satisfy the demands of Diplomacy, crossed the Ticino like a thunderbolt, the retreat would have degenerated into a rout, and perhaps Austria, having lost the army of Italy, would never have risen again.

As for the population, unarmed, misled by uncertain and contradictory reports, it neither knew how nor could bring itself to attempt great deeds. Yet inveterate hatred and a virile spirit broke forth here and there. Melegnano, which had flattered itself that it might cut off the enemy's road, was swiftly reduced to submission by fifty pieces of artillery. I saw the Croatians sacking it, drunk and savage, flinging dishevelled women and defenceless children from the windows.

Not a day passed but I was obliged to witness scenes of ferocity and cowardice, often entirely unprovoked, and the suppressed rage tempered my patriotism all the more swiftly.

It found a first outburst at Desenzano. The garrison of Brescia, commanded by one De Domini, my maternal uncle, had effected its junction with Radetzky at that place. Learning of the presence of his two nephews, he wished to see them. He questioned us as to our political opinions. The uncle was a rabid *austriacante* — a zealous partisan of Austria — and I was well aware of it. I expressed myself in bold and unrestrained language.

The old soldier went pale and interrupted me. "These," he said, "are the accursed sentiments of your father. He has instilled them into your blood. Poor fool! In what do you trust? In what do you hope? Do you believe that four peasants armed with pitchfork and cudgel will suffice to frighten the most battle-hardened army in the world; to call into question the stability of the empire?" I contented myself with coolly alluding to Radetzky's retreat; to the revolution bursting forth from one end to the other of the usurped dominion.

"Ah, were you not my nephew..." he muttered between his teeth. "Thank your mother; she is what saves you! But so be it!... sooner or later you will leave your head in the hands of the headsman!"

And I, smiling: "I would sooner face the headsman a hundred times over than be a vile renegade, as you are!"

He departed fuming, and I never saw him again.

But those were mere words. Another terrible incident soon came to fill the measure to overflowing, to give the fateful turn to my existence.

The army had left Peschiera behind when informants arrived to report that the small town of Castelnuovo dared to barricade itself. The spirited inhabitants, reasoning — rightly — that Radetzky could not reach the strong shelter of Verona without passing through their locality, heeded the call of Carlo Alberto, who urged them to obstruct the retreat, and resolved to oppose their breasts to armed force, to offer themselves in holocaust to the Fatherland.

In those days of heroic enthusiasm, one did not deliberate overmuch upon the probability of success. The greater and more certain the sacrifice, the sharper the spur that drove one to embrace it.

The imperial artillery soon made short work of Italian patriotism. Castelnuovo, riddled with shot and in flames, was abandoned to the licence of the soldiery. We, cadets of the Academy, watched that carnage drawn up near the gate of the old walls, at one end of an elliptical piazza. And as the Croatians, in their blind fury, did not aim with any great precision, we had sought cover behind several abandoned carriages.

All at once, I saw a young girl burst from a burning house, her hair unbound, barefoot, scarcely covered by a simple shift... evidently someone taken ill, surprised by the fury of the fire and of men. And behold, from that same house, a filthy Croatian came rushing out, weapon in hand, in pursuit of her.

I thought: "If that cur catches her, he will cut her throat!" And following an irresistible impulse, I was already throwing myself forward to come to her aid, when another brutish soldier, forgetful that in his own country too there were mothers and sisters trembling with dread, levelled his carbine and fired. The young girl fell, and a scarlet streak ran across the white linen that enveloped her.

An imprecation, a leap, and I was at her side, trembling with pity and with rage. At the touch of my hands lifting her head, she opened her eyes; but no sooner had she glimpsed the hated uniform than she shuddered and turned her face away... that pallid face on which, standing out more sharply than the deep marks of the end that hung over her, there reigned an expression of utter revulsion.

"Do not be afraid. I am one of your own; I too am Italian!" and I touched her flesh to find the wound.

The unhappy girl gestured towards her heart, sighed, and her young limbs were overtaken by the ineffable lassitude of death. I remained to contemplate her. She was of a beauty rendered sublime in my eyes by the aureole of martyrdom. Then it was as though those lips spoke to me in a passionate and vibrant tongue. My emotion grew overpowering. I suffered as though I were receiving into my soul all the sorrows of the Fatherland, and a surge of new resolves broke loose suddenly from the depths. Nor ever, after that day, in moments of danger, did I invoke the sacred ideal of Liberty but that, to renew that surge, there reappeared before me, sharp and clear in my mind, the delicate features of that young girl driven into eternal silence by the lead of the oppressor — unconsciously great, tragic, and beautiful.

Night fell, and it was the last night for certain tormentors who had expected to savour much longer the juice of our grapes. Livid lips gave up no secrets. Nor was the matter examined too closely. To what end, in times of inextricable confusion? But I can speak now without fear and without bravado. It was that night that my hands and Achille's came to know their first blood. An act in defence of my resurgent race, an act of Justice!.. The pregnant Lombard mother and the young girl of Castelnuovo did not long remain unavenged!
