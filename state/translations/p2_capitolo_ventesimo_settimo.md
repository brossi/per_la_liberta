The little vessel glides on, glides on... until there appeared before our weary, harried eyes, drawn against the blue sky, the black hull of a cutter. It had emerged as if by surprise from the shore, at so short a distance that the mere thought of evading it would have been madness. We flattered ourselves, for a moment, that those on board would take no notice of us; but a cannon shot, fired as a warning, blew away every illusion. The little herring moved toward the shark, the shark moved toward the little herring... the distance vanished with such rapidity that before long we were able to make out the flag flying from the sternpost.

Lift up your hearts! It was the symbol of pride and liberty; of humanity and progress... the English flag!!

Leaning over the rail of his cutter, a black captain, surrounded by half a dozen black sailors, demanded of us, in a tone of command, who we were and whether we carried on board any goods subject to customs duties. When I had answered him, he straightened up in astonishment, and pointing his finger: "You don't mean to tell me you have crossed more than a thousand miles of sea on that span of hollow wood?"

I pointed in turn to our trousers which, despite the crust of mud that covered them, revealed here and there something of our origins. Once persuaded, he expressed a desire to question us at greater length on board his cutter. It was as though an electric button had been pressed... we all leaped to our feet at once, simultaneously. But whether he had no wish to soil his vessel with that species of ambulant *vase* [cesspit]; or whether our appearance disposed him to stand on his guard, he cried: "Not all, not all! Come aboard, you — you who have spoken." I was the only one who spoke English.

On board, I had ample occasion to recount our vicissitudes to him in detail, and to request of him, by way of conclusion, assistance for our most pressing needs. He listened without interrupting me, expressing his emotion at the dramatic passages only in the manner natural to his race: by laughing uproariously. When I fell silent, he began: "Well, well, well!..." But he interrupted himself and, turning to the sailors, ordered them to lower into the dinghy a sack of ship's biscuit and fresh water.

I asked him for precise directions to reach the nearest English port and, to my intense relief, was informed that we were not far from the mouth of the Berbice and the town that takes its name from it. "When you have rounded the headland that shows dark over yonder, the town will rise suddenly before you. The tide will be running out then. Keep on this side of the buoys and you will run no risk whatever."

The crew of the cutter, composed of sailors who had once been slaves, felt sympathy for us. Once the biscuit had been lowered, they vied with one another in tossing us tobacco and other small gifts, and when the dinghy set off we were sent on our way by a chorus of "good bye" and "good luck" which for some moments surpassed even the resounding voice of the sea. We showed our gratitude by immediately setting upon the hard crusts with the teeth of crocodiles.

And the little vessel glides on, glides on... until it enters the turbid, yellowish waters that announce the imminent mouth of the Berbice. My companions, far from rejoicing at this, began to give voice to the strangest apprehensions. Who could assure us, among other things, that the black captain had not taught us precisely the way to blunder headlong into some trap?

We chanced upon a fisherman's smack. Two small, sun-bronzed men, Portuguese in appearance, were drawing in their nets. "Which course must we follow to avoid the current and enter the river without danger?"

We heard, in different words, instructions identical to those of the black captain. Half an hour later, to our astonished eyes, Berbice, or New Amsterdam, spread itself upon either bank. On one side, low houses set upon piles, between which, evidently, the water crept in at high tide; on the other, the broad and solid government buildings. In the middle, several anchored vessels traced against the sky the spars of their yards.

We remained not long in uncertainty as to which direction to steer our course. Scarcely had we entered when there came swiftly toward us a launch, in which stood upright an old man with long white whiskers. He was surrounded by a dozen policemen. In an instant he was upon us, and the old man, tossing his honest growth of hair: "Who are you? Where do you come from?"

As though determined, once and for all, to have done with us, fate had driven us directly into the path of the Governor himself. Who, perceiving us so utterly wretched upon a vessel flying no flag of any kind, and supposing us to be castaways, had drawn alongside to gather reliable news and render us such assistance as the case required.

I answered him in four brief words, and he, with the tact of the English, who never permit themselves to give a direct contradiction: "You surely cannot mean to say that you have come from Île Royale?" Then he turned to his helmsman, the only one of his retinue of white complexion, the only one dressed in civilian clothes, and said to him: "What do you make of it? In a shell like that! Our old England has taught them the way!"

He set one of his guards aboard our dinghy and, having given the order that a substantial meal be made ready for us at the police station, made a sign, and his launch darted away, nimble as a fish.

The police station was situated on the left bank, near the quay, and four strokes of the oar sufficed to reach it. But from the French oaths that my companions were chewing between their teeth, I was obliged to perceive that the dinghy was proceeding contrary to their wishes. True, the "substantial supper" was a splendid prospect; but the escort of black policemen smacked too much to them of a grim preamble for them not to suspect that they would be slipped, straight from the table, into some large room locked with a double turn of the key. And matters grew worse when, upon arrival, an officious braid-decked officer launched into formal questions, writing in a register the answers that I translated for him. So we had faced death so many times only to tumble thus wretchedly from the frying pan into the fire?

To convince them that they were not prisoners, I feigned a desire to buy tobacco and went out. Then, freed from all excessive anxiety, I had leisure to observe the surroundings more attentively. It was the fifteenth of December, 1859, and the luxuriant vegetation pressed close about the settlement, as though intent upon dominating it and flooding it with sombre green. Superb, impenetrable forests stretched as far as the eye could see along both banks, and the sea water issued from them in little cascades to return, with the ebb, to the sea. The sky, deeply blue, bore in the west the glare of a thousand conflagrations.

The news of our arrival had spread rapidly and I was soon surrounded by a multitude eager for particulars. My convict's garb, filthy as though I had been wallowing in a pigsty, inspired no revulsion whatsoever. No question was put to me that was not discreet; I saw nothing but faces animated by the most unfeigned sympathy. And there was one who offered me a thousand *duros* for the cession of our dinghy.

I broke off the conversation and the handshakes to hasten at the call of a policeman, who announced that supper was ready. At the sight of the profusion of fresh bread, ham, cheese and beer spread upon the table, every trouble seemed to dissolve from the breasts of my companions. Not a crumb was left, all devoured in profound silence, amid the jests of the policemen, astonished and made merry by that swift, phenomenal disappearance.

The dishes were already being cleared away when an unexpected visit plunged us back into painful uncertainty. The old Governor with his long white whiskers had entered silently and stood before us, conducting a mute inspection. Behind him was a group of citizens whose sentiments toward us were perfectly dissembled by their impassive faces.

We remained still and expectant. The Governor began by expressing, in French, the following thoughts: "You have come to a land that does not turn away those who seek asylum in it. Respect the Laws of this Country and the Country will not fail in its duties of hospitality. But a guarantee is required of you — that of work. Work and thrift open the way before any honest man, however low he may have fallen." He was silent for a moment, then proceeded to question us one by one, asking us not our names, not the nature of our sentences, but what occupation we might honourably pursue. As my companions declared their trades, he turned to one or another of the citizens who had accompanied him and said: "This one I entrust to you... this other to you."

My turn came. I replied: "My profession is that of the sword."

— What do you mean by that?

— That I was educated at the military college of San Luca, in Milan.

— Where did you learn English?

— In England, where I have a wife and a son.

— You are Italian, you have a family in England, and you escaped from a French penitentiary. What a tangle is this?

I then revealed to him my name and the reason for my sentence. His face clouded: "I believe you are mistaken. The Count of Rudio is dead!"

I was about to reply when a young man of handsome appearance, notwithstanding the hue of his face, which was suggestive of chocolate — he was a mulatto — interposed and said in perfectly correct French: "If Your Excellency permits, I will take this man into my house. I make myself his guarantor."

That courteous man was the physician of New Amsterdam. He had completed his studies in Brussels, where he had managed to acquire, in addition to his degree, the love and the hand of a charming Belgian young lady. He was a freethinker, the proprietor of a local newspaper and correspondent of the London Times... not, perhaps, the least of the reasons that had impelled him to come to my aid with such warmth.

He assigned me a good bedchamber, where at last I had the opportunity to scrape off — literally! — the crust of the savage, and to restore my natural colour to the surface.

Thus transformed, I was presented to the lady of the house. She struck me by her very fine figure and by the refinement of her cultivation, notwithstanding that she had been educated in a convent. She spoke Italian with an uncommon sweetness for a foreign mouth, and was more than merely accomplished in drawing melodies from the pianoforte.

The hour before the brief tropical dusk I spent in friendly conversation, recounting, at the request of my hosts and the visitors drawn by the unusual circumstance, now this, now that of my adventures. At a certain point the lady, who was probably minded to deliver me from the excessive importunity of the curious, said aloud: "The Count of Rudio, as an Italian, undoubtedly loves music." And without more ado she sat down at the piano.

What enchantment!... and how adroit the human heart proves itself in lulling the importunate phantoms to rest and in reviving the rosy phantoms that were lulled! I still had in my ear the language of the penal hulks; I seemed still to see, as it were, at my feet, the chain but barely broken; my soul still trembled from the ill-usage endured — much of it unrecounted, for I shrink from the pose of the martyr — those nimble fingers that, materially, moved across the keyboard, were in spirit touching the hidden fibre that ached within me, light as the fingers of a *smagnetizzatore* [magnetist who lifts a trance]. And I experienced the very real sweetness that accompanies the cessation of all torment. There were notes too which, reverberating in the depths, conjured up a malediction, a groan, a blood-red apparition, the roar of a tempest; yet all of it appeared to me softened and made harmonious by time and by hope.

I believe I wept! But I have one thing to say in my defence. I felt the authority of art deeply, because I too belonged to art... I had once opened wide my mouth among the choristers of Drury Lane!

That enchantment was of brief duration. An acquaintance of the doctor's came hurrying in to announce that a French warship had moored at the quay. "Let us go and see her!" was said in chorus. And we set off. A few minutes later, in the fading twilight, the menacing bulk of the *Abeille* towered before my eyes.

The tactic adopted by Cousin of pressing our dinghy northward had produced its effect. When, forced by the spoiling of our provisions, we turned back toward the South American continent, the *Abeille* had already passed beyond the course we had traced. Not finding us at New Amsterdam, she had pushed on into Venezuelan waters, and the captain, disillusioned, had already turned back on his route, when, passing once more before the Berbice, he had fallen in with the smack and the Portuguese fishermen. From them he learned of our arrival and had come up the river to demand my extradition.

On this there was no doubt. At the rail of the *Abeille* stood, one beside the other, two odious figures — the warders of my work gang. For what reason were they on board, if not to identify me? They caught sight of me and were quickened by a sudden ferocity: "Look at him, look at him!" cried one of them, pointing me out with his finger. And he added: "Hey, you — the one with the bombs! Before long you'll be up here and tasting your medicine again!" Such mockery did not suffice him. He came ashore and, approaching me with his hand outstretched, a diabolical smile on his foul mouth, congratulated me upon the skill of our escape. A civility which I repaid by turning my back on him.

That very same evening a policeman presented himself at the doctor's house and announced that the Governor required my immediate presence at the Palace. My host consulted with the friends who had remained until that hour, then said: "Let us go to the Palace in some number. Some act of weakness... one never knows!"

I covered the long way with feverish anxiety. Then, climbing the steep stairs, I saw in the antechamber — a dismal preamble! — my two warders. A few minutes more and I found myself in a great hall in the presence of the Governor.

Beside his writing-table stood a man in uniform... the captain of the *Abeille*.

Said the Governor: "Is this the fugitive you are seeking?"

I protested: I had never sought to conceal my identity!

— I beg you not to speak when you are not questioned.

And the captain: "I do not know him personally. But I have with me two warders who will be able to answer Your Excellency in categorical fashion."

One of them was brought in. Who, imagining that I had lied about my identity, without waiting for any question shouted: "Yes, it is he! The Count of Rudio in person... none other than he!"

— Get out!

The other warder enters and the denunciation is repeated.

The Governor rose: "Count of Rudio, I had doubted your word. I beg your pardon for it." Then, turning to the other: "Captain, England does not grant the extradition of political prisoners. I am sorry... but I am not in a position to give you any other answer!"

I was free, definitively free! And in the excess of joy that seemed to overwhelm me, instead of drawing myself up in all the dignity of the man restored to his rights, returned to his affections, to the holy struggle in behalf of ideals, I became suddenly a child again. At the captain who was withdrawing and casting me a severe glance, I directed that impish derision which expresses itself by placing the thumb upon the tip of the nose and waggling the fingers.

The following morning... two important pieces of news. The *Abeille* had weighed anchor but, departing, left behind at New Amsterdam a spy charged, certainly, with reporting on my movements. Some precaution was necessary; for if the Napoleonic claws had seized me again, even illegally, there was no diplomatic note that would have sufficed to save me.

The other piece of news, though it did not directly concern me, made the greater impression upon me. My companions in flight, terrified by the spectre of extradition, had crept back by night into the dinghy to take to the open sea in mysterious fashion. It was a grave error... the French authorities, having claimed my person, had taken no account whatsoever of them. According to the doctor, they had unquestionably steered their course toward some port of Venezuela. Did they reach it?

I believe that their manly fibre made them worthy of the liberty they so ardently craved. But I received no further word of them, and for me it was as though the Ocean had swallowed them up.
