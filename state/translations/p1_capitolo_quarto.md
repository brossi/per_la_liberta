CHAPTER FOUR

Having found refuge and safety in Verona, the old Marshal offered the cadets entry, as non-commissioned officers, into the regular army, granting them the privilege of choosing their Regiment. No one accepted. We were interned as far as Gratz and incorporated into that Military Academy; until the Minister of War, acceding to our petition, permitted us to return to our homes.

September came. The valiant Piedmontese army, abandoned to its fate by the defection of the Italian princes, its impetus blunted by the mental infirmities of its own King, vanquished more by the deficiency of its own organization than by Austrian valor, had been compelled to evacuate Venetia, the Duchies, and Lombardy; to fall back behind the line of the Ticino. Milan had fallen once more into the hands of the oppressors... The royal war being virtually ended, there continued, according to the expression then in vogue, that of the People.

Among the individuals unadorned by any official insignia who most distinguished themselves that year, for valor and for integrity of spirit, was a patriot and a very dear friend of my family: Pietro Fortunato Calvi...

With a handful of mountaineers and with arms almost primitive, he had defended the Cadore from the first days of May to the first days of June, inscribing upon the annals of our epopee the names of Ampezzo, of Termine, of Rivalgo, of Chiusa di Venas, of the Passo della Morte, of Selva, of the Rindimera, and the ill-fated Mauria.

In this astonishing campaign, which certain historians pass over with a rapidity even more astonishing, Pietro Fortunato Calvi repulsed the attacks — first separate, then combined — launched by the Austrians against the three natural gateways of the Cadore. When, at the third phase of the war, he laid down his arms, it was a necessity created by the complete exhaustion of provisions and of munitions. Nor in that age, so ready to attribute reverses to treachery, did a single voice arise to diminish the glory of the hero. He made his way, by unfrequented roads, to Venice and, wherever he appeared, was received not as one vanquished, but as a conqueror.

In his footsteps, by the road through Portogruaro, the only one that remained open, I too entered Venice, together with my brother Achille. Calvi admitted us at once into that legion of the Cacciatori delle Alpi, whose name became synonymous with heroism and with glory.

During the close of 1848 and the first months of the following year, I thus had the opportunity to witness a marvelous spectacle. In my long existence I have known no other more potent in exalting the human soul... A people that, by sheer force of will, retempers its own fiber; that purges, in privation, in the grip of hunger, in the horrors of pestilence, in the baptism of its own blood, the shame of centuries of wantonness, the cowardly resignation to servitude.

In destruction, the glimmers of life renewing itself, of greatness reaffirming itself.

In those months, beyond my share of the common sufferings, I endured a grief intimate and intense. My brother Achille had his saintly resolves, his fervent hopes, inexorably cut short by pestilence. I came to kiss his icy brow and it seemed to me that, at the touch of my lips, within the inert cadaver there stirred as it were a last remnant of affection. I believe that only from that moment did the young patriot wholly cease to tremble with life.

I myself took part in but a single engagement of importance: the sortie towards Mestre which furnished the city with forage and with meat. On that occasion I saw, for the first time, without speaking to him, a man who was destined to have a great influence upon my future: Felice Orsini.

Two grave infractions of discipline removed me prematurely from that theater of sacrifice and of greatness. I had been assigned to the defense of the forts of Marghera, lunette number thirteen. A tedious duty; for Cavedalis, the Minister of War, under the pretext that munitions were lacking, had imposed upon us the order not to fire. We looked on with sullen idleness at the feverish labors of the Austrians who, concentrated at Mestre, were digging their parallels and preparing for the bombardment. Our inaction had emboldened them so greatly as to induce them, one day, to advance along the canal to within a rifle shot of the forts... and from that position they wounded in the arm the sentry of my lunette.

The Minister’s orders and the Minister himself vanished from my mind, not to say from the face of the globe... I snatched the linstock from the gunner’s hands and the shot was fired. Instantly, as though my fury had spread with the swiftness of electricity, all the cannon of the forts thundered... the few of the enemy who remained standing took to their heels.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi in person pleaded my cause before the Council of War. He adduced, in my defense, ignorance of the orders, my youth, love of Country, the irresistible impulse, and for the time being I was spared. But at the second infraction, political animosities rendered my position untenable.

The fatal dualism between republicans and monarchists, which in 1848–49 accounted for so great a part of our reverses, had of necessity its repercussion at Venice too. Republican at first, then annexed to monarchical Piedmont, the city had once more assumed republican governance when the royal war appeared irretrievably lost. The upper bourgeoisie, which even then arrogated to itself the exclusive possession of practical good sense, did not readily reconcile itself to the popular verdict, and several Ministers lent it their support.

Hence, hybrid measures that irritated the multitude and drove it frequently to assemble in the Piazza San Marco and to clamor.

In March of 1849 — the precise day I do not recall — the exasperation, having reached its height, seemed on the point of degenerating into open revolt. To forestall it, Cavedalis issued the order, to Pietro Fortunato Calvi, to disperse the crowd at all costs, and Calvi, having detached several patrols from his legion, laid the thankless duty upon the non-commissioned officers. However, at the moment of our departure, he called us aside and, certain of being understood, pronounced these exact words: “Remember that in Venice it is no longer the Croats who give the orders.”

I marched at the head of my patrol through a good stretch of the city without encountering any obstruction; but scarcely had I emerged into the Piazza San Marco than I found myself confronted by a tempestuous sea. Our green uniform braided in white and the drooping plume immediately attracted general attention, and a ringing voice cried out: “Look at them! They have come to shoot us down!”

I placed my *kepi* upon the point of my dagger and, waving it by way of salute, cried at the top of my voice: “We are here to defend the People, not to tyrannize over them!”

Farewell, repression! The crowd parted to give us free passage and, its sentiments transformed in an instant, roared from a thousand and a thousand throats: “Long live the Cacciatori delle Alpi!” Lost and as though engulfed by that stormy sea, we ended by becoming an integral part of it.

Fortunately, where our arms proved unavailing, Daniele Manin supplied what was needed with authority and with eloquence. I have still before my eyes his face, resembling, as one drop of water resembles another, that of Cavour, though his spirit was so wholly alien to every form of diplomatic duplicity. The thoughts he expressed I am able to repeat today still, almost in his very own words. He exhorted them not to aggravate the miseries of the Fatherland, which in its straits called for the concord of all its sons. To divide it, to diminish its strength, was a crime.

The urgent task, the supreme task, was that of vanquishing the foreigner, of breaking the chains being prepared for the Republic. There would be time afterwards to settle internal dissensions; when the glorious banner would once more wave over free lands and over free men. What might not then be achieved by a people conscious of its own rights, drunk with enthusiasm, strengthened by victory? What scheming, what blandishment would avail to wrest from it, even in part, the fruit of so many sacrifices? “Return to your homes, restore yourselves in the caresses of mother, of wife, of anxious children... poor great People, so harshly tried in the school of adversity! Bethink you that the cannon may, from one moment to the next, tear you from those caresses, summon your presence there where men fight, where men die. No Italian worthy of the name must refuse to present himself unsullied and without remorse to the supreme sacrifice!”

He fell silent, and that strong People and that good People dispersed, little by little. How much greater a glory would accrue even today from the wise and humane word, to those Governors who so lightly set it aside in favor of fratricidal arms!

The following day, Colonel Calvi sent for me. I found him more saddened than stern. “The Minister Cavedalis,” he said to me, “is beside himself with rage. Venice is full of informers, as in the days of the Signoria, and he has learned, chapter and verse, what... what has occurred. He demands the names of the non-commissioned officers who commanded the patrols; above all — you understand me? — the name of a beardless sergeant who uttered seditious phrases and whom he wishes to have punished. The escapade is provided for by military regulations...”

I set about piecing together a defense; but he interrupted me with a gesture. The superior officer would not and could not enter into that discussion; but the patriot extended his right hand to the patriot and gave me a clasp that spoke as much as an entire volume.

“It is the second of the kind,” he continued, “and there would be no remission. Sergeant di Rudio, you must leave Venice!.. You will find enemies to fight in every corner of Italy. Rome has need of defenders, and the Government of the Republic, at the solicitation of those Triumvirs, is sending thither the legionaries of the Unione, who came among us after the volte-face of Pope Pio. Venice has more need of bread than of soldiers, and Colonel Ferrara, who leads the legionaries, will set sail, precisely tomorrow, from Chioggia. Here is a letter for him... you will be well received. Go — I command it!”

I rendered the military salute and departed. The unforeseen awaited me. The Minister Cavedalis had already sent to Chioggia the order to arrest, and return to Venice, all soldiers who did not belong to the Unione, or who could not produce a pass signed by the commandant of the Piazza. I did not walk for long through the streets of Chioggia.

The barracks in which they confined me flanks a street that leads down to the waterfront. A single second sufficed to spring from the gateway and dart round the corner into that street; four squirrel-like bounds sufficed to traverse it and reach the shore. In the courtyard of the barracks there paced about, in mingled confusion, legionaries of every province and in every manner of uniform... at the wide-open gateway stood on guard a sentry, or rather a great lout who, to judge by appearances, seemed to be dreaming of the good soups of his village. Slowly, slowly, as though my sole concern were to amuse myself by watching the passers-by, I drew up alongside him. Then I flailed my arms and cried to him in a muffled voice: “Look! He’s escaping... escaping from that window!”

He looks to the left, I leap to the right, turn the corner, and am out of range. Oh the blue waters of the Adriatic, oh the brown *trabiccoli*, rocking gently on the waves, light as shells!.. the *trabiccoli* brushed by the wings of the gulls, resounding with labors and with songs! I had some forty of them before me, already crowded with legionaries, already maneuvering for the imminent departure.

I conceal myself in a small boat which, driven by two sturdy oarsmen, cleaves the waves swift as an arrow. But as I clamber up the side of a vessel, an officer on the shore caught sight of the green uniform braided in white. In that critical moment, the shifting maneuvers of the mobile flotilla proved providential to me. The officer had undoubtedly fixed his eye upon the *trabaccolo* in which I had sought refuge; but in the passage across he became confused and boarded, in his search, the vessel where I was not. He persisted in inspecting a second ship, then a third, and arrived at last upon mine... but by then I had contrived to put on a uniform of the Unione. I fell into rank with the other legionaries and he, passing within a hand’s breadth of my nose, scarcely deigned to glance at me.

Nevertheless I was not to set foot in Rome without first describing a wide circuit. Many combatants had, like myself, quitted Venice by stealth, not from cowardice, but because drawn, by various considerations, towards the heart of Italy. But upon reaching Ravenna, Colonel Ferrara, citing I know not what peremptory orders, refused to enlist us and, having organized us into a detachment, had us escorted as far as Bologna under strict surveillance, as though we were Austrian prisoners. Our ultimate destination: Venice. But I was conscious of having taken up arms to break chains, not to have them imposed upon myself... I resolved to face the vicissitudes of a new escape.

As the sole non-commissioned officer, I had been entrusted with the duty of drawing rations for the entire detachment... one fine morning I turned this to account by bidding farewell to the barracks and to San Petronio. The soldier Angeli, whom I had induced to follow me, put a few coppers at my disposal so that I might procure a disguise; but the Venetian coinage, unfortunately, was not current in Bologna, and an extreme expedient imposed itself: to strip off my green uniform braided in white, which I had put on again at Ravenna, and exchange it for a suit of fustian. Deep was the pang of bitterness this occasioned me; but in the end I consoled myself with the thought that the garb of the laborer of the fields — the honest and unfortunate *paria* of my Country — is also an honorable one. Besides, the heart that yearned for Liberty, the heart that wins battles, I carried always within my breast, unchanged.

At one o’clock that very night, we arrived, without mishap, at the walls of ancient Ferrara. The gates were shut; in the deep darkness, on the far bank of the Po, in the distance, shone the watch-fires of the Austrian encampment. With the exception of the fortress, the piazza was in our hands. I knock at the gate.

— Who goes there?

— Italians deserted from the Austrian encampment. We wish to fight for the cause of independence.

We are admitted, welcomed with open arms, escorted to general headquarters, plied with questions. Our intelligence, gleaned from the newspapers along the road, was found plausible... besides, we had passed ourselves off as common soldiers, and no one expected revelations of high strategy. The matter concluded with our being conducted as far as Cento, where Major Mambrini enrolled us in the Legione Garibaldi.

The fairest sun of Italy was shining when, from the heights of Civita Castellana, I saw Michelangelo’s dome stand forth in sharp relief against the blue. The weariness vanished as though by enchantment, and our arms stretched out towards the plain, towards that place where there throbbed the metropolis that had filled the world with its power and its wisdom. In those days, amid the vestiges of its ancient civilization, amid the superb creations of the Middle Ages, the red shirt blazed as the symbol of the new idea.

It was the last days of April. On the 30th, the troops of the French invader broke their impetus against our breasts, and the hearts of Italians opened once more to hope!
