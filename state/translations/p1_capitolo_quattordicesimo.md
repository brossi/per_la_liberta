CHAPTER FOURTEEN

THE EXCITEMENT of the moment did not prevent me from reflecting upon the strangeness of my situation. I, a patriot, and no mean one (if the intensity of one's feelings counts for anything), was being hunted out, almost as an enemy, from the one strip of free Italian soil, and I was feverishly longing to cross the median line of the lake in order to return, as the saying goes, to the shadow of the yellow and black — the very object of our execration become all at once the symbol of my salvation. I reached it, and breathed more freely. But it was a temporary salvation, and fate decreed that I could not attain the Swiss frontier without grievous hardship.

The weather, which had been threatening since morning, turned for the worse, and from the Alps, towards which we had set our prow, there came, more than wind, a full tempest. The waters, which presaged it, took on a formidable rolling, and our frail craft was tossing about wildly. I soon perceived that we were making no headway, and, seizing a spare pair of oars, I made myself a third in that desperate struggle between human will and the tyranny of the storm. But deeming it imprudent to turn my back upon the two boatmen, I was compelled to resign myself to keeping my face turned towards the Alps. And then there fell, slanting across us, a frozen sleet — nay, a whirlwind of tiny needles of ice that grazed the skin, that tormented my eyes, that took away my breath. And the waves, foaming at their crests, curled into shavings by the wind, threatened to capsize the little vessel. There was no doubting it: the elements had declared for Austria, and more than once I thought that I had escaped so many dangers only to end miserably in that pond of shad.

Numbness of the limbs came upon us, and then the night. It was both necessity and salvation to abandon Locarno and make for the nearest republican land, Brissago. We arrived there with ice-crusted mustaches and broken bones. Nor was it given to me to show my deliverers the full measure of my gratitude; for one of them, the very same to whom I counted out the agreed price and a comparatively generous gratuity, clapped a rough hand upon my shoulder and said: "Whatever comes of it, we served you because that double-mouthed implement of yours admitted of no argument. Go about your business; we shall look after our own. Good night and good fortune!"

Half an hour, and I was between the sheets. Scarce had I stretched myself out than Sander and Radonitz, the captain of the *Arno* and Fortunato Calvi, the lake and all Italy vanished into the dark.

The following morning, I found my anxieties waiting for me upon the pillow. After long reflection, I persuaded myself that my failure had been entirely personal. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, remaining free, had certainly known how to organize everything, even without my assistance. It was now the 4th of February — therefore the useful time had not yet passed — an initiative delayed, not lost.

I resolved to make my way on foot to Locarno, and without further ado I set out. The wind had dropped, but the snow was falling in broad flakes — the mountain and the valley were white with it. In that fresh snow my legs sank to the calf, and beneath my boots there formed a tenacious clog that robbed me, treacherously, of both my footing and my strength. But when I arrived before the house of the Olivieris and saw, through the gratings, a light filtering through, and deduced therefrom that people were still waking, still at work, my weariness vanished as if by enchantment.

The agreed signal opened the door to me. I was led into a room where, seated at a small table, their faces lit by the oil lamp, sat Sanders and Radonitz. Standing with arms folded, I found a third person: Pietro Fortunato Calvi.

Greetings and congratulations! I cut them short to demand news of the situation: "What have you arranged? What hopes remain to us?"

Sanders and Radonitz shrugged their shoulders. They were waiting for the Police to withdraw its ambushes and fall asleep again. My mishap, as related by Calvi, had not only confirmed them in their intention to let matters drag, but had further persuaded them that the Austro-Hungarian authorities, grown suspicious, had incited those of the Republic. "We are no longer safe here!" said Radonitz. "By the by, how are you placed for funds?"

And without awaiting my reply, he reached out his hand towards certain rolls of money which, in my first anxiety, I had not even noticed.

It was as though a veil had fallen from my eyes. I thought: "Late — yet in time!" And turning to Radonitz, who had unrolled one of the packets: "Since you consider our enterprise as having failed, restore the sum I brought you. It was destined to purchase arms, and not for your own benefit. It was counted out to me in the name of Mazzini; to Mazzini I shall render it back."

The expression assumed at that moment by Radonitz is among my most diverting memories. He became haughty again: "I have given you a regular receipt; your responsibility is fully covered. Do not concern yourself with what does not regard you."

With toil and sweat, the men who groaned under the lash had scraped together that gold — bread taken from their own mouths, from the mouths of their children. I persisted. And Radonitz, pale as wax: "Young man, you are a subordinate here; take care not to forget it."

Sanders, who had remained seated with his elbows propped upon the table and his face resting on his fists, thought it opportune to intervene:

"How do you expect us to make our escape, to preserve our work for better times, if we lack the means of doing so?"

I replied: "I do not believe that the watch-chain and the trinkets hanging upon your breast and your gold watch are quite indispensable to you. You swore to give to the Fatherland your time and your blood and your soul and everything — would you now deny her four baubles?"

I turned my gaze to the man who had not yet spoken, who had remained standing in the shadow — to Fortunato Calvi. He gave me a brief nod of assent. Then in a single bound I was at the door; I shut it fast, and put the key in my pocket. Then, drawing out that double-barrelled implement which at Intra had already rendered me such signal service, I leveled it, crying out: "Enough of words! Either the money is restored, or no one leaves this room!"

I replaced the gold in my money-belt and pressed the hand of Fortunato Calvi. To the others I gave the military salute — not without a touch of irony. Then I departed.
