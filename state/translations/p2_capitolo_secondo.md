## CHAPTER TWO

THE GRATIFYING nocturnal surprise continued with mathematical regularity — a neatly tied and well-stocked parcel every twenty-four hours. The Count di Rudio resolved to get to the bottom of the matter, and one fine morning, having tidied his room as usual, he curled himself into the recess of the window behind the curtaining, determined to remain in ambush and in silence for the whole day if need be. From up there he commanded a good stretch of the pavement to the right and to the left, and he reasoned, not without justification, that the parcels could not be reaching him through the air by flight. He would unmask his mysterious benefactor.

A little before noon, whilst leaning against the sill he was photographing with his eye the dark to-and-fro that stirred confusedly at his feet, a sudden awakening of vital vibrations, a gentle warmth, a sense of penetrating light, shook him. He turned with a start — he was alone no longer!

Wrapped in a white dressing-gown, her hair partly gathered up and partly flowing free, Miss Elisa stood erect just within the threshold. The fingers of her right hand held, by its little cord, the restorative parcel, and her blue eyes wandered in an ineffable agitation.

"Lo Carlo!..." she murmured. "I did not know you were here!" She recovered herself at once and added composedly: "I have brought this parcel for you."

"When? No living soul has entered by the door."

"Early this morning. Had I known you were at home, I should have delivered it to you straightaway."

"This morning, you say?"

"Early. A well-dressed gentleman, Italian in appearance and accent. I asked him his name; but he answered that, for reasons entirely his own, he did not wish to make himself known. It did not seem to me right to insist. Besides, he spoke English so poorly and I know so little Italian!..."

She added nothing more, and remained with her eyes cast down, waiting for some phrase, for a word — alluding, were it nothing more, to the fine weather. And as the word absolutely refused to come, she arched her brows with a nervous little movement and raised her eyes. The mild inquiry that shone within them soon lost itself in an expression of pained wonder. The Count di Rudio seemed absorbed in the thought of distant things — his mind was running after the well-dressed Italian who, without being a sorcerer, knew the secrets of his stomach so very well. And he was irritated by the mystery which, despite the tedious ambush, he had not succeeded in resolving. When he became aware that he was being watched, he silently, with dignity, brushed with his right hand the jacket whitened by its long contact with the sill.

The situation was growing awkward when, in the corridor, there sounded the tread of precipitate footsteps, and Miss Sara came hurrying in. She was bearing, clasped to her bosom, a great vase of roses.

She set it down beside the window, and after a few seconds of silent contemplation: "It fits perfectly!" She turned to Elisa and added: "The idea is yours, and it falls to you to give an explanation!" But she gave her no time for it, and fixing her gaze upon the Count: "One evening you said that the rose is the emblem of your family, and that for this reason, among others, it is your favourite flower above all others. Elisa had an inspiration. 'Let us procure a bush — perhaps he will be glad of it.' See what abundance! So as not to trouble you, we shall continue to water it ourselves. Elisa was pointing out to me that it will remind you of your mother and your Homeland — for Italy is the country of roses. Is it not so?"

Here she seemed, or pretended, that someone was calling her, and she ran away. Miss Elisa bade farewell with a nod of her head and, swift and light as a shadow, vanished.

The Count di Rudio stood for a long while motionless, his gaze fixed upon the void, as though something of the two young women had remained within it. From that day they occupied the greater part of his fanciful hours. They insinuated themselves, unbidden, into his thoughts, and no sooner did they appear there than they were received with an affection more than brotherly. The sad eyes, that had never witnessed anything save the torment of the innocent and the triumph of villainy, found renewal in that inward contemplation. And for the first time the Count felt profoundly that, above the spiteful world, above men who gnaw at one another, above insatiable cupidity, pitiless arrogance, and the varied and nauseating slime in which our civil life is mired and entangled, there hangs suspended in the blue sky — of which it is the very substance — a serene force that transforms, that redeems, that creates, that can make us good and happy: Love!
