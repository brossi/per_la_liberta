THE following day, at La Roquette. They made us put on the gray uniform of prisoners; they fitted us with the straitjacket... not from any lust for torture, but to forestall attempts at suicide.

We had been conveyed to the new prison separately and confined separately; yet our cells were so situated that we could see and speak to one another. A rectangular chamber, rather spacious, bare... four white walls, four windows, four doors. Whoever enters by the door communicating with the courtyard perceives, directly opposite, the door and window of Felice Orsini's cell; Pieri's cell lay beyond the left-hand wall; beyond the right-hand wall, my own.

This constituted, for us, a great advantage over the Conciergerie; death was in prospect, yet it was preceded by some measure of relief.

Another circumstance contributed to render the present sad hour more bearable to me. My guards had followed me to La Roquette. They would sleep in turns in order to keep me under continuous surveillance; but this did not trouble me in the least, for I had no intention whatsoever of anticipating my end by self-destruction.

On the very day of our removal, our lawyers came to counsel us to lodge an appeal. Nothing concrete was hoped for; nevertheless, both I and Pieri consented without raising any objection. But Orsini would hear nothing of it. He maintained that death would by now relieve him of a great burden, and that it would come the more welcome the sooner it arrived. Giulio Favre, to obtain his consent, was obliged almost to force his hand.

The days of agony began and passed swiftly. In a state of mind only too explicable, the memories of those days flash through my mind in sudden leaps, standing out like disconnected images against the somber background of those hours in which, meditating, I prepared myself to die.

The chaplain of La Roquette, Monsieur Hughon, paid us a visit at least once every twenty-four hours. He was a former captain of dragoons and had retained the coloring of that life; thickset and robust, in spite of his years; liberal in spite of his position. From his very first appearance I told him plainly that he would always be welcome on one condition: never to speak to me of religious subjects. He promised, and kept his word.

Giuseppe Pieri, on the other hand, willingly acknowledged his right to exercise his ministry, but not to undergo it passively. From the very first evening he provoked him to disputation with an air of open defiance, and allowed himself in subsequent days to be carried away by a demolishing spirit, by a manner of arguing *ad hominem* that was in the extreme degree sharp and almost offensive. Constrained by the pitiable circumstances, the ex-captain was obliged to put a good face upon more than one lance-thrust full in the ribs.

The voice of Pieri was, moreover, the predominant note in that confined and austere place. And how indeed could he, already so loquacious even in the most colorless circumstances of life, have brought himself to silence, now that powerful emotions exalted him?

He talked and argued about everything with obstinate persistence... at times even with an abundant vein of comedy, especially when it fell to him to confute the extravagances of our judges. But turn it as he might, he returned by preference to his political and philosophical ideas; as though a vortex drew him inexorably into the narrow spout of that funnel.

A strange contrast with the bearing of our leader, from whose cell there issued, for hours on end, not the smallest sign of life; so that more than once I had asked myself whether it were not the tomb of Felice Orsini; whether Felice Orsini had not become the tomb of his own soul.

One hour each day we were permitted to walk in the great courtyard; but not simultaneously. Pieri was taken out for his breath of air alone; upon his return, Orsini and I went out together. We wore our straitjackets even while walking, and were watched not merely by sight but at musket-point.

During that brief hour, Orsini would expand his lungs like a man reborn; but the gaze he turned upon the blue sky was full of melancholy. He walked at a rapid pace and I, reared in military discipline, followed him, instinctively keeping slightly apart. This would occur: if I was in ill humor and did not speak, he would turn his head and question me with a look, as though to say: “A storm brewing?” But if I spoke, he would stride on as though absorbed in the vision of other worlds, or, if he answered, the words came from the depths, drawn out as with a corkscrew.

Those brief words sufficed to reveal to me the man who was undergoing rapidly a radical transformation.

Felice Orsini had brought into prison two resentments; one against Pieri and one against Giuseppe Mazzini. It is well known that in his *Memorie* the impetuous Romagnol had portrayed Mazzini as a modern Mahomet who presumed to model the destinies of Europe upon the caprices of his odalisques — a circumstance of which the monarchist Cavour made admirable use. He distributed those *Memorie* in thousands of copies, thus cunningly throwing a bridge which afforded the opportunity to cross the Rubicon, to furl the banner bearing the motto *“Dio e Popolo”* to all too many Mazzinians, either wavering from weakness of fiber, or eager to justify with any pretext their base and mercenary calculations.

But the old resentment against the Master dissolved on the day when the guards and Giulio Favre informed Orsini of the noble defense of him made by the “Prophet” on the morrow of the attempt, at a time when other patriots, fastidious regarding the means of redeeming the Fatherland, had well-nigh branded him as a common assassin.

To this transformation, which I might call regenerative, another and vaster one of quite a different order corresponded. Better to face the subject at once. Felice Orsini was the only one of the three *morituri* who listened to the exhortations of the chaplain-dragoon more than passively, with a complaisant ear. And our daily walks insinuated in me a presentiment of what afterwards came to pass. The convictions slowly acquired through a life of struggle were, in the man, gradually disintegrating, and allowed to reappear, nay to predominate, the underlying strata saturated with the fables absorbed in unknowing childhood. In the imminence of his end, he returned to those religious practices to which only the blind multitude should still attribute essential virtue.

He therefore deceives himself, and unwittingly belies the truth, who, admiring the stoic fortitude with which Orsini mounted the scaffold, would make of him a model of consistency to be set against the figure of Silvio Pellico and others, softened, as it were, by their sufferings. But he would be equally mistaken who should presume to safeguard his greatness by passing over his conversion in silence. Silence attenuates nothing and cancels nothing.

One may well say in his defense that he succumbed to a cause that was anything but ignoble. Capable of leonine daring under a fleeting impulse, he was powerless, by reason of his supremely sensitive nature, against a thought that was tormenting, insistent, obstinate, fixed... not the thought of the death that hung over him, but that other, springing from the consciousness of his own responsibility in the ruin of high ideals; from the vision of the tragic death of others. His hair had rapidly turned white with it, and more than one delicate fiber of his brain must have been shaken and, as it were, scarred by it.

Felice Orsini, moreover, never posed before his contemporaries as a discoverer of philosophical or moral truths. A patriot he called himself instead, and was so from earliest youth; a patriot he was and proclaimed himself until his head lay beneath the blade of the guillotine. He had pledged to Italy his every energy and the last drop of his own blood. Italy has had greater sons; she has had none more unconditionally faithful. I say that there is no need to glean in other fields in order to establish the glory of Felice Orsini!
Beyond the chaplain-dragoon, there appeared almost daily at La Roquette a very powerful personage, the Prefect of Police, Pietri. He treated us in a most affable manner; nor did he ever fail to exert himself to instill into our minds some ray of hope... now affirming that the Court of Appeal would in all probability annul our trial; now assuring us that the Empress and a powerful court party showed themselves so openly disposed to clemency that Napoleon's mind would end by being influenced and overcome thereby.

But neither his manner nor his intelligent countenance availed to dispel the instinctive distrust, to bridge the abyss that separates the conspirator from a man who must, by force of circumstance, hunt him down and derive his own satisfaction from his ruin. This distrust was in me strengthened by other circumstances. On several occasions I had sent to Elisa letters of the most passionate character. When I wished to write, the guard on duty would free my right hand more or less; then, having bound it again, would carry the letter to the prison Director to have his endorsement placed upon it. But my ardent appeals received no reply... absolute silence, as though Elisa had utterly abandoned me, or were dead. It is easy to imagine my torment. Then, all at once, there entered my heart the suspicion that it was a matter of refined cruelty on the part of the Police, in pursuit of ends I could not clearly comprehend but whose existence I divined. From that moment my torment ceased... to give place to a sullen rancour against those who had inflicted it upon me.

Moreover, Pietri had the fault of bringing to the prison, almost without exception, one personage or another. A morbid curiosity appeared to drive the upper classes to verify, who knows, perhaps whether those who had dared to provoke such disorder were indeed composed of flesh and bone. To me, that being put on show, like a wild beast in a cage, weighed terribly.

One day I found in my cell, with Pietri, a high-ranking military man who had his head bandaged. I do not clearly remember whether it was General Macan or General Roguet. Pietri introduced him to me simply as “one of your victims”; which caused my ill humor to break forth. I retorted sharply that I had sought to strike no one save the man who had been the ruin of my Country. And as for being a victim — I would most willingly have exchanged places with that wounded man.

At this the officer stepped forward and, extending his right hand, declared that he bore not the least grudge, either against me or against any other. And I clasped, as best I was able, that hand; but I made no reply and, turning to the Prefect, asked him what interest the Police could have in intercepting my wife's letters.

He appeared surprised; but contented himself with a shrug of the shoulders.

The following day he returned alone, and before entering the cell stood for some seconds upright upon the threshold, filling the doorway with his fine and tall person. I thought I perceived in that countenance something unwonted, something inquisitive. He entered and, after repeating in a manner more affable and, I was about to say, more paternal than usual, the customary phrases, rubbed his brow and, lowering his voice somewhat — the guard had, moreover, withdrawn at his appearance — spoke to me with intensity of his deep sympathy for my youth. And, lamenting the sad fate that lay in store for me, partly through force of circumstances and partly also — he said — through the supreme recklessness of my companions, he assured me that he had pondered long to find a means of saving me, and not me alone! I already knew how at Court there was no reluctance to adopt measures tempered with clemency. But I was unaware — he added — that His Majesty the Emperor was himself animated by the most lively sympathies for Italy, whose greatness he admired and whose misfortunes profoundly moved him. Once set upon such a course, a man of that stamp — who could predict where he would halt? Certainly no sovereign in Europe was in a position to render greater service to the redemption of Italy than Napoleon. So that if I viewed the matter with the eye of an enlightened patriot, I would not be slow to understand that I could serve myself and my Country better by aiding, and so to speak allying myself with, the man I had attempted to kill.

I believe my face expressed my profound astonishment in full, for Pietri laid a paternal hand upon my shoulder and, lowering his voice still further, said:

“I know that I am dealing with an intelligent young man and I therefore expect not to be considered a boor. Between His Majesty and his high designs stand the internal enemies of the Empire. Too weak to act openly, they have secretly allied themselves with the external enemies and have conspired. I have not come to propose anything dishonorable to you. The safety and welfare of States have their ineluctable demands. Aid the eminent personage upon whom the triumph of the Italian cause may depend; shield him against his internal enemies, and I give you my word of honor that you will not be long in rejoining your young wife. By what strange aberration would you leave her poor, abandoned, and alone to struggle in the world and against the world?”

Now the true reason for Elisa's pretended silence appeared to me not merely hinted at but manifest; now my indignation had twofold cause to break forth. “I know nothing,” I replied, “and have nothing to reveal. But even if I had... you yourself, if I were to accept your proposals, would be the first to feel horror for me. Nor should I have expected such proposals from you, a Corsican... Italians abhor traitors!”

My natural vivacity urged me to gesticulate, and since I could not, I trembled. But without intending it, I had touched a sensitive chord in a heart not entirely stripped of noble feeling.

I saw that man, hardened as he was to so many emotions, cover his face with his hands. A few seconds passed, and he looked me in the face... his eyes were red with tears. He departed, promising that he would do everything in his power to save our heads. Should he fail in his purpose, he would tender his own resignation.
I have no reason whatsoever to doubt the sincerity of that man; but what he actually did, or attempted to do, I cannot say. This much is certain: on the eleventh of March he came to La Roquette and communicated to Felice Orsini, if nothing else, his hopes. It was shortly before the hour appointed for our walk. The conversation between the two was still at its height when that hour struck and the guards led me into the courtyard, alone. On my return, Felice Orsini, always so taciturn and, one might almost say, somber, appeared to me suddenly transformed and, I was about to say, cheerful. The moment he saw me he said in a loud voice: “Di Rudio, take heart. We shall not be executed!”

This occurred, I repeat, on the morning of the eleventh of March. That same evening, the Court of Cassation rejected our appeal. If Orsini's affirmation was destined to find its correspondence in reality, the events of the twelfth of March would have demonstrated it. The day passed and our expectations were disappointed. That very night of the twelfth, by torchlight, work was proceeding in the square of La Roquette to erect the scaffold.

The explanation of the enigma?

I received it later from the lips of Mathieu de la Drôme. Public opinion was exerting a powerful pressure in our favor. In defiance of the Police, in the popular meeting-places a stirring song was being sung in praise of “the heroes of the fourteenth of January.” The Empress and a powerful court party had declared themselves for clemency. Napoleon III called their demonstrations indecent; yet he lacked the heart to oppose them openly. To eliminate the enemies who had fallen into his power, to strike terror among those who had escaped him... such was the secret yearning of the liberticide; but it had to be done while affecting to have had his hand forced, as though from scrupulous respect for the Law, shifting the responsibility onto others.

The Council of State was convened to decide upon the greater or lesser expediency of the imperial pardon, and the Emperor made a brief appearance before it. “The Councillors of the Crown,” he said, “were free to vote according to their own conscience; but he could not refrain from expressing one conviction of his own. Within that Council, traitors lay hidden.”

It was the somber threat. The Councillors were assembled not to give way to the tide of pity, but to render a service to their master. Could the dignitaries of an Empire whose very essence was sharp dealing and corruption — and which in such a vortex would have swept all France along with it — hesitate between their lucrative sinecures and the lives of a few outcasts, rendered incapable of reaction and of doing harm? And had not the reason of State dissimulated far other acts of cowardice?

As if this were not enough, the Archbishop of Paris, scenting the situation, deadened the mild influence of Eugenia Montijo by interposing between her and the Emperor the measured, subtle exhortations of the Church. Never had the hatreds and interests of clericalism and of the State shown themselves so closely akin... the conspirators smelled of Freemasonry; they had fought body and soul to put an end to that temporal power which is the ill-concealed and supreme goal of every religious imposture; they, were it in their power, would have renewed the struggle. It was well worth suppressing them, even if in doing so one disowned Christ yet again.

The Council of State and the clergy... Napoleon III obtained from them more than he had hoped, and his mind, which knew how to sound characters in spite of their masks, must have exulted darkly, and perhaps, like the despot of tragedy, was saying within himself: “Oh, how many are the consciences that are sold!”
