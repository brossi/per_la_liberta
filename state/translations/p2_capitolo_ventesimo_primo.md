CHAPTER TWENTY-FIRST

"FREE... in a year!" I had not said it out of selfishness alone, nor out of pity alone, still less out of vainglory. My first escape attempt had been pondered, precisely in those days at La Roquette. There I had made the acquaintance of an Englishman who was serving six months' imprisonment for fraudulent bankruptcy. He was a man endowed with a certain culture, of few words; he possessed, from what I could conclude, means more than sufficient. Like the great majority of his countrymen, he took an interest in politics and hated Napoleon. He himself came, so to speak, to meet my own inclination, and offered to help me with all his resources. *"In chiesa coi santi, in taverna coi ghiottoni!"* — with saints in church, with gluttons at the tavern! And, on the other hand, if hypocritical society finds it convenient to take account only of a man's faults in order to depress him, despite his virtues; I find it more logical, more fraternal, to fix one's mind upon his virtues and to exalt him, despite his faults. Had my Englishman truly acquired his wealth by reprehensible means? To spend it in restoring a husband to his widowed consort, a father to his son, was to turn ill-gotten gains to good use. I accepted.

We had calculated that he would return to freedom before I was taken to Toulon. We knew that the condemned, before being transported to the tropical territories, make a halt, of greater or lesser duration, at that penal station. We had no plan of the locality, and contriving a well-defined scheme proved a disjointed affair; but it was not unknown that if it is possible to induce, for money, a human creature to commit base acts, it is equally possible to induce him, for money, to attempt acts of generous daring.

Once out of La Roquette, my Englishman would have made his way to Toulon. It would be easy enough, after my arrival, to ascertain the place where I was confined and the persons to whose custody I was entrusted. Simultaneously, he would have organised a means of rapid transport, by sea or by land. Money, lavished with an open hand, would have broken my chains, offering me thereby the opportunity to profit from that means... Italy and Spain are not, from the French coast, the ends of the earth. The more I studied the character of my Englishman, the more I became convinced that he possessed qualities rendering him capable of bringing the enterprise to a good conclusion.

The day of his release came quickly enough, and I, embracing him, slipped clandestinely into his hands a letter for Elisa... the only one, as I later learned, that she ever received from me.

A week afterwards, I was transported by railway, in a cellular compartment, as far as Marseilles, and from there by horse-drawn coach, as far as Toulon. It was the month of April (1858), and nature was in a wondrous state of flowering when I was locked in a casemate below sea-level and chained, together with other wretches, to an iron bar fixed into the damp wall.

One wishes to know what occupation Count Carlo di Rudio was put to during a period of approximately seven months? I wore a red jacket and a pair of yellow trousers; I wore a green Phrygian cap, the distinctive mark of those condemned in perpetuity (those condemned to a term wore it red); I was without moustaches and had my hair cut in strips. I slept, when I could sleep, on a wide beam. The hours in which I lay wakeful, I passed in waging a furious hunt against the insects that tormented me, and in rubbing away at the ring that bound me. This last labour of mine was so persistent and tenacious that, on no fewer than four occasions, I managed to be taken to the blacksmith and thus to walk, holding my chain between my hands, a quarter of a mile in the open air... on four separate occasions seeing the Sun once more.

As at La Roquette, morbid curiosity exposed me to the humiliating visits of this or that personage. At that period, the Czarina was attempting to restore her ailing health by breathing the balmy air of the Riviera. The officers of a Russian squadron, en route for Nice, where they were to take the pallid sovereign on board, put in at Toulon and wished to see the man who had attempted to kill Napoleon... the man who had inflicted upon their Fatherland the humiliation of Sebastopol. They offered to bestow upon me a sum of money to provide for my most pressing needs. But the Regulations allowed me nothing beyond bread and water, and the jailers opposed it. So that I derived from it nothing but the sterile satisfaction of being able to say that even the steppes of eastern Europe contributed their contingent to the swarm of the importunate.

But among my visitors there was one, of an altogether different kind, who deserves a mention entirely apart.

Sister Teresa had not contented herself with her first letter and my first reply. She had sent me at least a dozen more. They all exhorted me to trust in God, to repent of my transgressions; to turn myself, with fervour, towards the wide-open arms of divine mercy, which would surely not reject me. Wearied to death, I had written to her, a few days before leaving La Roquette, that I had been educated to place faith in facts, and facts alone. Words slid inexorably off my soul, and hers were nothing but words. I thought I had put an end to the comedy... but one does not become worthy of an apparition of Christ for nothing. Unexpectedly, I beheld again, even at Toulon, the fine and elegant handwriting of my sister in God.

Nothing less than the visit of a bishop was announced to me — a bishop travelling, as a missionary, to China. I was to open every thought of mine to him. He was a holy man, brimful of celestial grace, and he would not fail to "touch my heart."

One day, at a wholly unusual hour, the gate of the casemate swings open and in comes an old man of about sixty, wrapped in a violet-coloured cloth. The jailer points me out to him and departs. I found myself face to face with the announced missionary bishop... who evidently proposed to inaugurate his apostolic triumphs by rendering, through my conversion, a service to the Empire.

He gave vent to the conversation by declaring that he was in that place at the behest of Sister Teresa, and straightway added that, in compliance with the exhortations of that pious soul, he had provided, with sufficient generosity, for the earthly needs of my wife and my child.

At this point I scrutinised his face intently, and perceived in it nothing but an expression of candid good-nature. And yet those saintly episcopal lips had lied like those of a common rogue.

However that may be, presuming that he had thus well disposed my spirit by means of the supposed beneficence, that most pure vessel of celestial grace proceeded to spread the balm he had deposited upon my breast and, in a tranquil voice, announced to me how Sister Teresa had thought of me as well. He was the bearer of a gift from her. Without further ado, he drew from beneath his violet vestment a coral rosary mounted in silver, from which I saw suspended a crucifix and a medal.

I observed it without extending my hand, and in a voice no less tranquil: "A most beautiful gift... if only I could pawn it and exchange it for tobacco. But many things are denied in here that are permitted to simple mortals. Please thank Sister Teresa on my behalf, and write to tell her that I have refused."

The bishop's eyes flashed with an Inquisitor's gleam. "My son," he said, "you will not refuse at least this further pledge of brotherly love. It is blessed by the Pope and will bring you good fortune!" And here he set swinging before my eyes a pair of small scapulars of satin, with the features of Christ embroidered in gold.

— "Oh, father! In this wretched place, with so much filth... of what use could they be save as a comfortable nest for my lice?"

He did not consider himself beaten, and without uttering a word, drew out and spread before me an elegant prayer-book. But I observed to him that for some time I had no longer prayed, and that in that jumble of words my mind would find no nourishment whatsoever.

The conversation came to an abrupt end. The bishop gathered up his gifts and, turning his back on me, departed without bidding me farewell, with a step that sought to appear dignified and solemn. I believe he was thinking: "If the Chinese are all like this dog, I am in for a fine time!"

Then, shrugging my shoulders, I looked at my fellow captives in the chain. Some watched me with an expression of stupefaction, as though I were the devil himself; others with sparkling eyes, as if my hair cut in strips could not quite conceal from them the features of a hero. But I was neither a hero nor a devil. I was simply a reasonable man who, guided by his own deep convictions, had evaded the subtle arts of imposture.

I find it fitting to relate here, by analogy, an incident that befell my wife while I was already serving out my sentence at Cayenne.

Nottingham, where Elisa had withdrawn to spend, with her mother, her days of widowhood, was, and is certainly still, an episcopal see. Catholics regard that bishopric as an advanced bastion of truth against heresy and, giving free play to fancy, surround it with the serene air that inspires chaste thoughts... a kind of oasis where, by day, the doves flutter; at dark of night, the luminous angels.

Unexpectedly, Elisa received an invitation to present herself at the episcopal palace on a matter of the utmost importance. Mindful of my counsel, she did not venture to go alone. But here I think the words are better suited to the lips of my Elisa...

He pressed the button of a bell, and the lady came at once. He begged her to recount, simply, the conversation she had had with the Bishop of Nottingham.

"There is not a great deal to tell. I went to the episcopal palace with my mother. She was asked to wait in the antechamber; I was immediately conducted into Monsignor's study... a tall, lean man, all skin and bones. He received me with a grave face and, unfolding a letter that he took good care not to hand to me, told me that he was exceedingly grieved to have sorrowful news to communicate. A colleague of his, stationed at the penitentiary of Cayenne, had written to notify him of my husband's death; which had moreover already been confirmed by the press and by the French Authorities. Although the misfortune was irreparable and he sympathised with my inevitable grief, it was not without a deep and serene satisfaction of his priestly soul that he could assure me that you had sincerely repented of your transgressions, that you had passed away acknowledging the sanctity of the religion of your fathers. On this point you had, moreover, expressed the ardent desire that I, for my eternal salvation, should embrace the Catholic faith and become, if at all possible, a nun. You further enjoined upon me, as the expression of your last, absolute will, to entrust our son to the care of some Catholic, apostolic, Roman Institution, so that he might be raised in the principles which had sustained you in your misfortune and comforted you on your deathbed.

— For a moment I found neither tears nor words. Then a flash crossed my mind and, looking him steadily in the eye, I said: 'Count Carlo di Rudio cannot have died in such a fashion. And since this is a lie, it is equally a lie that he is no more. I feel it within me — my husband lives!'

— The priest crumpled the letter, flung it on the writing-table, and with a terrible face (which betrayed the desire to burn me alive) loosening and clenching his fingers as though they were talons, said through gritted teeth: 'Ah, if only we were in France!'

I was seized with great fear and, uttering a cry, I fled. I was so shaken that I could say nothing to my mother until we were out in the street, far away, very far away!"

Thus — Count di Rudio continued — ended the Catholic clergy's attempt to act as spy on behalf of the Second Empire.

While I, amid the horrors of the casemate, laboured at gnawing through the link of my chain in order to catch a fleeting glimpse of the Sun, my Englishman was engaged, for his part, in bringing our agreement to fruition, with a cunning and pertinacity wholly Anglo-Saxon. He had gathered every possible item of information about the place where I was confined and the routines of the prison; he had procured disguise clothing; he had prepared places of temporary refuge; means of transport as far as the Pyrenees. With the help of some woman or other, he had managed to establish direct communication with my immediate jailer. It had even been settled that the day of my escape should coincide with that of my next excursion to the blacksmith. Unexpectedly, I was brought before the Commander of the Arsenal.

I heard myself informed that on the morrow I would be embarked aboard the frigate *La Dure*. "You are going to Cayenne," added the Commander in a dry tone, though not entirely devoid of an underlying affability. "Vile climate! Europeans compelled to live in your conditions cannot survive there more than two or three years. I am authorised to tell you that if you reveal your accomplices, you will be immediately set at liberty and furnished with the means necessary to leave the frontiers of France. It is within your power to die in the penitentiary, or to live in the bosom of your family. Choose!"

I replied that I knew nothing. But had I possessed revelations to make, the opinion in which I held the servants of the Empire was far too low for me to place the least trust in their promises. I chose the penitentiary!

— As you wish! retorted the Commander bluntly, and gave orders for me to be taken back to the casemate.

On the morrow I was on the high seas. My first escape attempt had failed!

CHAPTER TWENTY-SECOND

*La Dure* sailed on, bearing in her ample hull, divided into three decks, some three hundred condemned men. Gomez and I were confined in the deepest. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and could not have harboured excessive antipathies towards us, no sooner learned who we were than he had us transferred to the upper deck. Indeed he pressed his benevolent partiality to the point of permitting me to go up on deck from time to time to smoke freely... the Regulations prescribed the trifling penalty of fifty lashes of the rope for anyone who had done so in the compartments below.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz in the Canaries to take on water and remained there two days, with hatches closed to deprive us of any possibility of escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale of the Salut archipelago, where some two hundred convicts were disembarked.

Gomez, myself, and another hundred or so wretches transferred to a small steam-vessel, and on the 11th of December 1858, after a day and a half of additional navigation, we were anchored off the Montagne d'Argent, which rises at the confluence of the São Jorge and the Oyapock, at a relatively short distance from the border of Brazil. There had flourished, in former times in that place, a free colony where free hands cultivated coffee and every tropical fruit that might tempt the human palate. The French Government had purchased it. Its officers occupied the planter's house; numerous reed-thatched shelters, supported on stakes, were erected there for the shelter of the condemned; the earth was pierced here and there in order to bury, in a slow agony, those refractory to discipline; a few substantial buildings arose too, for the storehouses, for defence and for offence... the vanguard of civilisation had been transformed into a penal establishment. But the natural beauties concealed admirably the hell that men had accumulated there.

The commandant, a Major of the marine infantry, whom the Republic had placed on the retired list and the Emperor had reinstated and adorned with badges of honour, hastened to receive us "in consignment" and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it and, looking me up and down from head to foot: "Ah, so you are that —" (here came an expression fit for a pigsty!) "I am glad to see you here, and I swear to you that you will leave your skin here!"

That piece of coarseness was so entirely uncalled for that even the guards pulled a grimace of disgust. But I did not lose my composure, and, almost as though flattered by a compliment: "I thank you for the good wish, and return it with all my heart!" He clenched his jaw; but he possessed Gallic ferocity, not the French spirit, and remained tight-lipped.

That small incident persuaded me that at the Montagne d'Argent the air was blowing ill for me, and from that very moment I resolved to repress every impulse. The relative freedom granted to the well-disciplined — I thought — may afford me the occasion to earn myself a greater one... the freedom of civilised men. But it was fated that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the hut assigned to me were some fifty convicts... they formed my squad. I passed the first night sleeping, in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise we set out, our bones aching, towards the forest. I wore a large straw hat, a pair of wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, a brown blouse, upon whose back was marked, in large characters, my number... the baptism of the penitentiary!

In crossing a swamp, I had the misfortune to lose one of my clogs, and the stumps of reeds, cut flush with the water to facilitate the ford, cut into the sole of my foot. I was obliged to contrive a way to cover three miles limping and marking, in blood, each footprint; until I arrived, the last of my squad, in the depths of the forest... a splendid forest which, for a little, almost made me forget that I was a convict and that I was in pain.

A pile of logs. The day's task was to carry them, on one's shoulder, to the penitentiary, making five consecutive trips. I singled out a log that seemed, to the eye, light; but it was *bois de fer* — ironwood — and when I had raised it to the height of my knees, it fell back noisily upon the pile. Then, instead of making another attempt, I sat down, resolved to rest for as long as it might take to recover my breath. The convict-overseer stood watching me, and when he was certain of my intention: "Get up, you filthy creature! If you don't get a move on, I'll send you packing with kicks to your backside. A curse on you and all Italian stock!"

Had he contented himself with goading me with a mere threat, I should in all probability have taken the matter with complete philosophical calm; but that contempt for my nationality stirred in my breast the anger accumulated against the bullies who made sport of it. I swung my *sabre d'abattis* and leapt forward with a bound of which, given my condition, I should not have thought myself capable. "Take care! Italians do not suffer insults! The first — let it pass! At the second, your pumpkin will see the sun no more from atop your shoulders!"

My companions, who had set out one by one, were already far off. Alone, perfectly alone in a deserted place! He swallowed, turning pale, the pill. Nevertheless, in a show of bravado, he asked me my name. "Content yourself with the number!" and I turned my back on him.

We were making a bad beginning! But as chance would have it, my overseer, though he could puzzle it out more or less, did not know how to write. To make his report, he had to apply to the clerk of the supervising Engineer; who, with the help of the number, verified, in the registers, my name and who I was. What sentiments animated him towards me, I cannot say; but I learned that he persuaded my overseer not to press his cruelty too hard. And into his exhortations he skillfully insinuated the element of fear: "That man," he said, "fears neither Laws, nor punishments, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later, you are a dead man!"

On his return to the forest, the overseer drew me to one side. "We have a certain sort of stuff here at the penitentiary!... But I know how to make my distinctions." And to prove it, he begged my pardon for his ill words and granted me licence not to work, either then or thereafter. I was to conceal myself daily in the depths of the forest... I was to reappear at the last departure of my squad. And he extended his right hand, as though to seal a pact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours allotted to labour stretched out amid a wondrous vegetation, smoking a cigarette, listening to the intricate murmuring of the forest. In my eyes there was, in those hours, a fantastical interlacing of embroidery I had never seen before... enormous leaves cut from velvet, tall straight trunks like minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their colours, and subjected strange insects to their not quite concealed amours; birds with metallic iridescence, darting out of shadows all a rustle, passed above my head on spread wings, swift as lightning. There were caverns of a deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults, dripping dew and fragrance, through which the Sun gazed placidly, lordly, as though keeping watch that every blade of grass had what it needed... the Sun which, worshipped consciously or unconsciously in all ages by all peoples, had with secular energy drawn into itself that temple, worthy of a God. Oh how beautiful is the Earth when it is not defaced by the cupidity of man!... A temple worthy of Elisa, too!... Her image, intensely evoked, held dominion over me as though she were present.

It was in that forest, and in those hours, that I pondered my second escape attempt. The plan to which I finally resolved was suggested to me by the primitive art of the Indians ranging the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a great tree, to hollow it out so as to fashion a serviceable dug-out canoe. With it I would have ascended, by night, the Oyapock for a stretch of fifty miles, and then crossed the São Jorge, to make landfall on Brazilian soil. Having escaped the pursuit of the French Authorities, I should then have been obliged to face, in all probability, the ferocity of savages, and beyond any doubt the nearly insuperable obstacles of unexplored regions and of the climate. But the prize was worth the risk.

None of this could even be begun without the help of several accomplices. I sounded the spirit of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, satisfied with his position as assistant canteen-keeper and probably dreading the worst, feigned not to understand me. I was obliged to have recourse to less noble material. By what criteria was I to choose it? How was I to acquire over it the ascendancy necessary to bind it to my purposes?

In this case too, my good star, more than my own wisdom, came to my aid.

The penitentiary received its supplies of fresh meat from Brazil. At intervals more or less regular, a sailing vessel would arrive with a cargo of cattle. The shore, which shelved gradually into the ocean, did not afford sufficient depth to permit it to moor close to land, so the anchor was cast offshore, at a distance of some three miles. The overseer would then choose one convict from each squad of ten and form them into a special party which made its laborious way, wading with water above the waist, up to the side of the vessel. The wild bullocks, maddened with rage, were thrown into the sea, and the men drove them, with great shouts and great blows, into a stockade within the penitentiary.

Once that work had begun, it proceeded without interruption until the unloading was complete, skipping, as needs required, even a meal or two. At the Montagne d'Argent, meals were taken by squads of ten. The squad-leader received in charge the provisions, diminished by the sordid speculation of the officers, and set them out, upon a sort of table, in the ten mess-bowls. Each convict ran to his own and devoured it, seated on some step, or on a tree-trunk, or on the bare ground. On days of unloading, it fell to the squad-leader to keep the absent convict's ration and hand it to him on his return.

Having been chosen to lend a hand at that frenzied work, one day I made my way back at eight o'clock in the evening, when my squad was already snoring in its hammocks. Hunger, sharpened by incessant exertion, by the prolonged bath, by the keen sea breeze, had become ferocious. I shake the squad-leader, and cause a half-commotion, and he, showing me his fist instead of the mess-bowl: "*Tais-toi, Italien de m...*" (the word of Cambronne).

I chew on nothing and hold my tongue. On the evening of the following day, I take my place in the rank with the squad and wait around the table. When the squad-leader arrives, holding up the pot brimming with soup, I lunge at it and give it a mighty kick. The leader stands there dumbfounded, arms outstretched, hands empty. "It takes," I say, "nine Frenchmen to make an Italian go hungry, but a single Italian is enough to make nine Frenchmen go hungry!"

Now it is the others who raise the commotion. The overseer, a certain Cosson, comes running, and having been informed of the matter, asks me curtly: "Why, instead of taking your revenge, did you not file your report?"

— "Because Italians do not inform."

Cosson, stealing Salomon's trade, condemned both the squad-leader and Count Carlo di Rudio to fifty lashes of the rope. But my resoluteness and, above all, my fierce reply, had instantly raised me in the estimation of those wretches. They surrounded the overseer and spoke to such effect that he consented to swallow his sentence. From that day, the convicts condescended, in their goodness, to treat me fraternally as one worthy of their fellowship; from that day I found it a relatively easy undertaking to study the various temperaments, to select eight individuals sufficiently enamoured of freedom to face cheerfully, in the attempt to regain it, whatever danger might arise.

We left our hut by night, crawling out like beasts emerging from their lair, in order to deceive any eyes that might still be awake. We proceeded by separate paths to a pre-arranged spot on the edge of the forest; then, united, we pressed on into its labyrinths. From the very first night, we had marked our route with signs nearly invisible to other mortals, averting in that way the danger of losing ourselves.

A tree of gigantic proportions was felled at its base with comparatively little effort; but in falling it caught in the dense undergrowth and remained propped upon it. We had to climb up and hollow it out while clinging to it... a complication that consumed precious time. We finally resolved to have recourse to fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary, and the smoke was rendered invisible by the night; but amid so much resinous vegetation, amid so many dead shrubs, amid so much kindling, amid the debris accumulated over centuries, the operation demanded great circumspection, lest one were to feed a conflagration so swift and so vast that even the tropical deluges would have found difficulty in mastering it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constitutes, for me, one of those incidents whose impression only death can efface. We were not in that land as pioneers of civilisation, and someone might even remark, not without irony, that we were not of the stuff of missionaries. But the setting — oh the grandeur of that setting, rendered ineffable by the silent night — how it penetrated, together with its own, into our very being, and how it transformed us! All about us, there was nothing that had not been born and grown in the most boundless freedom, and freedom became almost palpable in every atom of air we breathed. It seemed to me that my labouring muscles, in their very exertion, exhaled from every fibre something luminous, as though giving back to things the unstained life that things lent to us. There were moments in which I could have sworn I was as vigorous and eternal as the virgin forest. And we became so thoroughly a homogeneous part of the setting that, after the first few nights, numberless troops of monkeys, drawn by the glow of the resin, came to contemplate and almost to encourage, brightening it with their presence, our work.

After a couple of months, or a little more, we succeeded in dragging into a bend in the river, and in concealing among the lianas, a canoe seven feet wide and twenty-five feet long. It floated admirably. "And now," I exclaimed, when we parted full of enthusiasm, "to the oars and the sail!"

Unexpectedly, an event to which, at its first manifestation, no one had paid serious attention, threw the entire penal colony into disorder, and with it every hope of mine.

In broad daylight, beneath a cloudless sky that is a whole lyric poem in itself, with a sun capable of hard-boiling ostrich eggs in its direct rays... a sentry is seized with violent trembling. Relieved as quickly as possible, he was seen to make his way with slow steps, still trembling, to the antediluvian hut that bore the pompous name of "cabinet of decency." He emerged from it ashen, undone. He declared that try as he might, he had been unable to pass a single drop of water. The convicts laughed loudly... the thing seemed to them so comical. By evening, the poor wretch was vomiting clotted blood, black as coal... two days later he was a corpse.

But before he had drawn his last breath, five, seven, ten convicts, while showing at the thermometer a high temperature, were trembling like leaves. The penitentiary had been invaded by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was swift beyond all telling. In those days, it was held that the seat of the disease lay in the intestines; the remedies, scarce in the extreme and wholly inadequate to the vastness of the mortality, were applied, for greater misfortune, on erroneous principles. The Commander gave orders to throw open the warehouses and the cantina and to leave them at the full disposal of the able-bodied. From that cantina, flung wide and black with shadow, there seemed to issue the sensation of fatality. If yellow fever was not crouching in there, it had there some mysterious ally. The convicts were inexorably drawn into it by vice and by fear. They brutified themselves within it and passed from drunkenness to delirium; from delirium to nothingness!... with a death all the more sudden the more robust they were.

The arrangements made for the victims were of the simplest: to cast them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Each evening, the chief overseer went to make his report at the Commander's house.

One night the Commander lost his wife and two children almost simultaneously, and he ceased to concern himself with my welfare. Not long afterwards, it was the chief overseer who ceased to make his customary report. He had too easily yielded to the temptations of the cantina, and when he was found stretched out on the beaten earth, it was at first suspected that he was simply drunk. But his gums were coated with bloody froth... he died without having recovered either speech or the faintest glimmer of consciousness, and he was one of the many whom I was obliged to seize by the feet. "One, two, three..." Before casting him in, I should have wished to say a *Requiescat* over him!... he had spared me fifty lashes of the rope. But down there in that pit... it would have sounded like irony.

I lent my hands to the burying, one by one, in that fashion, of my eight fellow-conspirators in the escape attempt, and of others, and still others... I counted past a hundred; then I lost count and counted no more. Some wretch who already seemed to have crossed the boundary beyond which one suffers no more, hardly thrown into that horrible common grave, was seen to revive and writhe in agony.

When the fever, finding no more flesh to gnaw upon, at last disappeared, the French Deputies raised in Paris the customary humanitarian clamour. But the Minister answered them, in a tone of triumph, that he had already given the appropriate orders to transfer the entire colony to the Île du Salut. And indeed one evening, standing out against the crepuscular light, we saw the dark trail of smoke from a vessel proceeding at full steam towards our shore. It was the *Abeille*, bringing us His Excellency's orders. On the following day the entire colony was on board... Of more than six hundred — convicts, military men, civil servants, and attendants alike — we were reduced to sixty-three! The greater part of the survivors had already suffered from yellow fever at other times and on other shores; which had, it seems, rendered them resistant, if not absolutely invulnerable; several, shadows of their former selves, showed signs of having been but recently touched, if not cut down, by it... among these, Antonio Gomez... Oh, how transformed was that good-natured priestly face of his! Three alone had not contracted the infected blood, either then or before. Among the three — the convict di Rudio!

From the deck of the *Abeille*, already churning the broken ocean about her bows, I cast a last glance towards the Montagne d'Argent and think, with profound regret, of my canoe floating solitary and motionless beneath the dense lianas. The landscape dwindles rapidly. The hell that men had accumulated there was no more; what remained was one of its most baleful consequences, the vast charnel pit from which we had miraculously escaped. Nature, indifferent, spread over so much horror her enchanting hues, and in the scintillating air vibrated her mysterious amourings.
