## Revised Translation — Chapter the Twenty-Third

CHAPTER THE TWENTY-THIRD

I HAD scarcely flung my canvas sack upon the quay of the Island of Salvation when hope flooded my breast — intense, overpowering.

The island, with a little play of imagination, presented the shape of a figure 8. Upon the larger loop, which was also the higher, stood the dwellings, the hospital, a church; upon the smaller loop were the workshops, and at its outermost curve, the barracks. From that extremity, facing more or less exactly to the northwest, one could toss a stone by hand onto the long islet of the Devil — the very same that was later to become grimly celebrated for the squalid drama of which Captain Dreyfus was the victim. At low tide, it was not difficult, by leaping from rock to exposed rock, to pass from one island to the other with dry feet.

To the east, another small islet rose from the waters, called San Giuseppe — a rough, nearly circular plain, scattered with a few stunted and dwarfish trees.

The island upon which I found myself measured, by rough estimate and along its greatest diameter, an extent of some two miles. Its shores were rocky and full of inlets.

These were circumstances I had noted in passing. What succeeded in capturing my attention and fixing it so as to plunge me into deep reflection was a small rowing bark running over the waves at no great distance from the shore. It carried four or five convicts aboard, who were rowing and singing. I thought to myself: "What is there to prevent these men from making for the open sea? True, one does not cross the Ocean by oar alone. But why do they not procure themselves a sail? I left a canoe in the waters of the Oyapock, and here I find another at the Island of Salvation. Courage, di Rudio — you have lost nothing!"

I was still standing motionless, my eye fixed upon that nutshell of a craft, when I felt a touch upon my shoulder. I turned with a start and found myself face to face with a man wearing the grotesque uniform of a convict.

I studied him, and he broke the silence:

— I have been told you are Italian... are you not?

— And who are you? By your accent you seem a Roman of Rome.

— I am *Trasteverino*, and proud of it!

— Why are you here?

— That is another pair of sleeves entirely. But may I never see my country again and die with the noose about my throat if, given the chance, I would not do it all over again. A French dog seduced my sister and left her in a fix... I put a hole through his heart. Long live Trastevere blood, and to hell with whoever speaks ill of it! For this I lost my Rome... ten years on my back!... but they will pass. My name is Morbioli!

We exchanged, so to speak, our calling cards; then, all at once:

— How long have you been here?

— Seven years!

— Seven years!... You are Italian, Roman, *Trasteverino*, and you have not attempted to escape?

— Impossible! Alone, not even to think of it. With companions, worse than going out at night! In this accursed place, it is not the musket-shots that are granted to cowards who turn informer — it is clemency! Do you understand me? Denouncing a plot leads to freedom more surely than attempting flight.

— And what of it? One can see you are a newcomer to this place and have no precise notion of the difficulties. Ah, do you think there have been no men with the mettle to risk it? But to what end? Listen!...

And he told me that thirteen years before, thirteen convicts from Île Royale — another French penitentiary situated in those parts — had seized a pilot-boat and made for the open sea. The *Abeille* gave chase, but in vain. Some three months passed and men were already beginning to think they had reached free land, when, on one of her crossings from Cayenne to the Island of Salvation, the *Abeille* sighted a boat adrift. She came alongside — and who was found aboard?

— Three men only. Those three had devoured the other ten.

A flight had also been attempted overland, by way of the Montagne d'Argent. There were twenty of them, and once they had penetrated the forest, no one troubled to pursue them further. After several months, a man gone wild made an unexpected appearance at the penitentiary. He was the sole survivor... he confessed that he had kept himself alive up to that point by having, in one degree or another, stripped the flesh from the other nineteen. Having by then nothing left to set his teeth into, he resolved to give himself up. They ruled that he had eaten more than enough, and... cut off his head.

A flight had also been attempted from the islet of the Devil. The brother of Giacomo Pianori — precursor of Felice Orsini in both the attempt and the martyrdom — had been confined there as a suspect. He had observed that the tide cast numerous tree-trunks upon the shore. He gathered them up and managed to put together a raft. The sinuosities of the beach had lent themselves to the secret labour. He set out by night, with twenty companions. On the open sea, they were overtaken by a squall. The waves broke the raft in two. One part drifted long upon the currents until it ran aground somewhere in Dutch Guiana. The fugitives were given aid, and... handed back to the French authorities. Chained in the pits, exposed to the torrid sun and the torrential rains, they soon breathed their last amid their sufferings. Twelve corpses thrown to the sharks!

The other eight, Pianori among them, also reached the Continent upon the remaining part of the raft; but unfortunately they ran upon a *vase*. Do you know what a *vase* is? A bank of bluish mud that grips like cement. The air is thick with *mosquitos* capable of stripping the carcass of a whale to bare bone within a few days; the earth swarms with crabs — the terrible crabs of Guiana, which burrow long tunnels underground, invade the cemeteries, and... fortunately for them, the corpses sleep sound. The *Abeille*, passing through those parts, found the raft and what little remained of Pianori and the other wretches. The crabs and the insects had not even had the consideration to wait until they were dead, and had most certainly made a feast of them then and there.

I had listened without interrupting. Meanwhile the nutshell of a craft had drawn nearer to the island and, skirting its shore, disappeared now and then behind some rock, only to reappear ever closer. The convicts were gathering on the beach and exchanging shouts with the oarsmen. I asked: What is that boat, and what are those men doing?

— They fish on account of the penitentiary, and when the catch is plentiful, they sell, more or less openly, to anyone who wishes to buy.

He scratched his head and added: "Morbioli is no empty gourd. You are thinking that one might make use of that boat to attempt something new. Do your sums without our four gunboats. They would draw a bead on you merrily, as at a shoot. They would laugh to see you go under. And then there is the *Abeille*..."

I was assigned to the anchor workshop. I laboured half-naked — a heat that shrivelled the skin; the sparks leaping from the anvil spotted my body all over. Yet never, as in those days, did I understand the power of the intellect to resist painful sensations and to master them. The stubborn meditation upon a plan of escape transported me elsewhere — among the rocks of the island, upon the open sea — and the impressions of the workshop reached me as though softened by distance.

My plan was taking shape from the very history of the failures of my predecessors, and it seemed to me that its concatenation issued from my mind harder and more resistant than the iron I was hammering.

Pianori had rushed headlong into his dreadful fate by trusting to a raft — *ergo*, no raft! And why, besides, should I undertake the slow and thankless labour of building one, when I had ready to hand a small vessel lacking only a scrap of sail? I calculated that seizing her by a bold stroke need not prove an excessively arduous undertaking for men of resolute temper, the more so since the fishermen would without doubt submit with good grace to a compulsion that would restore them to liberty.

True it was that being aboard a vessel had profited the other fugitives not at all; but evidently they had known nothing of her handling and had been short of provisions. It was therefore necessary to secure the complicity of some seafaring man; it was necessary to lay in a great quantity of stores.

Where? If the low-lying islet of the Devil had offered a safe hiding-place for a raft, the high, rugged shore of the Island of Salvation could surely offer a secure one for a few sacks of bread, a few demijohns of water and rum.

The *Abeille*? She made regular crossings between the island and Cayenne — one must put to sea during her absence.
