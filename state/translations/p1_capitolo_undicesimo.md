## Chapter Eleven

IN APRIL of 1852 I was once again in Genoa, working for a certain Ferrari, a merchant tailor, when I was informed that Don Bastiano Barozzi, parish priest of San Pietro in Campo, was living as a refugee in Turin. Barozzi, during the siege of Venice, had served as chaplain to a Legion composed almost exclusively of students from Padua. He was one of those priests in whom the cassock did not stifle the love of the Fatherland and who, to passive obedience, preferred to follow the impulses of their own conscience. Italy boasts a good number of such men, and the Church of Rome might have drawn great prestige from them, had she not preferred, in order to please the powerful, to hear their confessions and even lend a hand in their torment.

Deeply versed in ancient history, Don Bastiano judged men through the prism of the Roman world; to the level of Attilio Regolo, of the Scipios, of the Gracchi he would have wished to raise our entire race. During my school holidays, while walking with me along the Ardo and without seeming to make anything of it, he would steep my mind in heroic episodes. I believe that I owe to him, in no small part, my devotion to Liberty.

The priests of murky waters, who dared not wage open war upon him for his patriotic principles, dragged in the Decretals and took exception to his moustache and his goatee, which smacked of the modernist. He stubbornly refused to have them shaved off, and for it he lost the right to say Mass. In his straitened circumstances, he was aided by my father, and that alone had been sufficient for Don Bastiano to cherish towards him a gratitude that bordered on veneration.

It was to this man that I resolved to turn, in the hope of re-entering, with his help, the vortex of action, and thinking that two words spoken face to face are worth more than four long letters, I set off for Turin… on foot, just so as not to lose the habit.

My first care, upon arriving there, was to make my way to the Caffè Nazionale, the favoured haunt of political refugees. Before I reached it, however, I caught sight of a gentleman standing planted before the playbill of a theatre, his face so close to the print that he seemed to be reading it with the tip of his nose. Don Bastiano Barozzi's extreme short-sightedness was known to me, and that single clue was enough to reveal him.

Great was his astonishment at finding me; boundless his joy. He could not contain it and, there and then, without the least regard for the passers-by, pressed two resounding kisses upon my cheeks: "Here... this on my account; this on your father's account!" He then asked me what lucky wind had brought me to Turin. And I:

"Don Bastiano, you know that the di Rudios are not cowards. What is being contemplated, what is being prepared? I have come so as not to grow rusty, and I am counting upon you."

He lowered his eyes and did not answer. But, after a brief silence, as though concluding his own thoughts: "Fortunato Calvi is here. In a few leaps, we can be at his house. Come!"

Fortunato Calvi, the glorious organiser of the Cacciatori delle Alpi, my Colonel, the intimate friend of my family, received me with folded arms, without moving a step, with an expression that was almost one of disdain. The flood of affection that was on the point of bursting forth remained painfully compressed in my breast. I did not even dare to provoke an explanation. But the explanation came of its own accord.

Calvi had been informed by a person "worthy of belief" that I, having left Venice upon his orders, instead of proceeding to Chioggia, had gone over, with arms and baggage, to the Austrian. He even cited the name of one who had sworn to have seen me, behind the trenches, firing upon my own countrymen.

Calumny! — what man has not been, to some degree or another, its victim? Little by little, the breast hardens to its blows so that in time they no longer avail to stir any storm within me. I know bold men who, by their very bearing, almost provoke it of set purpose, for the acrid pleasure of showing themselves superior. But that was the first time I had found myself face to face with it, and its bite was extremely cruel to me.

Nevertheless, I found it easy to demonstrate its absurdity.

Fortunato Calvi, who had no less a desire to find me innocent than I had to prove it to him, smilingly extended his right hand, which I clasped with feverish emotion. While Don Bastiano, mute witness to that scene and a man of exceptionally sensitive feeling, turned upon his heel to conceal a tear.

The consequences of that conversation were, as far as I was concerned, of no slight moment. Giuseppe Mazzini, Luigi Kossuth and Ledru Rollin had openly founded, in London, a European Revolutionary Committee, to which innumerable branches were soon attached. Each People that, having vanquished that nefarious apathy — the cryptogam of souls — understood how regeneration springs from agitation and sacrifice, organised its own. Italy was a hive of them, and by force of circumstance the Committee of Turin held predominance over all the others. Upon it fell, above all, the task of inciting the spirit of rebellion that snaked through the Peninsula and of co-ordinating its movements. Its head, in 1852, was Count Grillanzoni; but the command of eventual military operations fell to Fortunato Calvi.

From Calvi and Count Grillanzoni I received the commission of traversing, as special envoy, the principal cities of Lombardy-Venetia; first to distribute subversive manifestos and bonds of the Mazzinian national loan; then, on a subsequent journey, to inspect the various garrisons and report precise particulars of their strength, their nationality, and their temper.

The system adopted was simple. A passport was procured for me in the name of Carlo Moretti, a silk merchant. It sufficed for me to write: In such-and-such a place I have sold two, three, four bales of white, or green, or yellow silk. The number of bales corresponded to the number of Regiments; the colour indicated their uniform and arm of service. A studied allusion to the particular quality of the merchandise, or to the character of the customer, sufficed to convey whatever other circumstances I deemed it necessary to bring to notice.

I recall the admonition Calvi gave me on the very day of my departure. I was concealing in the stem of my pipe the list of the various Sub-Committees, and I was smiling. Calvi suspected that smile to be the fruit of levity and said sternly: "Do not delude yourself, young man! A slight imprudence, a simple fortuitous incident, and you would be hanged!" I did not fall into the gallows' hands, but I passed within very close reach of them more than once.

My first halt was at Stradella, where I shook hands with Nino Bixio — who had survived the wound sustained at Villa Corsini — and with that Winkler who had been, at Venice, the commander of a Hungarian legion, and who was to close, a few years later, his heroic existence fighting on the banks of the Volturno. Contact with men of such mettle was a source of pleasant excitement for me. But having crossed, with the help of a smuggler, the blue Ticino and ventured but a little way into the domains of Austria, it seemed as if an iron hand were closing around my throat. The oppressor had, after 1849, sharpened his sluggish wits to transform those lands into a pit of hell.

As a general rule, Austria regarded the Provinces subdued by her own arms, and the others reconquered by the allied arms of Russia, as a den of brigands. To vent upon them a barbaric hatred, to gratify the most atrocious vengeances — this appeared, in courtly spheres, the height of political wisdom. Persuaded that he was carrying out a mission entrusted to him by God, Francesco Giuseppe did not even consider himself bound by his own word or by the capitulations he had stipulated. General Klapka, the heroic defender of Komorn, had obtained excellent terms of surrender for the garrison. Every one of them was violated. A code of honour exists even among thieves; but under the pretext of reasons of State, none such existed for Francesco Giuseppe.

Of the thirty-four Hungarian generals — chosen minds, heroic fibres — who had repeatedly compelled the Imperial troops to show their backs, three were condemned to perpetual imprisonment, twelve were with Christian charity shot or hanged. And the carnage would have been greater, had the others not saved themselves by flight and exile. For several months a swarm of executioners, clad — as though in crowning mockery — in the colour of hope, busied themselves in Hungary heaping up the corpses of patriots. All this now seems blotted from the memory of those who shout in the streets their cheers for the contemptible old man. And yet one never ceases to curse the generous souls who, having taken into their own hearts the anguish of the victims, hazard their lives to administer to despots a measure of their own medicine.

In Italy, it is true, Francesco Giuseppe had his talons blunted by Vittorio Emanuele II who, having ascended the throne amid the glacial distrust of his subjects, knew how to bow to the terrible necessities pressing upon him without compromising the future. He refused to sign the peace treaty with Austria unless she undertook the obligation of granting full amnesty to the hundred thousand Italians who, from Lombardy-Venetia, had taken refuge in Piedmont, whether to make war upon her or to escape her persecutions.

Vittorio Emanuele II disposed, even in defeat, of a force not to be lightly reckoned with, and the Emperor kept the pact. Nevertheless, his "pardon" was tacitly conditional upon complete submission to the Austrian yoke. Were pretexts ever lacking? Sequestrations, arbitrary impositions, chains and the gallows were not doled out sparingly by the paternal solicitude of Francesco Giuseppe, and the rod was applied to the backs even of those, men or women, who, without rebelling, gave expression to manly sentiments.

Poor young Italian women, your souls open to every fair and gentle thing; and you mothers, aristocratic or of the people, who were made to pass, with commanded slowness, between two files of hirelings bringing down their cudgels with full force upon your bare limbs — lend me, you, the voice sufficient for a worthy apotheosis of the apostolic beast!

And I name Francesco Giuseppe deliberately, and not Austria; because when the sum of power is concentrated in one man, upon his head, by equal right of justice, must likewise be concentrated the sum of hatred and of infamy.

Mingled among the Italians, I encountered in my wanderings a good number of Hungarian officers, degraded because they had loved their Fatherland and defended it. Harassed with ferocious persistence, they nonetheless had the courage to rebel against the odious commands issued to the detriment of a People bound to them by common misfortune. And there were those among them who, despairing of better times, put an end, by suicide, to a life that had become unendurable.

Fortunately, as though to confound the oppressors, Nature has so disposed her affairs that death itself is the great sower of life, and tyranny itself the great fecundator of every liberty!

Despite the defeats, and the blood drawn from it, and the persecutions, life pulsed in our Fatherland — underground, but intense. Italians who were organising, who were conspiring, or who betrayed the feverish impatience to do so, I found in abundance in every centre. The logical conclusion drawn from the dreadful situation was that one must begin again from the beginning, do better, and conquer at any cost.
