FROM that moment I harboured no further doubt as to the fate that awaited me, and if I resolved to struggle to the end, it was solely, as one says, for honour's sake; that I might be able to cry out, upon reaching the bottom of the abyss: "I have done everything humanly possible to avoid being cast into it!"

That very same day, I was compelled to satisfy myself that a masterful will was at work in the contrary direction, reinforcing the means honourably at its disposal with fraud and perjury.

I was confined in a ground-floor cell. A companion was assigned to me — an agent of the secret Police, dressed in plain clothes, who announced that he would spend both day and night in my company. Certainly not to keep my spirits up, I thought, but to snatch some word that might escape me, in the travail of my soul, during sleep.

He appeared to perceive my thought and begged me not to judge by appearances. If he could not do me good, he would at least do me no harm.

Half an hour later, the bolts grated and the heavy door swung on its hinges. Two persons entered: the Examining Magistrate and a man dressed as a municipal street-sweeper, with one empty eye-socket, hideously scarred. Two guards remained standing at the threshold.

"Well, then! Kimf —" said the Magistrate after a brief silence.

"It is he!"

"Are you quite certain?"

"As certain as I am that I am baptised."

"Repeat your account."

"I was on duty at the Opéra, spreading sand at the entrance reserved for His Majesty. It would have been seven o'clock, or a quarter past, at the most. I saw two gentlemen chatting in low voices, gesticulating. They had the air of persons studying the locality. I warned them that this was no place for the public and asked them to move along. They told me to mind my own business. The gentleman who is here said: 'Hold your tongue, you scoundrel!' I made as if to strike him, and the two of them cleared off."

"When did this take place?"

"Yesterday evening, the fourteenth of January."

"An hour, or a little less, before the attempt upon His Majesty the Emperor?"

"An hour, or a little more."

I had understood, perfectly understood! They wished to establish, in a manner that should appear incontrovertible, our presence at the scene of the attempt. For Orsini there was his wound; for Gomez the arrest effected in front of the Opéra. To fry me and Pieri, they had put into the mouth of that wretched creature a tale invented out of whole cloth.

Yet I felt none of that anger which impels a man to fury, and was content to ask my strange accuser how much they had promised him in payment for his lies. He turned away the single greenish eye that remained to him and made no answer.

Late in the evening, a second agent was locked into my cell. Scarcely entered, he greeted me and, offering his hand, said that he likewise would leave me neither by day nor by night. They were shut in with me — so I could only surmise — solely to prevent any communication between myself and the accomplices they suspected I had outside. He hoped that all three of us would endeavour, as good comrades, not to embitter our imprisonment with needless friction.

Several days of relative quiet passed. No contact with the outer world, of which but little air penetrated my cell, and still less sunlight, while the warder appeared only at rare intervals. He had orders to speak neither with me nor with my guards, who thus became the only human beings with whom I was permitted to exchange my thoughts. I knew nothing save that my companions were, like myself, confined at Mazas: subject to the same snares, sliding inexorably toward the selfsame fate. In such moments I would sit apart on the plank bed, motionless, eyes fixed upon the ground. And here I owe my guards a word of thanks — somewhat belated, it is true — who, so as not to disturb my meditations, moved about on tiptoe.

I attempted to occupy myself, to put myself, as it were, in the place of the Authorities and thereafter to prepare my defence. But my thoughts defied all such endeavour; the subtle conjectures and the intricate reconstructions ended always in the same thing: nervous irritation, exhaustion. Then nothing remained but to have recourse to the great remedy of youth — I slept.

The first clear proof of the inextricable net that was closing about me came when, conducted under close escort before the Clerk of the Court, I saw placed before me first a certified copy, and then the original itself, of a letter written by me from London to Felice Orsini. In it I informed him how a certain Carlotti, a native of Brescia, had, to my great surprise, spoken to me of our conspiracy. I had good reason to suspect this man of being a spy in the service of the French Government. He must be left alone. If he already knew too much... either one must rid oneself of him by whatever means, or renounce the enterprise altogether.

Felice Orsini, a warmer patriot than a prudent conspirator, had preserved this most compromising document among his papers, and the English Police, at the instigation of Paris, had carried out a search of his premises and taken possession of it.

My intimate connexion with the conspirators, and my knowledge of their designs, appeared only too firmly established. Another long stride down the fatal slope! And when I attempted, *pro forma*, to deny the undeniable, the Examining Magistrate answered only with a mocking smile and dismissed me back to my darkness, casting after me, by way of *viaticum*, the shattering phrase: "Oh, we have far other proofs! And your accomplices have fully corroborated them!"

The following day I was transferred from Mazas, in a closed carriage, to the Conciergerie. I was assigned cell No. 1, and soon learnt that Pieri was confined in No. 2, Gomez in No. 3, and Orsini in No. 4.

My new place of sojourn measured, roughly, ten feet by fifteen. Light entered timidly through a small window set with heavy iron bars, situated near the ceiling. In the door, at the height of a man, was a circular aperture — a five-franc piece would have passed through it only with difficulty. It permitted the warders to observe my every movement; it permitted me to observe, up to a certain point, whatever passed in the corridor. On the walls, the hands of the prisoners who had preceded me had undoubtedly carved verses and maxims, afterwards scraped away without mercy by the warders' knives.

What fragments of hearts and what reflections of souls refined by suffering, stolen from my meditation by the narrow observance of duty! The history of one of those souls I knew, in every particular, from my childhood. It was indeed from my cell that Marshal Ney had gone forth, to have his patriot breast shattered by the Bourbons' lead!

At the Conciergerie too I had two guards. Of them I must say something. The more congenial was a former non-commissioned officer of the navy. The State, having wrung from him the best years of his life, had rescued him from destitution by entrusting him with tasks of dubious stamp that evidently went against the grain of his nature. A large family, poor and without connections, kept him bound to his lot; but his resignation was only apparent, and I was not long in perceiving that his secret rancour, finding no outlet, was accumulating drop by drop in his heart. Nature had denied him the resolution and the impulse that drive men to virile acts; unless a foolish discipline, too long endured, had cut away his every force of will. Yet he still admired in others the virtues that were not his own, and his instincts revealed themselves, in the substratum — and perhaps without his own knowledge — as rebellious. The years have effaced his surname from my memory. His Christian name was, if I am not mistaken, one very common among the Parisian people — a mythological name: Saturn!

The other guard was a former soldier of the marine infantry — a rather stout man of few words, who would sit for hours on end with his hands upon his stomach, fingers interlaced, placidly revolving his two thumbs one about the other, now in one direction, now in the reverse. He was fond of dozing.

At the Conciergerie, I received my first intimation of what was passing in the world. The two guards maintained, in this regard, a sealed silence. But other persons attached to the service of the prison supplied my thirst by a most simple expedient: planting themselves before the cell and conversing in loud voices one with the other.

By this means I came to learn that public opinion, which had at first shown itself hostile to us, had rapidly changed in our favour. The substance of entire newspaper articles threaded its way through the narrow circular aperture to my ear. Among them, those translated from the English press seemed to me especially explicit, and almost laudatory.

But why did the staff of the Conciergerie go to such trouble? I believed I divined the reason on the day I heard a warder affirm that "in this affair (our affair) there were mysterious and powerful influences at work." He had added: "It is not difficult to foresee that none of the four prisoners will be executed." I deduced from this that he who had spoken thus was not altogether unconnected with the direct inspiration of the "mysterious influences" to which he had permitted himself to allude.

And now we are at the precipice! From the far end of the corridor a voice cries: "Prisoner of cell No. 4!" It was Felice Orsini's cell. I press my eye to the circular aperture. Let it be seen, then, how capable of gnawing away a man's existence — even an undaunted one — is the anguish of a great enterprise failed and the consciousness of one's own responsibility! Felice Orsini passed swiftly across the narrow arc of my vision; but even that sufficed for me to discern, upon that sculptured head, the traces of the tempest that must have raged within. It was almost entirely white!

Half an hour of sorrowful reflection passes, and I hear Orsini's step returning. Immediately, from the far end of the corridor, the selfsame voice: "Prisoner of cell No. 1!" I was ushered into a long, narrow room. The single window that lit it stood before me. At a writing-desk beside the window sat the familiar Examining Magistrate, and with him another individual clad in black — the Imperial Prosecutor. But my attention was violently seized by a third figure, seated a little apart from the desk, to the right of the Magistrate. Her profile stood out sharply against the light of the window. I knew that profile — it was that of a small, slender, dark woman, not beautiful in the classical sense, but very attractive; and at the sight of her there stirred within me the recollection of a persistent smile of hers. It was Miss Elisa Cheney, the friend of Felice Orsini. I dissembled the agitation that seized me so intensely as to prevent me from distinguishing the other persons who stood about me, and barely noticed a second woman who, seated at a table near the entrance, leaned her arm upon it and hid her face upon it.

The interrogation proceeded with a rapidity that seemed to me like lightning. The Magistrate addressed himself first to the English young lady, speaking to her in her own tongue.

"Is this the man who brought the letter found upon your mantelpiece, and to whom you delivered a valise belonging to Signor Felice Orsini?"

"It is he."

"Signor Conte, what have you to say?"

Persons of chivalrous disposition, seated at table in a warm room with their feet upon soft carpets, will perhaps not be able to forgive my discourtesy. But it was a question of keeping my head from the guillotine and preserving it for better use. I replied: "How can that woman possibly claim to know me? She will have seen me one night, fleetingly, as she plied her trade along the pavements of Haymarket. The testimony of a woman of equivocal character signifies nothing."

The young lady turned pale, and with a voice broken by anger: "Let the valise be examined. On the leather, at the bottom — in indelible characters..."

She could not continue. And the Magistrate: "Compose yourself, compose yourself, Miss!"

"— in indelible characters, the name of Felice Orsini is written!" At a sign from the Magistrate, the Clerk flashed before my eyes — produced from I know not where — the tiger-skin valise. Time and dust had blackened its base. But it was at once washed, and the indelible characters appeared. Mere participation in a conspiracy that had led to the shedding of blood was enough — those characters were my death sentence!

But I did not admit defeat, and attempted one last desperate move. I had understood clearly, there and then, that Miss Cheney was acting neither from venality nor from hatred. The spirit that animated her could only be love. The Police, master of the art of turning passions to account, I thought, must have promised her the salvation of her adored one in exchange for a complete disclosure. I learnt later that I had intuited the truth. To the detective Sanders, who was urging her to reveal all she knew, she had replied: "Everything, everything, to save that man!" And she had allowed herself to be brought to Paris.

Be that as it may, impelled by this thought, I raised my voice as I had never done before my judge, and turning to my accuser: "You are attempting to save Felice Orsini, and to succeed in this, you are betraying the innocent!"

What followed I had not expected. My voice, ringing through the room, had penetrated a heart, had shaken a creature, had lifted a head bowed upon a hand in painful languor.

Two trembling arms clasped me, and my muscles were forced to make an effort to support the weight of a being who had given herself over entirely to me. And I, who still did not understand, felt as if a wave of rose and blue were flooding the depths of me and stilling my tempest; while a cloud of hair, golden and light, caressed my face and enclosed it, as though to hide it from profane eyes. A resounding kiss, long —

For a moment, the Magistrate, the Imperial Prosecutor, the room, the conspiracy, France, the entire world ceased to exist. I was in the arms of my bride!

They separated us. The Magistrate was the first to break the silence. How shrill his voice seemed to me! He said: "Is the Conte di Rudio your husband?" And she, with honest pride: "I should not be here, were he not!"

Then they allowed us ten minutes for a private meeting. But when we drew apart, Sanders, the head of Scotland Yard's detectives, who had accompanied my poor Elisa to Paris, followed and planted himself at our heels. I foiled him by beginning our conversation in Italian.

"The child?"

"Adorable! Mama is taking care of him. Carlo, Carlo, what can I do for you?"

And Sanders, curtly: "Speak in English!"

"Go to the devil!"

He lodged a protest with the Magistrate, who, perhaps thinking that speaking the language of Shakespeare might prove difficult for me, gave orders that a Corsican interpreter should attend our conversation. Sanders withdrew. And I then resumed my discourse, but in English.

"Elisa, this is no time for illusions. All is lost and I shall be brought to the scaffold. What can you do? Nothing but bear your fate with fortitude. Unless —"

Here, loud protests from the Corsican, who insisted that we speak Italian. And the Magistrate ordered that both interpreters remain by our side. But before Sanders had returned, I had already expressed my thought — "unless you succeed in engaging on behalf of my cause, your own, some English personage of great influence. Then, perhaps —"

At this point, the arrival of the second intruder exasperated me, and my powerful emotions lost all restraint. Which of them drove me most strongly? Whether it was the horror of prolonged dissimulation and the need to vindicate, in that supreme moment, my own part; or whether it was the presence of Elisa that rekindled my pride — the fact remains that, raising my voice once more, I flung these words into the astonished face of the Magistrate: "Yes, I too attempted to destroy the murderer of my Country and of the French People. One regret alone: that I did not know how to strike home. The second bomb was hurled by me!"

The Magistrate, pale and grave, made as if to speak. But I forestalled him: "Do not trouble me with useless questions. What I have said is sufficient for your Justice!"

I saw him cut the air with the gesture that dismissed me back to prison, and I turned to my bride to embrace her once more. Alas! The rose colour had vanished; the blue eyes had lost their vivacity. Elisa, who had fainted, had fallen back in her chair, arms hanging limp, head thrown back against the rest. My kiss — the last, perhaps — I laid upon lips made pale and senseless, yet still trembling.

Then I ran out, so swiftly that the guards, hurrying after me, seemed almost to be giving chase.
