# Chapter the Thirtieth

In the very year of my arrival in London, the French Police made, so far as I was concerned, its last attempt at corruption. My finances were then at their lowest ebb. The five pounds from Allsop had evaporated; the modest savings accumulated during my lecture tour had likewise vanished. Elisa, on the point of becoming a mother for the second time, required considerable care. Fortunately, the assistance I no longer sought, out of pride, from my fellow countrymen, I received from Le Houx, the former French convict, who, having found employment at a marble saw-mill, had induced his employer to take me on for polishing slabs. My inexperience, however, kept the wages thin.

The step taken by the French Police at such a moment was not without its tactical shrewdness. The Inspector who entered my humble dwelling launched his attack without any preamble, with his cards on the table, with a blunt frankness worthy of a better cause. He dwelt upon my isolation, upon the ingratitude of those at whose "instigation" I had faced the scaffold, upon the indigence betrayed by my lodgings and my appearance. Was it worth sacrificing myself for people who, in the best of circumstances, cared nothing for my sacrifices? He understood perfectly well that I might harbour ill feelings toward the Government of Napoleon III; but that Government harboured none whatsoever toward me. One single word, and all my misery would be at an end, and I would be able to restore my wife to the comfortable circumstances she deserved, to which she was rightfully entitled.

And he gave greater weight to his words by drawing from his pocket a fistful of sovereigns, which would — he said — be periodically renewed.

But cunning had failed to reckon with a subtle and sovereign coefficient: the honest conscience that transfigures the sufferings endured for a worthy cause and transforms them into profound satisfactions; that transfigures the satisfactions born of wicked deeds and transforms them into unutterable torments.

My Elisa? She would without question have scorned the comfort offered at the price of my honour. What was a handful of gold, set against her love? Happy — thrice happy — the man who, in difficult moments, can see himself reflected in the serene eyes of a woman worthy of the name! He passes over the social mire as the Christ of legend walked upon the waters, with feet undefiled and dry.

When the importunate pressure of the Police had ceased, there came the more nauseating pressure of the priest. One day, while I was busy at my writing-table, I found one of them at my feet — affable, insinuating, a master at concealing the state of his soul beneath a smile... that smile irritated me from the very first moment I saw it. And so, cutting short his opening phrases, in which he expressed his pleasure at making my personal acquaintance, I asked him, curtly, in what way I might be of service to him.

— My parishioners are all, in a manner, my children. I have left the ninety-nine sheep safe upon the mountain...

A first interruption on my part, to establish a necessary premise. Of all the four-legged creatures to which, with a figure of speech not altogether of the most genteel variety, he might have compared me, the sheep was the one I found most detestable.

— "For goodness' sake," he said, "let us not quarrel over mere words. The reasons that brought me here are grounded in a sense of duty, and in the name of duty I may well ask for a few minutes' attention, even at the risk of proving tiresome."

At a gesture from me, he sat down and, protesting that he wished to come at once to the heart of the matter, reminded me of my quality as an Italian, which he held to be inseparable from that of a Catholic. Not from any eagerness to meddle in the affairs of others, but from the conscientious discharge of his own ministry, he had come to ask my permission to baptise my child, sustained by the hope that I would not refuse to render that dutiful homage to the religion of my father.

— My father has no faith in priests... that key, therefore, cannot lock or unlock my heart. But even if it could... if children were under an obligation to practise the faith of their ancestors, we should still be casting human victims, here in the full light of the nineteenth century, into the flaming belly of Moloch; we should still be worshipping onions.

With that reply I had furnished the opening for a discussion which it had been my intention to avoid. The priest seized the opportunity on the wing and, still smiling, observed that the ancient forebears had believed in error and practised falsehood. It was therefore incumbent upon their children to seek out the truth, even if by groping in the dark. But from the moment that truth had been revealed to us by Him who is the very essence of the true, why depart from it, why obstinately close one's eyes to the light? And if even, out of a misguided pride, I wished to go headlong into evil, by virtue of what right did I deny redemption to an innocent creature?

And I replied: — If my son is innocent, why would your God hold him responsible for sins not his own?

— These are mysteries of our holy religion; nor is it given to the human mind to fathom them...

— Not even to the men chosen by "holy" vocation and anointed of the Lord?

— That would be to presume too much upon poor clay!

— From which I must infer that you make yourself the herald and teacher of things you do not know; which, by your own confession, you will never be in a position to know. What would you say of me if I claimed to teach Greek, when I am ignorant even of its alphabet?

— Faith, Count, faith!...

— If it is a virtue to believe the absurd, I fail to see what difference there could be between a bishop and the savage who worships the aerolite fallen from the sky.

And here, by my own foolishness, I allowed myself to be drawn into expounding for him the essence of the Brahminic legends, the fertile matrix from which so many other religious legends sprang, the Christian included. Indeed, pressing further back into the succession of centuries, I demonstrated — an easy enough task! — that all the religions which cloud the human intellect derive, in the last analysis, from the astronomical knowledge possessed by the ancients, and that upon the *scienza del Sole* — the science of the Sun — every sacred allegory, every pretended sacred mystery, is precisely founded. He was coming to me, therefore, unconsciously perhaps but unquestionably, to propose that I bend the knee, bow the soul, before the majesty of Apollo, of Bacchus, of Mithras... of the Sun!

He let me speak, endeavouring to assume, here and there, the expression of a learned man who listens, out of benevolent condescension, to the fables woven by the exuberant and wayward fancy of a child. But at that final phrase, his thin lips took on the hardness of sarcasm, and seemed to stretch toward the corners of his ears.

Was that sarcasm sincere? Perhaps!... In the Church, as in an army, the General Staff knows the secret plans; but the multitude of subordinates operates in the dark.

He said: — Count, permit me to employ, in defence of the truth — which has, moreover, no need of defence — the selfsame frankness you employ in defending heresy. Human fancy has indulged in far wider flights than that particular one of yours. But in the field of positive facts, the Holy Catholic, Apostolic and Roman Church has nineteen centuries of good works to her credit, which neither you nor any other unbridled spirit would know how to destroy.

And I replied in kind: — The good existed in theory and in practice even when humanity had not so much as dreamt that, upon a small and picturesque peninsula of the savage West, a Rome would arise, and in Rome, after a long succession of intrigues and outrages, a Catholic coalition of superstitions and cupidity, which would take morality as its sign, or rather, as its picklock. And I maintain that good will continue to be practised even when of the Rome of the Popes there remains, as a pale memory, nothing but the name.

Individually, and despite the habit he wore, even a priest might practise virtue, since goodness lies as a seed within human nature and the day would come when, once the obstacles were broken, it would at last triumph upon the Earth. But the evil which the Catholic Church, regarded in its exclusive essence, had wrought and continued to wreak — that, now, could not be eclipsed with a handful of phrases! Did he truly wish me to recall it, by evoking nineteen centuries of obscurantism, of deception, of tyranny, of torture, of the stake, of tears and of blood?

He lowered his eyes and appeared to reflect. Then came the stroke of the venomous tail. — Let us leave aside for a moment, he said, opinions which, after all, can do little or nothing against the fatal march of events... Could one not, with a little goodwill, find a common ground upon which two men of honour might reach an understanding, notwithstanding their difference of opinion?

He lowered his eyes again and, in a subdued voice: — Allow me, not as a priest, but as a man of feeling who might, by reason of age, be your father... If you were to authorise me to baptise your child, I would undertake to procure for you — upon my word of honour! — a lucrative post, such as would restore you to a decent standing in society. Around me I perceive signs of poverty only too manifest. Forgive me!... Between serious men, between upright consciences, who yet know how to weigh the demands of life... why sacrifice reality to mere abstractions? Does it not seem to you that the greatest of our duties is precisely that of providing decently for those we love? And what greater satisfaction for a father and family man?

It was the noose of necessity flung about my neck! Whether General Staff or common subordinate, the indelible priestly character never belies itself.

I rose sharply to my feet and, in a voice full of anger: — The greatest of my duties is to leave my son a legacy of manly example. In any case, I am not a priest, and I do not traffic in my convictions! And I showed him the door.

That very same year, after an existence of but a few weeks, my second-born child ceased to live. It was Christmas Eve... the anniversary of the day on which I had set sail to voyage at full canvas toward the fellowship of free men... and I had not so much in the house as would allow me to provide for his burial. I gazed with dry eyes upon his small white face, pale as the pillow on which it rested, and, striving to dry Elisa's tears, I said:

— Sooner or later, once the obstacles are broken, the good will triumph upon the Earth. And the credit for it will belong, in great part, to those who, knowing the truth, are not afraid to live it. But now... Peace, peace! An uncompromised conscience expects no reward from men. And perhaps this utter destitution... is the only true glory that is ours.
