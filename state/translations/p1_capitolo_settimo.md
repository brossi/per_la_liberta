CHAPTER SEVEN

I DO NOT intend to narrate the detailed history of a siege that extended beyond all expectation. With his original plan, General Oudinot aimed to take possession of the Gianicolo by direct assault. However, the fierce, unexpected resistance offered by the Casa Giacometti and above all that of the Vascello — which was reduced to a heap of ruins, yet which French arms never succeeded in conquering — had compelled Oudinot to abandon his first design. He turned the difficulty by extending his trenches toward the south and preparing a flanking attack. This prolonged the agony of the Republic by a month.

During that month I had, between one exchange of rifle-fire and the next, more than one occasion to listen to the inspired words of Ugo Bassi and to engage in vibrant conversation with Ciceruacchio. Poor martyrs of a cause more than holy, they return at times to quicken my solitude, and in those moments I hear their voices still and dare not let fall an eyelid, lest reality reassert itself and the vision fade. Precisely as a son who, having had the bier that contains his father's remains unsealed after many long years, and finding the body intact, fears that a mere breath might scatter those dear features and reduce them to a shapeless handful of ash.

Among my memories of that time I count also the sad and ineffable smile of Anita Garibaldi who, on the verge of bringing into the world the child of the hero, yet found the strength to share our labors and to gather into her frail breast the sorrows of a country that was not even her own.

And I had occasion to exchange a few words with the Princess Cristina Belgioioso, of whom I shall speak briefly. That accomplished gentlewoman, who by her very nature — elect yet morbidly sensitive — was profoundly seized by the passions of the age, had, after Charles Albert's abandonment of Milan, lost for a time all her faith in the Savoyard Monarchy and flung herself, with an impetuosity all her own, into the republican ranks. She had hurried to the defense of Rome and revealed a resourceful intelligence in the organization of the hospitals, and an unflinching abnegation in the care of the wounded. And there her hand was sanctified by the touch of a great dying man: Goffredo Mameli.

I shall add, incidentally, that after the fall of Rome the Belgioioso undertook a journey to the Orient. But the slaughter of so many heroes could not but arouse her noble indignation against the man whose treachery had provoked it. She drew up and published, in language lofty yet burning, her act of accusation, in which allusion was made — all too plainly — to the dealings that had passed between the liberals and the prisoner of Ham; dealings which she promised to document.

Before she could, or resolved to do so, she was struck repeatedly by the knife of a Bergamask, her own servant. And there were those who discerned, in that attempted assassination, the influence of Louis Napoleon. It is true that the servant himself harbored a strong resentment toward the Princess, who had dismissed him; yet it was observed that the agent of a powerful man would certainly not have exposed himself to personally committing so execrable a crime. To inflame the servant's hatred and drive him to strike was an undertaking at once less compromising and more sure.

The Bergamask, already imprisoned, contrived to escape punishment by flight, and of him nothing more was ever heard — a thing most difficult, not to say impossible, without the assistance of powerful allies. Nor should it be left unmentioned that certain publications of the Princess's became, in a mysterious fashion, unobtainable.

I hasten toward the end. The French take possession, in the dead of night and almost by surprise, of the pontifical walls... our defense is reduced to the inner line of the old Aurelian walls, where stands that Villa Spada which will shortly witness Luciano Manara fall gasping. The Genoese Medici, half buried among the ruins of the Vascello, triumphs still over the numbers and the arrogance of the foreigner; Giuseppe Garibaldi has transferred his headquarters to the upper Villa Savonarelli, not far from the Porta San Pancrazio. The villa crumbles under the blows of the artillery posted at Villa Corsini; yet from it he can dominate the field of battle... The night of San Pietro arrived.

Notwithstanding the hardships and the fate that now bore down with terrible imminence, Michelangelo's dome was illuminated like day. The Florentines, besieged by the army of Charles V, had three centuries before illuminated that of Santa Maria del Fiore... History was binding the new heroism to the old. Toward midnight, lightning flashes, thunder rolls, hail crashes down, water runs in torrents through the streets, and no sooner has the fury of the elements spent some of its force than the French fury pours upon the city.

“Courage — it is the final trial!” We rush to the defense, drenched to the bone, firing when the lightning flashes. Darkness, confusion, curses, death-rattles... Amid that chaos,

Amid that chaos, I stumble and fall headlong.

Before I could rise again entirely, I was enclosed in a ring bristling with bayonets — and it was well for me that the order to give no quarter proved insufficient to extinguish French generosity.

Taken prisoner, I am marched back, from trench to trench, as far as San Paolo. I am in a courtyard among a crowd of fellow soldiers... some lie stretched out staring wide-eyed; some pace about like beasts behind bars... here and there bandaged arms, faces streaming with blood.

Thunder still grumbles in the distance; a few paces away, musket-fire and cannon; the air saturated with electricity and with rage... my nerves transmit the impulse to brain and heart. It was an instant!... in the wide-open doorway the sentry stood black against the dark. He fell beneath my dagger like an empty sack, without a groan, and I flee across the open country, lie flat, crawl, leap; I cross the trenches again one by one, undetected. From the last I catch sight of the breach and am already flattering myself that I may once more hurl myself into the thick of the fray, when a lightning flash reveals my red shirt. I am seized again, brought back to San Paolo, and there I find the Judas who denounces me as the killer of the sentry. By daylight, I had been brought before a court-martial.

I have many times reproached myself, and still reproach myself, for having retained in memory so many incidents of my life that are of no importance whatsoever, and for having forgotten the name of the French officer who came to my aid in so dire a strait.

He spoke for over half an hour. By what quirk of procedure I was removed from the room, I do not know. Yet from the adjoining chamber I heard the most impassioned passages of the defense.

He maintained, in substance, that since I had not yet given my parole, I must be regarded as a lawful combatant, with the full right and duty to kill. And pressing his argument, he said to my judges:

"Does not the honored uniform that covers you impose upon you the obligation of silencing every resentment and of receiving into your breast, as into a temple, the sentiments of humanity and justice? The prisoner has shed blood. But what thought, other than cowardice, could have restrained his arm? Did not cannon thunder on every side? Was he not fighting for a cause that even his enemies must acknowledge to be lofty and legitimate? Was he not staking his life in the defense of the Fatherland and of Liberty?"

And he urged them to hypothetically reverse the roles: "Suppose Paris besieged by Roman arms; suppose that the beardless youth who awaits your verdict were of French blood and had slain, in the very same circumstances, an enemy sentry in order to rush to the last defense of our beloved France... which of you would not admire his ardor and his daring? Which of you would not feel proud to call him a fellow-citizen? Which of you would not have wished to follow his example? Which of you would refuse to embrace him with brotherly warmth and to honor him as a true hero?"

I was acquitted, and on the 1st of July, together with some three hundred prisoners of every uniform, I was dispatched to Civitavecchia. Having come to about twenty miles from Rome, I slackened my pace... no one pays me any heed, and I take advantage of the fact to slip behind a thicket, ready and disposed to justify my halt with evidence all too compelling. A few minutes later I am joined by another Garibaldino, a Venetian fisherman, who on his part is preparing to furnish the same proof... Soldiers and prisoners continue their march, they are distant... they disappear. Here we are free once more, and we congratulate each other in turn, clasping hands with great warmth.

We clamber over hedges, cross barren fields and pastures; we take shelter for the rest of the day in the hut of a shepherd who refreshes us with black bread and cheese. Well into the night, we set off again toward Rome. But we found her occupied by the French. Garibaldi had departed the previous night, from the Porta San Giovanni, with the nucleus of his men, to hasten to the defense of Venice... the last strip of Italy where the People, starving and bleeding, still stood upright and close-ranked about the banner of emancipation!

The self-styled procurator of the good Lord, who had left the Eternal City disguised as the lackey of a countess, was already making ready to re-enter it from Gaeta with theatrical pomp. The traces of the titanic struggle effaced, he would find "his" Rome resplendent in a magnificence wrought by the centuries... so that, the twin throne shored up once more, he would there conceal his own ignominy with consummate splendor.

And there were those who, on the eve of the catastrophe, proposed leaving not one stone upon another of all those marvels. With perhaps less practical intent, twenty years later the Paris Commune was seized by a similar fever of desperation. But Giuseppe Mazzini held sacred the manifestations of genius, and his authority prevailed.

To the Master's calm eloquence I had attempted, in my youthful presumption, to oppose the impetuous torrent of very different sentiments. "Genius is, in Italy, scattered broadcast along every road, and requires nothing to flower but the propitious occasion. That occasion would be offered by Liberty! The very hands roughened in the pulling down of temples would have raised them up again as splendidly, dedicating them no longer to falsehood, but to Truth. The multitude of the bigots, incapable of comprehending the arduous abstractions of philosophy, needed to be educated by the school of deeds. Let the idols they regard as possessed of supernatural powers be shattered in the public square. When, contrary to their expectation, they see the Sun continue its course from East to West; when they must persuade themselves that no legion of angels descends from heaven to arrest the arm of the supposed sacrilegists, they will likewise be persuaded that the demolishing hammer has broken nothing but wood and marble. Let the hydra return and find the hideous ruin that befits it. Catholicism, having survived so much light of science, is now drained of every regenerating idea, and to sustain itself requires outward magnificence. The eye of the common people halts at the surface, and its grandiose semblance suffices to make it venerable in their sight. Strip away the gilding... expose the woodworm... reveal the emptiness! Rome must belong to the Italians, or vanish from the stage of the world. Her ruins would attest to the peoples the desperation of a people that truly wills; they would be a monument more glorious than any basilica."

A train of thought that might appear inspired by barbaric impulse; but which I then believed was not wanting in grandeur.

I attempted to reach Garibaldi on the road to Venice; but the stratagems that eluded the three armies pursuing him led me astray as well, and near Tivoli I came within a hair's breadth of falling into the fraternal arms of the Spanish soldiers. I too ended by taking refuge in San Marino, where, at brief intervals, with uncertain and gloomy outlines, there reached me the horrible stories of the scattered legionaries — dispersed, shot like wild wolves... Ugo Bassi shot, Ciceruacchio shot, and his twelve-year-old son who clung to his arms. Such was, in my time, the penalty exacted for the crime of loving one's Fatherland. And amid so much horror there passed, sad and tender, the last moan of Anita.

It was at San Marino that I learned of the fall of the Lion of Saint Mark. With the surrender of Venice, the first part of the great Italian epic came to its close. The bloodied arms were laid away in their hiding-places; anger was stifled in every breast. The picturesque peninsula continued to be the garden of Europe, and to warm themselves in its sun the pale races continued to descend from the North. But around our souls there gathered a frozen sadness that found a sympathetic echo among the honest, the wise, and the strong, wherever the work of civilization burned bright — kindled, across the wide earth, by the great and generous Latin mother!
