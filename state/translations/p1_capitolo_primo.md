CHAPTER ONE

LOS ANGELES, a splendid summer morning. I had arrived there but a few hours since, on a train that had put me in mind of those which, from every part of France, converge upon the shrine at Lourdes.

For the metropolis of Southern California, situated near the ocean, between the desert and the orange groves, is a great centre of attraction for all those who have the misfortune to live beyond the Rockies: where, in summer, the sun prostrates men and herds alike, like withered grass; where the frost of the long winter makes the earth rigid and impenetrable even to the blows of the pickaxe; where cyclones sweep through the immense valleys, uprooting trees and shattering houses.

The wretched souls in whose hollow chests consumption gnaws find in the "City of Angels" a sky of deep blue and a balsamic air that give them illusion and hope.

How many of them I had encountered in the streets, in those few hours! They pass quietly, light as beings come from the realm of shadows, and they smile at children and smile at flowers. In their optimism, which here becomes extraordinarily tenacious, they accept as indisputable truth the merciful falsehood that their loved ones murmur in their ear at each fit of coughing.

They go and sit in the shade of the orange trees in blossom, and sometimes, whilst they cling, lost in reverie, to the sweetnesses of life; whilst the waxen corollas unconsciously exhale above their heads their invisible, most piercing fragrance — the waxen flesh unconsciously exhales its invisible, ultimate breath. The breeze blows in from the sea, and mingling that breath with that perfume, bears them away across the green countryside, away across the arid desert, away into the infinite mystery!

But I had not come to Los Angeles to prolong an agony, and when, after a long walk, I judged the opportune moment to have arrived, I halted before one of those little wooden houses which cannot be conceived of, in California, without associating with them the idea of a flora altogether special, altogether domestic — the flora that embraces and all but possesses the house.

A rubber hose coiled in spirals upon the green grass was driving from its metal tip, into the air, a fan of the finest droplets, which the sun was painting with the colours of the rainbow. A hummingbird, with its reflections of emerald, was attacking with swift and persistent flight a scarlet flower.

Beyond the climbing plants, within the walls of that little house, caught up in the social whirlwind, a man had sought refuge — a man who represented the last, glorious remnants of an epoch. Through the deep peace adorned with greenery, I could feel the suppressed longing of conspiracies, the fever of battles; and in that picturesque corner of the far West, I suddenly rediscovered the emotions of my youth, invoked in vain for so many years. Then I breathed it in, warm, warm, like a breath from the Homeland, and the fibres of my heart exhaled a word of thanks.

But a subtle thought crept in upon me. Three-quarters of a century; an unceasing succession of struggles, of dangers, of sufferings — what could have survived? Was I to regard that asylum as a temple enclosing a living flame, or as the final resting-place of a long pilgrimage, the sorrowful threshold of a sepulchre? Resolutely — Before raising my hand to the electric button, a figure took shape in the half-shadow of the corridor, beyond the glass door.

I found myself face to face with a stout lady, with a slight tendency toward portliness. Her hair was of a fairness that was fading into white and already showed signs of yielding; but the face was still fresh and rosy, and beneath the prominent forehead a pair of blue eyes shone, gentle and assured. The mouth and chin revealed a creature accustomed to confronting obstacles resolutely.

She asked me sternly what I wished.

"Forgive me — Major di Rudio?" And I handed her my visiting card.

Her expression brightened. "Ah! Ah! That name is familiar to us. The Major will be pleased to see you. But he is still abed!"

I thought: "The infirmities of old age — the threshold of the sepulchre!" And I was about to tell her I would return; but she forestalled me. "Come in! Come in, come in! This is precisely the hour at which the Major rises; he is methodical — he cannot be long. Truly? From San Francisco? Well then, a cup of coffee can do no harm. Quite so! I am the Major's wife!"

— Signora Elisa!

And I extended my right hand with the familiarity of one who, though never having laid eyes upon a person, holds them dear because they have long occupied one's thoughts. Then a whim took me, and as I followed her along the corridor I asked her why my name was familiar to her.

"What shall I say? The Major reads whatever you write, and grows animated and bangs his fist on the table. Please sit down. The water is boiling and in four seconds —"

She left me alone. I was in the dining room. The leafy branches trembling before the single wide window tempered its light. A green tint pervaded the room, and the china on the sideboard reflected it; on the table there glowed, in a vessel of blue glass, a bunch of amaryllis.

The dining room gave onto a study, from which it was distinguished rather than divided by a simple cornice. Through the opening, wide as almost the entire wall, I could make out in the background another broad, solitary window, likewise encumbered by an arabesque of trembling branches. In the air, the same green tint, restful and tranquil. I gradually came to discern a number of plain and massive pieces of furniture in the Mission style. I thought that among that artful antiquity I might have found a place for myself as well; the more so as a bookcase crammed with volumes had instantly aroused in me the old instinct of the library mouse. I found myself with my hands behind my back before those volumes, intent on running over the gilt lettering impressed upon their covers of brown leather. History, philosophy — works stamped with the spirit of freedom of thought.

I looked about me. From one wall there smiled upon me the pensive countenance of a man known to the People and to its tyrants in every part of the civilised world: Giuseppe Mazzini. From the adjoining wall hung a picture bearing the likeness of a martyr less universally known, but of no less great a spirit: Fortunato Calvi. And I exclaimed: The house reveals the man!

"Beg your pardon?" — It was Signora Elisa, just then returned. She had thought I was speaking to her. She had spread a napkin, and in setting the place another lady was lending her a hand — a lady who struck me by her extreme pallor, her very dark eyes, and her slender, slight figure.

"My daughter — Italia Adair," said the Major's wife, and the young woman confined herself to a nod.

— Your only daughter?

— Not at all! We have two others: Roma Scott and America Vickery. I am expecting them today, and you shall meet them. For I hope you will be one of our company today. I have a son as well, the firstborn, born in England and set upon his first steps — you are not unaware of this, I trust — in tragic moments. But he is far away, in Arizona. A mania for the unknown has seized him somewhat, and he spends his life in the saddle, in solitude, exploring.

The refreshment was ready. The gracious lady had added to the coffee some toast and a dish of fresh figs. I protested. I was not accustomed to touching food before midday, and such kindness was superfluous. And she insisted. The eggs had been taken that very morning from the henhouse — she kept many hens; her particular care. As for the figs, Italia had plucked them with her own hands, at that very moment. They were ripe and delicious.

She begged my pardon and went off about her affairs. I remained alone with the other lady. I was sipping my coffee and felt somewhat ill at ease. That air of an invalid — perhaps my conversation would fall upon her inopportunely. I made use of the brief moments when she was occupied in arranging some knick-knack to observe her closely, without betraying myself, without committing any rudeness. She had a mass of brilliantly black hair, perhaps too voluminous for her graceful head; fine eyebrows, magnificently arched; an aristocratic profile; an ethereal little figure. But I was not long in noticing a certain energy of movement that contrasted, in a strange fashion, with her appearance of an invalid.

I resolved to bring all my resources as a reporter wizened in the trade into play. She, little by little, grew animated, and from her lips came forth ideas such as I had not looked for.

I learnt that she had been brought to the last extremity by appendicitis, and that the doctors had saved her by resorting to the knife — a few hours' delay would have proved fatal to her. Children? One only! In homage to Uncle Sam, the eternal and glorious Uncle Sam, she had given him the name of Samuel. She wished to guide him toward a military career. At West Point, where she hoped to have him admitted, he would have the advantage of one of the finest educations in the world. She had spent a great part of her youth with her father, at the Regiment. Beautiful, superb, the military life! Discipline, servitude? You jest! Freedom boundless as the prairies over which one ranged, as the desert! Papa belonged to the Seventh Cavalry. "When I think —

"When the dust, sparkling with arms, announced them from afar, on either side there arose cries to shame a horde of Bedouins. What moments, what embraces! On their faces, together with the joy of homecoming, the serene pride of having repressed barbarism, of having snatched our bold pioneers from torture and destruction. On ours, together with the joy of seeing them again, the serene pride of being the daughters and wives of those strong men. Wherever the horses of Uncle Sam advance, they leave behind them the way smoothed for honest toil and civil life!" — she added — "when I think of the life of the Regiment, I feel a thrill of energy stir within me. Not a moment of idleness, not a moment of tedium! True, there was no lack of days of painful apprehension. The regiment was often called upon to drive back into their reservation the Indians who were committing some raid or other. It would remain absent three, four, eight months. But when the dispatch-rider, white with dust, burst into the encampment and announced its return at the top of his voice, joy was in the air and stole into one's whole being. In an instant, life redoubled in intensity. The ladies hastened to communicate the good news to one another; the children doubled their leapings on the grass; the rose-bushes were stripped bare; an endless coming and going of orderlies as we set everything in order, everything afresh. And from that noisy bustle we launched ourselves at full gallop, away as if in a race — ten, fifteen miles — to be the first to salute the phalanx of the returning men.

"You are mistaken, you are mistaken! Few bonds are so cordial as those that bind soldiers and officers together. The roughness is entirely apparent. When a subordinate receives a dressing-down, he looks his superior in the eye and can always read there a phrase very different from the one his lips have uttered. Severity is reserved exclusively for him who shows signs of being a coward. But have you ever heard tell of cowards in Uncle Sam's army?

"Intemperate? I will tell you; there are birds whose natural state is flight. To set foot on the ground and never rise on the wing again is one and the same thing for them, and what is worse, they walk with an awkwardness to move one to pity. The natural state of the Anglo-American race is movement, incessant, intense labour. The exuberance of life that does not take on arduous and glorious enterprises vanishes in the taverns and the gutter. And the American never does things by halves — not even evil ones. But at the arising of a difficulty, at the mere idea of danger, in the hour of trial — what a transformation! Where find a body of men of greater dash, quicker at expedients, more spontaneous in sacrifice, more steely in temper? And what enemy, equal in numbers, has ever known how to withstand them?"

I listened, full of wonder and almost disconcerted. That harmonious voice, rendered the more caressing by a residue of weakness, expressed ideas which I had up to that point believed to be the exclusive province of virile minds, of people born to face the dangers of battle, whole and happy only amid wrath and blood. But if strength bows to grace and sometimes adores it, why should grace not admire strength?

She bent her slender little figure over the table and set her white hand to work arranging the bunch of amaryllis that glowed in the blue vase. Then, in answer to a fresh question of mine:

"No, the Indian raids are by now, save for the rarest of exceptions, a memory of the past. Uncle Sam now keeps arms and spirits burnished by means of exercises and frequent transfer orders. One never leaves a post where one has lived happily without a sense of sadness; but it is a sadness that vanishes, in a short while, amid the wholesome emotions of travel. For the ladies, travel is a festivity and, I venture to say, a renewal. On the last one in which I took part, we covered, on horseback, more than seven hundred miles through a marvellous country. It is a most singular sensation. One's being, having forgotten its own individuality, feels itself transformed and almost a part of the elements. Strength, infinite motion — we are those things. One goes on and on, in an atmosphere of sunshine, and we ran with the wind."

She fell silent, lowered her eyes, then resumed. "No, true life is not in the great cities. The great city makes us small. An elegant prison, visits of convention, a childish prattle of gowns and bonnets. If this is civilisation — I hate it."

I was no longer surprised. The impetuous nature, the need for action in the grandfather had passed to the father, and from the father into that slight lady, so ethereal that, as she walked, she seemed barely to graze the carpet. What would she have become in one of those enclosed environments in which virtue bursts its banks and rushes headlong to victory or to martyrdom?

The mother returned. She had grasped the thread of the conversation and added much of her own to it. She concluded the series of vivid recollections with this melancholy lament: "Happy days, happy days — set, alas. For us they will return no more!"

Among the episodes she recounted was the following: Captain di Rudio (he was a captain then) showed in the execution of orders a meticulous precision. On a certain occasion, so as not to delay the departure by a few seconds, he cut clean away with his sabre the sleeves of a magnificent gown that were protruding from a trunk. For several years Signora Elisa had kept them, as mute accusers of an unheard-of crime.

The good lady, dissembling, ended by leading me out into the courtyard. She walked scattering handfuls of grain to a flock of clamouring poultry, and I followed her step by step, attending now and then to a learned lesson in poultry-keeping. My readers, I imagine, take no interest in hens and cocks that are not roasted with a carving-fork in their flesh. But an incident which I shall relate in due course obliges me to bring those poor feathered bipeds into this opening passage as well — pure-bred birds, moreover.

We arrived at a sheltered corner, in the lee of which a white hen was brooding in her basket. And the lady said: "Today or tomorrow the brood will emerge from the shell. What maternal love beneath that fistful of feathers! Is it not strange that the great affections of which we are so proud should be enclosed and potent even in hearts so very small? I am fond of the animals that have no speech."

At this point she set down the grain vessel and walked quickly away.

Planted upon the landing, standing out against the dark background of the kitchen, framed almost by the doorposts, I descried an old man dressed in a grey suit, a tuft of white hair upon his brow, pointed moustaches, the goatee of other countries and other times. The sun, beating full upon his face, threw his angular features into strong relief, as though carved in bronze. He was beating the floor nervously with his heel, while his deep-set but very lively eyes were scanning beyond the bushes, beyond the plants that cast their shade over the yard.

There exist in California isolated groves of ancient vegetation which botanists call *sequoie*, but the people call simply "big trees." They rise straight, without boughs, like the immense columns of a temple raised, in the night of the ages, by some race of giants. The tallest among the pinnacles of the Lombard cathedral would seem, by comparison, a slender reed. They were already resisting the wind when the Almighty was creating, according to the Scriptures, the Sun and the Stars. The prehistoric man who danced his bestial round, devouring his fellow man, has vanished; the patriarchs who, wandering through the solitudes, traced the first paths for young humanity, have vanished; the Brahmins passed the lit torch of civilisation to the Egyptian — and vanished; the Egyptians transmitted it to the Greeks — and vanished; the Greeks to the Romans — and vanished. What remains of the mighty Roman Empire? Race has pressed upon race; States have hurled States into nothingness, through such long vicissitudes that men have lost even the memory of their own origin — and those trees stand.

It is said that with invisible eyes they behold the short-lived pygmies who flock to their feet from every part of the world and babble out their wonder there. If that is so — what disdain in their austere rigidity!

But the lofty crowns, adorned with an evergreen tuft, quiver in the wind of the Sierra and, perhaps, murmur ancient stories to the sky.

I was thinking of those trees, as Count Carlo di Rudio and the companion of so many of his vicissitudes, standing together upon the landing, heedless of my presence, pressed upon each other's cheeks the morning kiss.
