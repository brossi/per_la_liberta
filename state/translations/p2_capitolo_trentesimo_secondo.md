CHAPTER THIRTY-TWO

SAD fatality that seems like punishment! From the misdeed of Aspromonte to the present day, in half a century of History, the House of Savoy, deserving of credit from various points of view, has no longer known how to lead Italy to victory — victory that yet smiled repeatedly upon the glorious victim of its fratricidal lead.(1)

In 1866 Venetia was wrested from the hateful yoke of Austria; but only by submitting to mysterious defeats, to humiliations that call to mind all too vividly another dark mystery — the one coupled to the name of Novara. As at Novara, so at Custoza and at Lissa, the enemy, sensibly inferior in forces, marvels at his own victory and is, for a moment, scarcely able to credit it. Meanwhile, a handful of volunteers, deliberately left wanting in arms, redeems, on the crags of the Tyrol, the honour of Italy. Once again, those who arrogated to themselves the monopoly of genius and gravity gave proof of their hollow pomposity, their inferiority before the band of "madmen" who obstinately persisted in demanding an Italy whole and glorious. And the peremptory order issued to Garibaldi to evacuate the redeemed lands fertilised with the blood of his men reveals, all too plainly, the royal displeasure at so glaring a contrast.

In 1870, Rome was wrested from the baneful yoke of the Pope; but the Monarchy did nothing more than gather, reluctantly and almost under the compulsion of an overbearing injunction, the fruit of the audacity of Mentana. Of that Mentana which saw the royal army stand by, motionless, while the dauntless men who sacrificed themselves to clear the way to the Capitol for the great King were cut down. Where were, on that day, the indignation and the generous impulse that make the Italians the most genuine, the most sympathetic among the races of old Europe?

Little by little, as the Fatherland gained each portion of her territory, that part of the Nation which had made itself comfortable under the leaden cloak lost a portion of its fine character. Ideality set below the horizon, and in its place there succeeded a bastard pragmatism distilled from all that smacks of the most vulgar. "We must be practical!" cried, until hoarse with crying it, the wiseacres of Italy, who looked on, with hands at their sides, at the fruitful labours of the "hot-headed." And they did not perceive that for a Nation, merely to live is less than nothing, when it knows not how to live gloriously. I say that the men who pass themselves off as sober and positive are precisely those who perpetuate the ills and the shames that afflict us; for where the effort is wanting that strikes and crumbles the sad reality pressing down upon us; where men do not ceaselessly aspire, in thought and in deed, to the realisation of "dreams," progress becomes a fable; while the utopia of today, nourished by sacrifice and blood, inevitably becomes the reality of tomorrow.

Be that as it may, the conduct of the royal army at Mentana must have cast a lurid light in the breast of the ruler of Italy.

The maladies of the mind reveal themselves through actions. To draw up in battle array, as upon the tiers of an amphitheatre, and to contemplate, motionless, the foreigner who, ensconced in the very sanctuary of Italian identity, bayonets the gallant insurgents to drive them out, as one drives out thieves who have entered the domestic sanctuary with a false key; to count the wounds; to hear the death-rattle of the dying; to see the countryside, all red with our blood, covered over with corpses, and to persist in immobility, neither levelling one's rifle nor striking the aggressors — even from behind... In vain do I search through my knowledge of modern and ancient History... I find therein no more complete an eclipse of every patriotic sentiment; no more abject a renunciation of the rights and duties imposed upon virile souls.

And what Sovereign, even one animated by views as clear as the hardest stretch of sky, would not have exclaimed: "Your bodies and your souls are mine!"

From that servile submission to the vain prestige of a crown, the Italian People, taken as a whole, has never recovered. Italy is no longer a geographical expression, no longer the land of carnival; but neither is she that of Brutus and the Gracchi. "His Majesty... His Holiness"... between the two runs not merely the analogy of the rhyme. The one by grace, the other by the will of God; the one sacred and infallible, the other sacred and inviolable; the one inseparable from Truth, the other from Good; to speak ill of either alike a crime! The heart bleeds to think of it...

We have cast aside one set of dogmas only to fall back into another that is its equal; our Fatherland is now the Peninsula of two Pontiffs!

The Monarchy, having thrown the national programme on the scrap-heap, placed boldly, after Mentana, its veto upon every aspiration of the Italians towards the North. Was it bound to Austria by secret pacts even before it thought to bind her by open alliances? I cannot say... but I know that from that time forward the motto "Italy belongs to the Italians" sets it shivering with something akin to hysteria. It dismisses the old generals who still dare to pronounce it; prepares the manacles for the patriots who refuse to resign themselves to treating it as a mere understood thing. It will then attempt, by repudiating the principles that were the foundation of our *Risorgimento*, to revive its own military glory by conquering in distant Africa the land of the "four brigands." But even with soldiers who are the very incarnation of dash and sacrifice, it will succeed in nothing beyond repeating the ignominy of its own defeat.

And yet, personally, the Savoyard princes possess in the highest degree that military courage which descended to them through a long line of ancestors, and at San Martino, where there was "glory for all," even the great King reaped a rich harvest of it!...

Vittorio Emanuele II knew also how to conceal his own weaknesses by assuming, before the astonished gaze of Europe, attitudes worthy of the bronze. Nevertheless, despite his essentially aristocratic nature, he ran, driven by his exuberant vitality, after pleasures bordering on the vulgar. These provoked a smile of complacency — fetid and indulgent — both among the refined of high society and among the multitude who, accustomed to picturing Kings as they are drawn in tragedies, admired them almost as revelations of a sincerely democratic spirit. The times granted him the spectacular rôle of the triumphator, and his robust individuality seemed fashioned expressly to play it. He took upon himself the mission of guiding, unscathed, through the stormy present, the Institutions that had survived from the past. A fateful mission... he kept himself ready to fulfil it; but at the cost of sacrificing the future. The common people admire him, and will admire him, so long as they are educated in the school that teaches prostration before success. The thinkers who analyse already reproach him for the subtly underhanded policy, almost that of an arriviste banker, which cast upon the treasury of our enthusiasms the seeds of dissolution.

My sympathies go by preference to his predecessor. Carlo Alberto is beautified in my eyes by the merit belonging to those who lead the way, and by his serene dignity in adversity. He did not gather the fruit of his own good works; from the works of others he gathered their bitter fruits. All he offered, and all he lost. Epic and resounding, despite their errors, were his battles; yet they become of small account beside those fought silently within his conscience. He betrayed... perhaps; but he was far more betrayed himself. His weaknesses, his waverings, his defections, even his intermittent cruelty, reveal the profound sufferings of a sick and extremely sensitive soul; so that the instinctive aversion towards the head of a system of privilege and reaction yields before the rising of pity for the man. It yields... No, it changes its object and pours itself, the more intensely, upon the nefarious sect which, sheltering behind things accounted sacred, succeeds in defiling natures as elect as his!

"The Hamlet of Italy"... well defined! His figure emerges not from triumphal parades, but from a tragedy of passion, all shadow and sudden flare. Is there, among my countrymen, one who feels himself equal to attempting it!

It has been written that I have no vision of contemporary Italy, free, united, politically and civilly reborn; advancing with a sure step towards the glorious goal that awaits her... loved and envied anew by the whole world.

It has been written that I have, nonetheless, the vision of the Italy of my own times, enslaved, unhappy, and full of grief; that I am still moved and suffer at the remembrance of the ills that afflicted her.

Whoever wrote this shows himself more a poet than an observer, or at the very least grasps but the smallest part of the truth.

I have the vision of an Italy such as was glimpsed by Giuseppe Mazzini; such as we all dreamed of, somewhat, when between her and us lay so much a Calvary of suffering. Contemporary Italy resembles her too little... that is why I am grieved! Bursts of virile energy are not lacking in the classes that have least reason to congratulate themselves upon the independence that has been gained. But their gaze is set upon a height that the times do not permit us to reach, and passes over — almost as if tracing their outlines — the intermediate vindications that would nonetheless be within our grasp. It is mere idle chatter to speak of universal brotherhood when the sorry plant of bondage takes root, by foreign force, in one's own domestic garden.

And I know that, with the exception of the government of Francesco Crispi — marked by an energy not always well applied, but virile — we have had from a long succession of Ministers nothing but manifestations of a neurotic ferocity that betrays fear; or of a myopic and senile cunning that commands no respect whatsoever, that arouses the envy of no one.

And I know that insolent Austria still stretches its iron hand on this side of the Alps. It could descend, in a single day, into the very heart of the valley of the Po, the most fertile region of Italy. And while her Emperor, in order not to retreat, even in appearance, from the position which from the outset he was pleased to assume, refuses to return to Rome the visit paid him by our Sovereign, our Ministers sign away with easy cheerfulness the alliances that guarantee him the peaceful enjoyment of the usurped lands; that repudiate the integrity and the greatness of the Fatherland. What a contrast!... and how the fibre of those who hold the destinies of the young Nation is shown to be enfeebled before that of the old reactionary and hangman Monarch!

I ask myself: Who will realise the aspirations consecrated by the blood of so many martyrs, if one makes love to the very enemy whose hands are at our throats? So far have we fallen: a special word has been coined — *quarantottate!* ["forty-eightisms!"] — to designate, with derision, the desperate heroism of the fathers!

One thought turned towards him who reigns at present, and I am done. For some years now our Monarchy has given signs of undergoing a partial transformation. The acts of royal humaneness aim less at theatrical and ephemeral show, and more at leaving some trace that may endure. Respect for the individual right is more keenly felt within it, and political liberty is extended. Perhaps the young King has been instructed by the tragic end of his father, violently removed for his frequent returns to antiquated and tyrannical methods that ought to have been the sad exclusive property of bygone times. May 1898, for instance, in its means and its effects, if not precisely in its aim, is too strongly reminiscent of the 2nd of December for the painful doubt not to insinuate itself into more than one heart, that it may be its sad and ill-judged imitation.

And yet, while the Monarchy makes concessions to liberalism and shows itself disposed to proceed alongside it, it draws back in secret, without letting it be seen, as if it aimed at maintaining its balance by blunting, through the increased power of the Church, the ever-growing demands of the labouring multitudes.

A Government that seats itself midway between the parties, moderator of all, superior to all!... This may seem wise to the *praticismo* [spirit of pragmatism] that cannot discern the future, or that, discerning it, fears it wrongly. I see in it, rather, the tactic recommended by the little treatises on political philosophy. A "living" Nation is of necessity in perpetual struggle, and the Rulers of a State, monarchical or republican, may temporarily impede or deflect it, but suppress it — never. In the social struggle that presses upon us, equilibrium is a chimera, and progress, now rapidly completing its fateful course, is the inexorable condemnation alike of economic rapine and of dogma. The labouring multitude will triumph and will annihilate, in the fever of its triumph, not only the hostile elements, but also the Institutions that do not range themselves openly, sincerely, beside it.

Let it not be thought that I harbour resentments. Distance in time and in space have worn down the sharp edges, have stifled many passions. Only love survives, intense. It blazes in the truth. Nor shall I go down into the earth with the remorse of having kept silence upon it.

As a republican I could wish that the Monarchy would commit a thousand errors; for, once the measure were full, it would plunge into the pit dug by its own lack of foresight. But as an Italian educated by his elders to place, above his political ideals, the integrity of the Fatherland, I do not hesitate to rekindle the last flame still alive in me, in order to give warning:

He who leans upon the past sets his foot upon ground that the waves are daily undermining and swallowing. Trust in the future and advance boldly to meet it. It is thus that you will succeed in mastering it. The past evolved out of barbarism and is saturated with violence and crimes; but History tends towards the triumph of Democracy, of equality before rights, before duties and before opportunities; towards the triumph of justice. He who does not second the tendencies of History cannot have a kingdom that is long, blessed, and glorious. In foreign policy the traces of the triumph of the violence suffered by the Italian people are too plain and drip with too much blood for your mission not to stand out clear and precise. The Monarchy exists because every patriotic party lowered its own banner before hers, as the vanquished lower them; but in the act of binding a solemn compact that guaranteed, as the counterpart of the sacrifice, an Italy independent from the Alps to the uttermost southern shores. The Monarchy, now on one pretext, now on another, has evaded the full observance of that compact. In the meantime, Austria, despite the notorious alliance, cannot slumber. She renews and multiplies her arms; sharpens her claws and points openly at the flesh in which she is eager to sink them. It is urgent to act humanely within the borders of the Kingdom, so as to acquire over the multitude the ascendancy that comes from respect; the respect that has its root in gratitude and in love; it is urgent to rekindle the drowsy enthusiasm and the virile spirit intolerant of insults — but not with empty commemorative celebrations, nor with the puerile rhetoric of the banquet table; it is urgent, I say, that the Nation be ready to breathe its own soul into the army. Cold and arid discipline is no longer sufficient for armed bodies that, at the first disaster, fall to pieces unless renewed and sustained by a nobler sentiment.

Upon Vittorio Emanuele III falls the exalted task of completing and correcting Vittorio Emanuele II. One wears a crown ill that has come by the ephemeral right of succession, if, in appropriating its privileges and its honours, one makes light of its burdens. All further delay is now superfluous; to temporise any longer would mean usurping, by virtue of an equivocation, the throne of Italy!

The Count di Rudio closed his eyes before our victories in Libya could dazzle them. What judgment he would have pronounced upon them, the present writer is not in a position to determine precisely. But if one reflects that he disdained material advantages when they were not coupled with a moral and regenerating triumph, one might readily wager that they would have wrung from him another of those cries of his that reveal a bleeding heart.

After more than a year of exploits officially depicted to us as worthy of the ancient Romans... a Treaty of Peace that calls to mind the ignominious "cession" of Venetia!

But in setting forth the reasons why they had not known how to achieve the better course, the Counsellors of the Crown showed themselves truly without equal! C. C.
