## Revised Translation — Chapter Thirty-Second

WHAT sad fatality, seeming almost a punishment! From the crime of Aspromonte to the present day, across half a century of History, the House of Savoy, meritorious as it is from various points of view, has no longer known how to lead Italy to victory — that very victory which continued to smile, again and again, upon the glorious victim of its own fratricidal bullets. (1)

In 1866 Venetia was wrested from the hateful yoke of Austria; yet only at the cost of mysterious defeats, of humiliations that recall all too keenly another dark mystery — the one coupled with the name of Novara. As at Novara, so too at Custoza and Lissa, the enemy, sensibly inferior in strength, marvels at his own victory and is barely able to credit it. Meanwhile, a handful of volunteers, deliberately left deficient in arms, redeems the honour of Italy upon the crags of the Tyrol. Once more, those who arrogated to themselves the monopoly of intelligence and seriousness demonstrated their hollow pomposity, their inferiority before the handful of "madmen" who stubbornly insisted upon an Italy whole and glorious. And the peremptory order issued to Garibaldi to evacuate the redeemed lands, made fertile with the blood of his men, reveals all too plainly the royal resentment at so galling a contrast.

In 1870, Rome was wrested from the baneful yoke of the Pope; yet the Monarchy did no more than gather, reluctantly and almost under violent compulsion, the fruit of the audacity of Mentana. That Mentana which saw the royal army stand motionless whilst witnessing the slaughter of the bold ones who were sacrificing themselves to clear the road to the Capitol for the great King. Where were they that day — the righteous wrath and the generous impulse that make Italians the most frank, the most admirable among the races of old Europe?

As the Fatherland recovered, piece by piece, each fragment of her territory, that portion of the Nation which had settled itself within the leaden mantle lost, fragment by fragment, its finest character. Ideality set like a declining sun, and in its place succeeded a bastard *praticismo* — a creed of mere practicality — distilled from all that smells most vulgar. "We must be practical!" cried, until they were hoarse from it, the *barbassori* — the self-appointed pundits — of Italy, who looked on with folded arms at the fruitful endeavours of the *scapigliati*, the free spirits. And they failed to perceive that for a Nation, to live is less than nothing when it does not know how to live gloriously. I say that the men who pass themselves off as serious and positive are precisely those who perpetuate the evils and the shames that afflict us; for where the effort that strikes and crumbles away the grim reality bearing down upon us is lacking; where one does not aspire constantly, in thought and in deed, to the realisation of "dreams" — progress becomes a fable; whilst the utopia of today, nourished by sacrifice and blood, becomes inexorably the reality of tomorrow.

Be that as it may, the conduct of the royal army at Mentana must have caused a lurid light to flash within the breast of the ruler of Italy.

The maladies of the soul manifest themselves through actions. To take up position on a field of battle as though upon the tiers of an amphitheatre and to contemplate, motionless, the foreigner who, ensconced in the very sanctuary of Italian feeling, bayonets the generous insurgents so as to drive them out — as one drives out thieves who have entered the household sanctuary with a false key; to count the wounds; to hear the death-rattle of the dying; to see the countryside, all red with our own blood, covering itself with corpses — and to persist in that immobility, and not to level one's rifle, not to strike the imploring ones, even from behind if need be... In vain do I search my knowledge of History, ancient and modern... I find therein no more complete an eclipse of every patriotic sentiment; no more abject a renunciation of the rights and duties imposed upon virile souls.

And what Sovereign, even one animated by a vision as clear as the purest expanse of sky, would not have cried out: "Your bodies and your spirits are mine... I have conquered!"

From that servile submission to the hollow prestige of a crown, the Italian People, taken as a whole, has never recovered. Italy is no longer a geographical expression, no longer the land of carnival; but neither is she the land of Brutus and the Gracchi. "His Majesty... His Holiness"... between the two there runs not merely the analogy of rhyme. The one by grace, the other by the will of God; the one sacred and infallible, the other sacred and inviolable; the one inseparable from Truth, the other from Goodness; to speak ill of either... a crime! The heart bleeds at the thought of it. We have cast aside one set of dogmas only to stumble into another of equal weight; our Fatherland is now the Peninsula of the two Pontiffs.

The Monarchy, casting the national programme upon the scrap-heap, boldly placed, after Mentana, its veto upon every aspiration of Italians toward the North. Was it bound to Austria by secret pacts even before it thought to bind itself by open alliances? I cannot say... but I know that from that moment the motto "Italy belongs to Italians" brings it out in fits of hysterical trembling. It dismisses the old generals who dare still to pronounce it; it prepares the manacles for the patriots who cannot bring themselves to regard it as a mere unspoken assumption. It will then attempt, repudiating the principles that were the foundation of our Risorgimento, to refurbish its military glory by conquering, in distant Africa, the land of "the four brigands." But even though it has at its disposal soldiers who are the very incarnation of dash and sacrifice, it will succeed in nothing other than renewing the shame of its own defeat.

And yet, personally, the Savoia possess in the highest degree that military courage which descended to them through a long line of ancestors; and at San Martino, where there was "glory for all," even the great King reaped a rich harvest of it.

Vittorio Emanuele II knew also how to conceal his own weaknesses, assuming before the astonished eyes of Europe attitudes worthy of bronze statues. Yet, despite his essentially aristocratic nature, his exuberant vitality drove him toward pleasures verging upon the vulgar — pleasures which provoked a smile of indulgent complaisance both among the refined of high society, idolatrous and lenient toward him, and among the multitude which, accustomed to picturing Kings as they are depicted in tragedies, admired these excesses as almost the revelation of a sincerely democratic spirit. The times granted him the spectacular rôle of the conqueror, and his robust individuality seemed fashioned by art to play it. He assumed the mission of guiding the Institutions inherited from the past safely through the present storm. A fateful mission... he knew how to accomplish it; but at the cost of sacrificing the future. The common people admire him and will admire him so long as they are schooled in the lesson of prostrating themselves before success. The thinkers who analyse have already reproached him for the subtle, almost parvenu-banker's policy which cast upon the treasury of our enthusiasms the seeds of dissolution.

My sympathies go by preference to his predecessor. Carlo Alberto is ennobled in my eyes by the merit that belongs to forerunners, and by his serene dignity in adversity. He did not gather the fruit of his own good works; the bitter fruit of the works of others he gathered instead. Epic and tumultuous, despite their errors, were his battles upon the field; but these become as nothing beside those fought silently within his own conscience. He betrayed... perhaps; but he was far more betrayed. His weaknesses, his hesitations, his defections, his own intermittent cruelty, reveal the deep suffering of a soul sick and exquisitely sensitive; so that the instinctive aversion for the head of a system of privilege and reaction yields before the rising tide of pity for the man. Yields?... No — it changes its object and flows all the more intensely upon the nefarious faction which, sheltered behind things held sacred, stoops to corrupt natures as elect as his.

"The Hamlet of Italy"... aptly defined! His is a figure that succeeds not in triumphal processions but in passionate tragedy, all shadow and sudden light. Is there, among my countrymen, one who feels himself equal to the attempt?

*(Long silence.)*

It has been written that I have no vision of contemporary Italy — free, united, risen again politically and civilly; advancing with a sure step toward the glorious goal that awaits her... loved and envied anew by all the world.

It has been written that I retain, instead, the vision of the Italy of my own times, enslaved, wretched and sorrowful; that I am still moved and still suffer at the recollection of the evils that afflicted her.

Whoever wrote this shows himself more poet than observer, or, at the very least, grasps only the smallest part of the truth.

I have the vision of an Italy such as was glimpsed by Giuseppe Mazzini; such as we all dreamed, more or less, when between her and us there lay so great a Calvary of suffering. Contemporary Italy resembles that vision all too little... that is why I grieve. Outbursts of virile energy are not wanting in those classes that have least cause to congratulate themselves on the independence that has been gained. But their gaze reaches to a height which the times do not permit us, and passes beyond, almost sketching them in outline, the intermediate demands that would yet be within our reach. It is idle to prattle of universal brotherhood when the dismal plant of servitude still takes root, by force of foreign tyranny, in one's own domestic garden.

And I know that, with the sole exception of the government of Francesco Crispi — marked by an energy not always well applied, but virile — we have had, from a long succession of Ministers, nothing but the manifestations of a neurotic ferocity that betrays fear, or of a myopic and senile cunning that commands no respect whatsoever and arouses the envy of no one.

And I know that insolent Austria still stretches its iron hand this side of the Alps. It could descend, in a single day, to the very heart of the Po valley, the most fertile region of Italy. And while its Emperor, so as not to retreat, even in appearance, from the position it pleased him to assume *ab initio*, refuses to return to Rome the visit paid to him by our Sovereign, our Ministers cheerfully sign the alliances that guarantee him peaceful enjoyment of the usurped territories; that repudiate the integrity and the greatness of the Fatherland. What a contrast!... and how feeble is the fibre of those who govern the destinies of the young Nation compared to that of the old reactionary Monarch and executioner!

I ask myself: Who is to realise the aspirations consecrated by the blood of so many martyrs, if one courts the enemy who has us by the throat? It has come to this: a special word has been invented — *quarantottate!* — to signify, in mockery of it, the desperate heroism of the fathers!

One thought addressed to him who reigns at present, and I have done. For some years now our Monarchy has shown signs of undergoing a partial transformation. The acts of royal humanism aim less at theatrical and ephemeral display than at leaving some trace that will endure. Respect for the individual right is more deeply felt, and political liberty is extended. Perhaps the young King was instructed by the tragic end of his father, violently struck down for his frequent returns to antiquated and tyrannical systems which ought to have been the dismal exclusive property of times long past. The May of 1898, for instance, in its means and in its effects — if not precisely in its aim — recalls the Second of December all too closely for it not to insinuate, in more than one heart, the painful suspicion that it was its sad and ill-contrived imitation.

Yet even while the Monarchy makes concessions to liberalism and professes to wish to advance with it, it retreats, underhandedly and without appearing to do so — as though bent on maintaining its equilibrium by blunting, through the augmented power of the Church, the ever-growing demands of the labouring multitudes.

A Government that seats itself between the parties, moderator of all, superior to all...! This may appear wise to the *praticismo* that cannot discern the future, or that, discerning it, is wrong to dread it. I see in it, rather, the tactic recommended by the little manuals of political philosophy. A "living" Nation is necessarily in perpetual struggle, and the Rulers of a State, monarchical or republican, may temporarily obstruct or divert it, but suppress it — never. In the social conflict that looms, equilibrium is a chimera, and the progress which now completes, ever more swiftly, its fateful course, is the inexorable condemnation alike of economic plunder and of dogma. The labouring multitude will triumph, and will annihilate in the fever of triumph not only the hostile elements, but also the Institutions that will not align themselves openly and sincerely with it.

Let no one think that I am animated by rancour. Distance in time and in space has smoothed away the sharp edges, has smothered many passions. Only love survives, intense. It flashes forth in truth. Nor shall I go to my grave with the remorse of having kept truth silent.

As a republican, I could wish that the Monarchy would commit a thousand errors; for once the measure is full, it would tumble headlong into the pit dug by its own improvidence. But as an Italian educated by my elders to set the integrity of the Fatherland above my political ideals, I do not hesitate to rekindle the last flame still in me in order to give this warning:

"Whoever leans upon the past sets his foot upon ground which the waves are daily undermining and swallowing. Trust in the future and advance boldly to meet it. It is in that way alone that you will succeed in mastering it. The past evolved from barbarism and is saturated with arrogance and crimes; but History tends toward the triumph of Democracy, of equality before rights, duties and opportunities; toward the triumph of justice. Whoever does not follow the tendencies of History cannot enjoy a reign long, blessed, and glorious.

"But in foreign policy the traces of the violence suffered by the Italian people are too manifest and drip with too much blood for your mission not to stand out clear and precise. The Monarchy exists because every patriotic party has furled its own banner before hers — not as the vanquished furl theirs, but by entering into a solemn compact that guaranteed, as the recompense for sacrifice, an Italy independent from the Alps to the uttermost southern shores. The Monarchy, on one pretext or another, has evaded the full observance of that compact. Meanwhile, Austria, notwithstanding the notorious alliance, has not slumbered. She renews and multiplies her arms; she sharpens her claws and points openly at the flesh into which she yearns to sink them. It is urgent to act humanely within the borders of the Kingdom, in order to win over the multitude by that ascendancy which springs from respect — the respect that is rooted in gratitude and in love; it is urgent to rekindle the slumbering enthusiasm and the virile spirit that will not suffer insults — not, however, with empty commemorative pageants, nor with the puerile rhetoric of banqueting speakers... it is urgent, I say, that the Nation be ready to breathe its own soul into the army. Cold and arid discipline is no longer sufficient to hold together armed formations that, at the first disaster, fall to pieces if they are not renewed and sustained by a nobler sentiment. Upon Vittorio Emanuele III falls the most exalted task of completing and correcting Vittorio Emanuele II. Ill does one bind one's head with a crown come down by the ephemeral right of succession, if, arrogating to oneself its privileges and its honours, one sets at naught its obligations. All further delay is now excessive... to prevaricate any longer would mean to usurp, by virtue of an equivocation, the throne of Italy!"

Count di Rudio closed his eyes before our victories in Libya could blind them with their dazzle. What judgement he would have pronounced upon them, the present writer is not in a position to say with certainty. But if one reflects that he disdained material advantages whenever they were not coupled with a moral and regenerative triumph, one would be inclined to wager that those victories would have wrung from him one more of those cries of his that reveal a heart that bleeds — given the manner in which those deeds were officially presented to us as worthy of the ancient Romans... and the peace treaty that recalls the shameful "cession" of Venetia!

But in setting forth the reasons why they had failed to achieve the better, the Advisers of the Crown showed themselves truly without equal.
