CHAPTER THIRTEEN

I KNOW of no Italian insurrectionary movement that brought upon Giuseppe Mazzini so many calumnies, so much bitterness, so many desertions as that of 1853.

The so-called practical men, with that short-sighted practicality which consists in leaving the world to run undisturbed even to its own ruin, maintained that a People recently bled white and broken could not respond with spirit to a call to arms; could not willingly submit to new sacrifices. And they pronounced, with great solemnity, that revolutions come to pass when they ripen of themselves, not when one wills it so.

A singular maxim which, stripped to its core, would lead to this other: "To win one's independence, to vindicate one's rights, nothing is better than to recline in one's armchair, leaving to the Sun the care of ripening events, as it ripens gourds."

The movements organised by Giuseppe Mazzini were never, in truth, either revolution or war; but they indubitably prepared the way for both and rendered them necessary.

Moreover, those of February 1853 were not initiated by the great agitator, but by precisely that People which, were one to trust the serious-minded men, would have had no thought save to make itself comfortable among accomplished facts and its own misery.

And Mazzini himself has said as much, and has written it, and has proved it. Yet even today there is no lack of supermen, the lustre and ornament of our Italy, who, unable to refute the declarations of that great man who never lied, skip over them with masterly ease and continue to extol the wonders of the armchair, persisting in the old calumnies.

The *Fratellanza*, a secret society bent upon insurrection, was already strongly organised in Milan three years after every hope for Italy had seemed to wither. Composed of several thousand workmen, it was subdivided into various sections distinguished by the letters of the alphabet. Its existence was utterly unknown to the Austrian Police, to Mazzini, to the intellectuals, to the aristocratic class, who, for all that, shared with it a common hatred of the foreign yoke.

Its principal organiser, the dyer Assi, entered into communication with Mazzini when he judged that the *Fratellanza* had sufficient strength to act. Assi requested arms, money, and leaders capable of bringing a plan of attack to a successful issue.

Mazzini received the news as proof that the propagation of his ideas had brought forth good seed even among the common multitude; but he did not place full credence in Assi's assurances regarding the numerical strength and the seriousness of purpose of the new organisation, until Major Eugenio Brizzi of Jesi and other informants, sent by him secretly to Milan, had confirmed them in every particular.

Nevertheless, he was urging patience when, in December 1852, Austria, giving fresh vent to her ferocity, crowned the ramparts of Belfiore with gibbets and, having chosen a victim from every rank of citizens, hanged them there.

In the face of this grim provocation, it seemed to the workmen of the *Fratellanza* a crime to deliberate any longer. They notified Mazzini that they had resolved to act, even without his aid and without his consent. Mazzini's inescapable duty then became that of seconding the inevitable, of endeavouring to ensure that this generous initiative should not remain isolated and fruitless.

In the last days of December 1852, charged with a mission received directly from Count Grillanzoni, I departed for Milan, and at midnight of the day on which I arrived, after a brief wait beneath the clock in the Piazza Mercanti, I delivered a packet of instructions to a man who, emerging from the shadow, had given me the agreed signal.

The definitive plan of the insurrection thus reached the hands of Major Brizzi himself, who was charged with overseeing its execution. The plan was, in its principal lines, the work of Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Its fundamental basis was the principle that weapons must be taken from the enemy by surprise, after his forces had been thrown into disorder by the killing of his officers.

By a privilege of regulation, these officers slept, as a general rule, not in barracks but in private lodgings. Note had been taken of their addresses, and their customary meeting-places were, of course, known as well.

The Austrian military establishment was, among other positions, quartered within the walls of Milan at the Palace of the General Command, at that of the Grand Guard, and at the Castello.

The first was guarded — rather than defended — by a picket of twenty-five soldiers. Their weapons stood ranged upon the gun-rack, beneath the passageway of the gatehouse, before which a vigilant sentinel paced unceasingly. The other soldiers whiled away the hours stretching their limbs in the courtyard or smoking in the barrack-rooms. At five in the afternoon, the Governor, the Generals, and the General Staff dined in that palace.

To reduce the sentinel to helplessness by a sudden assault would not have been a particularly difficult surprise. As a small cord draws a rope and the rope hauls the great cable, that first slight success would have put into the hands of the insurgents some thirty rifles, the disarmed picket, and the chiefs of the garrison.

Something similar, but with more powerful means, was to be attempted at the Grand Guard, which housed some hundred of the ruffians; but above all at the Castello, which contained in its armoury twelve thousand muskets. The two armourers who inspected them daily were men of ours. They undertook to leave the doors ajar, so that the time lost in breaking them down should not rob us of a precious moment.

In the premises of the Grand Guard, the insurgents were to entrench themselves and establish their own headquarters, to give a centre to the revolution. And from that centre, clad in the uniforms stripped from the dead and the prisoners, small patrols were to fan out who, beating the drum-roll, would have induced officers to emerge from their dwellings. On the threshold they would find those who would undertake to seal their lips — and for ever.

The sixth of February was chosen for the strike upon the enemy, a day of carnival festivity and of pay for the soldiers. Their quarters would thereby be left almost deserted. The surprise would thus have a far greater probability of success; and our men would have the greater ease to pour into the cafés and taverns, and to renew, though for a more sublime purpose, the blood-tide of the Vespers.

To smooth the path to victory, Giuseppe Mazzini had solicited and obtained from Kossuth, who was on the point of departing for the United States of America, a revolutionary proclamation addressed to his compatriots quartered principally in the barracks of San Francesco; and pledges had already been received that, at the first sign of a serious insurrection, the ill-subdued sons of valiant Hungary would have made common cause with their oppressed Italian brothers.

As the crowning touch of the enterprise — the barricades! The contractor for the city's lighting, in order to protect those who would raise them, had pledged to leave Milan in darkness. And to defend them, Giuseppe Mazzini had caused certain projectiles of a new invention to be smuggled into the city, to be hurled upon the assailants in the manner of bombs.

One must add that the students of Pavia, secretly consulted, had pledged to hasten to the aid of the capital; the agreed signal, a blaze upon the spire of the Cathedral. Acerbi was to have commanded them — and they asked nothing more than weapons. Mazzini had provided these by way of the Po.

Nor was the preparation of simultaneous movements beyond the walls limited to this. In Piedmont, the throng of political exiles kept watch, impatient for action. It was necessary to open them a broad and easy road to descend, in organised formation, upon the offensive. Giuseppe Mazzini entrusted this charge to Fortunato Calvi, who conceived the bold plan of seizing Lake Maggiore, a design I shall explain shortly.

And Aurelio Saffi, after having knocked at the doors of the wealthier patriots — on which occasion Agostino Depretis, the future Prime Minister, contributed twenty-five thousand lire — went to Bologna, defying death, and was fomenting, with fair hopes of success, the insurrection of that city and of the Romagna.

To put it briefly, Giuseppe Mazzini had secured the co-operation of the centres which might have interposed a barrier between the Milan garrison and the various Austrian nuclei scattered about the Peninsula. Fortunato Calvi confided in me later, at Zurich, that he had cherished the hope of turning, within a fortnight, with the revolutionary forces, the Austrian army of Italy, and of cutting it off at the Alps from its base of replenishment. It sufficed, to give spirit and time to Lombardy to rise, that Milan might hold firm and fight in her streets for at least a few days. And Italy would have won her independence by her own strength, as Greece had won hers, as the United States had won theirs, avoiding the fatal aftermath left by hybrid alliances, murky intrigues, and compromises that have clouded her greatness and whose effects, unhappily, will continue to be felt for a long time yet.

But here I do not argue, I narrate.

The workmen of the *Fratellanza*, with the exception of the leaders, were ignorant of the particulars of the insurrectionary plan; but they knew that on the sixth of February the blow would be struck. They had unearthed some hundred pistols and, on Mazzini's advice, had set themselves to fashioning daggers and knives. Even the rivets from the boats of their Naviglio were prised out and fixed upon shafts — everything serves the desperation of a People!

And I, seized by the general fever, was already weighing, in order to forestall them, all the foreseeable adverse contingencies of the assault upon the Grand Guard, to which Brizzi had appointed me, when I received from Count Grillanzoni the order to proceed immediately to Genoa and to await his instructions there.

These instructions were: to meet with a certain affiliated member, to have counted out to me, in Mazzini's name, four thousand lire in gold; to carry them to Sander and Radonitz, who were awaiting me at Locarno. To follow, on the journey, the road along Lake Maggiore and to watch whether anything unusual might be observed there indicating that Calvi's plans had been discovered, or that, by obstructing them, their modification had become necessary. To meet, on my return from Locarno, with Calvi himself, who would be found at Intra, and to report to him punctually all that I had seen and heard.

Austria maintained on the Verbano a flotilla of gunboats which ordinarily lay at anchor opposite Luino. Assigned to the surveillance of the shores and to the passenger service was the steamship *Radetzky*, which plied the lake daily and always had on board a company of soldiers. These, during the crossing, remained below decks, leaving their rifles ranged near the hatchway under the watch of a sentinel. With the sentinel overpowered, it seemed more than probable that one might seize the weapons and shut the other soldiers below in the hold. Sander and Radonitz, formerly officers in the Austrian navy and subsequently passed into the service of the Venetian Republic, would have taken command of the steamship and disposed of it as of an Italian vessel.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi had determined that the *Radetzky*, putting in at Luino well into the evening, should continue to fly the Austrian flag, with myself and a handful of our men on board, clad in the hated uniform.

It was necessary to approach as friends the gunboats unprepared for action; necessary to seize them by surprise. Then, having towed them out into open water, to turn their murderous muzzles upon the small garrison. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, having organised a sound body of patriots, would have assaulted Luino from the landward side. With the Italian flag raised over that strategic point, masters of the lake, the munitions, arms, and armed men coming from Piedmont would have found a comparatively open road to the very walls of Milan.

When I arrived at Arona there was still some hours to go before the departure of the *Radetzky*. I spent them walking along the shore of the lake. I recall that the magnificence of the landscape, all green and Alpine despite the winter, made upon my spirit an impression of such sweetness that, for a moment, it all but unmanned me. And I marvelled that men, being able to enjoy in fraternal ease so much of paradise, should spoil it for themselves by clutching at one another perpetually, as though nothing could take root in their breasts save base and ferocious passions.

But no sooner had the bell of the *Radetzky* announced that departure was imminent than my rebellious spirit reasserted itself. I thought: "Beautiful is our Fatherland. For that very reason they put the knife to our throats and attempt to make it their own. Therefore, all the more holy, as it is the more fierce, the rage that drives us to defend it. Let them recross the Alps and we shall be brothers again."

The steamship stood out into the open water. I lit my pipe and, walking with slow steps, this way and that, I endeavoured to assume the air of one who contemplates, in a kind of rapture, the landscape gliding past. But my mind was fixed upon my secret purpose. I saw the carbines ranged near the hatchway; I scrutinised — weighed, almost — the sentinel who, with German rigidity, with the regularity of a pendulum, trod back and forth across the planking; I heard chaotic voices rising from the depths of the vessel and understood them — they spoke, with coarse candour, of haunches and rumps, punctuating their discourse with coarse laughter. Nor did there emerge at any point during the entire voyage, from that dark recess, the pomaded moustache of a *kaiserlich* officer. Faithful to their orders, those geometric heads!

Our coup de main seemed to me so certain of success that more than once I bitterly regretted being alone and unprepared to attempt it.

But at Locarno I found spirits less willing than my own. Sander and Radonitz received me, not without a certain stiffness, at the house of one Olivieri, a dealer in sundry goods and also in arms. They took and counted carefully the gold which I drew from my money-belt. But when it came to settling the preliminaries of the action, the buts and the ifs grew thick and solid as a wall.

A few days before — I was told — two Hungarians with whom secret dealings had been in train had been thrown into prison; two of our men. "The Authorities kept them in complete isolation, and nothing had transpired regarding the grounds of their arrest. Why such precautions, such mystery? There was cause for alarm at far less than this."

Radonitz was firmly persuaded that the Police had caught some scent of what was being plotted. To attempt new ventures would be to walk headlong into the net. He urged that courage must not be confounded with rashness. It was a matter of the lives, sacred to him, of so many patriots. What would be lost by delay; by ascertaining, with due caution, the temper of an enemy so powerful and so cruel? Wisdom lay not in haste, but in acting in such manner that victory should prove certain and all but inevitable.

To all this I replied that if the arrest of some brother were enough to deflect Italians from their purposes, it would be as well to renounce action for ever. Since '49, not a day had passed without the Austrian baton being at work, without some chain being riveted afresh. The Police could not have suspected a movement as vast as ours without taking extraordinary precautions, which could not have escaped the notice of our brothers scattered everywhere. No alarm had come from any quarter whatsoever. I myself had traversed the lake from end to end, aboard an Austrian vessel, with my eye alert, and had noticed nothing — nothing at all — out of the ordinary. Moreover, the chances of discovery were in proportion to the time spent in organising the conspiracy and to the number of those who took part in it. All the more urgent, therefore, appeared the necessity for swift and almost lightning-like action. Fortunato Calvi must already have departed from Turin; he would be at Intra on the morrow. To him alone belonged the decision as to whether delay was more or less opportune. Our inaction would have robbed him of the possibility of choice and would have amounted to desertion. At all events, I had come to Locarno not to debate but to act. My orders were to report at Intra all that I had seen and heard. I would set out again alone, though it cost me my life.

Present while I spoke was Olivieri's wife. She left without a word and returned shortly after, bearing a packet of revolutionary proclamations and a bundle of daggers. She helped me, with her own hands, to conceal both upon my person, and all argument was thus cut short. On the following morning I embarked on the *Arno*, a Piedmontese steamship, and descended swiftly along the Verbano. Sander and Radonitz had remained ashore!

What a profound change in those few hours! The overcast sky heralded the fall of snow; the lake grey, and almost ashen. From the Alps there blew an icy wind that seemed to have swept away all festive life from the shores and the hillsides.

I found aboard the *Arno* muffled-up passengers with an air of self-absorbed preoccupation; common faces betraying the anxieties of commerce. My attention came at last to fix itself upon the captain, a bony man with a phenomenally large head. He was absorbed in the reading of a newspaper and cast only an occasional glance at the course. Who was he? What sentiments animated him? Could the name and the sufferings of the Fatherland find a place in his heart?

I answered myself with yes, and committed a grave error. He had suddenly risen and, as if following the impulse of a fleeting thought, walked with hurried steps to another part of the deck. He had left his newspaper on a seat. I drew forth a revolutionary proclamation, concealed it within the folds of that newspaper, and returned at a run to my place.

I did not wait long! A face painfully surprised, a knitting of brows, a swaying of that heavy head. He fixed me with a pair of eyes I have never since forgotten. Perhaps they were set in the face of the most immaculate Aristides that Piedmont had ever produced; but to me they seemed steeped, and all but drunk, with idiotic commiseration and bestial menace. I dissembled by whistling a cabaletta by Verdi into the wind that roared about me.

I am at Intra. Among the knot of curious idlers gathered to see the steamship arrive, I catch — as one might snatch something from the air — a well-known signal. I followed at a distance the person who had given it and was led to a solitary villa. The unknown brother remained outside and I went in. I found myself face to face with Fortunato Calvi.

What a flood of feeling, and how many things I should have wished to say to him; how many others, I imagine, he would have wished to confide to me! But we had to content ourselves with clasping each other's hand at length. For a moment, neither he nor I could speak a word. When I at last managed to unlock my lips, I said: "Are the sentinels at their posts?"

Having received the assurance that they were, I drew from my person the proclamations and every one of the daggers save one, and laid them on a table. I continued:

— All here and in the neighbourhood. They await the arms and the signal.

— I have had news of him from Milan. (1) It was an imprudence; but his shrewdness stood him in good stead. He is now at Lugano. Let the arms do their duty; the head watches and will do its own.

I then gave him a full account of my journey, and concealed nothing of the strange hesitation of Sander and Radonitz, who had failed to appear at the rendezvous. I had not yet finished when a window opened and in the aperture appeared the face of the same affiliated member who had guided me thither. He said, in an agitated voice: "Run, run! The carabinieri are at the gate. They are looking for you!"

In a single bound I was in the garden, which I crossed at a run, taking care that the view of the gentlemen at the gate should remain intercepted by the house. I climbed a tree and vaulted the enclosing wall. Four strides and I was at the lake-shore. On the shingle, chained to a landing-stage, several small boats lay drawn up in a row. One alone, propelled by two oarsmen, was gliding along close to the shore, as though seeking a place to run in and ground. Before the oarsmen, stupefied, had time to open their mouths, I had leapt aboard and was finding my balance amid that small shell. "Twenty francs for you! Quickly, to Locarno!" They understood and bent to the oars.

And then, followed by a crowd of children, two gendarmes arrived gesticulating on the shore. One jumped onto the shingle and busied himself, but in vain, with the boats. They were locked with padlocks, I believe. The other, flailing his arms like a Dutch windmill, shouted: "Stop, stop!" I judged it prudent not to entrust my salvation to another's compassion and, grasping my double-barrelled pistol: "Either to Locarno, or to the next world!"

From the shore came laughter and applause. The two carabinieri scanned the surface of the lake — no hope of assistance from that quarter. They vented their fury by shaking their fists at the horizon.

(1) That Mazzini was in Milan in 1853 was never stated either by him or by others, so far as I am aware. But the Count di Rudio insists that Fortunato Calvi's assertion admits of no ambiguity.
