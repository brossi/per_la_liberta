CHAPTER TWENTY-FIVE

IT WAS precisely in those days that the frigate *Amazzone* put in at the sun-kissed island and brought us a wave of news from the old Europe.

Men of retrograde ideas and men of the new order had continued their strenuous struggle for the conquest of the future, and great events had pressed upon one another and fixed themselves in history, though Orsini and Pieri and I and so many, many others had been wholly eliminated from the political scene.

What a fine lesson in humility! Magenta had carved its name there in brilliant, blood-red characters. And I was saddened by it — for what did I discern in it? The King of Piedmont had followed the tactics of his predecessors: making use of the French to beat the Austrians; only to ally himself, at a later hour, with the Austrians to put a check upon the French; and in either case, what results?... a niggardly aggrandizement of his Piedmont; a small additional source of revenue for the old dynasty — that was what I discerned in it! Napoleon would never have consented to have upon the borders of the Empire a strong Italy, capable of escaping his influence, his predominance; Napoleon, who exalted the Church and was himself made, in the hands of the Church, an instrument of reaction and oppression, would never have said to Italy: "Go, proceed triumphantly along the high road and reclaim your Rome!"

Vittorio Emanuele had insinuated among the Italian People that element of dissolution which would put an end to the heroic times — those times that regenerate character — and usher in those of vulgar opportunism and cowardly equivocation. I foresaw the profound scepticism and its attendant dissolution, that corrosive force which would invade the Latin soul, so prone to exaggerated despondency; the Latin soul that does not shine without enthusiasm, as blood does not burn and does not live without oxygen. The glorious goal, foretold by so many elect intelligences, the longing and the torment of so many virile consciences, seemed to me fatally receding. In the most favourable hypothesis, between the present and that goal stretched a treacherous wasteland, where liberty could not take root save in a stunted and debased form, warped by the inescapable demands of a Monarchy.

An officer of the *Amazzone*, struck by my sadness, gently asked me the reason for it.

"If Italy," I answered him, "in order to rise, however partially, to liberty, had absolutely needed to resort to foreign arms, I should have preferred to see men descend from the Alps who did not, as we do, bear the weight of chains upon their wrists. Magenta will doubtless mark a memorable date in the annals of Italy; whether equally auspicious, I know not. I know that it would have been auspicious had Napoleon been defeated and undone there!... Oh, what a providential misfortune that would have been! The two Peoples would have extended a hand to one another without hidden meanings, without ulterior designs; the one after having declared the usurper fallen from his throne; the other after having freed itself, through the third defeat of the Monarchy, from the equivocation, the nightmare that possesses and almost denatures it. Who could have resisted their combined impetus? They would have done far more than beat the Austrians and sweep away the petty tyrants that encumber the Peninsula; they would have practically abolished the Alps!"

"A generous dream!" said the officer, bowing his head. Then he leaned close and murmured in my ear words that made me start: "On board, we carry a Decree of amnesty — and it is for the political prisoners!"

An hour of anxiety, and we were drawn up in line before the barracks. The Captain of the frigate pronounced four sentences, alluding to the new glory with which the French arms had "covered themselves," and then followed the obligatory encomiums to the high wisdom and valour of the august Sovereign. Having extolled his clemency to the skies, he passed to the reading of the Decree of amnesty. The Director of the penitentiary pronounced, one by one, with a distressing slowness, the names of the individuals upon whom the unexpected dew was to fall. The name of Galli, the name of Rossini, the name of Tebaldi, my own — these were not pronounced. The Decree concerned French subjects only!

Several officers gathered about us and, expressing their regret, suggested that a petition from us, supported by the Director of the penitentiary, would, in all likelihood, be favourably received by the Emperor, who would thus remedy what was, beyond all doubt, an involuntary omission. Why not draft it at once? The *Amazzone* herself would carry it to France.

Galli, Rossini, and Tebaldi consented, and announced to me on the spot that they placed more trust in their petition than in the escape attempt, which they were naturally renouncing. And this was not, from my own entirely personal point of view, the least among the bitter consequences of the Franco-Piedmontese alliance and the victory of Magenta.

It fell to someone other than me — who did not sufficiently know the other inmates of that house of sorrow — to remedy that defection. I entrusted the task to Cousin, the former officer of Louis-Philippe, who set about it with such circumspection that it took him a couple of months. On the first of December he informed me that everything was ready. On the morning of the ninth, Morbioli and I stood at the pier watching the departure of the *Abeille*, bound for Cayenne. Its white wake was still faintly visible when I turned to the proud *transteverino* — the Roman from Trastevere:

"Morbioli, today we play the last card with resolution. Inform our comrades, with due prudence. I do not know precisely who the latest additions are; but I know they are not of the same mettle as those we have lost. I must, on this point, give you a recommendation I consider important."

And I urged him to leave the archpriest aside. True, he had contributed to the expenses; but the cargo he carried in his Bible — which was no longer much of a secret — might have stirred in others, if not in us, certain perverse instincts. To abandon him to his fate was, in all likelihood, to save his skin. Besides, we could not risk dishonoring, through dishonest actions, an enterprise which our very enemies would have been careful, in their hearts, not to censure.

Morbioli rubbed his hands together and observed that the archpriest would remain a caged bird, precisely on account of that little treasure upon which he had counted so heavily to be able to spread his wings. "It is plain that the holy unction never infused a shred of prophetic spirit into him." And he added: "The idea suits me; for, to tell you the truth, that priest, in so perilous an undertaking, struck me as an ill omen. It was as though we had taken him along to chant the *De Profundis* over us."

I gave him a few further instructions and we parted. At five o'clock, leaving the workshop, I saw him again, busy about the gunboats. An hour later, while the multitude of wretched souls sat in the refectory before their tin plates, I stood alone in a secluded spot, upright on the sand with my feet in the wash of the water, waiting in anxious expectation. In that low place, hemmed in among the rocks, no one could catch sight of me save from the sea.

And from the sea the oarsmen saw me. The desire for supper lent vigour to their muscles, and the little boat darted lightly over the waves, as though barely skimming them. I gave a shout and raised my fist high in the air, showing, pinched between thumb and forefinger, a five-franc piece.

They cursed; but, persuaded by Cousin, who commanded the small crew, they changed course, and in a flash the keel grated and came to rest. "Quick — quick — how many coins?"

For all answer, a whistle — and from the rocks five surly faces emerged, capable of inspiring respect in a band of savages. Before the fishermen could recover from their surprise, they found them alongside, with blades raised.

But the threat proved an almost superfluous precaution. Questioned one by one, the oarsmen had for some time already given expression to their ardent desire to escape. They had feared their companions, and not one had dared to utter an explicit yes. The kernel of my enterprise consisted, therefore, in binding together, with one bold initiative, all those pale individual affirmatives; in transforming them into a strong, collective, and dynamic will.

When I had drawn from their hiding-place the provisions, the mast, and the sail, the hesitant ones looked one another in the face and smiled. They contented themselves with saying: "Let it be understood that we yield to force."

It happens in precisely this fashion in great political and social affairs as well. How many truths are already firmly rooted in the heart of man, how many aspirations already germinate there in full vigour! Yet nothing is realized until the bold arise who dare to be the precursors. And it is this deplorable inertia, this fatal cowardice, that lends such power to the few to tyrannize the many.

Four strokes of the oar and we are as far from the shore as a musket-shot. We were eleven in all. The archpriest was absent; but he was not the only one. Morelli, the Roman tailor, who had likewise rendered us such effective service, had been seized by sudden fever and lay delirious in the infirmary. Morbioli and I were the only Italians.

Now the rocks no longer obstruct our view, nor the view of others, and the unwonted spectacle of a sailing-boat betrays in an instant the attempt so jealously concealed. The entire refectory is in uproar, and in the confused symphony of shouts, of laughter, of curses, borne to us on the wind, one terrible command stands out above the rest: *"Aux canonnières!"* A line of men pours from the refectory and stretches, with sinister windings, toward the pier.

Our little boat was making good speed; but with a manoeuvre I could not comprehend, Cousin, instead of keeping the prow pointed toward the Continent, described a wide arc, as though he intended to round the island. The convicts, who were following on the rocks a short chord of that arc, arrived promptly at the pier and lowered a gunboat in time to cut off our way. In other words, we were running at full speed, and almost deliberately, straight into danger.

I watched with anxious eyes the gunboat as she rode the water, apparently in perfect order; I watched with even greater anxiety Morbioli. He understood and said: "*Sangue transteverino non falla!* — Trastevere blood does not fail! You see? No one goes aboard, because the rowlocks are at the bottom of the sea — they are scrambling now to get new ones from the workshop! And the hull, on the bottom — four mortal holes!" And having said this, he clenched his jaw and pressed outward with outstretched arms against the empty air, as though straining to make the gunboat heavier and to do violence to the sea.

All at once he drives his fist through the air and bursts into laughter! The four holes had done their duty; the gunboat, laden with water, had gone to join the rowlocks where the light of the Sun does not reach. And the swarming mass of humanity darkening the shore acclaimed us with a torrent of cheers, with a feverish waving of rags and caps.

The tormented exulted at the defeat of their tormentors!

Now Cousin completed his mysterious manoeuvre and turned the prow resolutely toward the North. I cried out to him, with agitation: "What are you doing?"

"Be quiet! The *Abeille* will soon give us chase, and it will be along the coasts of Guiana that she will expect to intercept us. Northward, northward! Liberty lies in the midst of the tempests; our safety is in danger!"

We navigated strenuously through the whole of that night, always bearing toward the Pole. Great winds, great billows! The Ocean covered us with brine, roaring in a mighty voice through the darkness.

Was it warning us? Was it claiming us? Our senses, strained — terribly strained — could not make it out. At one moment it flung us upward as though it would hurl us clear of its domain; at the next we were drawn precipitously toward the abyss, as by a mighty, irresistible suction. Our sole assurance was the marble impassivity of the captain. No human voice amid that roaring fury save his — measured, calm, cold, metallic.

Dawn found us soaked and shrivelled. But we had left behind us the waters clouded by the great rivers that empty into the sea from the Continent, and already we were ploughing the deep blue. The Sun and the reborn hope transformed us within a brief hour. Let the Ocean rage, let it roar its unceasing menace. Each one of its waves might swallow us! What of it? Each throb of sail might bring us ever nearer to a final, tragic struggle — for upon every face there appeared the firm resolve to cut throats, or to have one's own cut, if the fruit of our boldness were to be wrested from us. What of it? We felt ourselves free as the wind that sped through the infinite!
