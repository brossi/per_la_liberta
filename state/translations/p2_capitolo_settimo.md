# Chapter Seven

THE next morning, I was pressing forward at full steam towards my destiny. To face it, I had my travelling bag... between the leather and the wooden panel of the base, it concealed the mercury fulminate and a blade that had already, during the insurrectionary attempts of 1853, known the beating of my heart. I had, moreover, a passport in the name of Giovanni da Sylva, Portuguese, commercial traveller for a brewery, and about ten shillings. The money and passport had been entrusted to me by Dr. Bernard during our last meeting. Above all, I had my youth and my courage. What would come of all this? The spark capable of altering the course of history, or nothing more than desolation and death?

Leaving the mouths of the Thames behind us, we encountered a most turbulent sea — a true tempest, indeed. The upheaval of stomachs and the general panic spread great confusion aboard. But the infinite, roaring on every side, stirred in me an excitement that redoubled my consciousness of life. For some time a certain quietism had been attempting, as it were, to impose itself upon my nature, and that manner of being plunged into force and motion seemed to regenerate me.

Yet the tempest itself, which I almost blessed, was — unbeknown to me — an answer from things themselves to the question I had put to myself, a question that pressed upon my mind with great insistence. The steamship, slowed by wind and heavy seas, put in at Calais at five o'clock in the morning of the ninth of January... twelve hours late! And when, in Paris, I stepped down from the train, I searched in vain among the crowded throng for the noble figure of Felice Orsini. This setback made imperative a visit to his lodgings in the rue Monthabor... a circumstance which was to have consequences far graver than its apparent insignificance might have led me to suppose.

Here, in brief, is what occurred during that first visit of mine. Orsini's apartment was on the ground floor. The concierge accompanied me to the door, knocked, and when the door opened, discreetly withdrew. I entered with a bound and was on the point of embracing Orsini as a brother — he stood upright and expectant — when I noticed the presence of a third person... a young man stretched out with easy familiarity in an armchair. He was fair-haired, with cheeks and lips completely clean-shaven, and the bearing of a good-natured priest. I then assumed the deferential air of a commercial traveller and, giving the name on the passport, offered to submit to "Tomaso Allsop" some samples of beer.

I was rummaging through the bag, already turned upside-down by the Customs officials, when the false Allsop broke into a loud laugh. "We are among friends," he said. And he introduced me by my true name to Antonio. He then asked me to account for my delay and, when I had done so, said: "How did Miss Cheney receive you? Did she hand over the matter?"

I tipped my few belongings onto the table and set about removing the panel that concealed the mercury fulminate. A moment later, it lay spread out upon a newspaper — *Le Droit*, if memory serves me. Felice Orsini was running his fingertips over it, as though to test the degree of its moisture. His face, so mobile and open by nature, had assumed an expression rigid with deep melancholy. He ended by saying: "The stock is now complete, and not for nothing shall we have cast twelve apostles." And he sighed.

Struck by that melancholic air and by that sigh, I recalled his first plan, inspired by his horror of bloodshed. Evidently, he still held to entrusting the liberation of France to cold steel.

"This devilry is very damp," he resumed, "we shall have to make use of the fireplace and the thermometer. But this does not concern you. I owe it to your wife and your son not to expose you beyond what is strictly necessary."

At that moment there came a knock at the door... assuredly a prearranged signal, for Orsini did not stir and left the fulminate on the table, in plain sight.

There entered a man of my own height, with a grizzled beard and an intelligent eye; the pallor of his face lent extraordinary intensity to his dark pupil. He was taken aback at the sight of me and contented himself with uttering a simple, commonplace greeting. But his words resounded in the room in an extraordinary manner. That voice recalled the monotonous drone of a *bag pipe*. It was the first time I had heard it, yet it would have sufficed to make me recognise it among a thousand, even many years hence. And, there and then, it seemed to me the most salient feature of the man. Beyond doubt, the beard concealed a full throat.

Felice Orsini made the introduction. Thus I came to know personally Giuseppe Pieri, with whom I had already had, by post, a brief correspondence. He launched at once into conversation with great familiarity, as though for long years we had shared good fortune and ill together. And so, in practice, that prudent resolution of Orsini's — to reserve for himself alone all knowledge of the participants in the attempt — was coming to an end.

I was intending to speak to him on the matter at the first opportunity, when he, as though to accentuate the ill turn things were taking, interrupted Pieri's voluble chatter: "By the way," he said to me, "where have you taken lodgings?"

I observed that I had arrived but a short while before... just long enough to snatch a mouthful at the nearest eating-house and to make my way here. I had been left with very little money in my pocket. Since the understanding had been that, for greater caution, we should lodge in a first-class hotel, I was awaiting his instructions.

"You know what?" he resumed, after scratching his head. "Our friend Pieri is staying at the Hôtel France et Champagne, rue Montmartre. He has a room with two beds. Go with him, and things will remain simplified."

He gave me no time to raise objections. He had leaned his body back and, letting his gaze pass in succession over each of us, he said: "A Venetian, a Tuscan, a Neapolitan, a Romagnol. And other, yet other fragments of Italian soil shall be represented in this proud protest. It is not this man or that, not this region or that, but all Italy together that is awakening, that is striving to clear its own path to redemption. The significance is lofty, and Europe shall understand it. And this union of hands that seek one another, today perforce in secret, pledges us that other union which shall be accomplished in the sight of the Peoples and in the light of the Sun... which shall be accomplished... shall be accomplished!"

He fell silent, and I bowed my head, and a powerful shudder ran through my bones, as though the great soul of a prophet had passed suddenly through the air around us.
