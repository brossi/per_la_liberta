## Chapter the Fourth

THE Count di Rudio speaks: The very year I took a wife I was drawn into an ill-starred affair that made a great deal of noise. The exiles who had no abundance of coin used to frequent a certain eating-house in Rupert Street, where for little outlay they could savour some dish prepared after our own fashion. I myself had gone there more than once and had struck up an acquaintance with one Rossi, a Mantuan, a young fellow who soon won my sympathies by his open and cheerful character. Rossi was excessively hasty in the matter of amorous relations, but no other vices could I discover in him; and when the Romagnol Foschini warned me, in strictest confidence, to keep clear of that "ugly customer" because he was a spy in the pay of the French Government, I observed to him that to lend easy credence to calumnies insinuated, in all likelihood for base ends, by the enemies of our party, was to lend oneself most childishly to their game.

Foschini, nettled, turned his back on me, and I... drew closer to Rossi... in part, also, from that instinct, so imperious in me, of wanting to see clearly, to the very bottom of things. I came to be convinced that Foschini was genuinely mistaken. That conviction, to tell the truth, is not so firmly rooted today. A certain Rossi, as I learned several years later, had been sinisterly implicated in the conspiracies at Mantua. Nevertheless the surname Rossi is exceedingly common in Northern Italy, and I never had the means of ascertaining whether my London acquaintance and the spy of the Austrian Government were one and the same person. For anyone interested in probing these minutiae, I will say that the Rossi of

London had a brother who was a priest — a circumstance which might serve as a guide to establishing the truth!

One evening, at a late hour, I found myself in the tavern in Rupert Street in the company of the "ugly customer." Foschini was there too, sitting entirely alone, apart from the rest; but from the black looks he had cast at us and from certain monosyllables muttered between his teeth, I had been able to gather that there was a risk of sailing in troubled waters.

The other customers thinned out and we were left almost alone. At a certain point, Foschini rose and, passing stiffly beside Rossi, jostled him at the elbow and said: "Spy!"

Rossi retorted with a worse compliment, and Foschini, lashed across the face, turned furious. I thought it my duty to intervene, and was urging them to make no scandal, so as not to compromise our whole emigrant community, when the Romagnol, having drawn a dagger, made a spring. One second more... and Rossi would have been finished! To forestall the deed, I seize a chair and do my best to bring it down upon the assailant. Unfortunately I strike the lamp hanging from the ceiling; it shatters and we are left in darkness. I remember the sensation of the cold blade entering my chest, then... nothing more!

When a glimmer of consciousness returned to me, I understood that I was in a hospital. I could not keep my eyes open; but I heard a voice, the physician's, pronouncing that I should not see the morrow's sun. They made me swallow some preparation or other, and that brief effort was enough for me to lose my senses once again.

But my youth reasserted itself and, in spite of the gloomy prophecy, my first impression was precisely that of the sun's rays beating upon my face. They stirred in me a firm will to cling to life.

I learned that the doctor, considering the case desperate, had resorted, in order to arrest the internal haemorrhage, to a heroic remedy of his own. He regarded the outcome as a wonder, and for a long succession of days brought to my bedside now one colleague, now another, entering upon discussions of presumably great scientific value. To me, a layman and a man of irritable nerves, they were supremely inopportune.

Before I received the regulation visit of a Police Inspector, the doctor informed me that Foschini had made two other victims. But Rossi, the one undeniable target he had wished to remove, had come out of it without a scratch.

Upon Foschini, who had gone into hiding, the Authorities placed a reward of two hundred pounds. But he was a good patriot, and though his hiding-place was known, no one breathed a word. Indeed, in the laudable attempt to shield him, the political motive which had driven him to act was greatly exaggerated, with scant regard for whether, in order to save the culprit's reputation, the reputations of innocent men were being diminished.

Now let one mark the strangeness of destiny: that in Europe first, then in America, I faced the gravest dangers, and though I was more than once within a hair's breadth of leaving my bones there, I always came away with my skin intact. It was reserved for my companions in faith to repay my disinterestedness with six-and-thirty dagger-thrusts, two of which penetrated into cavities and were adjudged mortal!

Foschini closed his days splendidly, fighting at the side of Carlo Pisacane in the ill-fated, glorious expedition of Sapri.

Sapri was the first serious manifestation of the new change of tactics adopted by Giuseppe Mazzini. The Crimean War being ended, the great Cavour, in spite of the splendid showing of our soldiers and their victory at the Cernaia, had obtained nothing for his King — not even the hoped-for annexation of Parma and of Modena, whose petty princes they wished to foist upon Moldavia and Wallachia. But Napoleon III had promised that one day he would do something also for Piedmont, and Mazzini held for certain, even from those days, the alliance of the Empire with Vittorio Emanuele II. He was, however, firmly convinced that from it nothing would derive save an enlargement of the Sardinian States, or at best, a federation of Italian States governed, for the greater part, by French princes. To forestall such a calamity, he abandoned the old field of action and labored, with his most trusted followers, to raise the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies in revolt: to set against the monarchical agitation of the North the insurrection of the southernmost extremity of Italy; to impose from Palermo and Naples the unity of the Nation. The brilliant conception earned for Giuseppe Mazzini and for Garibaldi a sentence of "ignominious death," but it was destined to prove one of the greatest factors in our future greatness.

Left entirely to one side — presumably because I was thought to have been softened by new affections — I was already resigning myself to a long inaction, when suddenly the call came to me from whence I least expected it.
