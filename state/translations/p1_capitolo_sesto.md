CHAPTER SIX

TO INSINUATE my own personality among the great men who labored in that titanic defense seems to me an act of irreverence. And I will say, moreover, that my spirit remains as though overwhelmed by the surging return of those powerful visions.

On the 30th of April, I too saw the backs of the invaders, and certainly, pressing the advantage of our victory, we would have driven the French into the sea, had not Giuseppe Mazzini deemed it expedient, for his diplomatic designs, to blunt our élan.

On the 9th of May, I was fighting with Garibaldi at Palestrina against the Bourbon troops of General Lanza. That easy victory sufficed to check the advance of Re Bomba, who quitted the Alban Hills and hastily recrossed the frontier a few days later, when Garibaldi, preferring victory to the appearance of discipline, urged our vanguard to engage the battle of Velletri.

In that memorable day I had a part in an episode which I recall with pride. The lancers of Angelo Masina, overwhelmed by an entire enemy column, were falling back at breakneck gallop along the road to Valmontone. Giuseppe Garibaldi, no sooner catching sight of them, spurs his white horse and, reaching a short distance from their front, plants himself across the road to bar their way. Behind him, mounted on a horse black as ebony, stood the giant Aguyar, his face no less dark and gleaming. Heroism forgot that motion has its laws. The lancers made superhuman efforts to pull up short; but to no avail. In less time than it takes to tell, the road was blocked by a tangled mass of men and horses... Garibaldi and Aguyar were buried beneath it. The enemy cavalry came on.

I was fighting along the road, to the right, among the vineyards. We were a handful of beardless youths... But we waited for no orders, and crying: "Forward! forward!" we rushed resolutely to the rescue. The Bourbon troops, assailed in front and on the flank, broke into headlong flight, leaving in our hands a goodly number of prisoners.

Garibaldi recognized in our impetuosity his own deliverance. In the evening, when by order of General Rosselli our offensive was unhappily brought to a close, he came among us. That man, so terrible when the cannon thundered, pinched my cheek good-naturedly. Of this sort of codicil to the episode, I smile now; but then... I felt all the heroic qualities I supposed in myself evaporate. That gesture of paternal familiarity reduced me, with unspeakable force, to the humble proportions of a child.

At the end of May, I was returning to Rome, and we were already on the point of marching against the Austrians, when General Oudinot, adding treachery to treachery, prematurely broke the armistice concluded after his defeat on the 30th of April, and seized, by surprise, the Villa Pamfili. There was no longer any need to go to Ancona to fertilize, with our blood, the soil of Italy!

That day — the 3rd of June — witnessed such proofs of desperate valor as to astonish Europe and the world, no longer accustomed to behold, among the hills of Rome, Roman greatness. But the hydra of reaction claimed, that day, too grievous a holocaust of our finest. Would it had been the last!

The French aimed at subduing Rome by taking possession of the Janiculum, which dominates the entire city. To reach it, it was first indispensable to carry our advanced posts. Having taken Villa Pamfili, they moved, under cover of darkness, against Villa Corsini which, by reason of its elevated and strategic position, might be considered the key to Porta San Pancrazio. They took it by surprise; but by a furious bayonet assault we retook it.

Unhappily, owing both to the enormous disproportion of numbers and to various topographical reasons on which I think it superfluous to dwell, the assault on that villa was more than arduous for those advancing from the city; easy for the French entrenched beyond Villa Pamfili. Our magnanimous contempt for our own lives might lead us, and did repeatedly lead us, to become masters of it once more; but to hold it was for us a superhuman and impossible undertaking.

Nevertheless we displayed incredible aggressive tenacity from the earliest morning hours until late evening. That superb manifestation of the reawakened, virile Italian temper had, so far as I was concerned, an interlude. In one of our first reconquests, as I was passing, weapon in hand, through a passage encumbered with corpses, I caught sight, through the crack of a door, of the red trousers of a Frenchman... an officer who had persisted in the defense and had been unable to withdraw with his men. To escape the first fury of the assailants, he had concealed himself in a privy. Upon my calling on him to surrender, he handed over his sword without a word.

I was charged with escorting him to the Chiesa Nuova, where the captured officers were being temporarily confined.

The Romans had treated the prisoners taken on the 30th of April not as enemies but as guests. Those were the days when the rosy hope of brotherhood between the two Peoples still trembled in the air, and the hopes of inducing the French to protect Rome against the Austrian hordes had been renewed by the very assurances of the Triumvirs themselves. Carriage-rides to the monuments, refreshments, songs, dancing... then liberty. The promise not to again take up arms against the champions of our independence was neither given nor demanded.

But on the 3rd of June, the spirit of the Romans had terribly changed. Oudinot's perfidy had roused to the point of frenzy the indignation of that people so alien to all duplicity, so hostile to treachery. I perceived this the moment I entered by Porta San Pancrazio, and the officer, whom it was not always in my power to shield from rough insults, had still more occasion to be persuaded of it.

On reaching the Ponte di San Sisto, I found my way barred by some militiamen of the Guardia Nazionale who, refusing to hear any argument and following the impulse of the moment, demanded that I hand over the prisoner to them. They wished to shoot him dead and cast his body into the river.

I cried out to them: "Let those who thirst for French blood run outside the walls, and blessed be he whose hands prove themselves capable and strong. But here, no! While there is breath in my body, I will not permit the Italian name to be dishonored by acts of cowardice."

The timely intervention of an officer calmed the agitated spirits and averted every evil.

We reached the Chiesa Nuova without further incident, and the Frenchman, grateful, recounted what had happened to the other prisoners. While not far off two peoples, made to understand and to love each other, were hurling themselves one against the other, thirsting for vengeance, in that relatively tranquil corner I heard the very invaders extolling our valor and the wish that, once rancour was dispelled, French blood and Italian blood might flow mingled together, but with fraternal solidarity, but for a common and holy vindication.

A prophetic wish!.. In those moments, however, I considered it more probable that I should be taken up alive into the glory of Heaven!

Retracing the narrow and steep way that leads up to Porta San Pancrazio, I was obliged to walk against the current... the current of the wounded, being carried or dragging themselves to the church of San Pietro in Montorio, which had been converted into a field hospital. Soldiers and civilians alike applauded them. From the top of the walls, a band scattered amidst the gunfire the notes of the Marseillaise, flinging back in the faces of the assailants their own hymn to Liberty. In the midst of so much blood, at such an extremity, Rome found the means to be ironic! I had left Villa Corsini in the hands of the Legionaries; I found it reoccupied by the French. Our Bersaglieri, commanded by Luciano Manara, were attempting to retake it. I watched them run up the slopes of the hill, leaving behind them ground covered with the slain and the dying. The fire cutting them down was so heavy that, at a short distance from the building, they were as though suddenly stopped in their tracks by the lead. But their breasts, swelling with love of country, had no room for faint-hearted sentiments. Exposed as they were, they knelt down and continued to discharge their rifles, presenting an undaunted front to the enemy and to fate. Surely they expected to be reinforced by our men massed at the Vascello and on the walls; surely they offered themselves, with serene spirit, to death so as to give those men time to come up; to renew, at their side, the attack.

What moments those were for us who watched their ranks grow thin; for us who suffered their wounds and were proud of their greatness! The awaited order never came. They fell back when the officers commanded it; when discipline forbade further sacrifice. But in passing out through the enclosure, which terminated toward Rome in an acute angle, they became the certain target of a converging and terrible fire, and then... O my Country, what carnage!

Thus the proud Lombard youth blotted out the shame of past civil discord, and saturating, with their own blood, the soil of the Gracchi, traced upon it, invisible to the eye yet blazing in the heart, the fateful words: "Italy united, Rome the capital of Italy!"

It was in that assault that Enrico Dandolo ceased to throb and to fight for his, for our Country. His death, raising to still greater heights the courage of his brother Emilio, drove him to attempt the assault of the villa, leading alone twenty of his men. That slender band, thinned along the way by enemy bullets, was almost lost among the rough ground and the orange trees of the slope. Thirteen of them reached the peristyle, discharged their weapons, and withdrew in the face of the inevitable. When they took shelter at the Vascello, they were reduced to six... not one of them unwounded.

Their enterprise is one that cold and calculating minds may well describe as mad... and let it be so! But it was the madness of natures almost superhuman who fought not to win. The defenders of Rome, it is well to make it clear, had set themselves the aim of giving the lie to the belief that Italians do not fight; of placing, between the Temporal Power and the longed-for third Italy, the impossibility of any ambiguity; of affirming, by dying heroically, our right to live. And we may now proclaim it aloud: they achieved their arduous end.

The turn of heroism and sacrifice fell, after Emilio Dandolo, upon Angelo Masina of Bologna. By now the upper floors of the villa, cannonaded from the bastions of Porta San Pancrazio, are half in ruins and in flames; but for that, several hundred unseen Frenchmen, invulnerable unless rooted out with cold steel, prove no less formidable to Masina's forty lancers.

They ride up the hill at a gallop, and reach the summit with nothing to stay them. Here, to the great astonishment of friends and foes alike, Masina, suddenly taking on the proportions of a figure conjured by the power of enchantment, launches himself on horseback up the steps, dragging behind him, up onto the terrace itself, that fistful of bold men.

And perhaps they would all have left their bones there, had we soldiers massed at the Vascello, exalted by that spectacle, not feared that our commanders, persisting in their error, would render even so much heroism fruitless. It was as though an electric spark, leaping from the heart of Angelo Masina, had in an instant kindled a thousand thousand hearts. We broke from cover and rushed to the assault spontaneously, without awaiting orders from any man.

A yet stranger circumstance! The populace, crowded upon the bastions as in a theater, divined our intent, and, seized by our fever, poured forth, surging like an impetuous wave, into the walled lane; spilled in great confusion and as though impelled by its own momentum up the slopes of the hill, soaked with so much blood. They had no weapons, or weapons altogether inadequate; but they seemed persuaded that their very presence would give strength to our arms... They would have shown, if nothing else, that they knew how to face danger, that they knew how to die!

Such a breach of discipline, such utter want of every elementary prudence, produced an effect contrary to that which might logically have been expected. Not only was every Frenchman swept from Villa Corsini; but, pressing the victory home, our bayonets retook likewise the Convent of San Pancrazio, where the invader had entrenched his outposts since the morning. And a band of resolute men, veering to the right, succeeded in taking possession of Villa Valentini, from whose windows the French had been harassing us, a little earlier, even within the Vascello itself.

Never, as in that hour, did the fortunes of the day seem so propitious to the glory of Rome!.. When, from Villa Pamfili, Oudinot hurled his seasoned and rested Regiments one after the other to recapture the lost positions, numbers, discipline, and superior arms prevailed once more; but not without enormous sacrifices. Neither our men, exhausted by so many ordeals, nor the almost unarmed People, fled.

We fell back to the Casa Giacometti, to the Vascello, to the walls, but always with our faces turned toward the enemy, always fighting.

Giuseppe Garibaldi carried in his heart the certainty that, with the loss of Villa Corsini, the fate of the young Republic was sealed. The improvident General Rosselli had not taken the trouble, during the armistice, to fortify it, to render it, if not invulnerable, at least stronger. To crown the disaster, he — Garibaldi — received from Rome only in driblets and at long intervals the fresh contingents necessary to sustain the battle. Seeing defeat inevitable, he was unwilling to submit to it without pushing his tenacity to the uttermost extreme.

Night was already near when he, placing himself at the head of the Reggimento Unione and whatever remnants of other corps the French lead had spared, personally led yet another furious assault. The valor the same as ever... but catastrophe the result!

I had just fired my last cartridges when I saw pass beside me, supported by his comrades-in-arms, pale but smiling, Goffredo Mameli. They told me he had been wounded in the knee... Italy was about to lose her young Tyrtaeus!

Night fell... Despondency, recriminations!? Not a shadow of either. There persisted in us, after the defeat, the exaltation that had sustained us, that had multiplied our strength a hundredfold during the desperate and unequal struggle. And as for Garibaldi, we felt we loved him as though he had led us not to slaughter but to victory. Our spirit felt itself so little vanquished, that, scarcely emerged from that bath of blood, we conceived and attempted a deed which, in an army made homogeneous not by the overmastering desire to act but by discipline, would have earned us the stern sentence of a court-martial.

Masina, the heroic Bolognese who for one moment had seemed to friends and foes alike a knight descended among the combatants by the power of enchantment, had given proof of being all too mortal. Among us were those who declared they had seen him fall during our retreat, at a short distance from Villa Corsini. We looked one another in the face, and barely a word sufficed: "Let us go."

Shortly after, we slipped out stealthily from the Casa Giacometti. We were twelve in number. We crept along the Via della Nocetta, hugging the wall that encircles the villa. The cannon had opened a small breach in it... through that breach we entered the enclosure, and still creeping, climbed the slope. Not a suspicious sound!.. we search, we find. But the stout-hearted men who lay their hands upon those glorious remains forget their prudence and, thinking to escape the danger more quickly, raise their burden and descend toward the breach at a run. In the night there rang out with sinister resonance a volley of rifle-fire... three fall in a heap; the others scatter... I leap behind a hedge and throw myself to the ground. Close beside me, touching my elbow, lay the corpse of an enemy with his skull shattered.

I was the last to return to the Casa Giacometti... four of our men never returned at all. And as I was recounting to my comrades-in-arms by what means I had extricated myself, one of them cried out: "Jesus... what a stench!" The sleeve of my uniform was all plastered with French brains.

The 8th of June was a torrid day... I stood for a few seconds in silence, contemplating the sad transformation undergone, in so brief a time, by the sublime organ of human thought, beneath the kisses of the splendid Roman sun.
