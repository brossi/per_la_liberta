CHAPTER TWO

Half an hour of discussion in the study, beneath the likenesses of Mazzini and Fortunato Calvi, who seemed to be listening to us. And I was already almost disposed to renounce my idea, when, to forestall future regrets, I said with some vehemence: "But do you not see, Major, that this is a duty to be discharged? The affair had a historical motive and to History it belongs. Why not clarify its circumstances and its intentions? Why permit inexactitude and falsehood to overlay the truth? If you persist in your silence, the word will remain unchallenged with your political adversaries, and you will make yourself a willing accomplice in their mendacity. It would be a grave wrong toward the martyrs whose memory you are bound to defend; toward your children, to whom you must leave, in all its entirety, the good name you received from your father."

The Major passed a hand over his brow and ground his teeth. It was his manner of manifesting strong emotions. All at once he fixed his eyes upon my face and began:

I was born in Belluno on the 26th of August, 1832, son of Count Ercole and Countess Elisabetta de Domini.

My family has old roots in that uttermost extremity of Italy. Angelo Biave wrote of it — and wrote, God rest his soul! — in Latin hexameters. A great-uncle of mine took the trouble of rendering them into the vernacular, thereby fashioning an instrument of torture for his great-nephews. For every escapade of ours, if punishment came it was mild enough for the times, yet terribly tedious: to commit to memory a translated passage from Biave. Papa presumed, in this manner, to retemper you in the glory of your forebears. I can still recall the opening:

*"I gird myself for an arduous undertaking; I tell of ancient personages, descended from illustrious lineage, who gave to their Peoples the most just of laws.*

*"In the ninth century, the Emperor Otto the Great ordained that the celebrated Nosadana family should govern Belluno and endow it with a wise order of Laws. Just recompense accorded by Caesar to virtue.*

*"For a long time the Nosadano ruled the city which our ancestors, with good reason, called Belluno; for therein Bellona proves herself and the citizens are valiant in arms, undaunted before peril.*

*"The Nosaidana family boasts personages of high renown in war and in counsel. It changed its name and was called Rudia."*

The poet then proceeds to recount the deeds of a goodly company of Nicolai, Ercoli, and Eustachi — some of them *podestà* [magistrates] of the native city; others celebrated for books dripping with learning; still others distinguished as advocates of the common people, or as commanders of the Venetian infantry, or for hoary heads rendered venerable by holy works and by the mitre...

But let us leave the forebears to sleep in peace, and speak of my father; from whom I believe I inherited something more than blood and name. An anecdote to make my meaning plain.

At the opening of 1848, when the gaze and the hopes of Italian patriots were converging upon Rome and popular songs repeated the praises of Mastai-Ferretti, the citizens of Belluno organised a demonstration in homage to the "liberating Pontiff." They filed through the streets bearing in triumph the bust of Pius IX.

My father sat at a table in a café. He sees, he trembles, and planting himself boldly before the demonstrators, he cries out to them: "O deluded souls, you carry in triumph the greatest enemy of our Fatherland." And without adding another word, with a powerful blow of his stick, he sends the idol of the multitude to pieces.

Intense was the excitement that followed the momentary stupefaction, and my father owed it to his deeply rooted popularity that he did not live, there and then, his last quarter of an hour.

Pius IX, repudiating the holy crusade for the independence of his Country and fornicating with the foreigner, presently took it upon himself to confirm that popularity and to demonstrate the perspicacity of the old and proud patrician.

A man of such a temper was bound to prove odious to Austria, gloomy and suspicious as she was. The fact remains that he never obtained permission to practise at the bar, despite his considerable grounding in the legal sciences. He consoled himself with the sweetnesses of fatherhood and had fourteen children. I was the fifth-born.

They called me the *"Moretto"* — the dark one — from the colour of my skin. I was the favourite, and I do not prefix "modesty aside," for I believe I was so more on account of my mischief than of my virtues. My maternal grandfather, Fortunato de Domini, Colonel of the Austrian infantry, blinded by intense affection, fancied he could discern in me the stuff from which something might be made; and in order to remove me from the influence of the liberal ideas that had seeped into the family, he wished to keep me constantly by his side. First in the barracks at Belluno, an old building which had once resounded, in its better days, with the psalmody of friars; then in the fortress of Palmanova, to which he had been transferred. The good man died before perceiving that liberal ideas were more dextrous than his vigilance, stronger than the walls of any stronghold.

The simple and rough education he had imparted to me was continued thereafter at the Military Academy of San Luca in Milan, which was at that time destined for the cadets of the Lombardo-Venetian Kingdom. And I should have remained there, with my brother Achille, until the completion of my studies, had there not suddenly arisen to overwhelm me the fierce tempest that, having gathered in the North, crossed the Alps and swept through and bloodied the whole of Italy.

Here, at the risk of recounting things too well known to some, I think it useful to summarise the Italian question as it presented itself in that momentous epoch. Memory is more fleeting than one supposes among the fervent Latin race, and the evoking of the ills that afflicted us will better explain why those who desired a United and Independent Italy could not and should not have remained in doubt before the greater or lesser correctness of the means employed.

Milan was the first centre where our fury broke forth. No plan long prepared, no understanding among the citizens. The news of the insurrection in Vienna had disposed men's minds to attempt something new; a yellow and black banner unfurled, at an ill-chosen moment, from her windowsill by a woman of easy virtue, kindled — as if stirred by destiny — the flame that refines men and transforms them into heroes.

He who had long prepared the material to feed the insurrection was the aggressor himself; it was the folly of the wise Metternich.

That dismal incarnation of underhand arts already regarded himself as the most powerful intellect that had appeared on the world stage in centuries, even before the day when, taking advantage of the embarrassment of Napoleon the Great, he had managed to leap at his throat and hasten his ruin. One can well imagine him after that!

Napoleon, from his great height, had treated peoples as a flock of sheep; why should he not have done likewise?

And so the one and the other, by excess of confidence in their own powers, fell, by different roads, into the same fatal error.

Napoleon I, by the very nature of his genius, having subjugated a People, redoubled its activity and fomented its ambition. Not for nothing does one trample underfoot the prejudice of birth and reserve the highest offices of State for positive merit. A son of the Revolution, he had usurped its lightnings; yet only through it, and upon the ruins of the past, had he been able to raise himself a giant.

Wherever his eagles appeared, there blew a potent spirit of modernity; wherever he passed with his victorious armies, he spread, of necessity, the ferment of the new ideas that smoothed the way to the future. And in every other respect, his entire life was a wholesome lesson: "one must become strong and be bold in order to overcome the strong."

Thus in the very yoke he imposed lay the germ of liberty and redemption; the very good that accompanied his evil works was destined — strange to say! — to undermine his power and precipitate him into the abyss of Leipzig.

Metternich was too keen an observer not to perceive that point of weakness. In his titanic enterprise of driving the human flock back toward the past, he took every care to be a tyrant — a dark tyrant and nothing else.

In 1814, when the power of Napoleon, though declining rapidly, still appeared menacing and the aid of the peoples seemed indispensable to forestall surprises, Metternich and the other European diplomatists who took their cue from him made the most lavish promises of liberty to the Italians. It sufficed, to obtain it, that they should show themselves worthy of it by helping the Holy Alliance to break the bloody yoke of the French adventurer; by rallying round a banner that signified the happiness, the regeneration, of their Country.

Once the Napoleonic peril had vanished, those deluded souls, divided in the choice of a prince, yet showed themselves agreed in openly demanding their independence.

The replies that came, at long intervals, from the mouth of Metternich mark the various phases through which the nefarious work of subjugation passed, and would suffice, of themselves, to arouse hatred in the heart of any people that has feeling.

— The affairs of the Italians will be settled in Vienna.

— Austria will never consent to relinquish its rights of conquest.

— Italy is a geographical expression.

Unfortunately, Metternich was able to give force to his own pronouncements not only through diplomatic intrigues and through the arts of the Church, but also — and above all — through the armies of the Holy Alliance.

The debt of gratitude toward the peoples being treated as non-existent, there remained to Austria that toward the princes. And perhaps, striking a line through even this last, she would have appropriated half the Peninsula for herself, had not the jealousies of Prussia and of Czar Alexander I arisen, most opportunely, to play, to however limited a degree, the game of the petty tyrants.

Compelled to divide Italy into morsels to amortise that debt, Metternich took care to create about himself a cluster of satellites that would gravitate of necessity toward the great centre of Vienna. A partial exception he proposed to make for Murat, for whom he reserved the kingdom of Naples. The wife of Murat, Napoleon's sister, had delighted him with kisses worth a kingdom... but the impetuous French general's untimely adoption of the role of champion of the Italians deprived Metternich of the opportunity to accomplish the only phosphorescent design of his entire political life.

The kingdom of Naples fell to Ferdinando I, who had, moreover, prepared his way to it by lavishing nine million francs in the antechambers of the Congress of Vienna. It was an Austrian army that conducted him triumphantly to his capital.

Rome and the Legations fell to Pius VII, the restorer of the power of the Jesuits; the future compiler of the Bull *"Ecclesiam Jesu Christo"* which excommunicated all those faithful who, knowing an affiliate of the Carboneria, did not degrade themselves by denouncing him — by playing the spy.

The Grand Duchy of Tuscany and the principality of Piombino fell to Ferdinando III, an Austrian archduke; the Duchy of Modena to Francesco IV, surnamed by his subjects the "executioner." Maria Luisa, daughter of the Emperor of Austria and wife of Napoleon I, had to content herself with ruling at Parma; whilst the other Maria Luisa, Infanta of Spain, was placed over the principality of Lucca.

Genoa, despite its protests and the eloquence of the Marchese Brignole, who sought to invoke its long centuries of liberty and its past greatness, was incorporated into Piedmont; upon whose throne, returning from his refuge in Sardinia, Vittorio Emanuele I took his seat.

Austria retained under its direct dominion Lombardy, Venezia, the Tyrol, Istria, and Dalmatia.

Having provided for securing Austrian preponderance by surrounding the empire with weak States, Metternich set himself to augment its power ever further by spreading a close-woven network of secret treaties. And by imposing, along with the obligation to adopt reactionary measures, the further obligation of reciprocal aid in the event of unrest, he reserved to himself, in substance, the right to intervene with arms in the affairs of each separate State.

The sovereigns of Italy, so haughty and cruel toward their subjects, had, in order to reign, accepted the part of Austria's hired assassins. Austria, having had its direct dominion over the entire Peninsula taken from it by the jealousy of its faithful allies, had turned the difficulty and had succeeded in securing for itself an indirect dominion no less substantial. And the chains that weighed upon the wrists of the august Latin Mother were none the lighter nor the looser for it.

By virtue of those Treaties, every Italian movement, even peaceful, aimed at provoking liberal reforms, was smothered in blood by the nefarious work of the army in white uniforms with pomaded moustaches; and not even Piedmont, whose Kings, not lacking in courage, had already given some fine proof of manly independence, was spared by the barbaric deluge.

In the great historical periods, glad or sorrowful, there unfold incidents that epitomise them and may serve art in figuring them forth. The symbol of Italy in those times was as though embodied and enacted in the torture practised under the diabolical inspiration of the Bourbon of Naples. An imprudent word, a suspicion, the hatred of a delator — the more ready for calumny in that the accused and the accuser were never confronted with one another — and an innocent man was torn from his family, stripped to the waist, bound astride a donkey, driven through the streets of the city.

A company of Austrian soldiers preceded him to the sound of a drum, another followed behind; at his side was the gaoler wielding a scourge armed with nails, and at every step came blows that penetrated into the flesh and tore it away in shreds.

The torture was suspended when the wretch, scarlet with blood, bowed his pale face and seemed about to breathe his last... Suspended, but to greater torment. It recommenced as soon as a groan, a convulsive movement of the muscles, revealed the revival of sensibility.

The passers-by did not react; they covered their faces and fled in horror... At the end of the long Calvary, the gibbet! It was the agony of Christ renewed daily by the work of most Christian Kings allied together — under the auspices of the Pontiff — in holy alliance!

More refined, but no less inexorable, was the oppression weighing upon those Italians subject to the direct dominion of the Empire. The air, impregnated with falsehoods, had begun to envelop tender childhood, which was taught, as its highest duty, as a religious obligation, servile submission to the tyrant; it penetrated the lungs of the university student, upon whom the institution was compelled to furnish the State not so much with learned men as with faithful subjects; it hung, subtle and dissolving, over every phase of social life. Free, the cherished subjects of His Majesty were, to move from house to house, from place to place; to attend to their affairs upon whose profits the Exchequer extended its rapacious claws... serfs in all besides; men resigned or swollen with rage... citizens never!

Not for this did the repression of refractory spirits proceed without due respect for the forms prescribed by strict legality — an easy task where the Laws are promulgated by despotism, without the collaboration and assent of the governed. And with generous Italians, lawfully struck down, the fortress of the Spielberg in Moravia was peopled — that fortress which, for the groans suppressed by its walls, for the noble intellects that were withered and extinguished there, for the crimes against humanity that it concealed in the darkness of its underground chambers, shall rise horrible in the memory of men even when the last of the Habsburgs has been confounded, for centuries, with the dust.

The flood of informers, high and low, who inundated the Kingdom provided admirably for the supply of innocent flesh to that Austrian Bastille, to the pillory, and to the flogging reenacted in its old forms. Bearing no distinguishing marks of recognition; concealed beneath plausible occupations, they insinuated themselves into every family, drew up reports on the tendencies, the habits, the conversations of every individual — even on the style of hat habitually preferred; they violated the postal secret; they spun plots; they besmirched or sought to besmirch the reputation of the most prominent and authoritative men, whose word might have incited men's souls to manly vindication of their Rights.

The population of the Lombardo-Venetian Kingdom, emaciated and corroded by a monstrous fiscal exaction, monstrously applied, had to provide not only for the expenses of the local administration but to replenish the imperial coffers handsomely as well. So true is it that when you scrape the tyrant, the freebooter is laid bare!

And the freebooter cast aside the refined and reptilian means to lay hold, like the other oppressors of Italy, of those barbarously ferocious, the moment the Milanese, refusing to smoke and to wear cloth manufactured by the foreigner, attempted to injure him in his purse. The prison gates were thrown wide, and the dregs of society, mingled with the police, roamed the streets of the metropolis, smoking and compelling others to smoke. The bold men who refused to press between their lips the leaf impregnated with German spittle were mutilated or run through with steel; and neither sex nor age offered any defence.

Martial law became ordinary law; treason, punishable by harsh torments, was the wearing of cloth of certain colours, the singing of certain songs, the applauding of certain passages in this or that drama; the adorning of oneself with beard or hair cut in a given fashion; allowing oneself to be carried away by any act which the Authorities, entirely at their own discretion, deemed a demonstration of a subversive nature. "Three days of slaughter ensure thirty years of peace," said old Marshal Radetzky. And the pretext to perpetrate the slaughter was sought with the pertinacity of one who pushes the craving for dominion to the pitch of lust: of one who knows no other inspiration than the dark interests of the dynasty.

Such were the delights that the well-groomed and smiling Metternich dispensed, with a hand more or less open, to a large part of Europe. And the Peoples escaped him by virtue of a quality inherent in the very arts that held them in bondage. For those arts of necessity aroused the hatred which is a regenerating energy, which drives men toward the luminous affirmations of Right as powerfully as, or more powerfully than, love.

And so intensely did this energy stir the hearts of the Milanese that on the 18th of March, 1848, they found themselves united by a supreme intent; without distinction of class, without distinction of sex, without distinction of age... to kill or be killed; to conquer or to lie buried beneath the ruins of their own metropolis.

No exhortations were required, no watchword, no leaders. The hundred and sixty thousand inhabitants of the city became one terrible unity... the PEOPLE; even as the thousands of millions of grains of powder constitute, upon exploding, the terrible unity of the powder magazine.

Arms?

They were drawn from beneath the earth, from within the thickness of walls, from the hiding-places where bold men, yearning for the day of vengeance, had smuggled them away... old implements of every description, rusted; to which the armourers, emptying their shops, soon added a supply of hunting-pieces. Then came the bludgeons, the rapiers, the halberds once gripped by mercenary soldiers; the great swords that perhaps broke the impetus of Barbarossa in the fine days of the Lombard League... the past issuing from the half-light of silent Museums, hurling itself into the vortex of the uprising, bringing its own contribution to the redemption of a People.

That formidable tumult of bursting passions did not shatter itself against the walls of the Military Academy of San Luca; but, penetrating within, it aroused in our youthful souls a fever which, though suppressed, lost nothing of its intensity.

On the first day of the insurrection, the Officers spoke to us with contempt of a handful of brigands whom the Austrian force, and the good sense of the "respectable" population itself, had soon enough brought back to their duty. Then... a hard face and silence. But that silence was worth, concentrated, the speech of several volumes.

The energy that agitated the living and made them greater than themselves had passed all at once into things. In little more than a night, more than a thousand barricades sprang up and blocked the narrow streets of the city. The pavement had yielded its stones; from the palaces artistic furnishings came crashing down; the workman's house furnished its household goods, its mattresses, its pillows. Sanctified by the feverish hand of a people that willed it, even the humblest utensils had been transformed into a bulwark of liberty... shapeless bulwarks; their disorder proclaimed the fury of men's souls.

The combatant who, pale, tormenting his carbine, waits at his post in ambush, rediscovers emotions refined almost to their purest essence. It was among that furniture that fathers, chastising him, had taught him to mouth secretly the name of Italy. And now that name he would cry aloud at the appearing of the enemy as a supreme provocation, as a challenge to the death; now he would prove that the ancient valour may languish in Italian fibre, but perish never. Conqueror or conquered, he would have accomplished a work not without greatness.

The assault came, and at a hundred disparate points, simultaneously. But the Austrian found everywhere men whom desperation had taught the art of defence. The barricades creak and shift form under the fury of assailants and assailed, are drenched with the blood of one side and the other... blood... but they send back the imprecation of the barbarian who falls, the battle-cry of the citizen who triumphs.

While the patriots fight in the foremost ranks, the mothers, the sisters, the children share the care of the wounded and prepare fresh means of attack. The cobblestones and tiles heaped upon the rooftops are transformed into deadly projectiles; the web of barricades extends and draws close about the barracks, the isolated garrisons. Besieged by the oppressor, the Milanese thus become, in part, the besiegers. And under the leaden sky, more insistent, more resonant than the battle itself, spread the peals of bells rung in alarm. Across the broad plain they spread the certainty that Milan is not beaten, that she still hopes and fights.

On the morning of the fourth day, we knew that the garrisons within the ring of the Naviglio had surrendered; that Radetzky was compelled to confine himself to the outskirts of the city; that a Provisional Government had been constituted; that the insurgents, certain of victory and impatient to achieve it, had rejected the offer of an armistice; that the wind and the balloons — one of which had flown above our very Academy — had scattered through the countryside incitements to the uprising; that the cities of Lombardy and of the Veneto were in full revolt; that the Piedmontese army was already moving to lend its strength to the Italian cause; that armed bands were ranging through the countryside and making even the problem of retreat arduous for the old Marshal.

In the afternoon of that same day, a committee of citizens, preceded by a white flag, came to the College and demanded the return to their families of the Milanese cadets. Then discipline availed no longer to set a barrier to our enthusiasm. Those who depart and those who remain embrace each other as brothers, and not without tears. Brief and solemn promises were murmured which not a few of those smooth-cheeked youths were later to seal with their blood.

Not many hours passed before we were concentrated at the Monte Tabor, near Porta Romana. The order was to fall in behind the imperial army, which was filing along the bastions. Radetzky — the iron Radetzky — despite the resources of his own genius, found himself compelled to avert utter ruin by a retreat, dissembled but precipitous.

Five days had the clash of virtue against the barbaric oppressor lasted. The latter, maddened by his own unexpected impotence, massacred old men and children, buried prisoners alive; bound body to body and burned alive lovers and husbands; bloodied the Sforza castle by severing, for sport, the limbs and the breasts of the gentle and majestic Lombard women.

The Milanese people had, in conquering, inaugurated the period of history exuberant in enthusiasms and disillusionments, in victories and defeats, in tumults and silences, from which — as from a labouring womb — our Nation was pouring forth to new life, to the tremors of Liberty and of Glory!
