# Chapter Two

It was an hour of debate in the study, beneath the likenesses of Mazzini and Fortunato Calvi, who seemed to be listening to us. And I had already grown almost disposed to abandon my purpose, when, to forestall future regrets, I said with some vehemence: "But do you not understand, Major, that this is a duty to be fulfilled? The event had its historical motive and to History it belongs. Why not clarify its circumstances and its intentions? Why permit inexactitude and falsehood to overlay the truth? If you persist in silence, the last word will remain unchallenged with your political adversaries, and you would make yourself a willing accomplice in their lies. It would be a grave wrong against the martyrs whose memory you are bound to defend; against your children, to whom you owe the transmission, in all its integrity, of the honourable name received from your father."

The Major passed a hand across his brow and ground his teeth. It was his way of giving expression to powerful emotions. Suddenly he fixed his gaze upon my face and began:

I was born in Belluno on the 26th of August, 1832, the son of Count Ercole and Countess Elisabetta de Domini.

My family has old roots in that remotest corner of Italy. Angelo Biave wrote of it — and wrote, bless his soul, in Latin hexameters. A great-uncle of mine took the trouble to render them into the vernacular, thereby preparing an instrument of torture for his great-nephews. For every escapade of ours of any consequence, when punishment came it was mild enough for the times, yet terribly tedious: to commit to memory a translated passage from Biave. Papa, in such fashion, presumed to temper one anew in the glory of one's ancestors. I recall the opening still:

*"I gird myself for an arduous enterprise; I narrate of ancient personages, descended from illustrious lineage, who gave to the Peoples most just laws.*

*"In the ninth century, the Emperor Otto the Great ordained that the celebrated Nosadana family should govern Belluno and endow it with a wise order of Laws. A just reward accorded by Caesar to virtue.*

*"For a long time the Nosadani governed the city which our ancestors, with good reason, called Belluno; for there Bellona proves herself, and the citizens are valiant in arms, undaunted in the face of danger.*

*"The Nosadana family boasts personages of high renown in war and in counsel. It changed its name and was called Rudia."*

The poet then proceeds to recount the deeds of a goodly company of Nicolai, of Ercoli, of Eustachi — some of them podestàs of the native city; others celebrated for books dripping with doctrine; others again pre-eminent as advocates of the common people, or as commanders of Venetian infantry, or for grey hairs made venerable by holy works and the bishop's mitre...

But let us leave the ancestors to sleep in peace, and speak of my father; from whom I believe I inherited something more than blood and name. One anecdote to make my meaning plain:

At the beginning of 1848, when the eyes and hopes of Italian patriots converged upon Rome and popular songs echoed the praises of Mastai-Ferretti, the citizens of Belluno organised a demonstration in homage to the "liberating Pontiff." They paraded through the streets, bearing in triumph the bust of Pius IX.

My father was seated at a café table. He sees, he trembles, and planting himself boldly before the demonstrators, shouts at them: "O deluded souls, you carry in triumph the greatest enemy of our Fatherland." And without adding another word, with a powerful blow of his walking-stick, he sends the idol of the multitude flying in splinters.

Intense was the excitement that followed the momentary stupefaction, and my father owed it to his well-established popularity that he did not live out, there and then, his last quarter of an hour.

Pius IX, by disavowing the holy crusade for the independence of his Country and fornicating with the foreigner, undertook, shortly afterwards, to strengthen that popularity and to demonstrate the perspicacity of the old and proud patrician.

A man of such temper was bound to be hateful to Austria, gloomy and suspicious as she was. The fact remains that he was never granted permission to practise as a lawyer, though deeply versed in legal learning. He consoled himself with the sweetness of paternity and had fourteen children. I was the fifth-born.

They called me *il Moretto* — the swarthy one — from the colour of my skin. I was the favourite, and I do not preface this with "modesty aside," because I believe I was so more for my mischievous tricks than for my virtues. My maternal grandfather, Fortunato de Domini, Colonel of the Austrian infantry, blinded by his deep affection, believed he could discern in me the makings of something worthwhile; and in order to shield me from the influence of the liberal ideas that had infiltrated the family, he insisted on keeping me constantly by his side. First in the barracks at Belluno, an old building which had in its better days resounded with the psalmody of friars; then in the fortress of Palmanova, to which he had been transferred. The good man died before he had time to realise that liberal ideas were more nimble than his vigilance, and stronger than the walls of any fortress.

The simple and rough education he had imparted to me was afterwards continued at the Military Academy of San Luca in Milan, which was then destined for the cadets of the Kingdom of Lombardy-Venetia. And I should have remained there, together with my brother Achille, until my studies were complete, had there not suddenly arisen to sweep me away the fierce tempest which, having gathered in the North, crossed the Alps and swept through and drenched all Italy in blood.

Here, at the risk of recounting things already too well known to some, I think it opportune to summarise the Italian question as it presented itself at that eventful epoch. Memory is more frail than one is inclined to suppose among the fervent Latin race, and the recalling of the afflictions that weighed upon us will make it better understood why those who desired a United and Independent Italy neither could nor should have hesitated in the face of the greater or lesser correctness of the means.

Milan was the first centre where our wrath erupted. No plan prepared long in advance, no understanding among the citizens. The news of the insurrection in Vienna had disposed men's minds to venture something new; a yellow and black cloth displayed at an inopportune moment on her windowsill by a woman of easy virtue fanned, as though stirred by destiny, the flame that refines men and transforms them into heroes.

He who had long prepared the fuel to feed the insurrection was the aggressor himself; it was the obtuseness of the sagacious Metternich.

That grim incarnation of subtle arts already accounted himself the most powerful intellect to have appeared for centuries upon the stage of the world, even before the day when, taking advantage of the embarrassment of Napoleon the Great, he had contrived to leap at his throat and to hasten his ruin. One can well imagine how, once Napoleon had been struck down from his eminence, he had treated the peoples as a flock of sheep; why should he not have been able to do the same again?

Thus the one and the other, through excess of confidence in their own strength, fell, by different paths, into the same fatal error.

Napoleon I, by the very nature of his genius, having subjugated a People, redoubled their activity, fomented their ambition. Not for nothing does one trample upon the prejudice of birth and reserve the highest offices of State for positive merit. Son of the Revolution, he had usurped its thunderbolts; but only through it, and upon the ruins of the past, had he been able to raise himself a giant.

Wherever his eagles appeared, there blew a powerful breath of modernity; wherever his victorious armies passed, he scattered, of necessity, the ferment of new ideas that smoothed the road to the future. And moreover, his entire life was a good lesson: "one must grow strong and be bold, in order to defeat the strong."

Thus within the very yoke that he imposed lay the seed of liberty and redemption; the very good that accompanied his evil works must — strange to say! — undermine his power and hurl him into the abyss of Leipzig.

Metternich was too fine an observer not to perceive that weakness. In his titanic enterprise of driving the human flock back towards the past, he took every care to be tyrant, grim tyrant and nothing else.

In 1814, when the power of Napoleon, though declining rapidly, still appeared threatening and the aid of the peoples seemed indispensable in order to forestall surprises, Metternich and the other European diplomatists who took their cue from him made the most sweeping promises of liberty to the Italians. One had only to show oneself worthy of it by helping the Holy Alliance to shatter the bloody yoke of the French adventurer; by rallying around a banner that signified the happiness, the regeneration of their Country.

Once the Napoleonic peril had passed, the deluded, though divided in their choice of a prince, showed themselves nonetheless agreed in openly demanding their own independence.

The replies which came, at long intervals, from Metternich's lips mark the various phases through which the nefarious work of subjugation passed, and would of themselves suffice to arouse hatred in the heart of any people possessed of feeling.

— The affairs of the Italians will be decided in Vienna.

— Austria will never consent to renounce her rights of conquest.

— Italy is a geographical expression.

Unfortunately, Metternich was able to give force to his assertions not only through diplomatic intrigues and the arts of the Church, but also and above all through the armies of the Holy Alliance.

The debt of gratitude towards the peoples being treated as non-existent, there remained Austria's debt towards the princes. And perhaps, striking a line through even this, she would have appropriated half the Peninsula had there not arisen, at a fortunate moment, to play the game of the petty tyrants to some degree, the jealousies of Prussia and of Czar Alexander I.

Compelled to divide Italy in morsels in order to settle that debt, Metternich took care to create around himself a group of satellites that would of necessity gravitate towards the great centre of Vienna. A partial exception he chose to make for Murat, to whom he reserved the kingdom of Naples. The wife of Murat, Napoleon's sister, had regaled him with kisses that were worth a kingdom... but the impulsiveness of the French general, who prematurely chose to strike the pose of a champion of the Italians, deprived Metternich of the opportunity to carry out the sole luminous design of his entire political life.

The kingdom of Naples fell to Ferdinand I, who had, moreover, prepared the ground for himself by lavishing nine million francs in the antechambers of the Congress of Vienna. It was an Austrian army that accompanied him in triumph to his capital.

Rome and the Legations fell to Pius VII, the restorer of Jesuit power; the future compiler of the Bull *"Ecclesiam Jesu Christo,"* which excommunicated all those faithful who, on knowing a member of the Carboneria, did not degrade themselves so far as to denounce him, to play the spy.

The Grand Duchy of Tuscany and the Principality of Piombino fell to Ferdinand III, an Austrian archduke; the Duchy of Modena to Francesco IV, surnamed by his subjects "the executioner." Maria Luisa, daughter of the Emperor of Austria and wife of Napoleon I, had to content herself with holding sway at Parma; while the other Maria Luisa, Infanta of Spain, was placed over the Principality of Lucca.

Genoa, despite its protests and the eloquence of the Marchese Brignole, who endeavoured to invoke its long centuries of liberty and its former greatness, was incorporated into Piedmont; upon whose throne, returning from his refuge in Sardinia, sat Vittorio Emanuele I.

Austria retained under her direct dominion Lombardy, Venetia, the Tyrol, Istria and Dalmatia.

Having assured Austrian preponderance by surrounding the empire with weak States, Metternich set himself to increase its power ever further by spreading a dense network of secret treaties. And by imposing, together with the obligation to adopt reactionary measures, the further obligation of mutual aid in case of disturbances, he reserved for himself, in substance, the right to intervene with arms in the affairs of each individual State.

The sovereigns of Italy, so arrogant and cruel towards their subjects, had, merely in order to reign, accepted the role of assassins in Austria's service. Austria, having seen the direct dominion of the entire Peninsula taken from her by the jealousy of her loyal allies, had turned the difficulty and had contrived to secure an indirect dominion, yet no less substantial. And the chains that weighed upon the wrists of the august Latin Mother were none the less heavy and none the less firm for that.

By virtue of those Treaties, every Italian movement, even peaceful, designed to provoke liberal reforms, was smothered in blood through the nefarious agency of the army in white uniforms and pomaded moustaches; nor did the barbaric deluge spare even Piedmont itself, whose Kings, not lacking in courage, had nonetheless given some fine proof of manly independence.

In the great periods of history, joyous or sorrowful, there unfold incidents that epitomise them and may serve art in portraying them.

The symbol of Italy in those times was as it were embodied and set in motion in the punishment devised under the diabolical inspiration of the Bourbon of Naples. A rash word, a suspicion, the hatred of a denouncer — the more prone to calumny in that accuser and accused were never confronted — and behold an innocent man torn from his family, stripped to the waist, bound astride a donkey, and driven through the streets of the city. A company of Austrian soldiers preceded him to the sound of drum, another followed; at his side was the gaoler whirling the whip armed with nails, and at every step there were blows that penetrated the flesh, that tore it away in shreds. The torture was suspended when the wretch, scarlet with blood, dropped his pallid face and seemed on the point of breathing his last... Suspended, but to greater torment. It recommenced the moment a groan, a convulsive movement of the muscles betrayed the reawakening of sensation. The passers-by did not react; they covered their faces and fled in horror... At the end of the long Calvary, the gallows! It was the suffering of Christ daily renewed by the agency of most Christian Kings allied, under the auspices of the Pontiff, in holy alliance!

More refined, but no less inexorable, was the oppression weighing upon those Italians subject to the direct dominion of the Empire. The air, steeped in lies, began to envelope tender childhood, which was taught, as the highest duty, as a religious obligation, servile submission to the tyrant; it penetrated the lungs of the university student, which institution was required to furnish the State not so much with learned men as with faithful subjects; it hung, subtle and corrosive, over every phase of social life. The beloved subjects of His Majesty were free to move from house to house, from place to place; to attend to their affairs upon whose profits the Exchequer extended its rapacious claws... slaves in all else; men resigned or seething with wrath... citizens never!

Nor for this reason did the repression of refractory spirits proceed without due respect for the forms prescribed by strict legality — an easy task, where Laws are promulgated by despotism without the collaboration and assent of the governed. And of generous Italians, lawfully stricken, the fortress of the Spielberg in Moravia was populated — a place which, for the stifled groans within its walls, for the noble intellects that were withered and extinguished there, for the crimes against humanity that it shrouded in the darkness of its dungeons, will rise up terrible in the memory of men even when the last of the Habsburgs shall have been for centuries confounded with the dust.

To furnish that Austrian Bastille — and the pillory and flagellation which had been restored — with innocent flesh, there served admirably the flood of spies, high and low, that inundated the Kingdom. Bearing no distinguishing marks; concealed beneath plausible occupations, they insinuated themselves into every family, drew up reports on the tendencies, the habits, the conversations of every individual, even on the style of hat preferred; they violated postal secrecy; they wove plots; they besmirched, or attempted to besmirch, the reputation of the most notable and authoritative men, whose word might have incited minds to the virile vindication of rights.

The population of Lombardy-Venetia, gaunt and eroded by a monstrous fiscality monstrously applied, was required to provide not only for the expenses of the local Administration, but to replenish abundantly the imperial coffers as well. So true it is that, scrape the surface of the tyrant, and the brigand comes to light!

And the brigand cast aside his refined and serpentine methods in order to resort, like the other oppressors of Italy, to those barbarically ferocious, the moment the Milanese, refusing to smoke and to wear cloth manufactured by the foreigner, ventured to injure him in his purse. The doors of the prisons were flung open and the dregs of society, mingled with the constables, swept through the streets of the metropolis, smoking and compelling others to smoke. The bold ones who refused to set between their lips the leaf steeped in German spittle were mutilated or run through with steel; neither sex nor age afforded protection.

Martial law became ordinary law; treason, punishable with harsh penalties, to wear cloth of certain colours, to sing certain songs, to applaud certain phrases in this or that play; to wear a beard or hair cut in a particular fashion; to lend oneself to any act which the Authorities, at their own absolute discretion, might deem a demonstration of a subversive character. "Three days of slaughter ensure thirty years of peace," said old Marshal Radetzky. And the pretext for perpetrating slaughter was sought with the pertinacity of one who pushes the craving for dominion to the point of lust; of one who knows no other inspiration save the sinister interests of the dynasty.

Such were the delights that the trim and smiling Metternich dispensed, with a more or less liberal hand, to a great part of Europe. And the Peoples escaped him by virtue inherent in the very arts that kept them enslaved. For those arts necessarily aroused hatred, which is a regenerating energy that drives men towards the luminous affirmations of Right as powerfully as, and more powerfully than, love.

And so intensely did this energy stir in the hearts of the Milanese that they found themselves on the 18th of March 1848 united as one in a supreme resolve; without distinction of class, without distinction of sex, without distinction of age... to kill or to be killed; to conquer or to remain buried beneath the ruins of their own city.

No exhortations were needed, no password, no leaders. The hundred and sixty thousand inhabitants of the city became a terrible unity... the PEOPLE; just as the billions of grains of gunpowder constitute, in their explosion, the terrible unity of the magazine.

The weapons?

They were drawn from underground, from the innards of walls, from the hiding-places where the spirited, yearning for the day of vengeance, had secreted them... old implements of every kind, rusted; to which the armourers, emptying their shops, soon added a supply of hunting rifles. Then came the clubs, the broadswords, the halberds once gripped by mercenary soldiers; the great two-handed swords that had perhaps broken the impetus of Barbarossa in the glorious days of the Lombard League... the past emerging from the half-shadow of silent Museums, hurling itself into the vortex of the uprising, bringing its contribution to the redemption of a People.

That formidable tumult of surging passions did not shatter against the walls of the Military Academy of San Luca; instead, penetrating within, it aroused in our young souls a fever that, though suppressed, lost nothing of its intensity.

On the first day of the insurrection, the Officers spoke to us contemptuously of a handful of brigands whom the Austrian force and the plain common sense of the "respectable" population had soon enough brought to heel. Then... a hard face and silence. But that silence, concentrated, was worth the eloquence of many volumes.

The energy that stirred the living and made them greater than themselves had passed suddenly into things. In little more than a night, more than a thousand barricades arose and blocked the narrow streets of the city. The pavement had yielded its stones; from the palaces came crashing down the artistic furnishings; the homes of the working people furnished their household goods, their mattresses, their pillows. Hallowed by the feverish hand of a people that willed it, even the humblest utensils had been transformed into a bulwark of liberty... Shapeless bulwarks; their disorder revealed the fury of men's souls.

The fighter who, pale, clutching his carbine, awaits his post, recovers emotions almost refined to their purest essence. It was among that furniture that fathers, while chastising him, had taught him to whisper secretly the name of Italy. And now that name he would shout at the appearance of the enemy as the supreme provocation, as defiance unto death; now he would demonstrate that the ancient valour may lie dormant in Italian fibre, but can never be extinguished. Victor or vanquished, he would have accomplished a work not devoid of greatness.

The assault came, and at a hundred scattered points simultaneously. But the Austrian found everywhere men whom desperation had taught the art of defence. The barricades creak and change form beneath the fury of attackers and attacked; they are drenched in the blood of both... blood... but they hurl back the curses of the barbarian who falls, the battle-cry of the citizen who triumphs.

While the patriots fight in the front ranks, the mothers, the sisters, the children share the care of the wounded and prepare new means of assault. Cobblestones and tiles, heaped upon the rooftops, are transformed into deadly projectiles; the web of barricades extends and presses close about the barracks, the isolated garrisons. Besieged by the oppressor, the Milanese become in their turn, in part, besiegers. And beneath the leaden sky, more insistent, more resonant than the battle itself, spread the tolling of the bells sounded in alarm. Across the broad plain they diffuse the certainty that Milan is not conquered, that she still hopes and fights.

On the morning of the fourth day, we knew that the garrisons within the circle of the Naviglio had surrendered; that Radetzky was compelled to confine himself to the outskirts of the city; that a Provisional Government had been constituted; that the insurgents, certain of victory and impatient to achieve it, had rejected the offer of an armistice; that the wind and the balloons — one of which had flown above our very Academy — had scattered across the countryside incitements to the uprising; that the cities of Lombardy and Venetia were in full revolt; that the Piedmontese army was already in motion to lend its strength to the Italian cause; that armed bands were sweeping through the countryside and rendering even the problem of retreat arduous for the old marshal.

In the afternoon of that same day, a committee of citizens, preceded by a white flag, came to the College and demanded the return to their families of the Milanese cadets. Then discipline availed no longer to restrain our enthusiasm. Those who departed and those who remained embraced one another as brothers, and not without tears. Brief and solemn promises were murmured which not a few among those beardless youths were afterwards to seal with their blood.

Not many hours had passed before we were concentrated at Monte Tabor, near the Porta Romana. The order was to fall in behind the imperial army as it filed along the ramparts. Radetzky, the iron Radetzky, despite all the resources of his ingenuity, found himself compelled to avert utter ruin by a retreat — dissembled, but headlong.

Five days had the clash of virtue against the barbaric oppressor endured. He, maddened by his own unexpected impotence, slaughtered the old and the young, buried prisoners alive, bound couples together and burned them living, lovers and spouses; he bloodied the Castello degli Sforza, hacking off for sport the limbs and the breasts of the gentle and stately women of Lombardy.

The Milanese people, by conquering, had set in motion the period of history overflowing with enthusiasms and disillusions, with victories and defeats, with tumults and silences, from which, as from a travailing womb, our Nation was born to new life, to the tremors of Liberty and of Glory!
