# Chapter the Seventeenth

AT LOCARNO, whither I had returned to convey to Calvi a missive from Mazzini, I was arrested and released a few hours later, at liberty, on my word to present myself to the Federal Delegate. I was subsequently tried for conspiracy against a friendly Power, expelled from Switzerland, and presented with the alternative of choosing between America and England.

England was nearer to the field of probable future enterprises, and I chose it. I settled in London, where I found not a few men compromised in the events of February, who had escaped Austrian vengeance by means of adventurous flights — among them, a Milanese Jean Valjean who had been carried out beyond the city walls in a coffin, with all the solemnity of a funeral.

Bound together by a common ideal and common sufferings, we lived for a time in community, in the quarter known as Baldwin's Garden, and precisely in the modest inn of the Milanese Tressoldi, a good-natured soul who possessed, among his other virtues, that of turning a blind eye when we could not settle our accounts.

Giuseppe Mazzini arrived in London in April. From him we learned that Pianciani, who was then dictating his *Storia dei Papi*, tempered the labours of the intellect by altering and re-altering the garden of his villa — a probable source of employment for several of us. We were introduced to his secretary, one Bardini, a Paduan law student, and he, in his turn, presented us to Pianciani, who there and then entrusted the manual work of those incessant modifications to young Varisco, granting him full authority to avail himself of the services of as many among us as he deemed necessary. I too was promptly engaged, and thus derived my sustenance for some months from the spade and the flowers.

That simple and laborious life was troubled by terrible images. Austria raged with ever greater fury, and every newspaper, every letter brought us the sorrowful certainty that the phalanx of the generous was being inexorably thinned.

I spoke of it to Giuseppe Mazzini during our long walks, and fixed my gaze upon that olive-hued, intellectual face, as if to say: *si decide — si decide?* — is it decided, will it be decided? Shall we let them be butchered like dogs? The Master quickened his step. His expression was one of ineffable melancholy; but of the thoughts that whirled within his mind — not a syllable!

One day in August he sent for me with urgency. Why, one might ask? Certainly not to bid me good morning or good evening. What, then?

I drove my spade into the middle of a flower-bed: "Stay there! You are not the only noble instrument!" And away I ran.

But when I stood before the Master, my arms fell to my sides, so sparse was his welcome in words. I was asked to carry a letter to Kossuth and to wait for the reply. Not one syllable more!

Kossuth held at that time, near Regent's Circus, a species of Court. At the door I found carriages and liveried servants; on the staircase a coming and going of measured Englishmen in evening dress; Hungarians in the national costume, elegant and martial; men bearing broad scars; uncertain figures, soliciting under the pretext of the Fatherland. Honest and dishonest alike, rich or poor, with difficulty obtained access to the Dictator who, even beaten and in exile, exercised, beyond the prestige of fame, that other prestige which comes from the habit of command.

While waiting in the antechamber for his leave, I endeavoured to beguile the time by observing the pictures that hung from the walls — Kossuth in the guise of the plain citizen; Kossuth in hunting dress; Kossuth in the uniform of the general — now in an armchair, now on horseback, now beside a shattered cannon; but always Kossuth, nothing but Kossuth!

At last, amid so many likenesses, the great man appeared in flesh and blood. I must confess that art, in its endeavour to idealise him, had slandered him. The figure of the illustrious Magyar recalled, more than conventional heroism and carnage, paternal affections and the mild emotions of the domestic hearth. It is the privilege of artists to alter, at their caprice, the tints of History — for this reason Napoleon I has been handed down to posterity crossing the Saint Bernard on a prancing colt, with manifest injustice to the poor donkey that lent its back to so great a weight.

The reception reserved for me was not such as the Count of Rudio, the Italian patriot, and the trusted emissary of Giuseppe Mazzini had any right to expect. Kossuth read the letter I proffered while standing erect upon the threshold, and, having tossed it aside, drew a memorandum from his pocket, wrote a few lines, and commanded me, curtly, to deliver them to their destination. Not a word of greeting! — and I, descending the stairs, asked myself whether the Cossacks of the Czar and the Croats of Jellačič, by lending their aid to Austria, had not, in substance, spared the brave Hungarians the inconveniences inherent in a change of dynasty.

The few lines consigned to me by Kossuth were addressed to a high-ranking officer whose name I am now searching for in the recesses of memory. I received from him several rolls of sterling — "The same," he told me, "that we received from Mazzini for our participation in the movements of 1853. May they serve your great, unfortunate country!"

Kossuth, on the morrow of that failure, had publicly denied all complicity in those very movements!

On my return, the Master opened his heart. He began by asking whether I felt disposed to hazard my life once more for the cause of the Fatherland, and having received the answer from my bearing alone, he pointed to a chair and we reasoned quietly, face to face, for upwards of an hour.

He explained to me that, on the advice of Fortunato Calvi, future insurrectionary attempts would be conducted in the Alpine regions. The reasons he advanced were the following: Our country is rich in latent forces, ready to burst forth; but he who undertakes an initiative must know how to give assurance of serious resistance. The mountain is the natural bulwark of the weak and the oppressed. There it is possible for the few to hold their ground against the many; possible to evade pursuit; to swoop with the swiftness of an eagle upon the plain; to surprise and to intercept. In place of pitched battle, which was denied to us, we must substitute guerrilla warfare, which is of necessity the daughter of the mountains. If the banner of redemption could be kept upright and flying for three, for two months, there would not be a patriot without the fever, not a corner of Italy where action did not blaze forth.

This last thought appeared to draw him into another train of reflection. A shadow spread itself, as it were, across his face; then he spoke, in words nearly these: "I know it! Cowardice, masked as prudence, accuses me of driving ardent youth to the scaffold, of provoking desperate attempts that offer Austrian tyranny, and our own native tyranny, the opportunity to redouble their cruelties. But no one reflects that the audacity and the sacrifice of the few always end by shaking the torpid and dragging the many into action. Nothing great without audacity! Napoleon III would in all probability never have smoothed his way to the Empire without the failures of Strasbourg and Boulogne. No King of Italy will show spirit great enough to champion the redemption of our country, if Italians show that they are content to bask, in peace, in their slavery. Would Carlo Alberto have crossed the Ticino, had it not been for the magnanimous self-abnegation of the Milanese? This keeping open, this pressing forward of the great Italian question is an imperative necessity. Europe cannot have peace so long as our agony endures. She will intervene, sooner or later, in our favour, and the sooner the more we show ourselves intolerant of the yoke and threatening. And what are good ideas, what are vigorous resolves, worth, when the want of initiative confines them to the realm of mere abstractions? Action cleanses our centuries of guilt. Between us and a free Italy lies this baptism of blood. Ought we to remain on this side of it, for the slanders of cowards?"

That same evening I procured a passport in an assumed name and set out for Switzerland. Fortunato Calvi was already awaiting me in Zurich. "The likelihood of the new movements rests with him," Mazzini had told me. "The execution falls to him to direct. From his own lips you will receive the necessary instructions."

At Brussels, where I made a brief halt, I delivered a letter from the Master to — the Fatherland is not redeemed by spontaneous process; nor is Liberty watered like the cabbages of a kitchen-garden! — Ungherrischio, the Venetian Catullus. "What is the Master doing?" he asked me, almost feverishly. And I, with his own verses:

*"Mazzini is in every place where men tremble*
*That the hour supreme may reach the traitor's ear;*
*Mazzini is in every place where men hope*
*To shed their blood for Italy entire!"*

Arrived at Zurich, before going to Calvi I wished to have my hands free, and hastened to call upon de Boni, to whom also I delivered a message; a third I carried to the Comasco Caronti. Giuseppe Mazzini had warned me to be very much on my guard with this man, of whom he was greatly suspicious. He did, however, contribute sums of considerable size to the good cause, and this held Mazzini back from dispensing with him.

The brazenly inquisitive questions that Caronti put to me came to me, already ill-disposed, like a sharp irritant, and when I set foot once more beyond the threshold of his villa, I felt a relief, as if I had suddenly freed myself from a nightmare.

At last I could restore myself in the embrace of an authentic patriot, a conscience without stain — Fortunato Calvi! What joy to see him again, still handsome, still proud, still exhaling that indefinable sense of serenity which breathes from honest souls!

The substance of our conversation had a beginning which I shall relate without false modesty.

"You are not," he said to me, "and must not be, a mere instrument to execute blindly orders you do not understand. I shall confide to you the plan I have devised, and which the European Revolutionary Committee has fully approved. You will thus know how to parry, with unity of purpose, the inevitable reverberations. If something concrete is achieved, you will be my aide-de-camp; if nothing is achieved — we shall in all likelihood be levelled by the same recompense — martyrdom!"

My particular preliminary mission was to make my way into the valley of the Trevis and to meet with certain smugglers, known by the sobriquet of the Burrasca brothers, in order to induce them to carry into the Agordino a large consignment of munitions. I was then to cross the border, enter Belluno clandestinely, and make contact with Don Bastiano Barozzi, who had returned there and was awaiting me. Barozzi had knowledge of certain weapons concealed in 1848, upon the return of the oppressors. In the Cadore there were abundant rebel spirits who had covered themselves in glory in its defence; the name and the exhortation of Fortunato Calvi, who had been the good genius of that defence, would have rekindled in their breasts the slumbering enthusiasm. It was necessary to distribute among them the old weapons. A band of exiles awaited the signal to descend from the Alps — Fortunato Calvi, leading them, would direct their fortunes.

The Austrian colossus was to be struck precisely at Belluno, and by a triple surprise: at the Revenue Office, at the Gendarmerie, and at the barracks of the regular troops.

The Revenue Office, situated near the old church of Santo Stefano, a solid building erected to serve as a convent, encloses a vast colonnaded courtyard. Between one column and the next stretches a parapet of such height that a man, walking close against it on all fours, cannot be seen by a sentinel who, however vigilantly, is patrolling other parts of the colonnade. The gate had no doors and was left unguarded. As the building's sole custody, a meagre body of militia, posted at the far end of the courtyard, to the right — three men in all and a corporal.

In the dead of night, a handful of our men lying in ambush by the gate; one bold figure creeps, barefoot, along the parapet, surprises the sentinel from behind — a muffled signal — men rush forward! — the three sleeping militiamen are rendered helpless. A bold enterprise, not a difficult one!

It was autumn, the season for the collection of taxes; the coffers of the Administration, by reliable reckoning, were expected to contain a million and a half florins. To seize them — the sinews of war!

Simultaneously, another band of patriots was to conceal itself near the Gendarmerie barracks. They would simulate a brawl and ring the bell of the Gendarmerie as if to seek assistance. The guard who opens the door must be cut down. They were to burst into the den, and the thirty men-of-arms sleeping within would pass, perforce, from the category of those who put on handcuffs to that of those on whom handcuffs are placed.

The garrison of Belluno at that time consisted of a hundred and fifty soldiers. Access to the barracks was gained, to the right and to the left, by a winding circular road — a *tourniquin*, as the engineers call it, flanked by trees and benches, a favourite resort of nursemaids and urchins. As a general rule, when the alarm sounded, the small garrison would pour out, in dribs and drabs, through the gate and form up on the esplanade awaiting orders. It was necessary to post a body of picked marksmen along the edges of the winding road, sound the alarm, and shoot down the militiamen as they came rushing out.

Once the triple surprise was accomplished, the insurgents would seize the civil and ecclesiastical authorities — the hostages! Then we would commandeer the means of transport, put on Austrian uniforms, and advance in military order as far as the fortress of Osoppo, in Friuli, taking prisoners and compelling all those we encountered along the way to follow us, so as to forestall any indiscretion.

At this point Fortunato Calvi, who had throughout been speaking in a low voice, as if meditating upon his own words, grew animated and said with passion: "We approach the fortress preceded by the Austrian flag and with drums beating, as friends. Once the bridge is crossed and a foot set under the archway, the scene changes, the Latin valour bursts forth. On the bastions, which will have become ours, we raise our own banner, and woe to whoever assails it! The hostages remain with us, with us the arms, the money, and the audacity. Osoppo becomes our base of operations and is an impregnable instrument of war. Through the winter we organise the guerrilla campaign on the Alps, we hold in check the troops that would seek to descend into the Peninsula. Come spring, Lombardy, Venetia, Hungary, France perhaps also, lend their support to the movement — the oppressed and the oppressors measure their forces one more time. If the errors of the past serve as our schooling, the secular vow is fulfilled, Italy rises, never to fall again."

I listened with head bowed, thinking what any other man would have thought: "To what extent could we count upon the ability of the European Revolutionary Committee to extend our initiative, to transform it into an international rising?"

Fortunato Calvi must have understood, and he said to me: "I suppose that the chances of success are twenty, and eighty those of dying — either with a bullet in the forehead, or at the hands of the executioner. The Fatherland is not redeemed by spontaneous process; nor is Liberty watered like the cabbages of a kitchen-garden. I take the risk! — either triumph, or death!"

Could I draw back when an entity like Fortunato Calvi was flinging himself wholly forward?

Three days, and I set out again on my journey.
