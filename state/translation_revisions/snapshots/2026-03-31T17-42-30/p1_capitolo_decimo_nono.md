# Chapter the Nineteenth

AMONG all things capable of stirring the noble rebel impulses that reach the heart of a son, nothing surpasses the sobs of his mother. Those of mine, in the silence of the night, resounded with terrible force.

I hastened to support her with one arm and placed my right hand upon her lips as if to stifle them. *Una ronda* — a patrol: that was what I meant to say, but no voice came, and, overcome by a surge of tenderness, I too began to sob.

The first to utter any coherent syllable was my brother Giustiniano, who had remained to one side, almost unnoticed. He said: word had come of new arrests. Our friends, the best of them, had been seized without impunity — Don Bastiano too had been arrested in the same fashion, and poor father had been left behind in chains.

And continuing, he recounted for me the particulars of the catastrophe. Don Bastiano had serenely extended his wrists for the manacles, "glad to contribute, through his own sacrifice, to completing the series of sufferings demanded by Providence for the redemption of his Country." But father had wanted to break his bamboo cane across the shoulders of the ruffians, and it was only with considerable effort that the caresses of his family had been able to calm him.

I reflected for a moment, and out of that reflection came light: the important thing was to bring mother home safely. We would think of the rest afterwards.

But the poor woman, shaken beyond measure by the terrible ordeal, rendered still more helpless by the darkness, stumbled at every step. I handed one of my pistols to Giustiniano and whispered in his ear: "Go ahead of us. Think of whom you are defending."

The di Rudios are not to have their toes trodden upon with impunity — he was satisfied with that.

I bent down, and before mother had time to realise what was happening or to resist, I had lifted her into my arms and walked on, proud to carry the one who had once carried me in her womb, and with how much greater a travail.

At the first uncertain light of a street lantern, I set the sainted woman down. I overcame my own resolve with difficulty, pressing upon her brow a long kiss. I sensed already that what lay ahead would bring grave misfortune — two women left to themselves, and such women! "For my own part, I do not intend to leave the vicinity of Belluno until I have looked well into the heart of these troubles." And I promised her that, however adverse fortune might prove, I would always conduct myself in such a manner that, when we met again, or when she thought of her love for me, her conscience would not reproach her as unworthy of it.

At the place where we had paused to rest, I spoke to Giustiniano in a sharp voice. "The situation admits of no delay. I choose as my refuge the house of Domenico. That man is made of the right stuff and will not betray us. The road offered me excellent cover. I told Giustiniano that no one expected me where I was going. If he discovered anything new, he was to inform me. My intentions? He would learn them upon his return."

I did not wait long. Mother and Giustiniano had reached home without encountering a living soul. My sister Luisa had prepared her a good cup of coffee; the poor woman showed herself, if not resigned, at least calm.

I left them below, in the oppressive darkness, and found once more that crisp and pure air, the glittering sky — and what became of all those anxieties that had troubled me! I had set down no burden along the ascent; yet the weariness had suddenly vanished. I climbed with great strides, and at every step the serenity of the night sank deep into my thoughts; my spirit came at last to mirror the picturesque majesty of the mountain.

And Giustiniano's voice beside me spoke again: "The di Rudios are not to have their toes trodden upon with impunity — that is your maxim; but it is mine as well! Let us reckon up the sum: poor Achille has left his bones at Venezia; you are undoubtedly sought by the Police, hunted like a malefactor through the countryside."

The quiet of the night penetrated deep into my thoughts; my spirit ended by reflecting the picturesque majesty of the mountain.

What I am about to narrate I have drawn from letters written to me by Giustiniano. I have them impressed upon my memory in indelible characters.

In seeking to reassure mother, Giustiniano sketched the situation for her, tempering it with a generous measure of optimism. Luisa would undergo some questioning, but would soon be set free. Father was marked as an old patriot, an irreconcilable man, and would not manage to extricate himself so easily — but how to condemn him without evidence? The judicial authorities were rigorous beyond all telling; yet they were anxious to appear irreproachable, and would not dare commit an open infamy. As for himself, he had personally accompanied me as far as the mines at Agordo. From that point, I had taken paths known only to me and to the chamois. By now I must already have crossed the frontier, and the Police still believed me to be in Belluno and were searching for me there. Before long, letters would be received from Switzerland, or from some other country over which the yellow and black did not fly. All that remained, then, was not to give way to despair, so as to preserve one's health — so necessary in that storm.

But the confidence he laboured to instil in his mother's heart was far from dwelling in his own. The newspapers, subject to rigorous censorship, seemed to concur in treating the latest conspiracy with a conspiracy of silence. Yet news finds its way a great distance even without the aid of newspapers. Now from the lips of trusted friends encountered along the road, now at the café, gathered around a table, between one remark about the fine weather and another about the price of agricultural produce, there slipped into his ear those words which could not be openly spoken without danger — and from them, as always in such cases, the hopeful reassurances appeared barely sketched in, whilst the sinister predictions were laid on in heavy strokes.

One evening, those predictions seemed to find in the facts a tragic fulfilment. "Among the gorges of the Alps, a patrol had barred the path of the fleeing patriot — the order to surrender disregarded — the noble heart broken by Austrian lead — the series of generous impulses severed for ever. That very night, the body would arrive in Belluno for the formalities required by Law."

Giustiniano hastened home to prevent the sad news from reaching his mother through incautious lips. He found her — he would have had time enough, and more, to weep. He walked the length and breadth of the room until very late; then, overcome by weariness, he sank into an armchair and fell asleep.

Through the half-opened shutters the first light of dawn barely peered, and already mother, impatient for news, was bringing him his usual cup of coffee. She looked at him, and guessing at the sleepless, anguished night, instead of waking him she went to the untouched bed, drew from it a coverlet and spread it over his knees and chest. At that very moment, the doorbell rang with urgent force.

Giustiniano started awake, looked about him, remembered, understood. He threw his arms about the poor woman's neck and said to her:

---

She was brought to a large, cold room. From the opposite wall there jutted a plank, one end of which rested upon a trestle; upon the plank lay a sheet; beneath the sheet, with sinister uncertainty, the outlines of a man in the rigidity of death could be made out. A soldier, his rifle at the order, stood stiffly beside it. Another, in a simple waistcoat, his sleeves rolled up, his left hand plunged into a large boot upon which his eyes were fixed intently, was working his brush with savage energy and splattering it. Four officers, arranged in a circle, were conferring in low voices. A burning brazier adulterated, with its fitful glow, the clear light of morning.

At the entrance of the mother, the officers broke off their conversation and turned their faces toward that austere apparition. She appeared not to hear; her whole spirit was concentrated in her eyes, which blazed beneath the black veil.

Then a single sob rang out in the sepulchral silence — one only — but it was enough to shake a heart.

And with a voice in which vibrated the muted threat of an entire people, she said: "No — this poor wretch is not my son."

I was obliged to exert myself to calm that youthful fever, and, insisting upon the impropriety of abandoning two women — and what women! — to themselves in moments of grave calamity, I made my case. I prevailed with difficulty. "Besides, I do not intend to leave the vicinity of Belluno until I have looked well into the heart of these troubles. And where find a more trustworthy person than yourself to serve me as informant and, should occasion require, as intermediary?"

In the bewilderment of his young triumph, he rose. She walked toward the door. The soldier in the waistcoat blew a warm breath on his boot and laid on the brush with strokes more furious than ever — he was wishing, perhaps, in that moment, that the barracks and the Empire were concentrated in that muddy toecap. The stiff soldier beside the corpse dared not draw breath. The situation, like a uniform awaiting a command, admitted of no delay. "I choose Domenico's house as my refuge. That peasant is made of the right stuff and will not betray us. I shall await you there tomorrow. Come by a wide circuit and make sure no one follows you. I shall judge by your shrewdness whether you are worthy of greater enterprises."

And having pressed his hand, I turned my back — and they had been borne in thought, perhaps, to some far country beyond the Alps, where other mothers suffered.

---

I left below the dark plain, the air thick and heavy; I found again that crisp and pure air, the glittering sky — and what became of all those anxieties that had troubled me! I had set down no burden along the ascent; yet the weariness had suddenly vanished. I climbed with great strides, and at every step the serenity of the night penetrated more deeply into my thoughts; my spirit ended by reflecting the picturesque majesty of the mountain.

I recognised Domenico's small cottage, backed against the cliff, the dark doorway beneath the pergola, now stripped of its clusters and leaves — the pergola that stretched as far as the road and divided the little kitchen garden's clearing into equal parts. From the foot of the old walnut tree, whose trembling boughs tickled the roof of the cottage, two hounds launched themselves barking; but they soon abated their impetuosity upon hearing themselves called by name. They followed in my footsteps, stretching out their muzzles and anxiously nudging my calves.

One, two, three knocks at measured intervals — sleep clings tenaciously to mountain labourers. A fourth, a fifth reverberated through the void. At last, through the cracks of the old door, a light filtered through; two wooden clogs advanced noisily.

"Open up, Domenico. It is Carletto."

The door creaked, and I was left as though dazzled by the lantern which, with a sudden movement, was raised from the ground and threw a beam of light into my face.

"Carletto! — Taller, fiercer, darker, with the goatee — but Carletto! And I had reckoned him at the ends of the earth! Our elders were right: only the mountains stay in their place. But what brings you here? At this hour! Have you lost your way on the path?"

I did not reply, but pointing to the right hand of the good old man, which gripped a billhook: "Were you meaning to cut off my head?"

In the kitchen with its smoke-blackened walls, beneath the wide chimney-hood, a fire crackled briskly — enough to roast a kid. We sat one facing the other, in the lateral recesses hollowed into the thick wall. And while I made clear to him the situation, he tormented the embers with the nozzle of the bellows-pipe. When he heard that father was in chains, he drove that iron implement with a formidable thrust into the log, which showered sparks. From the clenching of his jaws and from the fury in his eyes, I would have said that the blow was intentionally aimed at the head of some villain. He said to me:

"You did well to come up here. From our clearing, the valley can be commanded for more than five miles. They have barely set foot outside Belluno before we know already where they mean to cast their net. With five miles in hand, there is no candle-eating bureaucrat who can outrun an Italian's legs — not to mention that the mountain is thick with our people on every side. And should the devil help them and they came upon us unawares — well, there is a remedy for that too! Do you recall the old cave, where I used to drive you as punishment when you ruined my almond trees? A single leap from the house; the entrance narrow and so well hidden that you would not notice it even if you knocked your nose against it. In a desperate case, one man alone with an old musket, a rusty old thing, could ruin the stomach of half Bohemia there. There is no denying it — you showed sound sense in taking refuge up here."

He paused a moment as if suspended in thought, then added: "Here I am chattering, and you need rest. I shall go and rouse the old woman. Half an hour more or less — what does it matter? She must prepare the mash for the cow, which is near her time anyway. For tonight you must content yourself with sleeping in our own bed — we will see about making up something a little more fitting afterwards."

And as I protested that I was hardened to far greater fatigues, and that I preferred to keep watch beside the fire, he assumed the air of one who has been touched on the raw, and said by way of excuse: "In our own bed — but have no fear! — We shall put freshly laundered sheets on it!"

That same day, in the afternoon, Giustiniano arrived up there. He had left the city on the pretext of going after chaffinches and goldfinches; he carried with him the decoy owl, the perch and the bird-lime. Nothing suspicious in his long circuit; but the news he brought was very grave. A police raid, a full-scale search; mother in terrible agitation — the more so because Luisa had kept a highly compromising letter. Fortunato Calvi had a sincere admiration for Luisa, a friendship that led him to confide in her, as in a sister, his most intimate thoughts. In those days he had sent his instructions to father, addressing them, in order the better to deceive the Police, precisely to Luisa. They were written in veiled language, but not so veiled that they would not have aroused the suspicions of anyone who already had an inkling of our plot. Moreover, yielding to the impulses of his heart, Calvi, in a brief postscript, had expressed the hope that, "the Fatherland once freed, nothing more would stand between him and his union with Teresa Duodo," a gentle young lady from Padova who adored him and whom he adored.

Fortunately my sister, seizing her opportunity, had swiftly thrown the letter into father's guitar — a great plucker of strings was father! And the searchers, who had ransacked every corner of the house and every crack, had paid no heed to that humble instrument hanging on the wall.

They were already preparing to leave, the two women were already beginning to breathe freely, when the Inspector, who had undoubtedly received precise orders, declared Luisa under arrest. A long embrace — then mother had fallen back in her armchair, seized by a terrible nervous crisis.

On hearing that news, it seemed to me that the heavy air of the plain had risen to the mountain and was suffocating me. What purpose did it serve to linger any longer in the Cadore? I was held back by a chivalrous feeling which made me regard as cowardice the seeking of my own safety while my loved ones were persecuted and exposed to every danger; I was held back also by the hope, however vague and fleeting, that Fortunato Calvi and Giuseppe Mazzini, learning of the ill turn taken by their plans, would set about righting them and would have need of me. But events, tumbling forward, were soon to sweep away even those scruples.

One morning I was walking a short distance from the farmhouse and, by instinct, studying the terrain with a tactical eye, when, rounding a crag, I came face to face with an Austrian officer. I do not believe he noticed my agitation, for he greeted me in a manner of irreproachable courtesy and told me he had set out from Belluno with other officers before sunrise. He had strayed from his companions in pursuit of a covey of partridges. He asked me, in conclusion, whether I had seen his hunting companions pass through those parts.

Having received a negative reply, he offered me a cigar and then, with a certain wonder: "Do you live in these solitary places? One needs real spirit not to grow bored here!" Before I could reply, some rifle shots echoed from crag to crag. He turned his thumb over and said: "Those are my friends!" He saluted and moved off with long strides.

A few hours later Giustiniano arrived up there. I understood, from the mere sight of him, that some other unhappy news was pressing upon his heart. He told me that, early that morning, a clerk of the Police — in whom the trade had not paralysed every honestly sensitive fibre — had knocked at our door. He had confided to mother that positive word had reached his office that I was to be found in Belluno, or in its surroundings, for purposes that could not be welcome to the Royal Imperial authorities. These had issued a peremptory order to seize me, dead or alive. We should govern ourselves accordingly, and with promptness. He had done his duty as a good Italian — he trusted that, once he had left the premises, we should know how to do ours, maintaining, even under oath, that we had never so much as laid eyes on him.

In my mind, the chance encounter with the Austrian officer and what Giustiniano had told me linked themselves together of their own accord. If the Police want me dead or alive, they will have circulated my description to their direct and indirect agents. And if the officer, explaining his absence to his fellow soldiers, has spoken of me —

The thread was slender; but it would have sufficed to weave a noose — mine.

I turned to Giustiniano: "Assure mother that tonight I shall not sleep in Domenico's house. Tomorrow, at first light, I shall be far away."

The sun had barely set when I was already seeking refuge in the ancient cave, past which I had never walked as a child without a sense of profound apprehension. I entered it cautiously, without a light; then I replaced the ivy that concealed its mouth. From that hiding place, peering through the foliage, I found myself looking directly at Domenico's kitchen window — without bars and without shutters — which lay spread before my eyes as clearly as though I were inside. Domenico, at that moment, was slicing some cheese — he was preparing my provisions for the next day's journey. Caterina was sitting in a recess by the hearth, telling her rosary beads.

Suddenly the dogs barked. This was not unusual. At evening, foxes roam the mountain, and the hounds, scenting them from a distance, keep them at bay with incessant circling. But this time, as their barking grew gradually more furious, it gave way to yelping, and there was a circumstance extremely suspicious: instead of running through the fields, they were retreating toward the house. They had before them, therefore, an adversary capable of commanding respect.

I strained my ear and was not long in detecting a measured tread, a dry clash of metal; then — between my eyes and the lighted window, several shadows filed past on tiptoe. Plumes and carbines erased from my mind even the last traces of doubt — the ruffians of Austria had surrounded the house.

Inside, the two honest souls appeared entirely unaware of the storm that had gathered around the old homestead. All at once, the scoundrels brought their rifle butts down upon the frail door, which flew open with a tremendous crash. I saw Domenico spring back, and instinctively his right hand, armed with the large knife, drew back to gather the impetus needed to strike. But four levelled muskets dispelled, with the terror of reality, the courage that imagination had put into him.

A lanky fellow, his moustaches twirled to a point and a bureaucrat's snout upon his face, made a gesture, and two gendarmes hurled themselves upon that bewildered man, manacled him, and tied him to a leg of the table.

No one had spoken. The lanky one snatched the dark lantern from a soldier, cast its light about, and catching sight of Caterina who, having let her rosary fall at her feet, had remained as if paralysed, said to her: "Get up and come with me." She did not stir from her rigidity and appeared not to understand, until the lanky one, grasping her by a shoulder, shook her, forced her to her feet, and dealt her a powerful blow with his fist between the shoulder blades. I saw Domenico grind his teeth distinctly.

The lanky one, a sergeant, disappeared through the opposite door, preceded by Caterina and followed by four of his men. I heard him give the order to put four ounces of lead in the stomach of anyone who attempted to escape; then a sound of heavy footsteps on the wooden staircase; then a muffled shifting of furniture, a banging of drawers on the flagstone floor. They searched every room, went down even to the cellar.

When the sergeant reappeared at the head of his men, he was wiping his pointed moustaches with the back of his hand — he had taken the trouble to make his search less tedious by tapping some barrel or other. But evidently the wine had not made his heart generous. He seized a chair and, planting it beside the prisoner, sat himself firmly astride it.

He began to speak with a hypocritical calm, and so quietly that the words reached me only now and then. I caught, at intervals, in an Italian larded with Venetian dialect and encrusted with Croatian flavouring, these more than significant phrases: "We did not climb all the way up here for the pleasure of being made fools of. — The mouse is here and His Majesty Emperor and King — *el aaron o un falò de la tua casa e di to stressi* [Either they find him or they'll burn your house and you with it]. — The dungeon, the club on your... Dead irons and the gallows!"

After this last phrase, he rested his fist on the back of the chair, his chin in the hollow of his fist, and waited. Domenico did not move a muscle.

The sergeant shouted: "*Orsù! bisogna farla finna!*" [Come now, let us have done with it!] And he sliced the air with one of those gestures of his that had the power to infuse a burst of life into the stiff automatons standing behind him.

Domenico was untied and dragged, with his thumbs twisted behind his back, into the middle of the kitchen. In less time than it takes to tell, one ruffian slipped a running noose around his neck; another threw the other end of the rope over a beam. Meanwhile the sergeant, having become extremely calm once more, had busied himself with lighting a cigar, and, tormenting its tip between his fingers and blowing words and smoke together, said almost syllable by syllable: "Either you sing — or I hang you up like a sausage."

The mountain man turned pale. Yet his face, ordinary if not common, appeared transfigured by a noble fierceness. He said: "You are mistaken. There is no one here. But you are the stronger — do as you please." He had barely finished when two thugs, seizing the end of the rope, hauled it in slowly, slowly. The poor man's arms struggled with a supreme effort to free themselves, his chest swelled horribly, his face turned livid. When his tongue was already protruding from his twisted mouth, they suddenly let him drop. His wooden clogs struck the floor with violence and his knees buckled under him, as though he were a rag doll. The noose was at once slackened.

A brief silence followed, during which Domenico's face became of a cadaverous pallor.

"What do you think of my medicine? Has it brought you to your senses? Are you now prepared to reveal the truth to me?"

Domenico gave an affirmative nod of his head.

"Well and good!" continued the sergeant. "Give him a sip of water." And he blew a long, thin mouthful of smoke — it was a quick way of expressing his inward satisfaction.

But Domenico straightened himself and, stepping forward as far as the ruffians holding the rope allowed him, snarled through clenched teeth: "For you, and for all the carrion that has befouled my Country!" And he launched a broad spit full upon his tormentor's chest. The man gave a spasmodic start and burst into a stream of exotic oaths. But with a supreme effort he contained his intense fury and dissembled it with the slowness of his gesture and an icy tone. He said: "Hang that toad."

The end of the rope was hauled in slowly, slowly —

From my cave I watched and reflected. More than once I had tormented the butt of my pistol, but a whole, imperious voice had cried out to me: "Do not act rashly!" A corpse, two corpses, a spectacular revenge — to what end? I resolved to play the last desperate card only when the game was irretrievably lost. My anguish at Domenico's torture was free of apprehension on my own account. I knew the fibre of that fierce old man and I sensed that he would not betray me. But Caterina — the horrible spectacle, the certainty that a single word could end that atrocious agony — how would her woman's heart hold out, her companion's heart?

Caterina ran frantic from wall to wall; she invoked, waving her arms, the Virgin and the saints. And when, seized by a sudden frenzy, she made to throw herself with her nails upon the impassive tormentors and the iron arm of a gendarme had thrust her back, almost to the ground, she shrieked and tore her hair out in handfuls. But the secret kept deep in her breast — the more furiously the tempest of the spirit rages, the more tightly it clings to the flesh — and deep it remained.

Domenico was lowered for the second time. He was so far gone that the renewed questions reached his brain in all probability too indistinctly for him to be able to answer. The sergeant called his henchmen into consultation. They must have concluded that the mountain man knew nothing, that the Royal Imperial Police had caught a crab. In the still air, orders rang out; about the farmhouse the measured tread of the "armed force" disposing itself for departure could be heard. A few minutes, and all was silence —

Domenico turned his searching eyes about him, and his chest swelled with deep sighs. Then he rose, looked at his companion with a long, intense gaze, and stroked her head. A little while later, having taken up his knife again, he returned to slicing his cheese, as though nothing extraordinary had occurred. Caterina gathered up the rosary from beside the hearth and began to tell it once more — this time, on her knees.

I allowed as much time to pass as seemed sufficient to assure me that the accursed pestilence had truly gone back down the slope; then I pushed aside the ivy and appeared upon the threshold. Caterina did not so much as turn. Domenico contented himself with opening the front of his shirt and pointing to the weal scored into his neck by the noose. I took him in my arms and, pressing my lips to that livid furrow, I kissed it again and again, sobbing.
