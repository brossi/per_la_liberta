# Chapter One

LOS ANGELES, on a splendid summer morning. I had arrived only a few hours before, on a train that had put me in mind of those which, from every corner of France, converge upon the shrine at Lourdes.

For the metropolis of southern California, situated near the ocean, between the desert and the orange groves, is a great centre of attraction for all those who have the misfortune of living beyond the Rockies: where, in summer, the sun prostrates men and herds alike, like withered grass; where the frost of the long winter makes the earth rigid and impenetrable even to the blows of a pickaxe; where cyclones sweep through the immense valleys, uprooting trees and demolishing houses.

The wretched souls whom consumption gnaws at their hollow chests find in the "City of Angels" the deeply azure sky and the balmy air that give the illusion and the hope.

How many of them I had encountered in the streets during those few hours! They pass quietly, light as beings come from the kingdom of shadows, and they smile at the children and smile at the flowers. In their optimism, which here becomes extraordinarily tenacious, they accept as indisputable truth the merciful lie that their loved ones murmur in their ear with every fit of coughing.

They go to sit in the shade of the flowering orange trees, and sometimes, while they cling in reverie to the sweetnesses of life; while the waxen corollas, all unknowing, breathe above their heads their invisible, most piercing fragrance... the waxen flesh breathes out, all unknowing, its invisible last breath... The breeze stirs from the sea and, mingling that breath and that fragrance, carries them away across the green countryside, away across the arid desert, away into the infinite mystery!..

But I was not in Los Angeles to prolong an agony, and when, after a long walk, I judged the opportune moment had come, I stopped before one of those little wooden houses that one cannot conceive of, in California, without associating with them the idea of a flora altogether particular, altogether domestic... the flora that embraces and almost possesses the house!

A rubber hose coiled in spirals upon the green grass was driving from its metal tip, into the air, a fan of minute droplets which the sun painted with the colours of the rainbow. A hummingbird, with the glint of emerald, was assailing with swift, persistent darts of flight a scarlet flower...

Beyond the climbing plants, within the walls of that little house, caught up in the social vortex, a man had sought refuge — a man who represented the last, glorious remnants of an era. Through the deep peace adorned with green, I could sense the suppressed longing of conspiracies, the fever of battles... and, in that picturesque corner of the far West, I suddenly recovered the emotions of my youth, invoked in vain for so many years... Then I breathed deep, warmly, warmly, as if it were a breath of the Homeland, and the fibres of my heart exhaled a word of thanks.

But a subtler thought succeeded it. Three-quarters of a century; an unceasing sequence of struggles, dangers, sufferings... what could have survived? Ought I to regard this refuge as a temple enclosing a living flame; or as the final resting-place of a long pilgrimage, the dolorous threshold of a sepulchre?

I advanced resolutely... Before I could raise my hand to the electric bell, a figure took shape in the half-shadow of the corridor, beyond the glazed door.

I found myself face to face with a stout lady, with a slight tendency toward plumpness. Her hair was of a fairness that was fading into white and had already begun to yield; but the face was still fresh and rosy and, beneath the prominent brow, shone eyes of blue, gentle and assured. The mouth and chin revealed to me a creature accustomed to confronting obstacles with resolution.

She asked me severely what I wished.

"Pardon me... Major di Rudio?" And I presented my visiting card.

Her expression brightened. "Ah! ah! that name is familiar to us. The Major will be pleased to see you. But he is still in bed!"

I thought: "The infirmities of old age... the threshold of the sepulchre!" And I was about to tell her I would return; but she anticipated me. "Come in, come in! This is precisely the hour at which the Major rises; he is methodical... he cannot be long. Is that so? From San Francisco? But then a cup of coffee can do no harm. Precisely! I am the Major's wife!"

— Signora Elisa!

And I offered her my right hand with the familiarity of one who, though never having seen a person, holds them dear because they have long occupied one's thoughts. Then a fancy took me and, as I followed her along the corridor, I asked her why my name was familiar to her.

"What shall I say? The Major reads whatever you write, and he grows animated and beats his fist on the table. Do sit down. The water is boiling and in four seconds..."

She left me alone. I was in the dining room. The foliage trembling before the one large window tempered the light within. A green tint pervaded the room, and the crockery on the sideboard reflected it; upon the table there glowed, in a vessel of blue glass, a bunch of amaryllis.

The dining room gave onto a study from which it was distinguished, more than divided, by a simple cornice. Through the opening, nearly as wide as the entire wall, I could discern in the background another broad, solitary window, likewise obscured by an arabesque of trembling foliage. In the air, the same green tint, restful and tranquil. At length I made out clearly several simple and massive pieces of furniture in the Mission style. I reflected that, amidst that artistic antiquity, I might well have found a place for myself; all the more so because a bookcase crammed with volumes had at once caused my old instinct as a library mouse to prevail. I found myself with my hands clasped behind my back, before those volumes, intent on scanning the gilded lettering pressed into their brown leather spines. History, philosophy... works stamped with freedom of thought.

I looked about me... From one wall there smiled upon me the pensive likeness of a man known to the People and to its tyrants in every part of the civilised world: Giuseppe Mazzini. From the adjoining wall there hung a picture bearing the effigy of a martyr less universally known, but no less great in spirit: Fortunato Calvi. And I exclaimed: The house reveals the man!

"Beg your pardon!"... It was Signora Elisa, just then returned. She had supposed I was speaking to her. She had unfolded a napkin and was laying the place setting, assisted by another lady who struck me by her extreme pallor, her very dark eyes, and her slender, slight figure.

"My daughter... Italia Adair," said the Major's wife, and the young woman confined herself to a nod of the head.

— Your only daughter?

— Not in the least! We have two others: Roma Scott and America Vickery. I am expecting them today and you shall meet them. For I hope you will be one of our party today. I also have a son, the firstborn, born in England and trained in his first steps — you are not unaware of this, I suppose — in tragic moments. But he is far away, in Arizona. A kind of fever for the unknown has taken hold of him, and he passes his life on horseback, in the solitude, exploring.

The refreshment was ready. The gracious lady had added to the coffee some toast and a dish of fresh figs. I protested. I was not accustomed to touching food before midday, and so much kindness was superfluous. She insisted. The eggs had been taken that very morning from the henhouse... she kept many hens; it was her particular care. As for the figs, Italia had picked them with her own hands, just at that spot. They were ripe and delicious.

She asked to be excused and went off about her affairs... I remained alone with the other lady. I was sipping the coffee and felt somewhat ill at ease. That appearance of suffering... perhaps my presence would prove unwelcome to her. I took advantage of the brief moments in which she was occupied in arranging some trifle or other to observe her minutely, without allowing it to appear, without committing any rudeness. She had a mass of hair of a lustrous black, perhaps too voluminous for her graceful head; thin brows, superbly arched; an aristocratic profile; an ethereal little person. But it was not long before I noticed a certain energy of movement that contrasted, in a strange fashion, with her invalid appearance.

I resolved to bring into play all my resources as a reporter seasoned in the craft. She grew animated by degrees, and from her lips there issued thoughts such as I had not expected.

I learned that she had been brought to the last extremity by appendicitis and that the surgeons had saved her by resort to the knife... a few hours' delay would have proved fatal to her. Children? One only! In homage to Uncle Sam, the eternal and glorious Uncle Sam, she had given him the name of Samuel. She wished to guide him toward a military career. At West Point, where she hoped to have him admitted, he would have the benefit of one of the finest educations in the world. She had spent a good part of her youth with her father, at the Regiment. A beautiful, magnificent life, the military life! Discipline... servitude?.. She was joking. The life of the Regiment was boundless freedom; at the mere thought of it she felt a tremor of energy stir within her. Not a moment of idleness, not a moment of tedium! True, there were days of painful anxiety. The regiment was often called out to drive back into their reservation the Indians, who committed some raid or other. It would remain absent for three, four, eight months. But when the courier, white with dust, burst into the camp and announced its return aloud, joy was in the air and stole through one's entire being. In the twinkling of an eye, life redoubled in intensity. The ladies hastened to communicate the good news to one another; the children doubled their leaping on the grass; the rose-bushes were stripped of their flowers; a coming and going of orderlies putting everything in order, everything afresh. And from that noisy bustle we would launch ourselves at full gallop, away in a race... ten, fifteen miles, to be the first to greet the column of the returning men. "When the dust, gleaming with arms, announced them from afar, on both sides there were cries to shame a horde of Bedouins. What moments, what embraces! Upon their faces, alongside the joy of homecoming, the serene pride of having suppressed barbarism, of having snatched our bold pioneers from torture and destruction. Upon ours, alongside the joy of seeing them again, the serene pride of being the daughters and wives of those strong men. Wherever the horses of Uncle Sam advance, they leave behind them the road cleared for honest labour and for civilised life!..

"You are mistaken, you are mistaken! Few bonds are so cordial as those that bind soldiers and officers together. The roughness is entirely apparent. When a subordinate receives a dressing-down, he looks his superior in the eye and can always read there a phrase very different from the one the lips have uttered. Severity is reserved solely for one who shows signs of cowardice. But have you ever heard of cowards in the army of Uncle Sam?

"Intemperate? Intemperate! I will tell you; there are birds whose natural state is flight. To set foot on the ground and never again to rise upon the wing is, for them, to be finished, and what is worse, they walk with a clumsiness that moves one to pity. The natural state of the Anglo-American race is motion, incessant, intense labour. The exuberance of life that does not confront arduous and glorious enterprises wastes away in taverns and in the gutter. And the American never does things by halves; not even evil. But at the rising of a difficulty, at the mere idea of danger, in the trial of strength... what a transformation! Where would one find a body of men of greater dash, quicker in expedients, more spontaneous in sacrifice, more steely in temper? And what enemy, at parity of numbers, has ever known how to withstand them?"

I listened, full of wonder and almost disconcerted. That harmonious voice, which a residue of weakness rendered more caressing, expressed thoughts that I had until that moment believed the exclusive province of virile minds, of folk born to face the perils of battle, whole and content only amidst anger and blood. But if strength bows before grace and sometimes adores it, why should grace not admire strength?

She bent her slender little figure over the table and set her white hand busily to arranging the bunch of amaryllis glowing in the blue vase. Then, answering a fresh question of mine:

"No, the Indian raids are by now, save the rarest exceptions, a memory of the past. Now Uncle Sam keeps arms and spirits bright with drills and frequent orders of transfer. One never leaves a place where one has lived happily without feeling a sense of sadness; but it is a sadness that vanishes, in a little while, in the wholesome emotions of the journey. For the ladies the journey is a festivity and, I am tempted to say, a regeneration. On the last one in which I took part, we covered, on horseback, more than seven hundred miles, through a marvellous country. It is a most strange sensation. One's being, forgetting its own individuality, feels itself transformed and almost part of the elements themselves. The force, the infinite motion — that is us. One goes on and on, in an atmosphere of sunlight, and we raced with the wind."

She fell silent, lowered her eyes, then resumed. "No, the true life is not in the great cities. The great city makes us small. An elegant prison, the calls of ceremony; a childish babbling of gowns and bonnets. If this is civilisation... I hate it."

I was no longer surprised. The impetuous nature, the need for action of the grandfather had passed into the father, and from the father into that slight lady, so ethereal that, as she walked, she seemed barely to brush the carpet. What might she have become in one of those confined environments in which virtue bursts its banks and runs headlong to victory or to martyrdom?

The mother returned. She had caught the thread of the conversation and added much of her own. She concluded the series of vivid evocations with this melancholy regret: "Happy days... all too sadly gone. For us they will return no more!"

Among the episodes she narrated was the following: Captain di Rudio (he was a captain at the time) showed in the execution of orders a meticulous precision. On one occasion, so as not to delay the departure by a few seconds, he cut clean off with his sabre the sleeves of a magnificent gown that had been left protruding from a trunk. For several years Signora Elisa had preserved them, like silent denouncers of an unheard-of crime...

The good lady, laughing it off, ended by leading me into the courtyard. She walked along throwing handfuls of grain to a flock of cackling hens, and I followed her step by step, heeding, now and then, a learned lesson in poultry-keeping. My readers, I imagine, take no interest in cocks and hens that are not roasted with a fork in their flesh. But an incident which, in due course, I shall relate, compels me to introduce into this opening passage those poor feathered bipeds as well... of pure breed, moreover!

We came to a corner, sheltered in which a white brooding hen sat upon her eggs in a basket. And the lady said: "Today or tomorrow the clutch will break from the shell. How much maternal love beneath that fistful of feathers! Is it not strange that the great affections, in which we take such pride, should be contained and powerful even in hearts so tiny? I am fond of creatures that have no speech."

At this point she set down the grain-vessel and moved away with a brisk step.

Standing on the landing, standing out against the dark background of the kitchen, framed almost by the doorposts, I made out an old man dressed in a grey coat, a tuft of white hair upon his brow, moustaches pointed, and the small beard of other lands and other times. The sun, beating full upon his face, threw into strong relief his angular features, as though carved in bronze. He was tapping the floor nervously with his heel, while his deep-set but most lively eyes scanned the scene beyond the bushes, beyond the plants that shadowed the courtyard.

There exist in California isolated groves of ancient vegetation which the botanists call *sequoie*, but which the people call simply "great trees." They rise straight, without boughs, like immense columns of a temple raised, in the night of ages, by some race of giants. The tallest among the pinnacles of the Lombard cathedral would seem, by comparison, a slender reed. They were already withstanding the wind when the Almighty created, according to the Scriptures, the Sun and the Stars. The prehistoric man who danced his bestial round-dance, devouring his fellow man, has vanished; the patriarchs who, wandering through the solitudes, traced the first paths for young humanity, have vanished; the Brahmins passed the lighted torch of civilisation to the Egyptian... and vanished; the Egyptians passed it to the Greeks... and vanished; the Greeks to the Romans... and vanished. What remains of the mighty Roman Empire? Race upon race has pressed upon race; States have hurled States into the void, through vicissitudes so long that men have lost within them even the memory of their own origins... And those trees stand!

With invisible eyes they behold the pygmies of ephemeral life who flock from every corner of the world to their feet and there stammer out their wonder. If it be so... how much disdain in their austere rigidity!

But their high summits, adorned with an evergreen crest, tremble in the wind of the Sierra and, perhaps, murmur ancient stories to the sky.

I was thinking of those trees, when the Count Carlo di Rudio and the companion of so many of his vicissitudes, standing together on the landing, heedless of my presence, pressed upon each other's cheeks the morning kiss.
