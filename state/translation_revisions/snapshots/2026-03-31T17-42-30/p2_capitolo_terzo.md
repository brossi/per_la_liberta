CHAPTER THREE

LOVE!... for Miss Sara or for Miss Elisa? The problem, simple as it was, stirred in his breast a singular alarm, succeeded by a no less singular irritation. "Why torment oneself to resolve it," he concluded, "when marriage is forbidden me by other and higher ideals?"

Di Rudio was acting unconsciously, and in a different sphere, much as a man acts when prejudice has taken root in his brain and he is confronted by new ideas. He senses the reasonableness of those ideas, and yet — he shuts his eyes obstinately to the truth, and the very certainty that his idol is crumbling makes him a more strenuous champion of the old fables and the old lies.

And now, here is how the long drought of di Rudio's purse came to an end.

From the day on which the Coup d'État had triumphed in definitive fashion, Victor Hugo, taking the road of exile, had settled on the island of Jersey. There he published "L'Homme," a periodical whose pages blazed forth, with all the intensity that genius lends it, a condemnation of the baleful imperial politics. The hero of the Second of December, as sensitive to the stings of criticism as any criminal who aspires to glory, complained of it adroitly to the Cabinet of London, which with equal adroitness deflected the complaint. But when the complications of European politics indicated to England that Napoleon III was the sovereign best placed to lend her a strong hand in the meditated enterprise of breaking the pride of the Czar, it became an act of wise diplomacy to humour that man's caprices. And the freedom of thought and the traditionally generous hospitality of the English people were, at least in part, curtailed and sacrificed to the real or supposed interests of the Nation.

Victor Hugo was surrounded by various brilliant minds, among whom Pianciani had joined himself. That nest of intellectuals was suddenly thrown into disarray. And so great a sacrilege did it seem that even the French reactionaries dared not applaud.

Pianciani resolved to take refuge in London once again. Before leaving the island, he dispatched, together with his other papers and insured for ten thousand pounds, the manuscript of his *Storia dei Papi* — his "History of the Popes." The other parcels arrived at their destination; the manuscript disappeared. And rumour had it that the Jesuits had taken possession of it, most probably to consign it to the flames. A certain colour of truth was lent to this rumour by the fact that the Insurance Company paid out the considerable indemnity immediately, without initiating the smallest inquiry, without uttering a word.

Pianciani and the Count di Rudio met, and the Roman gentleman — who, with his expansive cordiality, his generous impulses, made people forgive the deformity of his face, which the smallpox had robbed of its nose — received the gentleman from Belluno as an equal among equals. At parting, and as if in passing, the conversation fell upon the latter's financial straits.

"You could not have come at a more opportune moment!" said Pianciani. "I have set myself to attempt the *Storia dei Papi* anew. Will you be my amanuensis?"

The man accustomed to the bold ventures, the fatigues, the dangers of action was thus confined to an armchair; and he made a virtue of it, though not without the occasional dark brow and muttered grumble. In compensation, he was brought into contact with those men illustrious for genius, for character, for sufferings manfully borne, who frequented Pianciani's house — rebels persecuted, calumniated, without roof or bread, yet unvanquished; great in their loves, their hatreds, their utopias. A noble and proud company!

Most of them nourished a fixed idea. In those days the Crimean War was limping forward. From the very first stirrings of the troubles that led to that war, Giuseppe Mazzini had proposed making appeal to the sense of unity in Germany; seconding the national aspirations of Italy and Hungary; sending a breath of new life through the Balkans; forming, in short, around the Muscovite Empire a great bulwark of free States that would have compressed and paralysed Russia. This conception was not unwelcome to the Cabinet of Turin. But Napoleon III and England aimed at not provoking Austria's hostility; at inducing her, rather, to take active part in their alliance. And it was precisely because Austria would never have resolved to take the field so long as the terrible hornet's nest of Italy hung about her neck, that the cooperation of the House of Savoy was accepted with good grace — indeed, actively solicited. The Piedmontese army was the only strongly organised nucleus; the only one deemed capable of precipitating events in our Peninsula. Austria remained unmoved, and thus spared the Italians the humiliation of seeing the heroes of our independence fight side by side with the Croatian hordes, in order to maintain upon the European continent "a melancholy projection of Asia."

And the great meditated insurrection of peoples was reduced, in practical terms, to a simple, long, and tedious siege. England, shaken in her military prestige and short of soldiers, resolved to open enlistment in order to organise a few foreign legions.

Scarcely had the appropriate decree been promulgated than Fumagalli and Leoni presented themselves before di Rudio. Leoni had decided to depart for the Crimea and had come to pay a farewell visit. "And if it should be for the last time," he said, gripping the Count's hand, "well then — for the last time — farewell!"

They went to pass the few remaining hours before departure at the public house of a certain Mrs Shaw, wife of one of our countrymen — the very meeting-place of the recruits. The establishment, fitted out with a show of elegance, was already exceptionally animated — a confused animation, full of sudden bursts of laughter. Soldiers and civilians, gathered in knots, clapped one another on the shoulder and exchanged nicknames and hearty, broad epithets. In the secluded corners, a few gentle figures in bonnets, with pale and tearful faces. Flowers and sprigs of green on every breast; bluish spirals of cigarette smoke from almost every mouth.

But the noise and confusion ill suited di Rudio's temper, the more so as the sight of so many Italians on the point of shedding their blood for a cause he held foreign to the Fatherland brought to mind the sorry days of the *soldati di ventura* — the mercenary soldiers. And when Leoni, half in earnest and half in jest, said to him, "Come, Moretto! Make up your mind and sail with us!" he asked with an excess of bluntness: "What harm have the Russians done you, that you should offer up your youth as a holocaust, with the aim of swelling the number of widows and orphans amongst them?"

And Leoni replied: "Italian colours are flying in the Crimea!"

— "Any flag is a mockery when it does not stand as the symbol of truth and justice!"

The conversation was taking a bad turn. Fortunately a Milanese conscript, picking up the thread of a previous discussion, gave voice to his regret at not having thought to bring with him the *Sepolcri* of Ugo Foscolo, which would have contributed so greatly to shortening for him the hours of the crossing.

"I have a copy at home," di Rudio told him. "Will you accept it? It is a cheap little edition, the margins covered with scribbles — but they do not spoil the substance."

In four strides he was back at Baldwin's Garden. He mounted the stairs and entered his room, leaving the door ajar. He was already turning the chest of drawers upside down when from the other side of the corridor there reached him distinctly the sound of sobbing and sighs. What could this be? Stepping onto the threshold, he found himself face to face with Miss Sara, who, colouring fleetingly and without waiting to be questioned, said: "Elisa is weeping because she is convinced you are leaving tonight for the Crimea. Love is consuming her. And she has also compromised herself in Mama's eyes. It was that business of the receipts — you remember? Mama has drawn everything from her heart, and the least she can expect now is a thorough scolding. The mysterious stranger — it is there that she is in despair. It was Elisa who provided from her own savings — for everything, everything!"

The Count di Rudio picked up the great poet, turned him this way and that, and finally thrust him with an angry motion to the bottom of his pocket: "Tell Miss Elisa that the di Rudios are no mercenary soldiers. For Liberty — always! Mercenaries of foreign despots — never!"

And he descended the stairs at a headlong pace. But the storm that Miss Sara's words had raised followed him into the street; it entered through the crack of the door into Mrs Shaw's public house, and eddied about his head the whole evening long — so that the conscripts and the literature and the politics and the uproar and the tumultuous scene of the embarkation and the farewells reached his brain not as a vivid and well-defined reality, but as the pale evocation of images emerging confusedly from the mists of times long past.

A second surprise awaited him. Coming home towards one o'clock in the morning, he made out, seated on the landing, a female figure in a white gown — in the darkness it seemed to him phosphorescent. A moment of stupefaction seized him; and she rose, and retreating, seemed to dissolve away. He would have taken her for a phantom, had not a door creaked on its hinges. Miss Elisa had passed so great a part of the night keeping vigil — in tears, perhaps — to assure herself that the sea and the war had not taken him from her.

When he presented himself, rigid and master of himself, to the two young women, Elisa received him with the words: "Are you unwell? You are pale this morning!"

— "It seems to me that you are both pale!" observed the cousin. And she offered to prepare a cup of warm milk — Mama too, on her return, would have been glad of one. When she reached the threshold she turned, and smiling: "Do not commit any foolishness. Every moment lost is stolen from your happiness, and by dint of concealing yourselves you might end by not finding each other at all. You love each other; you were born for one another. Is it really so very difficult?" And she was gone.

The decisive moment had arrived. Elisa was nervously twisting a ribbon of hers, and di Rudio observed her, remaining at a respectful distance. He began by thanking her for all she had done for him. Denial was useless — Sara had confided everything to him. The misfortune was that he could demonstrate his gratitude only in words. And he went on to outline the sad vicissitudes to which one who champions a holy and persecuted cause must submit. With what heart could he have dared offer her his hand — to drag her into his tumultuous life, into his poverty? Where could he find her a nest that would not be, sooner or later, contaminated and destroyed by the fury of men? Would it be wise, humane, honourable to reserve for children born to perpetuate the gentle maternal virtues an atmosphere haunted by the terror of conspiracies, the menace of weapons, the clang of chains, the dread of blood? Childhood yearns for the kisses of smiling lips; it wishes to mirror in its candid soul the serenity of quiet eyes. And what else could he say to her; what happiness could he promise her, without resorting to mendacious phrases? He had chosen, between two evils, the lesser; he had resolved to take himself away — for ever. He entreated her to interpret his resolution with the very rectitude of feeling that had suggested it; to preserve a kindly memory of him.

Here — and here only — Miss Elisa raised her face and fixed her eyes upon his. With the keen discernment of simple souls, she knew how to sound another soul. She expressed herself as a creature conscious of the power to dispose of her own destiny, with the calm and chaste eloquence which to some of my readers, accustomed to extolling "the opulence of the hip and the tremors of the flesh," might seem exhumed from some museum of archaeology — other times, other men, other loves!

"Towards a soul such as his she could advance without suspicion and without artifice — she would have preferred to be definitively rejected rather than to become his through simulated virtue. There were Italian women, she was not ignorant of it, who had suppressed their serene aspirations and imposed upon their hearts solitude and silence, in order not to become wives and mothers in calamitous times. She admired their austerity, but could not bring herself to approve it. The wicked bring up the wicked freely, and should the good withdraw from the duty of rearing those who will perpetuate honourable traditions? The very environment that seemed unfavourable to the cultivation of the gentle virtues he attributed to her in tender hearts would have fostered the development of the virile paternal virtues. The tempering of manly characters is a mission that refines a mother's pride. Or did he perhaps think her, like his ancient Roman women, fit only to oversee the good order of the kitchen and to spin wool? Was the anguish of hearts that sweat blood, the terrible straining of muscles that swell to break their chains, the tormenting labour of minds that seek everywhere equity and light — were these to have left her indifferent? She had a profound sympathy for the country he had painted for her so beautiful, so great, so unfortunate. The love she bore the Fatherland did not make her jealous of his — to draw him away from his purposes, to blunt his generous impulses!? Precisely the contrary! They would have worked together, and with redoubled energy, for the triumph of Liberty. He had kindled a flame that made her greater than herself — let him try to extinguish it, if he had the heart for it, if he had the strength!"

The Count was thinking: "Blessed be the rosy lips that conquer the eloquence of so many martyrs, living and dead!" And yet he insisted:

"No, Elisa! The conspirator is solitary even in the bosom of his family. He must at times forget that he has a mother and a wife and children. If his predestined hour arrives, he closes his ears to their prayers, does not see their tears, and goes — goes without confiding his secrets even to the air he breathes. If that hour were to come—"

"— One last embrace, a wrench of the heart, perhaps even that — but no indiscreet questions. Am I not certain that you can do nothing of which I should have cause to be ashamed?"

When Miss Sara returned with the cups and the warm milk, the lover who but a short while before had wished to flee far, far away, had one knee upon the ground. Holding Elisa's hands in his, he kissed them — kissed them again and again.

On the ninth of December, 1855, a flame was made lawful — upon which misfortune hastened to blow, but in vain. The stern vicissitudes of more than half a century were destined not to extinguish it, but to make it burn ever brighter.
