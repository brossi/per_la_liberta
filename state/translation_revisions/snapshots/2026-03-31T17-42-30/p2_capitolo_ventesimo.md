## Chapter Twenty

AT nine o'clock precisely, Monsieur de Collet was in my cell. He did not elaborate at great length, but what he told me was more than sufficient to put an end to my conjectures. Influential English personages had interceded on my behalf with his august Empress. The second birthday of the Crown Prince fell in those very days, and she, who was above all else a woman and a mother, had been pleased to solemnise the happy occasion by granting a pardon that would lift a woman, a mother, from her affliction. My wife had already been informed by telegraph of my good fortune.

Monsieur de Collet then lavished congratulations upon me; and afterwards made to take his leave, but turning back upon his steps: "It was the Empress who sent me this morning. She wished to ensure that her will had not been contravened. Nevertheless, it is well to remember that much good, and much of severe justice, may proceed from her august consort. A phrase of gratitude addressed to him could certainly do you no harm. I say this on my own initiative; but I am persuaded that I am interpreting the thought of Her Majesty the Empress, to whom you are also indebted for your life."

What was I to answer? In the space of a couple of hours, a profound reaction had been working within me, and to that first, strange regret at the unexpected return to the sensations of the living, there had already succeeded a desperate yearning for intense life, for freedom! Hope, violently expelled from the soul, returned in torrents, and no human force could have withstood it. I say that if the expression of feigned sentiments could contribute even so much as a straw to the projects — devoid of any definite form, yet unquestionable in their tendency — that were already germinating within my mind, I must not hesitate to write one, ten, a hundred letters... to the Emperor, to the Pope, to the devil himself! I was entirely defenceless in the hands of omnipotent enemies... any subterfuge, any deception became a legitimate art of war and of salvation. To slip free from those talons, at whatever cost, in order to stand more proudly with unbound hands; to fight once more — this was the problem!

I gave my promise.

I was brought once more to the Palais de Justice, before that President Delangle who had infused so much of his venom into our trial. By one of those hypocrisies dear to a society that attaches more importance to appearances than to substance, it was desired that I should seem free in the Tribunal, and my chains were removed. The relevant portions of the trial record were read aloud; whereupon the Advocate General, Oscar de Valle, requested the ratification of the imperial letters patent commuting my death sentence to perpetual life imprisonment. These letters bore the date of the 12th of March. Why, then, had I been led to the very foot of the scaffold? Was it the tardiness of bureaucracy that was to blame, or the deliberate design of subjecting me to a moral pressure that might induce me to make revelations?

I must note that the official account of the execution dwells at length upon Pieri and Orsini — of myself, not a single word! I believe that no wish existed to offend the susceptibilities of a people, by implicitly admitting that chivalrous France had abolished physical torture only to substitute in its place the torture of the intellect.

The pardon having been ratified, President Delangle made one last attempt to strike a blow on behalf of the social order, as he understood it, or pretended to understand it, by asking me whether I repented "of my crime." Upon the protest of my counsel, who feared some fierce reply, he did not insist, and contented himself with saying to the gendarmes: "Take him away!" The manacles were replaced upon my wrists. I was conducted to the Conciergerie, and thence, in a prison van, to La Roquette, where I was thrust into the courtyard amongst the other prisoners. One of them came forward at once, offering me his right hand. It was Antonio Gomez.

What a glance was ours, and how many thoughts we exchanged in a few seconds, without a word! It was Gomez who broke the silence. The carelessness that had brought about my arrest still gnawed at him, and he begged my pardon for it.

"Do not trouble yourself about it," I said to him. "We have all of us committed errors, one way or another. But it may well be affirmed that our souls knew no base impulses."

We ended by leaning against a wall, as though exposing our limbs to the sun — a cheerless sun that gave no foretaste of spring. We spoke of the two martyrs... they who had shed no blood were the ones decapitated! A sensation colder than winter crept into my breast and grew there, and the words died upon our lips. I was roused from my thoughts by a warder who, as he passed, shook a great bunch of keys with a violent clatter.

Several days went by, rendered the more painful by the humiliating regulations of prison life, and prolonged by the feverish waiting for news from my wife. I imagined that the Police would no longer interpose any obstacle to our correspondence, and Elisa, by delaying to send me word of herself, had revived all my anxieties. My start, therefore, was violent when I heard myself announced as being required at the Chancery.

I was led directly before the superintendent, the Marquis Lassalle, who handed me a letter, providing me by that simple act with a threefold surprise. The first, because contrary to the iron discipline of the place, it was still sealed; the second, because the fine characters visible upon the envelope were not Elisa's — they were entirely unknown to me; the third, because upon looking the superintendent full in the face, as though to draw from it some explanation, I perceived that, despite his outward gravity, he was smiling beneath the skin.

I tore open the envelope and experienced a fourth surprise, before which the three preceding ones grew pale and insignificant. The letter began by exhorting me to read to the very end before passing judgement. It then proceeded to inform me that its author was the Viscountess of Hastings, a Scottish Protestant who had converted to the Catholic faith and become Sister Teresa of the Discalced Carmelites. She was cloistered in the convent of Montauban, near Toulouse. "I must also preface my words," wrote Sister Teresa, "by explaining that the rules of our Order oblige us to go every two hours during the night to pray in the church of the Convent." One night, whilst she was at prayer, Christ had unexpectedly appeared before her. He, in a voice beyond all utterance, had communicated to her that within the prisons of La Roquette there was a young man of patrician family, courageous, his soul nurtured upon noble and elevated sentiments. The influence of wicked persons had led him astray from the righteous path and driven him to perdition. Christ had concluded by entrusting her with the holy mission of saving the soul of that errant young man, who was, despite his transgressions, dear to the Heavenly Father.

This was followed by a detailed account of the life and conversion of Sister Teresa, and a fervent exhortation that I should imitate her example; that I should return to the bosom of the true Church of God.

The letter bore also a postscript, in which Sister Teresa assured me that she had obtained from the Archbishop of Paris permission to carry on a fraternal correspondence with me. The Archbishop himself had then prevailed upon the prison authorities to grant me leave to seal my replies, which would be delivered to her unread, neither by those authorities nor by any person whatsoever.

No great perspicacity was needed to comprehend that the fine, elegant characters of the Discalced Carmelite concealed the foul alliance of Church and Empire. To please the adulterer, the bride of Christ had assumed the vile trade of the spy.

I returned the letter to the Marquis Lassalle, observing to him, with irony, that if certain stupidities were still possible in the full light of the nineteenth century, under the Empire, in the France of the Encyclopaedists, they had no purchase whatsoever upon the souls of Italian patriots. The Marquis Lassalle shrugged his shoulders without reply; but his ill-concealed smile would no longer be contained, and it oozed from every pore of his skin.

Nevertheless I reflected that from this farce, thrust into the midst of so full a drama, I might contrive to draw some advantage for my family; leaving aside also the consideration that writing would serve me as a distraction. Sister Teresa received my reply.

What did I say to her? I thanked her first of all for the interest she had taken in me. Christ, who is acquainted with all things, must certainly have informed her that I was a freethinker, and that I believed not at all in him, nor in his doctrines. If, however, there truly existed a Divine Mercy disposed to do something for me, let it set aside the soul for the moment and address itself to the extreme needs of my earthly existence. I had a wife and a child, to whose welfare I was indispensable. Should Christ work the miracle of restoring me to liberty, my scepticism might well be shaken thereby, and — one thing leads to another. At large, we should speak at greater leisure of my soul!

Now mark the subtlety of the servants of God! They took very good care not to induce their principal to work any miracle; but, understanding that something must nonetheless be conceded to me, if only to lure me on with the hope of better things, they quietly pulled the strings, and... One day — no more than five or six had passed since I had written to Sister Teresa — while I was walking in meditation, a hand, amicably rough, descended upon my shoulder. It was the head warder, who invited me to follow him to the Chancery. He wore the mysterious air of a man who leads a child to where the apple is hidden.

A few minutes later, I too had my apparition. Not imaginary, not feigned; but real and living and trembling more for me than for herself. She stood upright in the shadows of the great room, like the Madonna in a Flemish painting, and no sooner had I entered than she ran towards me with the impetuous force of passion and embraced me. Elisa!... Elisa!... She was not alone. She had with her her little child, who was toddling forward on uncertain steps. Freeing myself from Elisa's arms, I lifted him up and kissed him soundly; whereupon he clouded his round face and fixed his great eyes upon his mother's countenance, as though to ascertain from her expression that he was in no danger. His mute language said: "Ought I to cry, or ought I to laugh?"

The warder, seated beside the door with a large key in his right hand, warned me that for our final farewell we were granted no more than a quarter of an hour. We might, he said, speak freely in English. Our brief interview was exceedingly agitated. She informed me that, following my advice, she had succeeded in interesting in our fate a certain Sir Walter, a native of her own Nottingham, who was one of the pillars of the London *Times*. That powerful journal had circulated a petition, which was then signed, through the humane influence of Sir Richard Main, by several personages of considerable weight at Court. Queen Victoria, moved by a pressure skilfully dissimulated, had authorised the English Ambassador in Paris to exert himself in order that the petition might be delivered personally to the Empress Eugenia.

Elisa had learned of the pardon granted me since the night of the 12th. A telegram descending through a long chain of command as far as the head of the detectives, Sanders, and by him transmitted to her, announced that "not out of any consideration for myself, since I merited none whatsoever, but out of compassion for a young mother and an innocent child, the Empress had ordered that my life be spared."

Elisa had alluded to the first part of the telegram with a smile... a smile through tears, an ineffable smile! Moreover, as no letter of hers had been delivered to me, so not one of mine had reached her hands.

I looked at her and thought: what will the poor creature do, bound by the Laws and still more by love to a man destined, perhaps, never to see her again? I felt that I must there and then release her from every obligation, and it seemed to me a virtue to exhort her to seek a support worthy of her. But one does not renounce, without attempting some supreme effort, the dominion of a noble heart. I began by informing her of Sister Teresa's letter, and continuing: "They will certainly send," I said, "some nun or some priest to you as well. Whatever they may tell you concerning me, take no notice of it. If they ask you indiscreet questions, answer consistently that you know nothing. If they offer you money, accept it. If they invite you to go to some place, never go alone... never! Within a year I shall be free; I shall find a way to be so myself. If when the year has passed I have not rejoined you... act as though I were dead — and so I shall be! A di Rudio cannot live long in chains!"

The quarter of an hour — ah, how swiftly! — had elapsed. We embraced, and her tears began afresh... but I perceived clearly enough that they were tears of compassion, not of despair. Evidently she still placed all her trust in me and expected that I should keep my promise to the letter. To comfort her yet further, I added: "Yes, you will see me free again... within a year!" Then I sought my child with my eyes. He was seated upon the warder's knee and was clutching in his slender fingers, in a state of wonder, the great key, as though it were a toy. The warder himself, as he held the child out for my embrace, remarked upon the strange whim of fortune that amused the little one with the very key that robbed him of his father, that made of him an orphan!...

The door swung open. Upon the threshold I turned to engrave the image of Elisa upon my heart.

I saw her once more, standing upright in the darkness, like the Madonna in a Flemish painting. The child was reaching for her kisses and tugging at her skirts. One second more, and my apparition had vanished!...
