THE EXCITEMENT of the moment did not prevent me from reflecting upon the strangeness of my situation. I, a patriot — and no mean one, if the intensity of feeling counts for anything — was being driven out like an enemy from the only scrap of free Italian soil, and I burned feverishly to cross the midline of the lake in order to return, as they say, to the shadow of the black and yellow... the very object of our execration become all at once the symbol of my salvation. I reached it and breathed more freely. But it was a salvation of short duration, and fate had ordained that I should not reach the Swiss frontier without considerable hardship.

The weather, which had shown a threatening aspect since morning, turned for the worse, and from the Alps — toward which we had turned our prow — there came not merely wind but a full tempest. The waters, as if forewarning its approach, took on a formidable undulation, and our frail craft tossed about in wild abandon. I soon perceived that we were making no headway at all, and seizing the spare oars, I made myself a third in that desperate struggle between human will and the tyranny of the storm. But judging it imprudent to turn my back upon the two boatmen, I was obliged to resign myself to keeping my face directed toward the Alps. Then there came sweeping obliquely across a pelting of frozen sleet — or rather a whirlwind of tiny needles of ice that stung the skin, tormented my eyes, and took away my breath. And the waves, foaming at their crests, sheared off by the wind into thin curling sheets, threatened to capsize the little vessel. There could be no doubt of it — the elements had declared themselves for Austria, and more than once I thought that I had escaped so many perils only to end miserably in that pond of agoni.

Numbness of the limbs came upon me; night came upon me. It was necessity — and salvation — to abandon Locarno and to make instead for the nearest republican shore, Brissago. We arrived there with our moustaches crusted with ice and our bones half broken. Nor was I able to show my deliverers the full measure of my gratitude; for one of them, the very man to whom I counted out the agreed sum together with a comparatively generous gratuity, laid a rough hand upon my shoulder and said: "Whatever comes of it, we served you because that double-barrelled instrument of yours admitted of no argument. Go about your business; we shall see to ours. Good night and good fortune!"

Half an hour and I was between the sheets. Scarce had I lain down when Sander and Radonitz, the captain of the Arno and Fortunato Calvi, the lake and the whole of Italy vanished into darkness.

The following morning I found my anxieties again upon the pillow beside me. After long reflection I persuaded myself that my failure had been entirely personal. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, remaining at liberty, had surely known how to organise everything, even without my assistance. We were now at the 4th of February... therefore the useful time had not passed... an initiative delayed, not lost!

I resolved to make my way on foot as far as Locarno and, without more ado, set out. The wind had dropped, but snow was falling in broad flakes... mountain and valley were white with it. In that fresh snow my legs sank to the calf with every step, and beneath my shoes there formed an obstinate clog that stole away, treacherously, both the road and my energy. But when I came in sight of the Olivieri house and saw light filtering through the iron grilles, and inferred from it that men were keeping watch and were at work, my weariness vanished as though by enchantment. The agreed signal opened the door to me. I was led into a room where, seated at a small table with their faces lit by the lamp, were Sanders and Radonitz. Standing upright, with arms folded, I found a third person: Pietro Fortunato Calvi. Greetings and congratulations! I cut them short to demand news of the situation: "What have you arranged? What hopes remain to us?"

Sanders and Radonitz shrugged their shoulders. They were waiting for the Police to lift their ambuscades and fall back to sleep. My mishap, as recounted by Calvi, had not only confirmed them in their intention of *menare il cane per l'aria* — to spin out matters indefinitely — but had further persuaded them that the Austro-Hungarian authorities, grown suspicious, had incited those of the Republic. "We are no longer safe here!" said Radonitz. "By the way, how are you placed for funds?"

And without awaiting my reply, he stretched his hand toward certain rolls of money which, in my first preoccupation, I had not even noticed.

It was as though a veil fell from my eyes. I thought: "Late... but in time!" And turning to Radonitz, who had unrolled one of the bundles: "Since you regard our attempt as having failed, restore the sum that I brought you. It was destined for the purchase of arms and not for your own benefit. It was counted out to me in the name of Mazzini; to Mazzini I shall make delivery of it."

The expression assumed at that moment by Radonitz is among the most comical of my recollections. He became haughty once more: "I have issued you a proper receipt; your responsibility is covered. Do not concern yourself with what does not pertain to you."

With hardship and with sweat, men who groaned beneath the lash had scraped together that gold... bread taken from their own mouths, from the mouths of their children. I insisted. And Radonitz, pale as wax: "Young man, you are a subordinate here; take care you do not forget it!"

Sanders, who had remained seated with his elbows resting on the table and his face in his hands, thought it opportune to intervene: "How do you expect us to make our escape, to preserve our work for better times, if we lack the means of doing so?"

I replied: "I do not believe that the watch-chain and trinkets hanging at your breast, and your gold timepiece, are altogether indispensable to you. You have sworn to give to the Fatherland your time, your blood, your soul and everything... would you deny her now a few trinkets?"

I turned my gaze toward the man who had not yet spoken, who had remained standing in the half-shadow — toward Fortunato Calvi. He gave me a brief nod of assent. Then with a single bound I was at the door, I shut it, put the key in my pocket. Then drawing that double-barrelled instrument which at Intra had already rendered me such signal service, I levelled it and cried: "Enough of words! Either restitution, or no one leaves this room!"

I replaced the gold in my money-belt and shook Fortunato Calvi's hand. To the others I gave the military salute... not without a touch of irony... Then I departed. 69
