## Chapter the Ninth

THE evening of the following day, the 14th of January, Pieri and I dined, as was our custom, at the trattoria in the Galerie Montmartre. We repaired immediately afterwards to the hotel, and the doorkeeper, handing Pieri the key to his room, announced to him that half an hour earlier a gentleman had come asking for him. He had not disclosed his own name, but had assured them he would return shortly.

And so we found ourselves in the room, feverishly searching for a match to light the gas. We had left the door open, to make temporary use of the light coming from the corridor. Turning instinctively, I caught sight, outlined in black against that light, of the figure of Felice Orsini.

His words were brief: "Compatriots, the Emperor has given the signal. Come to my lodgings to collect the implements. I shall be there within the hour. Everything is arranged for the best... success cannot fail us. But given that the unforeseen may arise... here is the means to facilitate your flight and your safety." And with that he drew out a purse and counted out two hundred francs for each of us. Then he continued: "I should have wished to provide better for your needs, but it is beyond my power. To the triumph of great principles denied, one contributes more through integrity of soul than through the force of gold. Gold is wanting to the persecuted, and is more properly the weapon of despotism. Bear in mind that upon your punctuality, upon your steadfastness, depends the redemption of the Fatherland. I say no more to you."

He took leave of me with a powerful handshake and vanished. Yet something remained.

A tormenting spirit that seemed to saturate the air, and my heart was very nearly overcome by it. The moments that precede action always arrive more painful than those in which the action itself is accomplished. The doing produces a fever that mounts with the danger and leaves no room for reflection or repentance. Be that as it may, from the tumult of thoughts that assailed me, the sense of duty emerged triumphant, and my soul was left, as it were, clad in armour.

I cast a glance towards Pieri and, at that very moment, to my astonishment, I saw him fling upon the bed the money left him by Orsini, as though he were vexed by it. A moment later he drew from a case a revolver — that marvellous American invention which had but recently been introduced into Europe — and holding it out to me said: "Take it! It holds six rounds... six friends, true friends! They might serve you well!" He threw the case into the drawer of the chest, locked it, picked up his money once more, and we went out.

We went out to walk, in silence and for the last time, along the road that led to the well-known lodgings in the Rue Monthabor. Having arrived before the apartment of Felice Orsini, and as we were about to knock, the door opened and two men crossed the threshold. They had the collars of their overcoats turned up, their faces turned towards the darkness of the courtyard. I did not recognise them.

Once again we found ourselves gathered together — Pieri, Gomez, Orsini, and I — as on the day when I had wished to submit my samples of beer to Tomaso Allsop. But no sooner had Gomez caught sight of us than he begged leave to absent himself for a moment to make, he said, a small purchase. We awaited him in silence, Orsini leaning, almost seated, against the table. Those long moments of waiting made him nervous. He rose and said: "It crosses my mind that that man has gone to sell the mice to the cat."

But Gomez returned, and Orsini smiled. He then proceeded to distribute the bombs. He said simply: "For you... for you..." So that they should not slip from our hands, he had wrapped them in a black handkerchief which, held by the knotted corners, would have acquired the force of a sling. Gomez and I received one each of large dimensions; Pieri one small one; Orsini stowed carefully about his person two small ones, one in the right pocket, the other in the left pocket of his overcoat. We set out, I at Orsini's side; Gomez and Pieri went ahead of us. At this point I could not help but remark that Pieri had kept his lips continuously sealed, as though the imminence of the action, or some secret thought, had rendered him suddenly mute. I drew from this none too charitable conclusions. We soon lost sight of him, and Orsini observed to me, without my having opened my mouth, that "the loquacious Tuscan had every appearance of a man who intended to desert."

Now I must relate an incident which was recounted to me later by Orsini himself, and which Pieri confirmed. At a certain point, Pieri turned back along his path and passed close beside us; but he was not alone. Accompanying him was a stranger, correctly dressed, with a contented, almost cheerful expression. This man walked straight on, paying us no heed; Pieri, however, exchanged a meaningful glance with Orsini. Absorbed in my own thoughts, or distracted — I could not say which — I noticed nothing, and Orsini did not see fit to mention it to me. He imagined that some entirely harmless intruder had attached himself to the heels of "the loquacious Tuscan," who would undoubtedly have known how to rid himself of the fellow, perhaps in some liquor-shop, and would then appear at his appointed place at the designated hour. That silence exercised a decisive influence upon my conduct immediately after the attempt.

We were now on the point of setting foot in the Rue Le Peletier. Felice Orsini stopped and looked about him as though impatient to see someone he was expecting. A few seconds passed, and a man with a hurried step, long moustaches protruding above his upturned collar, emerged from the crowd and moved towards him. Out of courtesy, I drew back a few paces; but the ear of the conspirator acquires habits that are not precisely those of the well-bred man. For this reason, perhaps, and not by deliberate design, I caught the following brief exchange:

— How does the affair stand?

— All is well!... This evening!

And they clasped hands. The man with the long moustaches immediately withdrew, as hastily as he had come. We resumed our way, and Orsini said: "Do you know that man?" — "Certainly! It is Francesco Crispi!" Whereupon he, with a slight shade of vexation: "Confound it! I had supposed you did not know him!"

Ought I, or ought I not, to add that with that final handshake Orsini had concluded the distribution of the bombs, and completed his part as organiser? I noticed that only one pocket of his overcoat showed any bulge...
