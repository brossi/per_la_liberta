# Chapter the Nineteenth

ON the evening of the twelfth, the chaplain Hughon came to pay me his customary visit. He was grave, grave, grave. I spoke to him of our rejected appeal, and he became embarrassed. Then the suspicion of the grim truth flashed upon me, and I resorted to several subterfuges to confirm it. But Hughon promptly assumed the air of the ingenuous, and sheltering behind that simple artifice, became unassailable. Perhaps every suspicion of mine might have dissolved, had my visitor not extended his hand as he took his leave. Those fingers seemed to me to trace mysterious signs in the air, so visibly did they tremble.

I awaited the return of Saturno who, of the two guards, seemed to me the more candid, and without even giving him time to remove his cap: "Saturno," I said, "I know that tomorrow morning I shall be led to the scaffold. Heed me and see to it, you who are good, that my last wishes are carried out to the letter. At five o'clock you will wake me..."

Here he began to protest. Why had I put such nonsense into my head? The appeal being rejected, there remained still, thanks to our connections, a great deal of fighting to be done, and before anything... eh, eh!... it would take much more than that!

"My friend, Monsieur Hughon intended to hear my confession and instead... I have confessed him. He has revealed everything to me!"

At this, Saturno, who was still gesticulating to give emphasis to that *eh, eh!* of his, let his arm fall as though I had snapped it off. "He revealed everything to you? That is against the Regulations and he ought not to have done it." He stood for a moment with eyes downcast, then murmured: "Since you know everything... well, then — yes! there is nothing left for you now but to die manfully, as you have lived."

Then, all at once, a great silence fell within my soul, and with it a kind of great darkness; but in that emptiness, life still manifested itself powerfully through an instinct that drove me to crave something yet darker, yet more silent. *"Tanto meglio quanto più presto!"* — "The sooner the better!" — is Orsini's expression; only then did I understand its full meaning.

"At five precisely!... I have a great deal, a very great deal to write. My last thoughts, from the prison, to my kinsmen and to Elisa; my last thoughts, from the scaffold, to the Patria... And you?... Take this! (I kissed him.) Tomorrow, at this hour, think of me. Say to yourself: Human miseries touch him no more!"

Saturno was punctual. My first sensation on that memorable day was caused by the extreme pallor of his face. I wrote to my father; I wrote to my wife's kinsmen, commending that unhappy woman to them from the depths of my heart. Six o'clock struck at the Roquette.

Shortly after, ten or twelve persons entered the large room adjoining. Among them I recognised the chaplain Hughon and the Governor of the prison. They made their way to Orsini's cell, and I heard them announce to him his final hour. The Governor of the prison then pronounced, in a low voice, a few phrases, the meaning of which was plain to me from Orsini's reply. A magistrate stood ready to receive the dying man's disclosures, and he had been urged to make a full confession. The reply of the martyr rang through the sepulchral silence, subdued yet distinct: "Felice Orsini knows how to die; he does not know how to be an informer!"

Then came Pieri's turn, and his nerves gave way. "He was old, he was bound; but he would have had spirit enough..."

The Governor of the prison admonished him courteously, and he recovered himself at once. "Be it as it may," he said, "to die now, to die in some few years — what does it matter? My executioners too shall die..." He added that he intended to plunge into eternity singing.

My turn came. I replied calmly that the Count Carlo di Rudio was ready — an expression which, in the official reports, was afterwards attributed to Orsini. As my lips closed, I made out more clearly, in the dim light, the man who had accompanied the Governor of the prisons into my cell. He was an elderly abbé... A fine soul, perhaps. But to me, so ill-disposed toward everything that recalls the tonsure, he had the effect of an enemy come to ascertain, *de visu*, my last moments. And since death was near and certain, I could well, without fear of giving harm to any living soul, give free rein to my feelings. He drew close to me and made to speak. I imagined he wished to exhort me to die like a Christian, and I forestalled him: "I prefer," I said, "to consecrate my last thoughts to my wife and my little child. As for your God, I shall see Him, if He exists, but only to tell Him from what manner of ministers He has the simplicity to let Himself be represented upon this earth."

We were conducted into the large room. Our hands were unbound. Grave, and belonging to people who were tragically moved, the faces... the rest, almost like a scene of friends who bid farewell to friends, on the eve of a long journey.

Pieri, firmly resolved to treat the matter as an ordinary event, would not renounce his breakfast. The appropriate orders were given. While waiting, a round of introductions even took place. In this way I learned that Hughon was to be the spiritual comforter of Orsini; the chaplain of the Conciergerie, that of Pieri; the abbé fastened to my side was the chaplain of the House of Correction.

An elegant *cabaret* — a tray with coffee and rum — was brought. Felice Orsini sipped from the latter, toasting the health of the Governor of the prisons. At this point, my "comforter," humanely overlooking my rough rebuff, offered me a small glass; but he made the mistake of observing that it would give me courage. I refused out of pride, retorting that Italian patriots had no need of artificial courage. Then there was a rivalry in asking one another for small keepsakes. The three executioners, upright and silent along the wall, watched us with a strange persistence.

Suddenly the Governor drew out his watch and made a sign to the guards. The Regulations, heedless of conscience, required us to attend Mass. Felice Orsini moved sadly toward the prison chapel. I protested, and Pieri added to my curt refusal his own impetuous torrent of words. Those present glanced at one another; the Governor shrugged his shoulders and in the end consented to compromise. Orsini's absence was very brief. No sooner had he returned than they led us toward the Chancellery, whence we passed into an adjoining room. On entering, Pieri, who preceded me by a few paces, turned round and said to Orsini: "Well then, my old friend?" Orsini raised his head, looked at him steadily and replied: "Calm, calm!" Those were the first words those two men, united by their ideals and their martyrdom, had exchanged since their arrest... and they were the last!

We were in the hands of the executioner. They shaved my nape; they made me put on a low-cut shirt; they removed my shoes with their own hands. The same preparations, carried out rapidly, left Orsini absorbed in his thoughts; but the voluble man from Lucca grew almost cheerful from it all... the strangeness of nerves! He wished to see himself in a mirror, and his executioner — something of a dandy, most likely, despite his foul trade — drew a small looking-glass from his waistcoat pocket and held it out to him. He examined himself at his leisure and, smiling, exclaimed: "You have got me up like an old *cocotte*!" While they were removing his shoes he broke into a frank laugh and said: "Lucky thing I washed my feet!"

As for me... why should I not say it? Those moments were extremely painful to me. For several years my life had been a succession of events in vivid colours; but every trace of them seemed suddenly effaced by the memories of childhood that assailed me in a throng. And I saw again my poor mother, all tenderness and all smiles, as on a feast-day when she dressed me and, gazing at me, took her pride in it. What a contrast!

But the Count di Rudio was not to be seen, at such a moment, with tearful eyes, and if a tear or two wished to spring forth, his pride took care to drive them back into the depths and dry them there.

While they were wrapping my head in the black veil, Giuseppe Pieri was already walking out without a word. But I reached him in time to hear him utter that phrase which, slightly distorted, afterwards served almost as a pretext for demonstrating his grandiose and eccentric mind. To me it seemed, and still seems, the virile expression of a patriot who, having fulfilled his mission to the utmost limit of his own powers, feels himself lightened of every perishable and vulgar part of himself, and almost sublimated by martyrdom. The chaplain of the Conciergerie had taken his arm, as though there were need to support him. He, without even turning his head: "One does not feel fear when one goes to Calvary!" And he moved away between the two foul props of every tyranny, the priest and the executioner... and I saw him no more. But I heard his voice distinctly strike up, in the courtyard, the hymn of the Girondins: *"Mourir pour la Patrie"* — "To Die for the Fatherland." He went to the scaffold, as he had promised, singing!... it rains.

I am in the courtyard... it rains and snows. Weather that reconciles me to the thought of the imminent warm shelter underground. I pass alongside a company of prison guards drawn up in line, presenting arms... the rifles have their muzzles pointed to the ground, their stocks in the air. The great gate stood wide open; the scaffold, erected a few paces from the pavement, cut off my view; but not so much that I could not make out on the square a considerable body of foot-soldiers, of light cavalry, of artillery, drawn up as if in the imminence of action.

My hands were bound behind my back and fastened, moreover, to the bonds that constrained my feet. I advanced in steps necessarily small and slow. The executioner, on my right, stood a full head taller than I; the old priest, on my left, must with his white hair have contrasted strangely with my dark, restless youth. He was murmuring prayers, his eyes upon a crucifix. I had expressed the desire to draw a few puffs of smoke, and this had been granted me. But the water and snow, which I trod with bare feet, which fell upon my bare shoulders, provoked shiverings that I strove in vain to suppress, and that apparent weakness, vexing me, drove me to bite savagely at the stem of my pipe.

Near the gate, the field of my vision widened, and I saw the windows and saw the rooftops thronged with people. The spectacle of ill-starred virtue, the Greeks declared, is worthy of the Gods, and to witness it, men had paid as much as a thousand francs for the hire of a balcony. The mood of that multitude? "It" — says the official report — "remained constantly calm and silent throughout the great and legitimate expiation." In truth, a robust voice from above cried out: "People, bare your heads! This is the execution of heroes!" A commotion broke out on the square. They were attempting, I believe, to surround the house from which that human protest had come. But just at that moment my attention was drawn elsewhere... the unexpected occurred!

A man on horseback had entered through the gate... he was dressed in civilian clothes, his chest covered with decorations. He dismounted from the saddle and guards and soldiers saluted him in military fashion. It was Monsieur de Collet, the Empress's major-domo. He came up to me, looked me full in the face, and noticing my trembling, said: "It is cold, Monsieur Rudio!" I replied: "Yes, but they will not leave me time to catch a chill." A puff of smoke struck him full in the face. "I beg your pardon; my hands are bound and besides... it is the last!" — "Oh, not at all; you will smoke again!" He murmured a few words in the ear of the executioner first, then of the old priest, and both, slowly and silently, withdrew. Then, turning once more to me, as I questioned him with the expression of my face: "I shall come to your cell towards nine o'clock and explain everything." I spun on my heels and found myself face to face with Felice Orsini.

He slowed his step: "What is this?"

— They are sending me back to prison. I know nothing more.

— And Pieri?

— He is up there!

— Better two than three!

We continued on our opposite ways... he to fecundate with his blood the ideal of the Patria; I toward the prison... and that return to life came upon me as something grievous as a violence, so fully was I prepared for death! After a few steps, I turned my head, and, by a singular correspondence of souls, Orsini turned his as well. "Farewell!" he said, with a nod of his head. The black veil that wrapped it, grown thick and opaque here and there in its folds, concealed his face, so that he already seemed to me half lost in the mysterious shadow of eternity...

I found the large room and my cell crowded with soldiers and officers. A group was busily gathered about my bed, upon which lay, in a dead faint and in convulsions, my faithful guard, Saturno. I made my way through to him, and while a compassionate hand unbound my arms, I kept calling him by name. My voice proved more efficacious than the sprinklings of water and the smelling-salts... He opened his eyes, looked at me as one lost in a dream, then his face brightened with a light that revealed a friendship profoundly sincere. All at once, he assailed me with these words: "The wager! The wager!... you have lost!... you shall pay the champagne!"

"Later, my friend. This is an hour for grief. Down there, men are dying!" Almost as though to confirm my words, the clock of the Roquette struck: seven strokes. In the prison a sepulchral silence fell. The tragic hour bestows strange clairvoyance... the officers uncovered their heads. At that moment, the blade of the guillotine... the heads... the vile basket...

The Count di Rudio, overcome by emotion, could not continue. The insufficiency of the heart, which was already undermining his robust old age, took away his breath. I knew that to observe him at such moments irritated him and worsened the ailment, and it was with a true sense of relief that I heard the quick step of Signora Elisa. She entered and did not perceive the gravity of the moment. She stretched out her arms, and her left hand was laid over her open right palm. She was smiling with her lips, smiling with her blue eyes, as though she carried a great happiness within her soul and wished to parcel it out and share it with us. She uncovered her palm and displayed... two chicks nestled there, of a luminous whiteness. Who remembers the white hen that was brooding in the courtyard? "They have hatched!" said the Signora, still smiling. Those two little atoms of life looked at us in wonder, as though asking: "Who are you?"

I turned my face away... the Californian sun, near its setting, peered through the green foliage. My sad eyes, intolerant of light, closed; but the reddish disc remained imprinted on my retina. O ardent imagination! O vividness of evocation! In that blood-red glare, the severed head of Felice Orsini appeared... Gripped by the hair in the executioner's fist, it gasped, breathless, that last, fateful wish of his: *"Viva l'Italia! Viva la Francia!"*

My lips did not move, but my soul gave a cry: "Oh, how dearly the Patria has cost us!"
