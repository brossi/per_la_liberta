## Chapter Twenty-One

From the heights overlooking the mines of Agordo, I cast a final glance at my brother, who was shrinking into the plain below. Then I resolutely took the path up into the mountains, beginning a flight that was to prove full of thrills and truly singular. Upon reaching the zone of perpetual snows, I slowed my pace, both from exhaustion and from the growing difficulties, but also because I felt hidden and made safe by the space and the solitude. I peered into the depths... a great many of what, on the ascent, had seemed to be heights, now appeared level with the plain. The hatred of the powerful, I thought, could not reach so far as to disturb that virgin silence.

But having escaped the snares of men, I nearly fell victim to those of nature. It had been forty-eight hours since I had closed my eyes, and I felt terribly predisposed to that morbid slumber which the frost and the altitude can render mortal. At a certain point, I was seized as by a vertigo of the sublime; my legs would no longer hold me, and I fell prostrate upon the snow.

Now see to what extent dream and reality can intertwine and become confused! I was asleep and incapable of moving a muscle... of that there was no doubt. And yet my mind, vigilant within my inert body, was conscious of that boundless whiteness, of that fearful solitude; I could feel my fragile heart beating, the icy wind blowing about me, and, amidst the grim images of life that crowded into my brain, the idea of my peril predominated, rigid, motionless as a boulder.

Suddenly, I saw myself distinctly, with the constables at my heels. I run, I run, I run: or rather, it is the mountain that comes rushing towards me. But the constables are equally swift, and swifter... I can already hear the crunching of the snow beneath their boots; already their foul mouths thunder the *Alt!*, and I run, run... In my head, several shots rang out.

A second later I was on my feet, awake, perfectly awake! The shots were still echoing from crag to crag. I scanned the valley and spied a line of soldiers running along the forest, rifle in hand. They halted and formed a circle around a human figure, prostrate and dark against the snow. I noted their agitated gestures; then I saw, or so it seemed to me, a soldier prod the flesh of the fallen man with his bayonet, as if searching for a remnant of life. A smuggler.

A smuggler — I thought — mistaken, in these days of fearful suspicion, for game of greater importance. It was he who, stiff and unconscious, would later cause my poor mother so much anguish.

I crossed the summit as quickly as I could. The sky remained splendid; the snow, very hard on the northern slope, no longer gave way beneath my feet. I received a more than honest welcome, and bread and milk, from a mountaineer; then, at Neumark, I handsomely tempered my cheese with an internal ablution of excellent beer. As dusk fell, I set foot in Bolzano.

If the crowd helps to conceal, I could not have arrived at a more opportune moment. For several days the life of the entire district had been flowing back to Bolzano, attracted by the annual fair. In those times and in those places of difficult communication, the fairs took on the boisterous character of a village feast-day. Haggling brokers and frowning farmers on the hunt for a florin; mountain women, beautiful with that rustic beauty which is born of the open air and the sun, having acquired their plenary indulgence, departed in large bands, singing in chorus. There was a reek of wine-casks issuing in puffs from the taverns; there were herds of heifers and oxen that solemnly forced their passage through the human herds; mountebanks who drew the crowd with the grimaces of their painted faces, to the beat of the bass drum; sacks piled high and ironmongery spread out upon the paving-stones, and baskets brimming with fruit, and tables of confectionery sheltered beneath enormous umbrellas, and a great crush and a humming of voices and a riot of colours... and high above, reconciling every base human dissonance, a mighty wave of bells ringing out in festivity.

Not without working my elbows, I reached the main square, all cluttered with stalls and vendors. At one corner of the square stood the inn of the "Queen of England." With one of its sides it flanked the principal street, and it was upon this side that the main entrance opened.

I entered, went up to the first floor, and found myself face to face with a *chellerina* with a rosy little face and a snow-white apron... only to be told that the rooms were all occupied, that I should have to content myself with sharing a bed with some stranger — and good luck finding a willing fellow!

"Very well! In any case, I can begin with a good supper."

I was shown into the dining hall. Instinctively, I sat at the first small table, to the left, near the entrance. I was alone. The ample room was dimly lit by a single lamp. By its reflection I recognised, on one wall, the effigy of Ferdinand — the Emperor who last remembered he was a despot in order to demand, on his deathbed, a plate of gnocchi with butter. I looked, but in vain, for that of Franz Joseph... perhaps he had not yet found means to penetrate so far.

But I found something worse. Near the door was pasted a large notice upon which the double-headed eagle appeared conspicuously. Amidst the dark and jumbled lines, one name stood out in larger letters, a name that was well known to me — that of... Carlo Camillo di Rudio. The devil, what is this? I sprang to my feet and read. Austria was placing a price of six thousand florins upon my head. To take me alive or dead... to denounce me! — a trifling labour, the bounden duty of an honest citizen for some great votary of Order and the Law. By what a slender thread my salvation hung!

I should lie if I said I supped with a tranquil spirit. If being in a crowded centre had seemed my salvation but a moment before, now I glimpsed in it the greatest of dangers. How to emerge from that vortex unobserved? Was it possible that among so many people there was not a living soul who knew me? who might suspect, by intuition, the truth?

As if in answer, on the stairs, amidst the varied tread of feet, voices rang out speaking in the Bellunese dialect — voices which, I would have sworn, were not unknown to me. I hid my face in my right hand and spied anxiously between my fingers. The door swung open; several strangers entered, and behind them, one after the other, four stout fellows... the smugglers of the Trevis valley, the Burrasca brothers.

They looked at me, they recognised me; but they feigned ignorance. They sat at the neighbouring table, and the eldest, who was beside me, touched my foot with his; then, without turning his face, pretending to speak to his brothers: "I am glad to see you. Given the rumours going about..."

At that moment the *chellerina* with the rosy little face and the snow-white apron entered. She lit another lamp... the letters on the notice with the double-headed eagle now loomed energetically, almost doubling their eloquence.

I said: "Saved!... only to leap from the frying-pan into the fire." And pointing to the notice: "What a fine stroke of luck for you!"

Four faces darkened. The eldest of the Burrasca, striking a fist in the air: "Morsels seasoned with shame do not pass down this throat!"

And another of the brothers, in a deep bass voice: "The blood of traitors does not flow in the veins of the Burrasca!"

They had the chance to prove it. A few minutes more, and the reek of the gendarmerie spread through the wide hall. One ruffian remained on the threshold and, leaning his back against the jamb, barred the door. Another ruffian came forward and asked me, in a brusque tone, for my passport. I drew it from my wallet and was unfolding it when, growing impatient, he snatched it from my hand. He glanced over it, drew nearer the lamp to examine it better, and ended by saying: "I do not understand Italian. You must come with me to the gendarmerie."

"Hold fast!" cried one of the Burrasca, who well understood the dangers of the fine proposition being made to me.

An instant... a butchery! I snatch the passport back from the importunate fellow and, before he can react, the smuggler's fist strikes him under the chin like a sledgehammer. He fell, struck the nape of his neck on the tiled floor, and moved no more.

Meanwhile, another smuggler, who had approached the threshold on the pretext of reading the terms of the reward, lands his blow fair upon the teeth of the ruffian blocking it... blood spurts from his mouth, from his nose; he staggers and goes down in a heap.

"Wings to your feet!" came the cry. But I needed no exhortation... I was already leaping down the steps four at a time, clutching a pistol — a providential inspiration! A third gendarme was on guard at the foot of the stairs. He turns, sees me, sees the weapon, and understands its tacit threat, reinforced by a forceful imprecation. He lets me pass without lifting a finger, and I back away with my pistol at the ready.

But in that constable's compliance there lurked a goodly measure of strategy... he had averted his own danger and had driven me into a greater trap. A fourth gendarme, having heard the commotion above, had run to summon the patrol. Both were advancing at a run as I turned the corner into the square, and it was a miracle I did not fall into their arms. Before me the patrol, behind me the gendarme from the landing, who had followed me and was now pointing me out, gesticulating furiously.

Night had fallen. The square, crowded with stalls and people, was lit by variegated lanterns, Venetian-style. With a sidelong movement I leapt into that thicket of a crowd. "Stop him, stop him! Catch him, catch him!" But in the confusion, no one thinks to act. As for the soldiers, compact by virtue of discipline yet hampered by their arms, they content themselves with pursuing me at a run along the sides of the square... I slip through and cut across it. Then, put on the alert that I had ducked into a certain alleyway, they divided into two squads; one flung itself at my heels, the other rounded the block and ran to the other end of the same alleyway, to bar my passage and take me between two fires.

They ended by finding themselves face to face.

From beneath the entryway of a house, with my eye to the keyhole, I observed their movements, noted their faces, heard their words. "*Der Teufel!* Has the earth swallowed him up?" They debated for a moment and concluded that I must be — the goodness of them! — a pickpocket. The Police had cast good nets throughout the town and, sooner or later, I should be caught in them. They re-formed their ranks and departed.

Without a special warrant, the constables could not, at that hour, violate a private domicile. I felt myself temporarily safe.

I climbed the stairs to the third landing... the last. There were four doors; two opposite, one to the right, the other to the left. I listened... absolute silence! Then I sat upon the steps, and with my elbows on my knees and my face in my palms, I began to ponder. Imperceptibly, my thoughts darkened and I fell asleep.

When I returned to the reality of things, the first light of dawn was showing through the skylight, and already, beyond the door on the left, voices could be heard alternating in brief and indistinct snatches of conversation, with the clatter of plates and crockery.

I rise, dust off my trousers, and am about to descend when the door opens and a woman of uncertain age, in scanty dress, catches sight of me and lets out a cry.

"What is it?" a gruff, masculine voice demands from within. Before I could open my mouth, I found before me a stocky man in his shirt-sleeves, an apron at his waist, an awl in his hand.

I urged them not to be alarmed. I was a cattle-dealer arrived from Switzerland the previous evening, at a late hour. No matter how many inns I had tried, I had not managed to find a bed, even offering to pay any price imaginable. And rather than spend the night in the open...

"By Christ!" said the man. "From the pavement to a landing... what difference does it make? Come in, you can warm yourself by drinking a cup of coffee in our company."

I offered to add a drop of spirits, and drew out my purse which, to the eye, seemed well furnished.

What followed was a three-cornered contest... the cobbler affecting to take offence that I should think him capable of demanding payment for a kindness; the woman drawing back, with gestures of false modesty, so as not to let my gold coin touch her forearm and hand; and I thrusting aside the man's protests with a succession of "But... But really... for shame!" and pressing the woman and doing her courteous violence. I ended by triumphing, and then added: "Indeed, to tell the truth, the cold has given me an appetite — such an appetite!... and in the town, these days, one eats... one eats like a dog! If you would be so kind as to buy a few mouthfuls... to your own taste. A light repast does no harm to friendship!"

When the woman had gone down a couple of flights, I raised my voice and called her back to commend to her the wondrous virtue of holding water in one's mouth. I was not without friends in Bolzano, and if it reached their ears that I had preferred sleeping on a landing to disturbing them...

It was an imprudence — ninety-nine times out of a hundred, the most effective means of making a woman chatter is to recommend she keep silent. But my good fortune would have it that, precisely from that crack, my salvation should spring.

No sooner had my hostess reached the street and the outer door was heard to slam shut, than the cobbler took me by the sleeve and, dragging me inside to the kitchen, fixed me with a long, wordless stare. Then, abruptly: "You have been spinning me tales. A cattle-dealer, you! You are a deserter!"

— Quiet! How did you come to know?

For all reply, he twisted his lips and tapped his right eye with his forefinger — the gesture which, among all known peoples, signifies: "I have too much wit to be taken in!"

With the conversation once placed on that footing, I found myself, as the saying goes, on home ground, nor did I lack arguments and precise particulars with which to enmesh my host ever more tightly in his own misapprehension. And as he listened, he demonstrated his growing confidence by winking at me and giving me certain little claps on the shoulder that were meant to convey all the warmth of a demonstration of brotherly affection — for the deserter enjoyed, in my day among those populations, the fame that rightly belongs to heroes, and not to lend him a hand savoured to them of cowardice.

Had my spirit not been terribly preoccupied, that would have been a day of rest. My host showed a rough but thoroughly proved cordiality. Only, too much of a good thing does harm, and when, solicitous for what was to come, he began to devise plans for my escape, he became unconsciously the cause of no small anxiety to me. In his enthusiasm, he ceased not from contriving projects, each more fantastic than the last, and on the pretext of knowing the country better and of being a shrewder judge of men's dispositions, he persisted in attempting to force my hand — I who, in that particular game, was risking my skin.

In the end I persuaded him to hire a carriage and drive me out of the town in time to coincide with the changing of the stage-coach bound for Marano, which took place several miles away, in open country, late at night.

"The Police have cast good nets throughout the town," the corporal of the patrol had said the evening before. But no sooner had Bolzano and its fair fallen into slumber than I slipped through mesh and mesh, almost triumphantly, reclining upon the cushions of my carriage.

An incident, not worth the telling, was at most the cause of a little apprehension for me at Marano. But at Salzburg, in the German Tyrol, I ran a danger so great that to have escaped it without excessive effort seems to me almost a dream.

The postilion had pulled up at the inn of the Posta, indicating thereby the place where I was to pass the night, which was already far advanced. I went up at once, to wash my hands and face, to the room assigned to me, and when I was called down for supper, I took the key from the lock but, inadvertently, did not shut the door. Having reached the foot of the stairs — the recounting of these trifles is necessary — I set my hat upon a small table and hung the key on its little hook.

In the dining hall I found myself alone; but the inevitable pair of gendarmes soon arrived... unarmed this time. They ordered a tankard, a pack of cards, and struck up a game of tarocco. When supper was done, I drew near to them as though interested in the play, and, to be brief, we ended by starting a game in three. We passed in this manner a good hour, drinking to each other's health and exchanging banter, until those endearments between the devil and the holy water were cut short by the hostess, who came to remind us that the Law is the Law, and that the prescribed hour of closing had already struck some minutes since.

I asked her: "At what hour does the stage depart?"

— At seven precisely! — In that case, be so good as to wake me at four. I must go up to the mountain to bargain for certain heifers, and should like to be back in time to continue my journey by the stage. I can sleep in peace!

— Between two pillows!

I parted from the gendarmes with great shaking of hands; but, scarcely arrived in the corridor, I took up again my dark thoughts and made my way upstairs without reclaiming the key. Go back down? The door stood ajar; I could fasten it from within with the bolt. I entered, threw myself upon the bed, and snuffed the candle.

It had been many nights since I had laid myself down at full length upon a soft mattress, with a good blanket over my legs. When I awoke, the sun, already high on the horizon, was beating full upon my face.

Confound the hostess! I came downstairs with my spirit in tumult, ears alert... the inn seemed deserted. Whereupon, concentrating all my rage into my knuckles, I knocked upon the small table so energetically that the candlesticks rattled.

And out from the kitchen bustled the old landlady, her face framed in her cap. She was neat, dressed in her Sunday best. On seeing me she stopped short, her mouth agape. She allowed herself to be assailed by a tempest of reproaches; then, with perfect composure: "Good heavens! Whose fault is it, pray? The key to your room was there on its hook, and from that I concluded you were already on your way to the mountain. If you do not believe me, ask your two friends of last evening. They came by at about four o'clock. They wished to speak with you." — "Forgive me, I am sorry. I told them you were already far away... on the mountain." — "They came to speak with me? Prepare a good breakfast for three. I shall go and find them."

And I departed with every appearance of a man appeased. But the hostess lost both her labour and her expense.

It was a Sunday in September. The village population had poured into the little church, which must have been packed to the doors, for a human swarm was pressing outside on the small churchyard. Crossing the square, I saw the plume of a gendarme's hat rising above those dark heads, and rejoiced at the sight. "There is one who will not arrest me!" And I added: "So true is it that even the Mass serves some purpose."

Once clear of the village, I left the main road and struck off through the fields along hedged lanes; then I took to the pine-wood, which served me as an admirable screen.

The nearest territory was Muntzen, a cluster of houses situated astride the frontier. It had, like Janus of ancient memory, two faces: one gloomy, the other smiling — the former Austrian, the latter Swiss and republican. I knew this, and I knew moreover that the frontier line divided, politically speaking, the village square and the inn, which thus found itself under two jurisdictions. Hanging on the wall of the common room was a cuckoo-clock. The gendarmes of the Empire could not pass beyond that clock without provoking an international incident. On this side, the regime of the cudgel, the manacles, the gallows; on the other, the regime of a free people, breath drawn in full lungs, salvation!

I crossed the frontier at a solitary point, entered the village, and came into the inn from the Swiss side. Setting foot in the common room, I was looking about for the cuckoo-clock when my attention was drawn to a group of *Jäger* — the militia which Austria posted to guard the frontiers. Among them, welcome sight! the uniform and well-known face of one of those guardian angels who, early that very morning, had come to impress upon my wrists their holy stigmata. I could not restrain a burst of loud laughter.

"Holla, friend! Bad hunting this morning. And to think the blackbird was in his cage. Had you but climbed the stairs, the six thousand florins of the reward would have clinked in your pocket sooner or later!"

He admitted that I had proved the cleverer, and carried his graciousness so far as to offer congratulations. "Indeed, to show you my sincerity," said he, "come, and I'll stand you a bottle!"

— And I a whole cask, if it please you. But you must be so good as to come over to this side! — And I rubbed my hands together.

But that gleeful exuberance, so natural in one who has left the open sea behind him, was for many long months my last; for many long months, thinking back upon it, I felt as it were a pang of remorse.

The failure which had already been manifest to me grew more pronounced with every piece of news gathered as I pressed forward into free territory, until it assumed the proportions of a true disaster, and my spirit was left as though engulfed by it.
