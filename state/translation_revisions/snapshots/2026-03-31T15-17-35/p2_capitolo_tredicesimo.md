# Chapter Thirteen

And so it was that events came rushing headlong to their conclusion. First of all, the rash confidences that Giuseppe Pieri had made to Rosina Hartmann in Brussels had produced, as I had feared, their unhappy effect. Scarcely had Pieri departed than that wretched woman hastened to denounce him to the French Minister, who telegraphed to Paris information only too explicit concerning a probable attempt on the Emperor's life. When Pieri descended from the train, without the slightest suspicion, several secret agents were already posted at the station with orders to arrest him. Unfortunately for the final outcome of our enterprise, he had allowed his beard to grow and passed through the midst of those bloodhounds without being recognised. But the Police would not resign themselves to this rebuff, and charged the agent Hébert, who knew Pieri personally, with seeking him out. And perhaps even his patient efforts would have come to nothing, had not Pieri himself, meeting him a few minutes before the attempt, stopped him then and there with the most consummate carelessness. He greeted him with the words: "Comment ça va, mon vieux?" And the other recognised him at once, less by his appearance than by his voice, which, as I have said, had a peculiar timbre, almost like a bagpipe. "Ah, ah! I find you at last! You are under arrest!"

When Pieri saw him coming towards him and making signs, he did not trouble himself greatly about it, most likely because his own demeanour at that moment unconsciously reflected the stoic indifference of the Tuscan. The latter had the bomb in his pocket and found himself in a terrible predicament, perhaps, but he had not the slightest doubt of success.

A little of this, and a little of his boastful nature — which was far from feigning, in his character, a simple manner while they searched him, or dissembling in any way — led him to abandon himself to a deplorable act of braggadocio. "By now," said he, "your Emperor is not worth the sole of my slippers!"

Later, as we have seen, he revealed his lodging. Perhaps he believed me to be safe, as indeed I had believed myself to be. But he had the fault of worsening his own situation by admitting afterwards that the foreigner found in his room was precisely the man they sought to trace.

I recount these things as they occurred, but I am far from setting myself up as a stern judge. If it is exceeding difficult to perceive true correctness of judgment in a man acting in ordinary circumstances, with perfect calm in both mind and heart, how can one demand it of one who is thrown into confusion by the extraordinary gravity of events, of one who is led astray by passion? Having been witness to the last days and last moments of Giuseppe Pieri, and to the heights he was capable of reaching, I do not hesitate for an instant to affirm that he was heedless, nothing more than heedless. If I myself, as I have already had occasion to admit, believed for a moment otherwise, I was driven to it not only by the bitterness inherent in my own downfall, which I rightly attributed to him, but also because my temporary pessimism had been aggravated by an unpleasant incident that occurred in my presence between Pieri and Orsini — an incident which, at no great distance from the attempt, had raised as it were a barrier bristling with mistrust even between those two generous men. I shall not relate the particulars, for it had its root, in substance, in a misunderstanding and would perhaps interest no one.

One of the bombs which I had occasion to examine on the desk of the Prefect of Police was therefore the one confiscated from Giuseppe Pieri. The other was the very bomb that Felice Orsini had laid upon the pavement opposite the inn of the Broggi, in order to have a moment to wipe away the blood that, trickling down over his eyes, was blinding him. A passer-by, a certain Villaume, noticing on the ground a black handkerchief wrapped about something bulky, had first pushed it aside with his foot, then picked it up, and then handed it over to the Authorities. A little more force in that foot, and Villaume would have been hurled, blasted, into eternal darkness!

To what is the arrest of Gomez to be attributed? To that southern nature so susceptible to excessive exaltation, so susceptible to profound depression. Having thrown his bomb, instead of making his escape he had crossed the Rue Le Pelletier and taken refuge, together with a crowd in a state of utter terror, in the trattoria of the Broggi. It is said that he wept — he himself denied it to me. Nor, for that matter, would such a circumstance have had any further consequence than that of drawing upon him the innocent, passing attention of those around him. Unfortunately, he was carrying a revolver, and the thought of the probable complications attendant upon this circumstance, exaggerated as they were by his state of mind, led him to attempt a stratagem that amounted to the complete abandonment of all circumspection. He stealthily drew the weapon from his pocket and, furtively, concealed it beneath a shelf. He was seen, and the rash movement was interpreted as an explicit act of self-incrimination. From that moment, Gomez became a prisoner of the People.

Who delivered him, shortly afterwards, to the guards of the under-sergeant Michat, who, posted on duty upon the steps of the peristyle, having seen — or rather having imagined he had seen — the bombs thrown from the house opposite the theatre, was conducting a prompt search of the premises.

From the moment of his arrest, Gomez considered himself absolutely lost, and without reflecting, in his extraordinary agitation, that every moment gained might mean the salvation of his companions, he declared, then and there, that his name was Swiney and that he was in the service of an English gentleman. At the same time he named as his own lodging the hotel Saxe-Coburg in the Rue St. Honoré. True, he did not pronounce the name of Thomas Allsop; but the Police were now placed upon the right scent. And when, following it, they brought Gomez to the indicated address, the hall-porter of the hotel revealed that the supposed Swiney had been escorted there by the concierge of Number 10, Rue Monthabor — the address of his no less supposed employer.

Without delay, the Police proceeded thither and, upon the indications of the concierge, broke into the lodging of Thomas Allsop. He was in bed, his head bandaged — proof enough that he had, at the very least, been in the vicinity of the attempt at the moment it was carried out. And so the arrest of the head of the conspiracy was effected.

In this manner the Police, without displaying any extraordinary acuteness and without great exertion, succeeded in laying hands upon four of its principal authors within barely a few hours of the tragic event.

It remained to establish the individual part respectively played by each. But this was a simple question of time. What defence has a prisoner, intent upon concealing the truth, save his own denial? Which denial ends by becoming absurd and ridiculous in the face of the evidence that, sooner or later, overwhelms him. Man has arrived at the capacity to verify and even to minutely reconstruct events that unfolded upon this Earth before he himself appeared upon it; events that no ear recorded, that no eye perceived, that no creature could denounce. Let one imagine, then, whether he should not be capable of reweaving the history of exceptional events, of recent date, which unfolded beneath the thousand and thousand eyes of a curious and gossiping society! The hard truth is this: Woe to the suspect who does not know how to remain *uccel di bosco* — a bird of the forest, free and untraceable!

There was one man who remained such, though the snare was so close about him that all who observed the situation and were acquainted with the facts believed he had been caught in it. The French Police, shaken by the unexpected tragedy and by the responsibility of having failed to prevent it, had at first somewhat lost their heads. Nor did the mysterious rumours floating in the air do anything to restore them. For it was well understood that an Empire without deep roots, born of crime, has an undercurrent of terribly hostile forces ready to break forth at the slightest extraordinary event, however unforeseen. There was fear of the Parisian multitude, so ready to raise barricades; fear of certain Regiments that had shown themselves, unexpectedly, restless. And more than one highly placed personage, implicated in the grim events of the 2nd December, dreading popular vengeance, had secretly made ready to seek his own safety in flight. This explains the excessively courteous manner of the Police... For the persecuted of today might become the masters of tomorrow.

Nevertheless, something had to be attempted, and no sooner had it been established with certainty that Italians had a hand in the conspiracy than a veritable mass arrest of them was set in motion. In those dragnets, carried out moreover at random, Francesco Crispi was also caught.

The future Minister of the Kingdom of Italy was able to prove an alibi. He had remained — he declared — the whole of that evening shut within his own room, and his household confirmed his statement. He was set at liberty, and upon leaving he did not encounter his Gomez — that good, simple, and high-strung soul — who might involuntarily have plunged him back into trouble.

Nor was that the only grave error of the Police. They had occupied all the railway stations and given orders that no foreigner might leave Paris — with the exception of the English. I would not now wish to name names; but setting aside the fact that the conspiracy had been prepared in England, with the involvement of Englishmen, I know for a positive certainty that several of the latter had been present under arms at the scene of the attempt. The way of escape stood open, and doubtless they needed no urging to avail themselves of it.

And, speaking of insufficient penetration — look here! This dagger is the very same that was hidden beneath my cloak by Signora Olivieri of Locarno, while in Italy men were trembling with passion and facing the gallows. From that day, save for brief intervals, it has followed me on every one of my wanderings. I have said that upon departing from London I had concealed it in the tiger-embroidered valise. Well, the Police rummaged through it with their heavy hands more than once — during my interrogation they took it up, examined it, and made of it a piece of evidence. When it was handed over to Elisa as my private property, the dagger was still between the lining and the board where I had hidden it. A Damascus blade — a terrible blade!

It seemed that upon the face of the writer there appeared, as he examined it, a slight expression of repugnance, for the Count smiled bitterly and said: "Honest are the Governments and fortunate the countries where such weapons inspire horror, or serve as mere curiosities in a Museum. But in my time and under the Austrian Government the matter was altogether different. In his struggle against his oppressors, the People has no choice of arms; it defends itself and attacks, not as it would wish, but as it can. The fault lies with those who compel it to do so. And besides, motion, force, wisdom, morality — all is relative. The nails driven into the flesh of Christ were infamous. But in 1853 the common people of Milan, yearning for liberty, gathered together to strike with the very nails of their *barcacce* — their river-barges. Every piece of iron became sacred in their hands. Let us not disavow methods that tended, with desperate resolve, towards a desperate end. They form part of our glory. I maintain that the dagger cleared by two thirds the path that led to the wars which won independence for our Fatherland."

He fell silent for a moment, and I answered, smiling in my turn. Carlo di Rudio was quiet for a few seconds, and his expression grew calm once more. Then he resumed:

Here then, mounted step by step, is the staircase that led to the abyss destined to swallow up both us and every hope we had: the childish trust of Pieri in Hartmann; the storm at sea that prevented my meeting with Orsini at the Paris station; the contact struck up between the executors of the attempt, contrary to what had been agreed; the failure of Pieri to play his part and his too easy loquacity immediately after his arrest; the nerves of Gomez. Add to these — of which I shall speak later — the character of Felice Orsini, little or not at all suited to the life of a conspirator, and the good faith of an English young lady, blinded by love for him. If other causes have been put forward, they were invented either by the malice of men or by the imagination of novelists.

I had forgotten one thing! There was yet another cause, secondary perhaps, but one which must needs be taken into account: the defection of Simon Bernard. He did not come to Paris. While others, for having attempted to serve his France, were being driven at great strides towards the scaffold, he gave a fine proof of his own courage — strolling tranquilly about the streets of London, prattling of his own heroism!

On the very morning of my arrest, after a few hours of tormented sleep and as many more of wakefulness spent in brooding over the revelations that Gomez might have made, I was conducted to the Palais de Justice.

I had scarcely set foot in the antechamber when I was already obliged to convince myself of the ill wind that was blowing. Seated in a row along the wall, almost as though displayed in a shop window, were four persons of my acquaintance: the proprietress of the hotel "France et Champagne"; the Maître d'Hôtel; and Monsieur and Madame Morand, the concierges of Felice Orsini's house. All of them summoned, beyond any doubt, for the purpose of identifying me. And so far, nothing amiss. As da Sylva I had passed myself off, and as da Sylva they would denounce me. But I had denied all knowledge of Felice Orsini. The Pipelet couple would give the lie to that — my deposition was becoming untenable!

But the harshest blow came when I was brought before the examining magistrate. It was the same personage who had interrogated me a few hours before. He received me with a smile, as though seeing me again afforded him immense pleasure. "Ah, ah! And how do you do this morning, Signor Conte? No longer da Sylva this morning; but Carlo of the Counts di Rudio!"

No doubt remained: Antonio Gomez had let slip my name. I must say at once, in his exculpation, that both Pieri and Orsini had always addressed me, in his presence, either as Moretto or as di Rudio. He, in all probability, was unaware that I carried a Portuguese passport.

And so I told over again, point by point, the same story I had narrated at the first interrogation. But, reflecting upon the Pipelet couple waiting in the antechamber, I admitted to having paid a brief visit to Orsini. With no mysterious purpose, moreover. We had conspired together in Switzerland and I had wished to see him again in order to revive old and cherished memories.

— But why did you deny it last night?

— Simply so as not to compromise a man who, from what I could gather, was already in difficulties. I do not believe that any of your Laws required me to fail in this duty of honour.

— And how does it come about that you carry a false passport?

— As a most natural precaution for a political exile who might have the spies of Austria at his heels. And how can that be held against me by one who did the same when he landed at Boulogne with Luigi Napoleone?

The thrust struck full in the chest; but the magistrate contented himself with a grimace. He resumed: "Now tell me everything you know concerning this attempt."

— Only what the guards who arrested me have told me. I know that there was a wish to kill the Emperor, and nothing more.

— That may be. In any case I must inform you that your friends have denounced you as their accomplice, and that you will be tried along with them!

And he gave the order that I be taken to Mazas.
