CHAPTER TEN

Having abandoned the barricade to its fate, Trolli and I made our way toward the boulevard. But scarcely had we crossed the Pont Saint-Denis when we were compelled to agree that Martial Law was being rigorously applied, and that the surest way to invite death was to walk as a pair. We found corpses scattered here and there. I can still vividly recall one — a water-seller, lying face-down, his vessel upon his back, his arms outstretched, his head literally shattered to fragments. Blood, brains, and slices of lemon lay mingled in a horrible confusion.

We separated, having agreed not to lose sight of one another and to leave the city by the Barrière du Trône. To pass through it, guarded as it was by a good body of customs officers and soldiers, was a rather awkward undertaking. Before reaching it, I purchased a large loaf of bread and tucked it under my arm. Trolli guessed the purpose and did likewise. As workmen returning from their labour we were scrutinised from head to foot, but suffered no molestation.

And when darkness fell inexorably over the bare countryside, we rejoined one another. Trolli asked me where we should go to lay our heads.

"We have a half — but we are free! Where shall we lay our heads? What does it matter to a wayfarer where, so long as it be not within the lunette of the guillotine? Switzerland is far. But we have known how to cross Spain — we shall cross France as well!"

We had been walking for some miles when a traveller overtook us in a small carriage. Catching sight of us, he drew up his reins, and once the horse had resumed its trot, the worthy fellow invited us to climb up. I cast an appraising glance at him. He was decently dressed and had the air of a good-natured, unassuming countryman. His nose and eyes were exceedingly red, and not solely from the cold — he could not have been a spy. And besides — he alone, and we two, possessed of nerve enough. We accepted.

Scarcely had the horse resumed its trot when the worthy fellow asked us where we were bound.

"To Troy."

"A long walk! I am not going so far myself; but I shall shorten your road."

And he proceeded to give an account of himself and his affairs. He had gone up to Paris to sell the vegetables — the produce of his own little garden and his own labours. And he showed us his calloused hands. The harvests had been rather lean; but with a little thrift and a sharp eye, his rabbit and his little wine had never failed him. And he ran on, reeling off the long litany of his amours with the goodwives of the neighbourhood. Of Paris, of the coup d'état, of the horrors of that day — not a phrase, not a word; as though he had left behind him a city perfectly at peace and silent. The wine he had swallowed made him talkative, but not enough to draw him from a prudent reserve.

At a certain point something slipped out regarding certain speculations of his, and he concluded that if they proceeded according to his hopes, he would have enough to live comfortably upon until the "Lady without a Nose" came along to deliver him the fatal scythe-blow. And with that allusion to Death he fell silent, as if absorbed in deep meditation. Ten minutes later, he was snoring.

I then took the reins, and laid on the whip — trot, horse, trot. We passed broad stretches of land, we passed thickets, we passed villages. Dusk came on, night fell — and the peasant was snoring still.

All at once, in the still air, a brazen bell-stroke rang out slowly. It was eleven o'clock. Shortly after, the wheels of the carriage were grinding over cobblestones. And then the horse stopped dead, and for all my flogging it would not move a step forward nor a step backward.

The strange thing was that this abrupt halt had the power to break the peasant's deep sleep. "Sacré cœur!" he exclaimed, after boring his eyes into the darkness. "Where have you brought me? Twelve miles from home!"

He takes out his anger — mark the justice of it! — on the horse, which ought surely to have remembered where its manger was. But in the end he consoles himself with the reflection that we were not in a wilderness. In the village he had good friends. "Do you see that strip of light? The inn is still open, and the innkeeper is an honest man. I shall stand you a drink!"

Half an hour later he went to bed so as to be able to set off betimes. Trolli and I stepped out into the open. Warmed within and without, we faced once more the darkness of the countryside, and that yet deeper darkness of the unknown.

Our subversive Society enmeshed the whole of France in a network whose threads came together again at Troy, so that we found no great difficulty in making contacts there and obtaining passage on the stage-coach as far as Langres — the place where, the meshes drawing together, they formed the next knot.

The horses were still champing under the gateway of the inn at Langres, and already I was making my way in haste to the dwelling of the postal official. A letter of introduction, a few conventional signs, and he would have furnished us the means to continue our journey. I pulled the bell, and immediately there appeared upon the threshold a lady of uncertain age, yet not without beauty.

"My husband is not at home, nor do I know when he will return."

"I am the bearer of a letter which I should like to deliver to him personally."

The lady's mouth contracted, as though between tongue and palate she were pressing a slice of lemon. She became inquisitive: "A letter? From whom? Give it here, give it here! I will undertake to deliver it to him — in his very own hands."

"I beg your pardon! I have my orders, and —"

Her face had gone pale. She insisted and extended her right hand — her fingers were trembling. All the more did I consider caution my duty. She went back inside, slamming the door.

That evening I was seated in the common room of the inn, before the fireplace, conversing cheerfully with the other guests, when — to delay the sweet process of digestion — there appeared, unannounced, the sharp-tempered creature with a face at once sour and melancholy. She had two gendarmes at her back.

The purpose of the visit was made plain at once. The lady demanded the surrender of the mysterious letter. The gendarmes had come to confiscate it.

I rose, drew out my pocket-book calmly and, removing the letter of introduction from it, drew the company's attention to the fact that it lay in an open envelope. Then, with a leap toward the fireplace, I cast it upon the flames.

A little confusion ensued. The gendarmes, considering their "dignity in uniform" offended, wished to proceed to my arrest — the more so as the other guests had been unable to restrain themselves from laughing uproariously. I was obliged to protest with all my energy. The very Laws of the Republic specified that only sealed letters were subject to confiscation; I had therefore, and quite rightfully, escaped an act of unlawful coercion.

In truth, the two worthies had placed themselves upon false ground: the Law was against its own defenders; moreover, their orders had been to confiscate the document, not to arrest its bearer. "We shall go for more precise instructions." They withdrew, followed by the lady, crestfallen and almost in tears; and I saw them no more.

Late in the evening, the postal official himself appeared, and I received the key to the enigma. His wife, an honest woman and possessed of many virtues, had the weakness of being jealous to the point of madness. This passion of hers had of late been inflamed by the mysterious coming and going attendant upon our political Society, of whose existence she was entirely ignorant. Having fixed it in her head that I was bringing her husband the secret missive of some rival, she had taken this rash step to intercept proof of offences that existed only in her imagination.

But once past the Swiss frontier, our secret network of influence was of no further use to us, and from that point began the truly arduous part of our pilgrimage. Trolli had written to his family to send money to Berna. We had to trudge all the way there — our pockets desperately empty, in the dead of winter, the snow lying deep, barely clad against the cold.

We arrived. But the chill of free Helvetia almost crystallised, like a fringe of ice, around our very hearts. As had happened at Barcelona, so now at Berna — the money was nowhere to be found.

Night fell inexorably. We lay down in the market square, beneath a vegetable-seller's stall. Despite hunger and bitter cold, we fell into a deep sleep. Blessed be youth!

But at a certain hour of the night I was startled awake by an imperious voice cursing in German. The thing was perfectly simple. Trolli was endowed with legs that put one in mind of a battering-ram; while sleeping, he had stretched them out as far as the pavement, and was, quite involuntarily, tripping a watchman who happened to be passing that way. He was dragged, like a sack of rags, out of his hiding-place and pelted with questions. But he did not understand a single word of German, and for all answer he yawned and stretched himself. The policeman finally lost patience, seized him by the collar and drove him forward with curses and shoves. I heard the heavy double footfall fade into the distance; then I drew my blouse tightly about my chest and fell asleep again. What good would it have done to intervene?

At daybreak I wandered around the solid walls of the prison, whistling a little *cabaletta* well known to Trolli. A brief whistle of recognition was returned to me, and immediately, framed between the bars of a small window, appeared the face of my Cardinal.

Farewell to circumspection! I waved my cap furiously and shouted my greeting with the vivacity of a schoolboy tumbling out of a stuffy classroom. Nor did Trolli answer with any less enthusiasm. And then and there he let me know that, in deference to the Law on vagabondage, the Police intended to have him escorted to the Italian frontier. "Do you promise to follow after me?"

I had barely time to stretch out my hand in solemn pledge.

A warder, hearing the commotion, had seized Trolli by his dangling long legs, and the cardinal face vanished.

That day I wandered long about the market-place, snatching a turnip here and there, and managed in this way to still the cramps in my stomach. Night having fallen again, I found shelter, like Diogenes, in a barrel. My sufferings were such that, at dawn, I very nearly resolved to give myself up to the Police.

But it was precisely from that extremity that chance held out to me, in the strangest fashion, the thread that gave me the opportunity to rise again. I had sunk down upon a bench in the Public Gardens and was chewing, by way of self-deception, the stem of my pipe — my stock of tobacco had long since been exhausted. The pathways were deserted; snow blanketed the green spaces, and upon it a flock of sparrows chirped and hopped. Despite the rigours of winter, they had breakfasted.

Nettled, I seized a small pebble, and through those happy creatures there flashed, like a thunderbolt, the characteristic protest of empty stomachs. A sparrow gave a little cry, turned its tiny feet up in the air, and lay stone dead.

This was no moment for hesitation. In such circumstances man eats man — I could well adapt myself to devouring a raw sparrow. I was plucking it when I felt a hand laid upon my shoulder.

"What are you doing, young man?"

"Having a snack."

"You must have the hunger of a hawk. In any case, praise divine Providence for having contrived to put an end to your fast."

Beside me sat a neat little old man of respectable bourgeois appearance — a Protestant minister, in all likelihood. I observed to him that the Lord God had provided for my needs by means of the slaughter of the innocent, and in a manner too derisory for me to feel my heart overflowing with gratitude.

He jangled some silver coins in his pocket. After a brief silence: "Would you sell me your pipe for six francs?"

I pocketed the money; the stranger pocketed the pipe. Then he began: "You see that our Heavenly Father —"

One can imagine whether I cared to waste my time in philosophising. I was off in a bound, four bounds; nor did I give myself any rest until I found myself seated where the sweet fragrance of roasting meat hung upon the air.

With what remained, I was able to make my way to Lucerna, where the twofold problem of feeding myself and continuing my journey was to be resolved, on my behalf, by a man whom I had fought against — Pope Mastai Ferretti.

Switzerland is a marvellous country. Drawn together by its beauties, upon those mountain-slopes, among those winding valleys, peoples different in origin, in language, in character live together in peace — a miracle of Liberty, a glorious foretaste of a holy Utopia.

And yet those peoples have not shown the greatness of soul necessary to embrace the full measure of their historic mission. The heart of Europe, in ages gone by, beat mightily among the Alps; but in vain! Just as from the snow-white crests the waters plunge down to furrow the fertile plains and water and fecundate so great a part of the Continent, so from those crags inaccessible to the conqueror should have descended the bright and terrible battalions, to perform the work of brotherhood, to rekindle the hopes of the oppressed, to extend the dominion of Liberty.

They did descend; but as a devastating scourge — the blind instrument of the wicked, flesh and souls alike put up for sale!

Selfishness is fatal to nations because, among other things, the corrupted air of thy neighbour sooner or later poisons thine own as well, undermines and diminishes thy glory. The despot, defended by his Swiss mercenaries, confined within the narrow circle of the mountains the very men who had nourished him; the Republic remained a minuscule entity, and in the universal estimation became synonymous with venality.

At the time when I was crossing it, this ill custom was not very intense, and already the Federal Government was branding as a criminal both the citizen who did not scruple to prostitute himself to a foreign Government, and the go-between who recruited him and made himself an accomplice. Nevertheless Pius IX, who mistrusted his own Catholics, maintained secret agents in the Lutheran Cantons, gathering by dint of *baiocchi* the handful of vermin destined, in his heart of hearts, to alter the course of the stars, to annihilate the hopes of the new Italy.

One such agent caught sight of me upon the lake-shore, absorbed in my twofold problem. Attracted perhaps by my soldierly bearing, he opened conversation in the holy name of God, and concluded it by formally proposing that I take up the musket in defence of the pontifical throne and the altar.

My first impulse was to hurl him headlong into the lake. But a sudden thought restrained me. The Garibaldian tactic! — when one has no ammunition, one takes it from the enemy. The money of the Holy See to repatriate an adversary of the sacred old order — my twofold problem more than solved! And, incidentally, I might also have done some good to the cause of my Country, by unmasking the intrigue and clipping the nails of the unctuous go-between.

I smiled and, passing myself off as a Swiss from the Canton of Ticino, I accepted.

I was conducted, through tortuous alleyways, to a house of wretched appearance; I impressed its number well upon my memory, mounted a dirty little staircase, and entered a squalid room of amphibious character — something between a study and a bedchamber. The contract was duly drawn up and signed, and a few hours later, from the stern of the steamer that was swiftly making for open water, I was waving with large gestures to my recruiter.

Arrived at Fluelen, I presented myself at the Commissioner's office and related, point by point, all that had occurred. I was taken back to Lucerna for the identification of the gentleman in question. I retraced the tortuous alleyways, found again the house of less than modest appearance, remounted the dirty little staircase. The blackbird was in his cage — indeed, in his bed; the proof was manifest, the Law precise. He was arrested.

While he was dressing, the Federal Delegate drew out a purse and attempted to count out for me I know not how much money. I felt a flush of colour rise to my face and burst out: "Italians are ever ready to face with serene spirit whatever sacrifice is demanded of them, including that of taking on the appearance of a scoundrel. For when it is a matter of serving the Fatherland and the lion's leap is not possible, well may the cunning of the fox be welcomed and blessed. But learn this and let it serve you as a rule: if you find one who, having made his sacrifice, holds out his hand and demands a reward for it, you may say with certainty that he was not born beyond your Alps — or, if he was born there, hold it for certain that he is of bastard stock, unworthy of the name he bears!"

I was taken back to Fluelen, and then, quite alone, I set out for Goeschenen, whence, braving ice and blizzard, I reached the summit of the San Gottardo. From up there, something passed over my face like a foretaste of southern warmth — an air laden with tremors and with sobs, with hatred and with love.

And I came down almost at a run, my heart full — I could not have said myself whether of joy or of grief.

At Varese I was received by Trolli as a dear departed one miraculously restored to life. And in the hospitality of that family I found ample recompense for my hardships. But in Lombardia the Austrian reaction was raging; and yet the sight of that hated uniform would rekindle my fever — and at that age, with my temperament, it is far too difficult to bridle one's impetuosity. Friends who were privy to the secret affairs of the cause discreetly urged a word of counsel. I must not compromise my hosts, and I had myself escorted to the frontier.

Having taken leave of my escort and remaining alone, I turned to gaze upon the beautiful and ill-starred Land which, for the second time, I was compelled to leave. In the majesty of the place and of the silence, I went over in my mind the events that had passed — the desperate onrush of the few, armed with their right, against the dense masses bristling with bayonets; the gaze of the innocent turned in vain toward heaven; the silent tears, the streams of blood. And in the exaltation of that moment, my thoughts sped from the feet of the Alps, onward and onward, to the uttermost Sicily. Everywhere hearts that were trembling, hands that clasped one another in the shadows, that in the shadows honed their blades. Then the great soul of that noble race — strong and unhappy — seemed to penetrate me and magnify my own. I stretched out my arms as though I expected to clasp within them, tenderly, all, all my companions in bondage. I renewed the inspired oath of Giuseppe Mazzini, and, with tears upon my face, took once more the road of exile.
