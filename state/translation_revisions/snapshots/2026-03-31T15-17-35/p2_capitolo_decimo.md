CHAPTER TEN

A FEW minutes later we were in position opposite the Opera. The light cast from the premises of a restaurant — the Broggi, if I am not mistaken — struck me from behind; the extraordinary illumination of the theatre fell full upon our faces. The entrance to the restaurant was flanked by columns. Against one of these Orsini leaned his back; I placed myself upright to his right. We were among a great crowd, for the most part stationary, awaiting the imperial cortège. Opposite us, just beyond the pavement, a cordon of municipal guards had drawn up, arms at rest... beyond the rue Le Peletier, along the flanks of the little square, a dark throng stirred and pressed... among them, at his appointed station, I saw Antonio Gomez — tall, erect, motionless as a statue; but search as I might along the other side, I could not see Giuseppe Pieri.

And already one could hear, in the distance, the confused clamour of the multitude exalting the Emperor... a few seconds more, and the voices grew full and distinct. To raise to the heavens the man who had mown them down with grapeshot — what deplorable degradation of Peoples, who prefer the quiet life to the arduous struggle for Freedom!

The imperial cortège arrived. It was preceded by a carriage containing several Court officials. The carriage in which Napoleon III sat, as it were concealed, behind iron armour cunningly disguised, came second and was attended before and behind by half a squadron of mounted guides... the very same who had so ably seconded the heroic enterprise of the 2nd of December. After the intoxication of blood and the proclamation of the Empire, they had been rechristened with the designation of Lancers of the Imperial Guard.

A simple and strange thing occurred. My gaze had flown to the Emperor's carriage and was straining to pierce the opaque veil which the cold had condensed thickly upon the glass of the door. At that moment a white handkerchief — betraying the restless nervousness of the hand that clutched it — wiped away that veil, as though some sensitive soul, having received my full gaze and the hatred with which it was saturated, were searching the street with an unwary yet watchful eye.

I did not see, but I felt, the presence of Eugenia Montijo, and the magical power of the eternal feminine, which knows how to work its way through armour; which, by caressing and softening, bends force and triumphs over it.

But to overwhelm the sentiment of pity, I had presently the intuition of another presence — a doleful figure, with a nose like a vulture's beak, of vibrations in the extreme repulsive. And I felt — oh, let it be believed as truth, even though it wear the face of falsehood! — I felt the warm odour of republican blood made to drain into the sewers of Paris; blood of generous men, slaughtered under cover of night, their hands bound behind their backs; slaughtered like herds of cattle. And the Roman epic, glorious in its deeds and sacrifices, passed like lightning through my mind... not as a pale memory drawn from the remoteness of time and space, but almost as a present tumult, full of tangible shapes and maledictions. It gave me the fever that gunpowder gives on the field of battle. *"È giusto, è giusto"* — it is right, it is right, that the liberty of peoples should spring from the death of him who has betrayed; it is right that this man should be cast down in the very moment he enjoys the triumph procured for him by his crimes!

*"L'Italiano non paga il Sabato, ma paga!"* — The Italian does not pay on Saturday, but he pays!

The carriage slowed. I sought out Antonio Gomez with my eye and saw him again. He had bowed his head — he does not pay on Saturday. But overcome perhaps by a generous impatience, he could not wait. His hand, wrapped in the fatal black handkerchief, rose in menace before the carriage had reached the peristyle, and those few seconds of anticipation placed the trajectory of the bomb in a line perpendicular to the Broggi restaurant. I had barely time to throw myself to the ground and to give a jerk at Orsini's overcoat so that, imitating me, he might save himself. Orsini remained rigid as the column against which he had planted himself.

A sinister flash, accompanied by a deafening roar... then a darkness that seemed to me like night itself. I sprang to my feet, while the theatre, so lately ringing with cheers, shuddered with shattered glass and cries. *"A te, a pezzi,"* Felice Orsini whispered to me — "Your turn, and in pieces" — in a strangled voice. Blood was spurting from a wide wound on his forehead, and his face was all smeared with it. He stooped, and laid upon the flagstones the terrible black handkerchief...

I launched myself, bent low, over the cobblestones, teeth clenched, passing over the municipal guards who lay in convulsions, already dead. The Lancers of the Imperial Guard were at that moment taking to precipitate flight, abandoning to his fate the man they had sworn to defend. A few steps... the throw!... the second flash, the second roar! The diffused light reveals to me the Emperor's carriage lurching toward the theatre, one wheel in pieces, the *cocher* rolling on the cobblestones among the horses, and the ground ripped open. Every lantern is extinguished and a black night descends.

I had thrown myself face down among the dead and the half-living, waiting anxiously for the discharge of the remaining bombs. How long did I remain there? Ten seconds, it is affirmed. I was prepared to die, but I should lie if I did not say that they seemed to me a century. And a third bomb came. Who threw it? Felice Orsini said it was the Italian to whom he had entrusted it, in the rue Le Peletier, a few minutes before the attempt. He was not believed, and the question still awaits an explicit answer... for others, not for me, who saw and knew that Italian. Be that as it may, whoever threw the third bomb had a stout spirit and a steady hand. It went straight to its mark and burst directly beneath the shattered carriage. Its detonation seemed to strike my very brain... but an instant, and I recovered full awareness of the situation. I heard the ground thunder with the hoofbeats of approaching horses. The Lancers of the Guard came bursting in at the gallop and reacted against the impulse that had swept them into flight, firing point-blank upon those who fled. The little flames of their weapons flickered in the darkness and revealed to me, in fitful glimpses, the horror and confusion. And the victims — undoubtedly numerous — of that heroism which so closely resembled fear, were afterwards attributed entirely to the "blind and ferocious rage" of the conspirators.

From that horror, from that confusion, I tore myself free — now crawling on all fours, now lurching forward in bounds — escaping, I could not say how, from the horses that seemed to pour in droves out of the night, from the bullets that whistled about my head.

Repeatedly, in the course of my brief but tumultuous existence, my spirit had been saddened by the spectacle of innocent victims sacrificed to the security and placid digestions of despotism. But this availed nothing to render me insensible to a profound feeling of commiseration for those caused by our attempt. Nor, to suppress that sentiment, was it sufficient to reflect that by inexorable law, nothing is granted to a People without grave sacrifice of tears and blood.

Nevertheless, I must also say that the depressing effect of pity was, in large part, neutralised by the conviction that France, that Italy, had now cleared the way toward reaching their glorious destiny; that Napoleon III was no more; that my own work had helped to lift that nightmare from the world.
