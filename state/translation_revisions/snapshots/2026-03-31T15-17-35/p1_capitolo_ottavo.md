# Chapter Eight

I drifted, almost rudderless, through various cities, until I found refuge in Genoa. The *Superba* had on her hands somewhere between ten and twelve thousand political refugees. She provided for them with generous largesse; but it was like pouring water onto sand.

The city, alive with political passions, was troubled by frequent tumults, and the exiles, rendered more restless by their sufferings, added no small quantity of fuel to the fire. A child of my times, far from considering myself above my surroundings, I did as the others did. Little by little I fell into dire straits. My father, learning of this, wrote a warm letter of recommendation to Alessandro Lamarmora, and unexpectedly I found myself offered the rank of second lieutenant in the Bersaglieri... I refused!

Why?

Sudden conversions were not yet in fashion in Italy, and I did not feel it was my place to inaugurate the practice. A Mazzinian to the marrow, I would sooner have served Satan than sworn allegiance to a Monarchy. Add to this that the Bersaglieri were, at that time, terribly hated in Genoa — the fatal aftermath of the Piedmontese cannonades!

I continued to live by humble labour and by expedients, until an unforeseen event procured me the relief... of changing the nature of my embarrassments. A celebrated republican had died. His funeral proved memorable for the extraordinary concourse of the agitated multitude and for that, no less extraordinary, of the armed forces, intent on keeping it within prescribed limits. At the cemetery the passions broke forth. Bitter speeches were delivered, with open allusions to the monarchical abjection and to the hopes invested in the future. They roused a storm of approbation. All at once, the banner bearing the motto *"Dio e Popolo"* — "God and the People" — hidden against the heart of some Mazzinian, was unfurled aloft above the surging heads. It received from the Sun its vivid colours, its trembling from the sea breeze; from the generous and impatient, a long and thunderous salute.

That innocent banner precipitated matters. At the sight of it, the Bersaglieri launched themselves forward, resolute as if it were a question of avenging an affront. *Balilla* — the spirit of Genoese popular defiance, ever living in Genoa — met them with volleys of stones and there inflamed them. Then the veins of the rioters stained the enclosure of the dead with crimson.

Shortly after, arrests were made en masse and, as ever on such occasions, at random. I, who, rightly or wrongly, had been among the first to come to blows, did not wait for the pleasure of the gendarmes, and that very day, seizing on the wing one of those opportunities that seem to fall from heaven, I set sail for free America.

I did not reach it. A storm, which compelled us to put in at Cartagena, once again altered the course of my existence. Before us lay Spain with her manifold attractions; beyond Spain, France — political energy, life! I consulted with one Trolli, a Lombard patriot, exiled from Genoa under identical pressure and with the identical purpose as my own. We had but a few coins between us; yet we were endowed with nimble legs. We concluded, with unanimous cry: "To the Pyrenees!"

Trolli belonged to a most respectable family of Varese, which, proposing to make of him something in the nature of a Cardinal, had dispatched him to the priesthood. When the revolution broke out, he flung his cassock to the nettles and seized his rifle. An ardent republican, he invested his convictions with all the exuberance natural to a robust constitution and a flourishing youth — so much so as to declare that the most righteous shots he had fired were not against the Germans, but against that poor "execrated Carignano," whom he accused, among other things, of having betrayed the Milanese by abandoning them, without striking a blow, to Austrian vengeance. Which had not prevented him — he would add — from making off with the jewels donated by the Lombard women for the defence of their city.

After a few days of forced marches our coins had vanished, and the problem of appetite became for us as arduous as it was imperious. But my would-be Cardinal had an inspiration. He said to me, in substance:

"Garibaldian tactics: are provisions lacking? Wrest them from the enemy! We need bread? The enemy shall give us bread and something to go with it. I have studied as a priest; Latin is my forte. We come from Rome; we are two crows plucked bare by the revolution. I shall serve Mass; I shall preach in bad Spanish, but larded with excellent Latin. The curias and the monasteries will throw open their doors... to the refectory. A comedy with a laden table; players of mark on both sides, and... let our bellies rejoice!"

My disapproval was lukewarm. At the sight of the first bell-tower, Trolli put an abrupt end to it by acting.

By this artifice we made our way from Cartagena to Alicante, then to Valencia, then to Saragossa, then to Tarragona. Trolli gave proof of inexhaustible readiness of wit. The seeds deposited in his soul by the Seminary had not fallen on ungrateful ground. With a single glance he could distinguish the simple-minded and good-faith priest from the cunning one who, versed in the recondite ends of the Church, treated his "mission" as a comfortable trade. His transformations verged on the phenomenal. Humble, steeped in a seraphic candour with the former, he assumed with the latter an undercurrent of malice that asserted itself whilst scrupulously respecting the forms. If he encountered any suspicion, there he was, using and abusing his Latin. Those poor prelates, in their relative ignorance, felt themselves defeated, almost annihilated. Trolli would say to me afterwards, in private: "You see that the tongue of the dead works miracles in the mouths of the living!"

We did not always live in that fashion. In Barcelona, where Trolli was expecting a remittance from home which unfortunately did not arrive, we followed the counsel of a certain Piedmontese, a lame man who leaned upon crutches and made his living selling figurines of saints and madonnas. And in the Café del Liceo, a gathering place for political refugees, making good use of our brazen faces, we planted ourselves in the middle of the room and struck up, in thirds, the hymn of Mameli. For months we had not allowed our rebel souls any legitimate outlet, and that plunge into clean water had the power to impart to our singing an impetus which art alone could not have given it. There was applause. A gentleman with large grizzled moustaches asked us, in French, who we were. — Fugitives from Rome. That was enough. He turned to his companions and harangued them at the top of his voice. He told them that we had been driven from our Homeland for the crime of having loved and defended her. And having depicted, in vivid colours, the melancholy life of the exile, he added: "Who was the cause of these ills? Who has made the hearts of so many generous men bleed? We — we French, who, being strong, felt no shame in assailing the weak; we, who having risen in defence of a great principle, straightway disavowed it, turning our arms against a people who had risen as courageously as we, in vindication of the selfsame principle; we, who no sooner had we won our freedom than we abused it by strangling the freedom of others!" He concluded by recalling the inescapable obligation of every Frenchman to exert himself individually to alleviate the sufferings brought about by the Nation.

And, removing his hat, he made the round of the room, table by table, soliciting and collecting donations.

We left Barcelona with twenty-five *duros* in our pockets. Having crossed the Pyrenees on that provision, we arrived safely at Perpignan.

Here I was bound to acknowledge that, even outside the world of old-fashioned tales, a good deed may find a greater reward than that which derives from the mere satisfaction of having done it.

The remittance from Varese, which we handled, counted and recounted at Perpignan itself, had enabled us to transform ourselves from hat to boots and to enter a café for a little diversion.

The room was crowded with soldiers speaking loudly of affairs in Algeria. I was listening in silence, sipping a drink, when I felt myself fixed by invisible eyes. I turned sharply. At a nearby table sat several officers, one of whom was indeed scrutinising me.

— Pardon me. By your appearance... you are not French.

— Italian, and not of the worst.

— Were you in Rome?

— At the time of your siege.

— And... do you recall the bridge of San Siro? Oh, I am not mistaken! Your appearance, your voice...

And he embraced me.

In the room... a profound silence. I felt myself propelled, with an affectionate force, towards the group of officers.

— I wish to present you to my comrades-in-arms. The right hand of a French soldier always extends a hearty welcome to the brave — all the more so when, on a certain day, though bearing us no hatred, they found themselves arrayed against us.

It was my prisoner from the Villa Corsini, the officer to whom I had saved a mortal plunge into the Tiber. That evening we dined merrily in quarters. And as, in the course of conversation, we disclosed our intention of making our way into the territory of the Republic, it was insisted that, honoured as guests, we should accompany the Regiment — the 10th Light Infantry — as far as Marseilles, whither it was bound to embark for Africa.

It was a succession of dinners and receptions that stretched as far as Montpellier. The mysterious politics that already informed the affairs of France had sent thither an unexpected counter-order. The officer of the Villa Corsini gave me a last, warm embrace and continued with his men towards Paris; I went on with Trolli, on foot, to Marseilles — our paths and our souls met no more.

My sojourn in this last city is memorable to me, for it was there that I pronounced the inspired oath of Mazzini and affiliated myself with the Giovane Italia. At the same time I became a member of the Circolo del Paradiso, a French subversive association which, in many affairs, acted in concert with it. However, a month later — fresh troubles! Obedient to the reactionary wind blowing secretly from the capital, the Police surrounded the premises of the Circolo. All those found within did not sleep, that night, in their own beds. The foreign members were summarily expelled from France. To Trolli and myself, the Police issued a passport with a prescribed itinerary, which was to have conducted us, stage by stage, as far as England.

At Dijon, we slipped through the meshes of the net on the first train. The Authorities either took no notice of us, or were unable to recover our trail, and undisturbed we entered the great vortex of Paris.

It was June of 1851, and stormy days were being prepared for France; yet to the eye of the superficial observer nothing showed. Luigi Napoleone, having assassinated the Roman Republic, had contracted a taste for blood and was girding himself for a crime vaster, if not greater — the assassination of the French Republic. His Presidency expired on the 4th of March 1852. As the fatal date drew near, his hopes sharpened, his fears, his hatreds, his ambitions. But in dissembling his activity, in dissipating mistrust, he appears a master. His ideal — to aim at the heights of Napoleon I and to reach them by the arts of Julius Caesar. The careful study of the life of the great Roman had persuaded him that a new tyranny is not established when, having cast off the mask, one boldly proclaims it as such. The favour of the multitudes was indispensable to him. How to secure it? He had begun by giving credence to his own solicitude for the disinherited classes, publishing, on the subject of pauperism, pages that might not seem hostile to the doctrines of the socialists. Openly accused of ambitious designs, he replied with his famous comparison between Napoleon and Washington, concluding that, being unable to possess the genius of the former, his sole ambition was to imitate the virtue of the latter. And he drove the nail home by affirming, on every propitious occasion, that he would consider as an enemy of his country whosoever should attempt to make himself great by violating the Laws.

Such prattle lulled not only the gross multitude; it had its effect upon the National Assembly as well. This body was composed of the most disparate elements which, whilst extolling the excellence of the then prevailing regime, cherished altogether different ideals. And since, even in the political field, men readily believe what they desire, the honourable assembly was easily led, by those vanities, to the conviction that the President of the Republic, more than satisfied with his rapid career, entertained no thought of attempting any dangerous novelty. On the other hand, Legitimists, Orleanists, reactionaries, radicals exhausted their prudence and their energies in watching one another, and paid scant attention to Luigi Napoleone, who was reckoned, notwithstanding the warnings of his dark past, a man too feeble and insignificant to meditate enterprises virtuously great or greatly criminal.

Amid the general credulity, there were not lacking those shrewd enough to sense the danger; but conscious of their own weakness, they prepared themselves for the surprises of the future by working silently, in the shadows.

Unexpectedly, the National Assembly, disavowing by now even its own origins, abolished universal suffrage. Luigi Napoleone had found his opportunity!

He could now undermine the ground beneath his enemies' feet; embroil himself in a cowardly crime and find historians to extol its virtue; enslave the multitude and, assuming the posture of its champion, secure at one and the same time its neutrality — perhaps even its goodwill! He set in motion the sinister forces prepared long in advance, and carried out the Coup d'État!!
