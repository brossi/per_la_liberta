# CHAPTER THIRTY-THREE

THE sufferings of the prison-fortress had long been effaced by my lengthy immersion in the free light of day and in the warmth of family affection, which had almost reconciled me to idleness, when the announcement of the crime of Aspromonte came to shake me from it.

The conferences and public meetings organised in London to protest against the Temporal Power of the Popes had become a daily occurrence, and I threw myself into them, so to speak, headlong.

There was one incident which I recall with satisfaction. The Irish element, which obscures its fine qualities by subordinating them all too readily to the watchword of the tonsured camarilla, took offence at this newest agitation. One day, when an imposing international meeting was being held in the Park, the police cordon that was protecting us was broken, and the Irish set upon us furiously. The demonstrators were as though swept away, and the banners brought down. Two alone remained upright and flying: that of the French republican delegation, which stood at my side, and the one I myself held — upon it blazed, in scarlet letters, the motto: *"O Roma o Morte."*

The modest triumph was owed to the short blade concealed from the eyes of the French Police. On that occasion too it remained unstained by blood. The menace of its gleam had been enough!

Resolved to reconsecrate my energies to greater things, I set about finding a way to return to my homeland. Giuseppe Mazzini, while lending his support — when they seemed unavoidable — to the movements directed at the liberation of Rome, preferred, for practical reasons, to bring about first the liberation of the Veneto. And to that end, after Aspromonte, his efforts were bent. I hoped, by returning home, to enter fighting into the lands to which so many intimate memories bound me; to crown my modest endeavours under the very eyes of the two dear souls from whom, along with my blood, I had drawn my entire being.

For a moment it seemed as though the Italian Government was destined to fulfil my wishes. An Italian warship was in English waters — perhaps the *Vittorio Emanuele*. She was to set course for Genova, and a ministerial order granted free passage to such exiles as wished to return to their homeland. At the Consulate there was a press of individuals of every stripe, among whom I recognised some who, notoriously, carried both their honour and their Country in their pockets. The consul Corti — a functionary who subsequently made a career for himself, and with whom I was to find myself face to face in other lands and in circumstances very greatly changed for me — received me with courteous manners, but concluded by enjoining me to return the following morning — a suspicious delay! Nevertheless, at the appointed hour I presented myself before him. At his side stood a certain individual who, upon seeing me enter, whispered a few words in his ear. The consular mask darkened: "Why yesterday did you give a false name?"

— Not false; simply incomplete. I said that my name is Carlo Nosadano; my full name is Carlo Camillo di Rudio dei conti di Nosadano.

— I regret to say, there is no place for you on our vessel.

— I understand! You do not wish to confuse me with the common criminals whom you have welcomed with open arms.

— Take it as you please. I must however warn you that, where the King's flag flies, the Revolution and the wind no longer blow favourably for certain methods and certain persons. I believe, in all conscience, that were you to be found within the borders of the Kingdom, you would be imprisoned and handed over to the French Authorities, with whom, unless I am mistaken, you have accounts to settle.

The consul Corti was, on this point, perfectly right. The King of Italy and Napoleon III were joined together, and he who, under pressure from the Emperor, had not hesitated to fire bullets into the man to whom he owed a crown, could not remain in doubt between that pressure and the defenceless rights of a simple, obscure citizen.

They would not have me possess a Country! For some days I remained as one stunned; then I thought: "Are not all peoples linked even without the need of formal pacts, as all peoples are brothers? I shall find tyrants to combat everywhere, good causes to defend!"

I concluded from this that having done, in the service of my Country, all that lay within my powers, did not exempt me from my obligations towards the rest of mankind. I would have shown, if nothing else, that those who scorned the cooperation of a patriot were in the wrong — a patriot who, if not among the best, was not of the lowest either.

Heroic Poland, swept and swept again by Cossack hordes, was at that moment writhing in the agonies of her death-struggle. I sought to be received among the ranks of her defenders; but this time too, the encounter was to strengthen my resolve further still. Then, quite unexpectedly, a letter from Giuseppe Mazzini invited me to a meeting.

Swept along by the whirlwind of events, he had spent the last few years now in Italy, now in Lugano, now in London, but gravely ill and accessible only to his closest intimates. The profound emotions and sufferings had so deepened the marks of age upon that noble countenance that, on seeing him, I could not conceal a gesture of grieved surprise.

He perceived it, and pressing my hand: "Everything passes, everything breaks. It grieves me that our ideals have been realised only in part, and even that part disfigured by a fatal materialism which drives men to repudiate the most vital principles for the brief advantage of the moment. Oh, how many, without being themselves base, have acted basely!"

He lowered his great eyes — still beautiful, still serene in that face grown almost the colour of clay — and remained for some seconds absorbed in his own thoughts. Then he roused himself and added: "Let it be enough for us to have the conscience of having spent our lives in action, and the certainty of leaving behind an Italy better than the one we found."

I wished to say to him: "To leave it when the hour of rest arrives — that may be sad. But to be driven from it like a leper, after having loved it so greatly, and when, young and full of goodwill, you feel every right to be present at its resurrection, to struggle to secure it new triumphs — this is a grief that knows no consolation!"

He forestalled me by asking for precise particulars of the last moments of Felice Orsini. He insisted upon knowing whether, in the imminence of martyrdom, Orsini had thought of his old friend, whether he had acknowledged that their souls were made not to tear one another apart but to understand and love one another.

Having received the reassurance he sought, he exclaimed: "How greatly it pained me not to have known in time what you were plotting!"

I then reminded him of our last meeting in Cheapside. It was precisely in order to inform him of our conspiracy that I had sought, but in vain, an audience with him. He seemed vexed by some troubling thought, and after a brief silence: "I had not forgotten you, and had I suspected even remotely... But in the press of obligations... What will you have? There is a fatality in things!"

With a somewhat abrupt movement he passed entirely to another subject. He had been informed of my intention to fight for the independence of Poland. It was in order to dissuade me from it that he had written.

And on the subject of that unhappy people, he expressed the following thoughts: "The Polish cause is as sacred as any; but it is a lost cause. Russia has contrived to divide the interests of the labouring multitude from those of the landowners — a tactic fatal to Revolution. Nothing is more ruinous to Liberty than the tyranny which hypocritically waves the banner of liberal reform. Between compatriots who urged her to continue an uncertain, long, and bloody struggle, and the foreigner who offered her the fields fertilised by her own sweat, the agricultural class has not hesitated for a moment. Intellectual Poland finds herself completely isolated, and the hour of catastrophe is imminent. Unhappy the Country that fattens a minority at the expense of the sons who, by ceaseless toil, produce its wealth. The appeal to national solidarity, launched at the moment of danger, rings in the ears of the dispossessed as a mockery, and rapacious selfishness ends by reaping, in short order, the tempest accumulated over centuries. And perhaps the lesson will prove useful! Russia has embarked on a dangerous game for her own internal tranquillity; but in the tenacious struggle waged by the various Powers to overcome one another, that Power might triumph which, laying earnest hands to wise social reforms at home, finds itself in a position to guarantee their fulfilment, in time of war, and without internal dangers, within another's borders. The masses would welcome such a Power as a liberator, and its own armies would thereby be, in effect, quintupled."

He fell silent.

I shrugged my shoulders, and he then, with a few words, turned to the struggle which a part of the United States of North America was sustaining in order to maintain the integrity of the Union and to abolish the abomination of slavery.

"Despite this detestable anachronism, Liberty has deep roots there. Great is the ferment of human activity, and the vast environment favours great ideas and colossal enterprises. Once the present sad moment has been overcome and the opprobrium which has driven them against one another erased, the United States will advance without stumbling and will realise, in short order, such a degree of progress as will astonish the world. You who will live to see it, will see. I counsel you to sail westward. We of the old party of action must persevere in the old school: to fight even at the cost of adding a new triumph to the enemy; yet sustained by the certainty that every drop of blood shed hastens the advent of the ideal, and it matters little if it is others, not we, who gather the fruit of our sacrifice. I maintain that every sword-stroke is an active coefficient in the titanic war sustained by the American Union, and even should you leave your bones there, you will not have laboured in vain."

He looked at me, as in other times, fixedly in the eyes, and exclaimed: "The matter is settled — you depart for the United States!"

Without further ado, he seated himself at his desk and set about penning the letters which were to place me in a position to distinguish myself honourably in the task I had tacitly assumed. His pen ran swiftly, scratching across the paper, while I, standing in the embrasure of a window, observed him with deep affection and with thoughts — old and new — which time has been able to amplify, but which were never to receive the lie:

"What nobility, and what force of intellect in that frail body, which with the slightest effort of my arm I might have mastered!

"Why do Italians ever turn their backs on him, disown him with such precipitancy?

"'A dreamer incapable of practical vision' — he alone, with an almost mathematical precision, knew how to intuit events whose very possibility the great minds of the age did not so much as suspect. He alone knew how to photograph, as though they already existed, the hidden effects of the vices and virtues of his generation.

"Humble, not wealthy, without arms, he eluded for half a century the combined police of all Europe; wrested jealously guarded secrets from every Chancellery; undermined the pedestal of Metternich, the cynical colossus, ultra-positive, who weighed upon the Peoples more heavily than a nightmare. He devised and applied Laws which, renewed, would form even today the pride of any civilised Nation; summoned sacred armies to Liberty where nothing swarmed but slaves; added, through his deeds, to the modern history of Rome pages worthy of ancient Rome; devised plans of war which the arrogant Generals of the King rejected with scorn, only to rush headlong to their own defeat — plans which Bismarck and Moltke were to vindicate. He treated as an equal with Kings and stripped every despot of his self-assurance. He scattered with both hands upon the multitude, sick with apathy, a ferment that threw out flashes of light; he set upon a wider road the fateful onward march of peoples. Not a year of his youth, his manhood, his old age that is not a poem entire of fruitful action and of daring and of sacrifice. How many practical results, for a man incapable of practical vision!

"In the writings of Giuseppe Mazzini — who could deny it? — we stumble with excessive frequency upon mystical phrases, upon ascetic invocations to the void phantoms created by human hallucination and human pride. And that strong and gentle soul does not perceive that the very facts in which he stumbles are the irrefutable negation of those phantoms. For if a Mind governed things and aimed at the triumph of good and of justice, it would have simplified its own work by not creating Evil, and would not have permitted the criminals who inhabit Palaces to annihilate blameless consciences aspiring precisely to the good and the just. If nothing else, with a single simplest act of its omnipotence, it would have broken the noose around the necks of its victims.

"And what avails it to bow before a Most High who stands ever with the strongest? Would it not be more logical to labour, through manly action, to eliminate the causes of our weakness, so as to compel it, by acquired power, to declare itself on our side?

"In the belief in God every privilege takes root and sustenance, every act of rapine, every political and social injustice. Giuseppe Mazzini, by a singular aberration, consecrated so great a portion of his energy to reinforcing the very error which served as a picklock to his most bitter enemies.

"But setting aside this weakness, what integrity in the pages of that great man, what passion and what depth of conception, and what ocean of truth!

"Certainly, human thought does not crystallise in a few formulae and a few volumes, and towards ever more evolved ideals the human race presses onward. But I affirm that the writings of Giuseppe Mazzini will retain the power to raise men to high purposes even when our political institutions and our social order shall have attained all the perfection of which they are capable. Yet if the rising generation does not nourish its spirit on such food, let it not claim to be so far advanced as to be able to disdain it with impunity; let it say rather that in face of the encroaching spirit of self-advancement, the school of sacrifice is repugnant; let it say that from the pages of the Master there breathes, on this point, a rebuke which, for being silent, is none the less unwelcome."

He rose and came towards me with a smile — a smile that revealed all the sadness of a farewell. He handed to me, with a few parting instructions, several letters addressed to American political figures. In his right hand he retained a small sheet of the thinnest paper, such as he was accustomed to use in his correspondence during the period of his celebrated conspiracies: "This you will keep in your wallet. It will be a good *viatico* — a provision for the journey." And he wished to read it aloud. It said:

*"I take the liberty of recommending to all American citizens who sympathise with me, Carlo Rudio, valorous, active, resolute, and disposed to join the defenders of the American cause. He deserves to be helped."*
*Giuseppe Mazzini.*

That spontaneous attestation of esteem from the most venerable figure of our *Risorgimento* — I keep it still, and if I have ever acted and suffered for my Country, in it I found that one, but inestimable, recompense, and it suffices me!

I turned my last glance upon the Master from the foot of the short staircase. He stood leaning with both hands upon the balustrade. He too was watching me intently. I understood his thought, and I have always felt that he understood mine: *"Tutto passa, tutto si spezza!"* — Everything passes, everything breaks.

On the 8th of February 1864, when London was already making preparations for the triumphal reception of Giuseppe Garibaldi, I — having once more torn myself from Elisa and from my child — embarked at Liverpool on the steamship *Virginia* bound for New York. I left behind me peoples torn by grave upheavals and fierce wars; ahead, beyond the vast Ocean, other Peoples were tearing one another no less fiercely apart in a titanic fratricidal war. In antiquity and in modern times, in the old world and in the new, the road to Liberty is slippery with blood.

From my arrival in New York, half a century dense with events has now passed — yet even in the moments of greatest fortune, even in the hours of triumph, a secret grief has persisted in the under-stratum of my soul: my Country I have never seen again.
