CHAPTER TWENTY-TWO

HISTORIANS affirm that the spy Felicità Buanvecchiato, having caught some fragment of a conversation held by Pietro Fortunato Calvi, hastened to reveal our attempt to the Authorities, who thus had full knowledge of it before we had even taken our first steps toward putting it into action.

How far that denunciation extended I cannot say; but I know that in bringing about our ruin it was very nearly superfluous.

How I made my way from London to Belluno without molestation or mishap, I have already told. Others, setting out from different points but with the selfsame objective, were not so fortunate.

The foremost of all the qualities required of a conspirator is the ability to judge at a glance which papers must be kept and which destroyed — a simple art, yet so exceedingly difficult. Ignorance of it has brought down countless enterprises that might have altered the course of History; it has furnished the executioner with innumerable victims.

Francesco Chinaglia and a certain Cerruti, both from Lonato, both of our number, crossed the Alps in perfect safety and arrived at a small village on the border of the Tyrol and the Cadore. Here they committed the fatal imprudence of raising their elbows too freely and picking a quarrel with the innkeeper. They were arrested, taken to the gendarmerie, and searched. What was this? An itinerary marked with initials and annotations that aroused some suspicion. The Police became alert and detained them, and then arrested, at brief intervals, Luigi Moratti of Castiglione delle Stiviere, one Roberto Morin, and a Fontana. Either they carried in their pockets the same compromising itinerary, or they were unable to give any satisfactory account of themselves. The official report states that these men were arrested simultaneously with Calvi, who had passed through those parts on the 17th of September. When the latter was interrogated, he passed himself off as a certain Meyer, a subject of the Duchy of Baden, and produced a corresponding passport; but it availed him nothing. He was taken to Police headquarters and searched, like the others — and from the lining of his overcoat was drawn, signed by Mazzini, by Kossuth, and by Ledru-Rollin, his appointment as commander-in-chief of the Italian revolution. It was Calvi himself who, from an excessively modest estimate of his own influence, had insisted upon obtaining that document. A formality as vain as it was fatal. Who would have contested the first place, denied obedience to the modern Leonidas, in that Cadore which was to have been his Thermopylae?

He was taken to Mantua. The grim walls of that fortress had already swallowed Don Bastiano Barozzi, and my father, and his sister Luisa. Poor Luisa! — her indomitable instinct for openness constrained within a living tomb; her delicate constitution tortured by an iron, inhuman discipline; her sensitive heart torn by the thought of her father's sufferings and by the unheard-of miseries that surrounded her; the modesty of her girlhood violated at every hour by the coarse searches of the turnkeys, insolent and brutal — not one word of comfort, no love, no light!

Was she compensated? Margherita di Savoia, having become Queen of Italy, was pleased to pay a visit to the women who had, for political reasons, savoured the delights of Mantua. She haughtily forgot Luisa of the Counts of Rudio. From her point of view, she was right. Our family had fought and suffered, in those dark times, that Italy might be; but it had not fought and suffered to round out the domains of the House of Savoy.

The Castello di San Giorgio at Mantua had by now become what the Spielberg had been in the days of Ferdinand. Other fortresses compressed within their dungeons the beating of hearts that yearned for the redemption of the Fatherland; but in none of them, as in Mantua, did the ferocity of the oppressor manifest itself with tragedies so vast, so sinister, so relentless. And yet, fatally, from that torment of bodies the Italian spirit blazed forth. In their fearful caution, the Culoz, the Kraus, the Benedek did their utmost to conceal from the civilised world the identity of those imprisoned and the terrible barbarity of their methods of procedure. But there are virtues that cannot be hidden, that shed their light beyond the walls of any Bastille, revealing by their very excellence the full wretchedness of the ephemeral superiority that has sought to crush them.

Italy saw, trembling, within the narrow compass of those secret cells, pass by without ceremonial pomp, in rags almost, yet no less untarnished than of old, the iron temper of her Attilio Regolo, of her Thrasea Paetus; no less serene than of old, the soul of more than one Socrates — and it was no small conspiracy toward finding herself again. An age of history drenched in martyrdom, it has found diligent scholars who coordinated its pages and documented with meticulous care its every phase — but not its own Tacitus to immortalise it in granite prose, to draw from it an austere monument worthy of so much greatness. Let him arise! — on that day, the thousands upon thousands of statues which Italy lavishes upon her crossroads will seem like toys for pygmies.

When Calvi was admitted to the Castello di San Giorgio, Don Giovanni Grioli, Pietro Frattini, Angelo Scarsellini, Count Carlo Montanari, Carlo Poma, Giovanni Zambelli, Don Enrico Tazzoli, Tito Speri — the hero of the Ten Days of Brescia — giants of duty, a chosen contingent from every class of society, had already been subjected, by the application of the noose, to the ultimate insult of despotism.

A designated victim, Felice Orsini was instead buried there, after some months, and precisely on the 28th of March 1855 — he who was afterward to regain his freedom by sawing through the double bars of a window and lowering himself on knotted sheets, injuring his foot in the descent, and who was to have so great an influence upon my own destiny.

Felice Orsini was for a certain period locked in Cell No. 9, where my father was confined with several other patriots. He told me, in 1857 in London, that the good old man, breaking the prison rules with the benevolent connivance of the warders, would help his fellow captives to endure the bitterness of their long imprisonment by gathering them about him and humming the romances then most in fashion.

Felice Orsini also had the opportunity of corresponding, by knocking upon the wall, with Pietro Fortunato Calvi. They encouraged one another, wishing each other an early release, free among the free. But the heroic defender of the Cadore harboured no illusions whatsoever. He knew the systems of government dear to Austria far too thoroughly, and from the same wretched end of the ten martyrs who had preceded him in that place, he divined his own inexorable condemnation.

Throughout the long, tedious trial, he displayed the imperturbable calm of a man accustomed, on the fields of battle, to look death in the face, and he kindled with noble indignation only when his judges, confronting him with an abject informer, were not ashamed to prefer the purchased word of that creature to his own — the word of a loyal enemy, a man of honour.

His participation in the movements aimed at overthrowing by force a tyranny that had imposed itself by force, he admitted without reservation; the companions who were arrested with him he defended to the last, affirming that they had joined him to take part in an alpine excursion, knowing nothing of his subversive designs; the secret of the other conspirators' complicity remained jealously guarded within his adamantine breast, and my father, and Luisa, and Don Bastiano Barozzi, held for a long time on mere suspicion, were afterward released by virtue of a decree of amnesty.

Arrived at the foot of the gallows, he took the cigar from his mouth and gave it, still lit, to one of his guards. He brusquely thrust away the arm of the man who wished to help him mount the board placed beneath the column from which he was to hang. From up there he looked, smiling, at the Austrian officers, several of whom had removed their hats in a sign of respect. And having kissed Monsignor Martini repeatedly — a priest held in low esteem, for his honesty, in those places where prebends are distributed — he said in a firm voice: "I am ready." Then the executioner set about his work.

As a crowning misfortune, the gallows was not proportioned to Calvi's great height, and his feet brushed the ground. His agony was long and exceedingly painful. Edoardo Winkler, commanding the military escort, was seized with fierce indignation and could not contain himself. He cursed the executioner and, when the execution was over, walked away, almost fleeing, his hands thrust through his hair.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi was the last of the great men slain for the Italian cause. He remained hanging for several hours; taken down at evening, he was thrown into a pit dug at the foot of the gallows.

But his greatness moved even a hyena — the infamous custodian Francesco Casati — who composed his remains in a coffin and preserved his relics for the veneration of a reborn Italy.

And the virtue which the Mantuan Bastille had not known how to conceal seemed to blaze forth from that humble pit dug at the foot of a gallows. His moral influence was so great, so intense the treasury of latent energies that it awakened, as to alarm the oppressors. Marshal Radetzky, already decrepit, already on the point of quitting forever the hateful contests of the world, was not ashamed to urge the Special Tribunal that had condemned Calvi to compose a libel, in order to paralyse the "deleterious" influence of that shade.

He was to find in President Visentin the base soul ready to second him. How many official lucubrations — which yet serve as guides for exalting or vilifying the figures belonging to history — repeat a similar origin and have no greater value than that of Visentin's!

The sycophancy that blossomed in the hour of triumph, busily and jealously attributing all the credit of Italy's redemption to the great and the small who knew how to exploit it, glides with the lightness of a bird over the beneficent influence exercised, beyond the tomb, by the martyrs who sacrificed their existence to rouse the indolent. It is an influence that may be felt by intuition, but that eludes all calculation — which makes it all the easier to adulterate the truth.

Nevertheless Calvi was fortunate in this respect: the rancour of the cliques and factions, which spared no adversary however great and unblemished, seemed to blunt itself before this figure so disinterested, so calm in its audacity. And it was said that Italy "would have had in the hero of the Cadore a second Garibaldi." But the opportunity of becoming one was criminally taken from him.
