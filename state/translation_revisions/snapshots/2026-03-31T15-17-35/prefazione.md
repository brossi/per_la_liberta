THE PRESENT volume might offer some contribution to History; but it is not history in the sense in which that word is today understood and written... Something less for those who demand the methodical concatenation of facts and the cold judgment of men and events; something more for those who, regarding events as a mere resultant, might seek to grasp "the soul of the times," rummaging among the passions that stirred them.

The Count di Rudio, so distant, in time and in space, from the events he recounts, frequently protests that the miseries of politics no longer move him... Doubtless he deceives himself. Like a Minos who

"Judges and sentences according to how he coils"

he might have, in the estimation of many of my countrymen, a value altogether relative; yet he acquires a great one as a "human document," and it is precisely as such that he ought especially to be considered.

Why did I resolve to write of him?

Consider: In this far Western land, in this country of vulgar commerce,... to find a specimen of that era, now swept away by the centuries, in which the third Italy had its gestation; to find him pulsing with intense life, still distilling vigorous poetry, still capable of indignation and of tears... How could I have failed to be tempted, and well-nigh subdued? I wrote because my own existence was thereby enriched with edifying and pleasurable sensations.

And I have not cast these papers to the bottom of a trunk, especially if not exclusively, out of regard for the Count di Rudio, whom I know to be either calumniated or forgotten. In the torrent of men and events that poured forth from the European revolution which preceded my time, he possessed virtues that predestined him to be swept under. The triumph of political men is almost always a consequence of their most wretched qualities. It neither dazzles nor moves me. My sympathies are with those who fall with manly grace.

I sensed that this book would see the light when di Rudio would be no more. So that no doubt might arise as to its authenticity, I not only solicited written approval of each individual chapter; but I also asked and obtained that every page likely to give rise to controversy be authenticated by di Rudio with his own signature.

I might have softened certain discordant notes, made more jarring by so much high and virile language; might have erased certain heterogeneous traces that crept into the volume through the influence of those authors whom di Rudio most cherished... I did not. To adulterate the words that issued from lips now silenced forever may be a venial sin for a Minister of Public Instruction; for a simple mortal it is cowardice.

I have nothing else to preface. *Salute!*

Cesare Crespi.
