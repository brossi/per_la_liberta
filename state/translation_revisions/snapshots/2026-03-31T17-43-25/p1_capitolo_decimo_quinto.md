THUS it came to pass that on the fateful 6th of February I found myself neither at the assault on the Gran Guardia in Milan, nor aboard the Radetzky on Lake Maggiore.

Instead, I was climbing, in the early morning, a steep path that, branching off from the Ceresio, a few steps from Lugano, led up to a villa as white as the snow that surrounded it. Its green railings were the only note of contrast against that vast whiteness. Having reached the open ground before the villa, I paused to contemplate it, and through my mind passed the words of Fortunato Calvi: "Let the arms do their duty — the mind keeps watch and will do its own!"

But if the reaching of that height had been an easy undertaking, being admitted to the man within was to prove far less so. The surroundings, seemingly solitary and tranquil, concealed hearts in tumult and were as though saturated with a busy silence. I felt, beyond the walls, the suspicious eye that watched me, and in the robust servant who approached me, stooping and subdued, I glimpsed the conspirator ready to straighten himself and to attempt, if need be, even a stroke of violence. Rather than answering his questions, I pronounced the password and extended my hand to him as to a brother. I added that I was the bearer of a letter of introduction to the Maestro, on behalf of Colonel Calvi.

But the Maestro, being exceedingly occupied, had given strict orders not to be disturbed. And as I persisted and urged the urgency of the matter, the counterfeit servant snatched the letter from my right hand, assuring me that he would deliver it personally. It was a stroke of luck that he invited me to enter.

I found myself in a ground-floor room, spacious and cold — on the mantelpiece lay four pieces of wood neatly arranged, but not lit. A writing-desk, a few bamboo chairs — the walls bare. Here too the prevailing note was whiteness. In the recess of the wide window, between two white curtains, hung a cage of brass wire, wrapped in a rose-coloured gauze — within, a canary was hopping about. I planted myself before it; but my thoughts soon turned elsewhere — to the small great man, toward whom so much impetus of hatred, so much impetus of love, converged from every corner of Europe. I expected to see the sturdy "servant" reappear at any moment to announce that I was expected, that I would be welcome. I heard the door creak on its hinges and turned. The man who stood before me was slight, dressed in black, with a black kerchief at his throat. His brow was high and smooth; his cheeks pale, almost olive-hued. I felt my soul, as it were, enveloped by the warm and serene gaze of Giuseppe Mazzini.

He held between his fingers the letter that had informed him of our failure, and he had come at once to learn the particulars at greater length. He listened, with great composure, to my account, and when, by way of conclusion, I drew out the money I had salvaged from the grip of cowardly self-interest, he took it and placed it, without counting it, in the drawer of the writing-desk. Not a word, not a flutter of an eyelid — whether because he deemed honesty too proud for praise, or because grave thoughts prevented him from dwelling upon lesser matters.

He sat down and motioned me to do likewise. But I remained standing, to one side, and while he meditated, I observed with wonder that slight, spare frame, the pallor of the face, the intellectual profile, most delicate. The greying head seemed to me too heavy for the neck that bore it — an impression perhaps heightened by his attitude, for the small great man was supporting his broad brow with his lean fingers.

I felt perplexed. How could such a man have sustained so much toil, so many intellectual struggles, such a fury of battles? Whence came the force that had roused so many of the inert, formed so many consciences, kindled so many fires, and breathed new life into the Fatherland?

No sooner had he begun to speak than I was persuaded that a great heart has great impulses, even within a narrow chest. One is accustomed to hear the counsel: "Write as you speak." Giuseppe Mazzini, in grave moments, spoke instead as he wrote.

I summarise and piece together the various parts of what he said:

"Nothing has been attempted — therefore nothing is lost! One must set to work again — but not with such men as these. He who meditates an enterprise and yet trembles and quibbles works for his own enemy and unconsciously digs the abyss that will swallow himself and the hopes of the Fatherland.

"Today's sun is destined to illuminate new audacities. A people that was never sparing of its own blood will hurl itself, with the courage of desperation, unarmed against the mighty oppressor. The streets of their city, so full of gaiety, will be transformed into a pit of groans and slaughter. If heroism should avail nothing, if the brute force of lead and numbers should once more triumph over right, the youth of Milan will retrace alone the road of exile, will climb alone the steps of the scaffold. But when hatred of the present oppression, the manly impulse, the disregard of suffering, shall have power to ripen fate, we too shall gather the fruit of victory — we, the brothers of Italy. The Milanese rise up and sacrifice themselves for a cause that is ours. And shall we confine ourselves to the part of idle spectators? Shall we demonstrate, by our selfishness, to the enemy and to the world, that our chains sit well upon us — that the dignity of free men is unbecoming to our souls?

"Let Milan have the certainty of having aroused, beyond her walls, a sympathy of purpose and of passion — and not a barren sympathy! In arduous trials, fortunes hang upon a thread; nor are the supports limited to arms and munitions. Let our solidarity strengthen the arm of the bold; let our ardour feed the sacred flame that drives them to action. It is imperative — imperative!"

He remained a moment in thought, and his lean fingers tormented his smooth brow, as though to wrest an inspiration from it by force. "How to provide for it," he murmured, "in such a strait of time?" Then, suddenly, with great emphasis:

"Varese is the point! The way for reinforcements is easy there; the garrison small; the citizens patriotic and impetuous; the *Giovine Italia* well-rooted; the assurances I have received serious and almost tangible. The thing is to strike a spark —

"*Parva favilla gran fiamma!* A small spark, a great flame! A rough undertaking, but not a hopeless one. Let whoever departs from Lugano today be on the spot tomorrow; let him confer with the leaders, distribute manifestos, spread the news of the Milanese insurrection, revive men's spirits, call them to arms, take the enemy one by one, organise the barricades, sound the tocsin, rouse the countryside.

"To do this, a legion is not needed — one man is enough. But a man who has the agility and audacity of the feline, the sacred enthusiasm of heroes; a man who would prefer, if need be, to die with arms in hand rather than live as a slave. Where is such a man to be found?"

Such words, spoken by such a man and in such a moment, could not pass over my soul without taking hold. My youthful exuberance overflowed and, grown bold, I dared to presume upon myself. I said:

"Maestro, I began to serve my Country when the first down was barely showing on these lips. I have hazarded my life at Venice, at Rome. Exiled to Paris, I did my utmost to oppose the parricide arms of Napoleon. And Fortunato Calvi can attest that, even of late, in the Lombardo-Veneto, I more than once braved, in the good cause, the danger of dying by the noose. Certainly, I have accomplished no great things, but —"

"I understand, I understand! The spirit of sacrifice cannot be inexhaustible."

"But no, no, Maestro! Did you not teach us that right derives from a duty fulfilled? I have fulfilled mine as best I knew how. Now I claim the right to be deemed worthy of offering once more my arm and my blood for the benefit of my country."

He raised his head, and I felt myself once more, as it were, enveloped by the warm and serene gaze of the Maestro. He said simply: "The enterprise at Varese is yours."

I departed at dusk, escorted by some smugglers — the smugglers who gave Austria such endless trouble and whom resurgent Italy, prodigal as she is of marble and bronze to the heroes of the Five Days, appears to have entirely forgotten. We walked in silence, over crags and precipices; I marvelling at their agility, they, in all likelihood, at mine. By daybreak we were at the frontier.

There stood in those times, near the point at which we were to cross it, a small house. At the top of its door was fastened a branch of pine. This signified, in the figurative language of those parts: "Here one may drink" — even as the myrtle signified in ancient times: "Here one makes love!" The leader of the party directed his steps thither, and all of us, each at a discreet distance from the other, followed in his track.

That innkeeper, perched astride between two states, sold wine and *grappa* to give himself a social standing. His greater earnings came from the clandestine understandings he had woven with those bold spirits who defrauded the customs. The smugglers descending into Lombardy and those who, from the Republic, penetrated into the Kingdom, halted at his dwelling — to spy out the ground, to receive orders, to deposit there their goods and their subversive literature.

Around the innkeeper gathered those who had escorted me. They fell to conferring in low voices, with agitated manner; I, prudently, remained to one side, contemplating some images daubed upon the wall. Then toasts were drunk to one another's health, and drunk again. Growing impatient, I went out into the open air and cast a covetous eye away, away, across the plain below, in the direction of Varese.

But even as I was devising days of zealous endeavour, and a measure of heroism withal, a courier arrived at full gallop, one destined to thrust me back into grey existence, among the resigned, among the unknown. I was so absorbed that I was unaware of his coming until I heard my name called. "Ah, it is you! I had all but despaired of reaching you. I bring you a most urgent letter!" He searched in his greatcoat and handed it to me. As I broke the seal, my hand was trembling. I read:

*"Return immediately. Your sacrifice would be in vain. In Milan all is lost." — Mazzini.*

Brief lines — an immense calamity! They seemed to my eyes to have been traced by a pen dipped in the blood of martyrs. I retraced the road in haste, and before the setting of the sun I was once more in the presence of Giuseppe Mazzini.
