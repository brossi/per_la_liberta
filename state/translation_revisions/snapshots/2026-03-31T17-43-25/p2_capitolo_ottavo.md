## Revised Translation (Chapter Eight)

CHAPTER EIGHT

THAT VERY EVENING, I had taken quiet refuge at the Hôtel France et Champagne, in Pieri's room.

Giuseppe Pieri has found no biographers until now. The writers who have mentioned him in passing, relying on the documents of the trial, so magnified his faults as to eclipse his fine and uncommon virtues. I myself, for a time, held him to be the chief, and half-willing, cause of our ruin, and did not always treat him with the serene impartiality to which he was equally entitled. I shall not write his apotheosis here; but I say that, contrary to the majority of civilised men, who have polished their surface and rotted their core, he had nothing of the common about him save the surface alone.

In the brief days we spent in one another's company, following his open, trusting, impulsive nature, he revealed to me his every thought, every secret of his intimate life, and seemed happy to have found a propitious soil in which to pour out the torrent of his speech — less Tuscan than purely Lucchese in character. For all that strange, monotonous voice of his, what he said was anything but tedious; often, indeed, it proved most interesting. He knew how to make one forgive his somewhat unpolished manner of expression, interspersing it with flowers of practical philosophy that betrayed a singular mind and a life tumultuously lived.

His philosophical ideas, freely expressed, and too frequently with excessive emphasis, did not tend to multiply the friends around him. Even today, notwithstanding the fact that certain doctrines have become so democratised, what he said would generally be met with a hostile face. And yet that phenomenal, open-book sincerity of his, and that habit of acting in full harmony with his own convictions, ought to be enough to raise him above a legion of small men — the ballast of History — whose names have none the less filled pages of record, and whose nullity was honoured with a pedestal.

I will adduce one example of his manner of being. Pieri's wife lived in Paris; he had separated from her. But the complete cessation of all conjugal relations did not prevent him from bearing her deep esteem, or from visiting her as a tried and faithful friend. I myself, a man newly married and steeped in altogether different ideals on that score, looked at him with wondering eyes one day when he spoke of it; whereupon he clapped me on the shoulder and said, more or less: "Young man, a love that seeks happiness in the gratification of the senses at the cost of another's happiness is not love — it is the egotism of savages. You labour that a People may become the master of its own destiny, and you are worthy of praise. But would you have it, you little rogue, that the finest, the noblest half of that People should not be master even of its own heart and its own person! The tyranny of the male degrades the woman, who takes her revenge by repaying it with false kisses and betrayal; it leads to sorrows all the more terrible for being the better concealed; to sorrows that dissolve the integrity of the soul and make themselves felt in the souls of the children; to domestic tragedies that end with the pistol-shot. My theory? It reconciles freedom with human dignity, and, in place of a love that is fading, sets the gratitude founded upon the sweet memories of the past, the friendship founded upon mutual esteem."

In matters of religion, Pieri revealed to me a mind free of all prejudice — not through convictions acquired from reading, but by natural inclination. He had, I believe, settled into serenity of necessity, as a light body detached from the dark depths of a marsh must of necessity rise to the luminous surface. This atheism of his brought him at times into collision with Orsini, who, from the education received in childhood, had drawn — as had Mazzini — something nebulous, which persisted in both of them for the whole of their lives.

In the brief days that preceded the undertaking, both I and Pieri endeavoured to live as though nothing of gravity were impending. We usually dined at a trattoria in the Galerie Montmartre, and the frugal meal was preceded and followed by long, aimless walks. We paid a few visits, but he showed himself pensive and taciturn, and the calls were brief.

The afternoon of Saturday, the twelfth of January, was to prove an exception. We found Orsini and Gomez fully dressed, with their overcoats on and hats upon their heads. The moment he caught sight of us, Orsini let slip an exclamation that seemed to carry off at a stroke the impatience accumulated by a long wait. He asked us to accompany him. We went out, and as we walked, he said he wished to take us to the little square before the Opéra to study, on the very ground, a plan of action he had formed. We set off slowly along the Rue Le Peletier, and, having arrived in front of the theatre, we halted, as though to admire it.

There was a long silence. Felice Orsini broke it with these words: "My plan fits perfectly to this locality." And, without making any gesture with his hands, which he kept thrust constantly into the pockets of his overcoat: "Gomez, you will post yourself on that side of the street, to the right, as close to the peristyle as you can manage. Pieri will take the precisely opposite position. The Moretto and I will plant ourselves here, more or less where we stand at this moment. I know that the Emperor will shortly be attending a performance featuring the Ristori. The imperial carriage must be allowed to proceed, undisturbed, as far as the theatre. The moment Napoleon descends, Gomez will hurl his bomb. If it takes effect, each man slips away by his own road as best he can, and we then reassemble on the Place de la Concorde, where I shall give instructions as to what is to be done. If it does not take effect, one of two things: either the Emperor attempts to save himself by retreating into the theatre, in which case Pieri hurls the second bomb at his feet and finishes him; or he falls back into the carriage and it makes off at a gallop, in which case Pieri strikes it from behind, and I and di Rudio strike it from the side. That should suffice to shatter a small fort. But even allowing that the Emperor might proceed — whether to the right or to the left along the Rue Le Peletier — before he reaches the Boulevard Montmartre or the Rue Rossini, he will encounter patriots who will know how to send him the greeting he deserves. In any event, the moment the attempt is accomplished, all of us, by separate routes, to the Place de la Concorde!"

The following day, Sunday, Pieri proposed an excursion to Passy. The wide, open air of the countryside seemed to intensify in that Tuscan patriot the need for expansiveness. In his journey he had stopped at Brussels to bid farewell to a mistress. Under the impulse of emotions that incline any man to easy abandon, and making an exception to his usual self-command, he had become even more than ordinarily carefree in his expansiveness. To the mistress who had begged him to remain longer, he had replied that his immediate departure for Paris was irrevocable.

— What! You are going to France? And you do not fear falling into some ugly snare, you who were expelled from it?

And Pieri had replied in turn that this time he was going there with the hope of repaying in full measure those who had persecuted him.

Throughout that day, he opposed to all restraint an exceptional fervour of phrase, of image, of evocation. In that interminable succession of words, the man of fierce resolve thirsting for liberty, the practical philosopher, now imposed himself upon the sensual man, serene in his facile loves, now appeared subdued by him and as it were eclipsed. Never before had I found myself confronted by so complex an amalgam of solidity and vanity.

But of all his revelations, the one that made the deepest impression upon me was perhaps, in itself, the most insignificant. Pieri had come to know Orsini in Birmingham, and it was from there that he had set out to come to Paris. In the course of his journey he had stopped at Brussels to take leave of a mistress. Under the impulse of emotions that render any man prone to easy confidence, he had become, even more than usual, heedlessly expansive. To the mistress who besought him to remain longer, he had replied that his immediate departure for Paris was irrevocable.

That evening I knocked at Felice Orsini's door. One glance sufficed to tell me that he was more preoccupied than I. None the less, I pressed for an immediate conversation. He looked at me with searching eyes, but said nothing. And I, leaping straight into the matter, reminded him of his own first, prudent resolution to keep the conspirators entirely unknown to one another. That resolution, I added, had been mocked and almost swept away by circumstances — our security had become precarious and fleeting. I did not speak particularly of Pieri's imprudence, but insisted on the probability that some imprudence had been committed. Account had to be taken of unforeseen eventualities — doubtless many — which might imperil not only our lives but the destiny of two Peoples; and I concluded by affirming that, in my opinion, we were wrong to attempt the execution of the enterprise with a superfluous number of participants. Two resolute men, or even one alone, could suffice.

I reminded him too that the design of striking at the heart of the French reaction by suppressing Napoleon was not new in me — he knew it well, having full knowledge of the letter I had written as far back as 1854 to Giuseppe Mazzini. Why not leave me to accomplish alone an enterprise upon which I could claim a certain right of precedence? If I succeeded, our cause would have nothing to lose; if I failed, other unknown, intact forces, reanimated by the desire for vindication, would renew the attempt. The certainty of success lay in persistence.

— The imminence of the action, replied Orsini, not only persuades me that your fears are groundless, but renders all withdrawal impossible. How, in any case, do you propose to let slip an enemy who is destined to ride into our ambush at a trot, and as it were at a gallop towards his own defeat? What awaits us? Simply that the Emperor, in fixing the evening himself, places in our own hands the signal to strike. That is all.

I persisted, and pointed out to him that my plan fitted admirably upon his own, reducing the risks by its very simplicity. If he could procure for me — and it could not prove difficult for him — a uniform of a *sergent de ville*, I should mingle, thus disguised, among the guards assigned by their districts to protect the Emperor. Every section furnished a contingent, and those from one part of the city did not personally know those who had come from other parts. When the Emperor is about to step down from his carriage, one of these guards, by a custom that has become a right, hastens to open the door. Consider: Luigi Bonaparte advances, surrounded by his *sbirri*, as far as the peristyle of the theatre. I dart forward to the carriage door. I am a non-commissioned officer and—

Felice Orsini paced the room back and forth. He was agitated, and upon his most mobile countenance thoughts chased one another like flashes of lightning. At last he came and stood before me, seized several papers and flung them back upon the table, then burst out: "It is too late, it is too late! And besides — equal the glory, equal the risk. Let us say no more of it!"

With that refusal, he irrevocably sealed his own fate.
