CHAPTER THREE

ON THE VERY DAY of our *fuga* — our flight — I had my first plunge into life lived at full intensity... and it was to prove decisive.

The Germans were filing along the ramparts that run from Porta San Celso to Porta Romana, weary, dejected, and sullen all at once. And since we were obliged to fall in at their rear, we stood our ground and watched their forced withdrawal. From the ramparts one commanded a view of the whole city. Here and there houses could be seen in flames; amid the rooftops, the white cathedral towered aloft, and the Germans shook their fists at the tricolour that flew from its tallest pinnacle. The bells went on ringing the tocsin.

Between the ramparts and the dense mass of houses there stretched, in those days, an expanse of kitchen gardens, still green... a strange quietude held sway there, in that great city swept and re-swept by the impetuous tide of passion. Solitary beside the ramparts stood the farmstead of the market-gardeners. A child, serene as the patch of earth that had in all likelihood seen him born, came running out of it and, clambering up the grassy slope, stopped in silent contemplation beneath the horse-chestnuts that overhang the walls. Then, as though drawn by the marvellous world of small things, he bent down, and his slender fingers played among the little stones that glittered at his feet.

The troops continued to file past, each company preceded by three Croatians who went on ahead as scouts. One of these glanced sidelong at the child, lowered his rifle and, drawing it back the better to put force behind the thrust, drove a lunge at him as though the object were to skewer a wild creature on a spit. The bayonet entered the child's flank and came out through his back; the innocent boy, lifted clean off the ground by the ferocious impetus, opened his mouth wide. But the cry, intercepted by death, never came.

The little one's mother was at a window of the solitary farmstead and she saw it and screamed for him. She came running, struggling to clamber up the green slope — her belly, heavy and swollen with new life, made every step a burden. The wretched woman did not reach the child in time to gather his tender body in her arms. No sooner had she arrived than the tormentors of Italy drove in another bayonet thrust, and she fell backwards, a corpse with a corpse within her womb.

I was seized with fever; I was seized with delirium. On the point of levelling my rifle blindly at the assassins, I felt the iron hand of my brother Achille holding me back. He whispered a few words in my ear which, though I could not make them out, I understood. Then, instinctively, I lifted my face skyward, as though I were awaiting vengeance from on high. The sky was leaden, motionless... God, if He exists, must have been absorbed in the affairs of some better world.

It was then that in my heart the conviction began to take hold that the wretched have no salvation save in the awakening of their own rebellious energies. "To fools," I concluded, "the feeble prayer; to the strong who will with strength, action."

Our retreat from Milan to Verona was not of a nature to weaken that terrible impression. Radetzky had ordered the garrisons stationed here and there throughout the various parts of the Kingdom to contract upon the main body of the army. At the same time, to guard against the danger of being harried by an enemy that might decimate his forces and slip through his fingers, elusive as a swarm of mosquitoes, he commanded the evacuation of every town and village, large or small, lying along his line of march. At the least sign of resistance — cannon fire and the horrors of the sack!

The various garrisons came down almost unmolested, arriving in time to replenish Radetzky's forces, which had been weakened by incessant desertions. The confusion, the panic among the Imperialists, was such that, hardly having reached Lodi, all their discipline appeared ephemeral. Had Carlo Alberto, instead of wasting precious time manoeuvring to satisfy the demands of Diplomacy, crossed the Ticino like a thunderbolt, the retreat would have degenerated into a rout, and perhaps Austria, having lost her army in Italy, would never have recovered!

As for the population — unarmed, misled by uncertain and contradictory reports — they neither knew how nor could bring themselves to resolve upon great deeds. Nevertheless, inveterate hatred and a virile spirit broke forth here and there. Melegnano, which had flattered itself that it might bar the enemy's road, was swiftly reduced to submission by fifty pieces of artillery. I saw the Croatians looting it — drunk and savage — hurling dishevelled women and defenceless children from the windows.

Not a day passed but I was forced to witness fierce and cowardly scenes, often wholly unprovoked, and the repressed fury all the more rapidly tempered my patriotism.

It found its first explosion at Desenzano. The garrison of Brescia, commanded by a certain de Domini — my uncle on my mother's side — had effected its junction with Radetzky at that place. Having learned of the presence of his two nephews, he wished to see them. He questioned us on our political opinions. The uncle was a rabid *austriacante* — an Austrian partisan — and I was well aware of it. I expressed myself in language vibrant and free.

The old soldier turned pale and interrupted me. "These," said he, "are the accursed sentiments of your father. He has infused them into your blood. Poor fool! In what do you trust? In what do you hope? Do you believe that four peasants armed with pitchforks and cudgels will suffice to frighten the most battle-hardened army in the world; to put the stability of the Empire in jeopardy?"

I contented myself with a cold allusion to Radetzky's retreat; to the revolution bursting forth from one end to the other of the usurped dominion.

"Ah, if you were not my nephew—" he muttered through his teeth. "Thank your mother; she is your salvation! Be that as it may... be that as it may... sooner or later you will lose your head to the executioner!"

And I, smiling: "I would sooner face the executioner a hundred times than be the vile renegade that you are!"

He departed fuming and I never saw him again.

But those were mere words. Another terrible incident soon came to fill the measure, to give the fatal direction to my existence.

The army had left Peschiera behind when informers arrived to announce that the little town of Castelnuovo was daring to raise barricades. Its spirited inhabitants, reasoning correctly that Radetzky could not reach the safe shelter of Verona without passing through their locality, heeded Carlo Alberto's appeal to obstruct the retreat, and resolved to set their breasts against the enemy's arms, to offer themselves up as a burnt offering to the Fatherland.

In those days of heroic enthusiasm, one did not deliberate overmuch on the probability of success. The greater and more certain the sacrifice, the stronger the goad that drove men to embrace it.

The Imperial artillery made short work of Italian patriotism. Castelnuovo, riddled with shot and in flames, was abandoned to the lusts of the soldiery. We, cadets of the Academy, witnessed that carnage drawn up near the gate of the old walls, at the far end of an elliptical square. And as the Croatians, in their blind fury, were not taking careful aim, we had sought cover behind several abandoned carriages.

All at once I saw a young girl emerge from a burning house — her hair unbound, barefoot, scarcely covered by a simple shift — evidently a sick girl surprised by the fury of the fire and of the men. And then, from that same house, a foul Croatian burst forth with weapon in hand, in pursuit of her.

I thought: "If that dog reaches her, he will cut her throat." And following an irresistible impulse, I was already launching myself forward to go to her aid, when another of those ruffians, heedless that in his own country too there were mothers and sisters trembling with anxiety, levelled his carbine and fired. The girl fell, and a scarlet streak stained the white linen that wrapped her.

A curse, a leap, and I was at her side, trembling with pity and with rage. At the touch of my hands lifting her head, she opened her eyes; but the moment she caught sight of the hated uniform, she shuddered and turned her face away — her pale face, whereon, more telling than the deep marks of the end that was upon her, there reigned an expression of uttermost revulsion.

"Do not be afraid. I am one of your own; I too am Italian!" and I was touching her body to find the wound.

The unhappy girl pointed to her heart, gave a sigh, and her young limbs were overtaken by the ineffable loosening of death. I stood and gazed upon her. She was beautiful with a beauty rendered sublime in my eyes by the aureole of martyrdom. Then it was as though those lips spoke to me in a language passionate and vibrant. My emotion grew overwhelming. I suffered as though I were taking into my soul all the griefs of the Fatherland, and a surge of new resolves broke suddenly forth from my depths. Never again, after that day, did I invoke, in the moments of danger, the sacred ideal of Liberty but that, to renew that surge, there did not reappear before me, distinct in my mind, the delicate features of that young girl driven into the eternal silence by the oppressor's bullet — unconsciously great, tragic, and beautiful.

Night fell, and it was the last night for certain tormentors who had expected to savour yet a while longer the juice of our clusters. Livid lips kept no secrets. Nor was the matter probed too closely. To what end, in times of inextricable confusion? But I can speak now without fear and without bravado. It was that night that my hands and Achille's came to know blood for the first time. An act in defence of my reawakening race, an act of Justice! The pregnant Lombard mother and the young girl of Castelnuovo did not remain long unavenged!
