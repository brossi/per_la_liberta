# Chapter Five

IN September of 1857, whilst I was making shift as best I could by giving language lessons in Nottingham, an Italian exile, passing through that city, came to call upon me. He found himself in circumstances far more dire than my own. I assisted him with what I could, and that same day he continued on his way. Arriving in Birmingham, he applied to Felice Orsini for aid — Orsini being, if I am not mistaken, his fellow townsman. In recounting his tribulations, he made mention of the visit he had paid me. "By God!" exclaimed Felice Orsini. "The very man I am looking for!" And he wrote to me, appointing a meeting at the Great Northern station in London.

I had not seen Felice Orsini for a long while. But no sooner had the train come puffing under the station roof at Charing Cross than I spotted him standing among the crowd, with his cases and his coal-black moustache. Within seconds I stood before him. "Where the devil have you been all this time?" he said to me. And I told him. When I alluded to my marriage, his face clouded over. "Then farewell to patriotic intentions!" I disabused him of that notion. On the contrary, I was now under an obligation all the greater to conspire and to fight — my son was not to have a servile and unhappy fatherland.

He asked whether I knew the reason for which he had given me an appointment, and upon my replying in the negative, he invited me to step outside the station. Once in the open air he reminded me, without preamble, of the letter I had written to Giuseppe Mazzini and of my proposal to rid France of Napoleon by suppressing him. He had it in mind to translate that old idea of mine into action. The times seemed to him propitious. Was I still of the same opinion?

— What does Mazzini think of it?

At this, he made a gesture as if to say: Why do you speak to me of archaeology? And he pressed his point by affirming that he no longer spoke with Mazzini. Moreover, Massarenti, the *alter ego* of the "prophet," was a false specimen of the Bolognese breed and would, the moment he caught wind of the matter, most certainly denounce them. For this reason too, it was necessary to leave Mazzini to one side.

I ended by telling him that I was not averse to taking part in the enterprise. But I wished to see clearly. With Napoleon killed, what would happen? What were his plans for the aftermath?

I repeat, in summary, his explanations. They reveal, if not the plan itself, the ends of a conspiracy about which so much has been written, yet without ever laying bare, in its entirety, the truth of the matter. They constitute, at the same time, a posthumous refutation of the charges which certain historians, lacking the necessary faculties of judgment, saw fit to bring against Orsini.

Felice Orsini had reached a complete understanding with the French Republicans. The coup d'état had surprised and paralysed them, but had not subdued them. They were conspiring to prepare a revolution. A successful stroke in Paris meant the liberation of France, and France restored to liberty meant all of Europe in ferment. Ledru-Rollin — Orsini assured me — despaired of success so long as he faced the sinister figure of Napoleon. There is more force than one would suppose in a name. Were France to stir, Kossuth would answer for Hungary. As for our Italy — Napoleon suppressed, the barricades would be organised in Paris. The enemy, caught unawares, without an authoritative leader, with the cohesion of the army already undermined by subtle intrigue, would fall ignominiously. The Republic would be proclaimed, and I would immediately be appointed, with full powers, Commissioner to the French army stationed in Rome. These are thirty thousand seasoned and well-equipped men whom I gain, at a single stroke, for the Italian cause. What do I do? In a single day I cancel out the work and the shame of Napoleon's action of 1849. The Pope and the Cardinals become my prisoners and I lock them away in Castel Sant'Angelo — their fate will depend on the attitude of Europe. In the meantime I sever the temporal power from the spiritual, and from the Capitol I proclaim the Roman Republic restored. The French garrison leaves Rome and I drive it swiftly to the aid of the Italian revolution. Do we win? So much the better! Do we suffer a reverse? Republican France, stung to the quick, rises up like a fury and lays down her arms no more until the prestige of her own name is fully vindicated. Austria, attacked from every side, without friends in Europe, with rebellion blazing within her own borders, is swept away and never again sets foot in Italy.

True, the definitive political complexion of things would remain to be determined. But all of this depends on audacity! The first spark must be struck. Without it there is no conflagration, no independence is possible, no Republic is possible. You know that I am opposed to the shedding of blood. Nature has endowed me with a humane temperament: I aim at the heights, and the means imposed by these dark times are repugnant to me. But I say that the blood of Louis Bonaparte — of the man who knowingly broke his solemnly sworn pledges, who betrayed us, who put our Fatherland back in chains, who makes a desert of every noble aspiration of our Peninsula — that blood ought not to weigh in the balance more than the liberty of seventy million human creatures. To shed it averts the sacrifice of thousands upon thousands of honest souls, and the cruel act becomes a work of mercy. Decide!

And I decided. The integrity of the man was known to me; the French revolutionary undercurrent was known to me, more or less; the conditions of Italy were known. To probe further I deemed superfluous, and I contented myself with asking the names of those who were to carry out the material execution of the plan. But Orsini was evasive on this point. The great danger in great conspiracies — he pointed out to me — lies precisely in their very vastness. The more numerous those who carry the secret in their hearts, the greater becomes the likelihood that some lip will let it escape. To mitigate, in part, this unavoidable danger, he had decided that the participants in the attempt should not know one another.

To shelter from the cold, which that day was rather biting, we entered a public house. The conversation continued in fits and starts. Orsini had to be in Liverpool by the following day, and he begged me to return to Nottingham. "Stay quietly at home and get on with your work, until I make myself heard." If he could not come himself, he would send a trusted person, bearing a letter in his hand and carrying his instructions. And, as a further precaution, we established a password. I asked him whether, with regard to the attempt, he already had some well-defined plan. He knitted his brows and seemed put out. Orsini could not dissemble his own emotions. After a brief silence, he replied that he could communicate nothing precise to me. "My intention," he added, "would be to have us invited to Court. There are those who can procure us as many invitations as we desire. To approach the usurper during a ball is a matter of small moment, and one might, with cold steel, achieve the aim without spilling a single drop of blood more than necessary. But there is a fear that those in interest would close the doors of the palace and prevent the news of what had occurred from spreading, until all the orders necessary to forestall the consequences had been given; until they had proclaimed the Regency. Perhaps it will be necessary to resort to a noisier means. Be that as it may — your word is given. May I count upon it with full assurance?"

I replied that Carlo di Rudio had never broken his pledges; and since, by simple intuition, I believed I had grasped, more or less, what the "noisier means" might consist in, I observed to him that the usurper's entourage must necessarily be composed of supporters of the Empire and of soldiers who had been accomplices of the 2nd of December. The more of them one killed, the fewer we should find arrayed against us at the barricades of the morrow.

We parted that same evening. The day after, I was on my way back to Nottingham, and as I turned over in my mind, to the rhythm of the train, what had been confided to me and the likelihood of drawing from it a broad movement that might prove salutary to the popular cause throughout all of Europe, I understood immediately what an immense advantage the knowledge of Mazzini would prove to be. I knew of no influence more profound and more efficacious than his. To leave such a man to one side was to risk losing the fruit of our daring. The figure of the "Bolognese" crossed my mind, but seemed to me of no great consequence. I knew full well that it was enough to speak with discretion and to ask for a promise of absolute silence, for the Maestro to face and to endure any torture whatsoever without letting slip a single compromising syllable. The "coward" of the Savoy expedition, as certain moderate newspapers called him, was indisputably among the most humanly courageous individuals of the century.

The disagreements between the Maestro and Orsini? I felt certain they would vanish in the face of the supreme interests of the fatherland. And from that moment, I resolved to open my heart to Mazzini, to confide in him so much as would suffice to ensure that events should not catch him unawares.

My wife was pleasantly surprised by my return. She ventured not a single indiscreet question, and for several days our life passed in its customary calm, as though nothing were in the air. Towards the middle of November, a letter was delivered to me bearing the Birmingham postmark. It contained these simple words: "Go to London and send me your address."

I understood that the knot was drawing tighter, and reflecting that in all probability, once I had left English soil, I should be given no further opportunity to see again either my wife or my little child, I said drily to Elisa: "Prepare your trunk; I intend to take up residence in London." She set about complying at once. I observed her. In her every movement there shone through the loving conviction that she must seek no further; that whatever I demanded of her was for good purpose and for a just cause. She brushed and folded and put away — gentle and silent.

We departed and took up lodgings in Bedman Buildings, on the square at Soho. Two days of waiting. On the morning of the third, I was tickling the little one under the chin — Elisa had just set him in a basin of warm water — when a ring at the bell made me start. On the threshold I found myself face to face with a well-dressed gentleman, evidently a foreigner, who, having asked whether I was such-and-such without adding anything further, held out an open letter to me. It was from Felice Orsini, requesting me to place "full trust in Simon Bernard, a French physician from Carcassonne." As an added safeguard, I asked for and obtained the password. I then invited my visitor to enter.

The child was kicking his legs in the basin and beating the water with his little hands, spattering the table. At that sight, Simon Bernard fixed his eyes upon my face, as though addressing me a silent question; then he looked again at the child and finally cast upon my wife a troubled and perplexed glance.

I thought I divined the cause of his embarrassment and pointed out to him that he might freely speak in French; my wife would not understand a syllable of it.

"Oh, it is not precisely that!" And he became thoughtful again. Suddenly he seemed to make up his mind, and, recovering all the gallantry natural to his race, he lavished compliments upon the mother for the flourishing health of her little one, and upon the husband for his excellent taste in the choice of a wife. He spoke of London and its accursed climate; of France and the matchless gaiety of Paris; he touched upon a hundred subjects without delving into any of them, with an elegance of phrase and a volatility of thought more appropriate to a drawing-room of idle gentlemen than to the humble chamber of a conspirator and among conspirators.

When he took his leave, I accompanied him to the door, and he, touching my arm, said to me in an undertone: "Let us take a few turns about the square; we shall have more freedom to talk."

And out on the square, at last giving free rein to the thought that had been preoccupying him: "*Parbleu!* I did not know that you were a family man!"

— Felice Orsini knows it.

— But the Secret Committee here in London does not. In any case, this visit was necessary so that we might make one another's acquaintance. This evening the Committee assembles and I shall report. We shall meet again tomorrow.

And on the following day he came. From the moment I opened the door to him, I noted that his expression was that of a man in good spirits. This time he ventured to speak in Elisa's presence — in French, naturally. He had nothing new to impart concerning the conspiracy. But he was pleased to announce to me that the Secret Committee had decided to pay my wife, during my absence, fourteen shillings a week. In case of misfortune, the allowance would be continued until my child had reached the age of fourteen years.

Rightly or wrongly, the paramount preoccupation of being able to serve my country had almost entirely crowded from my mind the other preoccupation of the future. Elisa's mother belonged to that order of women whose name cannot be separated from the idea of veneration. The certainty that she would not leave either her daughter or the little one destitute had been sufficient to keep me, in this respect, perfectly tranquil. And to tell the truth, the news communicated to me by Simon Bernard at first nettled me. But had I the right to indulge the impulse of pride; to lay upon frail and unknowing creatures the full weight of my actions? Would it have been honest to refuse?

I gave my thanks, and Simon Bernard, pleading pressing business as his excuse — on account of which, moreover, he would not be able to show himself for some days — shook my hand and departed. My astonishment was therefore considerable when, the following morning, I found him once again face to face with me upon the threshold. Without even crossing it, he set forth the reason for that unexpected visit. The Secret Committee had reconsidered its decision and had concluded that it could not hazard the life of a man who had a wife and was a father. I was accordingly relieved, with a thousand thanks, from taking any part in the affair. I contented myself with replying: "Very well. Let us say no more about it."
