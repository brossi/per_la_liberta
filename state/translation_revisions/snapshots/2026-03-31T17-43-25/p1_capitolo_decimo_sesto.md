Here is the painful page: the secret of the vast conspiracy, jealously guarded; the Austrian Authorities utterly unprepared; the distribution of pay to the soldiers already carried out; the targeted positions stripped of their defenders; the garrison dispersed and in revel. Eugenio Brizzi himself, in the imminence of the planned assault, had penetrated into the inner courtyard of the Castle and had spoken with the two armourers. He found them steadfast to their given word; nor had any coup de main ever enjoyed more favourable auspices.

But at the moment of action, Assi and certain other leaders, instead of convoking the squads, vanished. The few brave men lying in wait, finding their numbers far too meagre, waited for a time, chafing; then, suspecting treachery, or supposing that everything had been postponed to another day, they dispersed.

Of Fortunato Calvi's plan, only so much succeeded as suffices to demonstrate that it was not built upon air, and that it would have met with a very different outcome had not certain individuals of great influence in Milan — men who desired the expulsion of the foreigner, yet who, under the colour of prudence, kept their eyes fixed upon the interests of their own caste — stood in the way.

The assault on the Gran Guardia, led by a common charcoal-seller, met with happy success. The insurrection would have had its stronghold and two howitzers with which to defend it. But the directing minds were conspicuous by their absence, and the popular impetus, intolerant of delay, cast aside the instructions it had received. Instead of barricading themselves within the palace, those few and valiant men poured out in tumult through the streets. They killed several officers, then fell back upon Porta Romana, where the innkeeper Giuseppe Monti organised a defence.

To suppress it, an entire battalion was dispatched. And while a handful of common people generously staked their lives, in the hope of holding out long enough for the middle class and the upper class to resolve themselves to descend, armed, into the revolutionary vortex — the wealthy, the *benpensanti* (respectable-minded), closed their street-doors, shuttered their windows, and lent to four-fifths of the capital the appearance of a deserted and silent city.

The insurrection failed because the intellectuals of Milan lacked the ardour, the heart, the spirit of sacrifice, and the faith of the People. Even in the preliminary negotiations conducted between them and Giuseppe Mazzini, Emilio Visconti Venosta — the future Minister of the monarchy — and those of his ilk had brought their acuteness of mind to bear upon the raising of objections. Compelled by argument to retreat from one position to another, they ended by declaring that, before resolving to lend a hand, they intended to seek the opinion of a competent military man. And they turned to Giacomo Medici, who was then residing in Genoa.

The hero of the Vascello, the future general of Vittorio Emanuele II, held precisely the same view that had governed the conduct of Giuseppe Mazzini: "To counsel against the rising; but to support it strenuously once it showed itself inevitable."

This did not please them, and they did not follow it. I believe that from the lips of Medici they had expected the phrase that would lend propriety to their timid egoism. The conspiracy gained substance and began its fatal course; but they gave neither a soldo, nor a man, nor a rifle. And they possessed them!.. But with an expression that foreshadowed the myopic and corrosive opportunism of times all too vivid in the memory of Italians, they declared "that they reserved the right to make good use of them on the second day!"

The flight of Assi and the others, however blameworthy, had no other motive than these underhand prevarications. The Milanese working class was not yet accustomed to the modern, subtle distinctions between proletariat and capitalism; it had not yet learned to stand upright upon its own spine. It had heard and read so many times that all Italians are brothers, and in its ingenuous good faith it had taken them for such. The first doubts are always the bitterest. Why did the *marsine* — the frock-coats — now refuse to make common cause with the *blouses*? Were not the sons of the People risking their lives for a holy common cause? Because they hurried all the risks fell upon them alone.

I knew Assi in exile. He was expiating, in muffled anguish, his own error and those which were unjustly attributed to him. And he defended himself, saying: "They wished to drive us alone to the slaughter, so that they might disown us if we were defeated. Had victory smiled upon us, without any risk to themselves, they would have stolen the fruit from our hands!"

The bourgeois and aristocratic inaction of 1853 had called to mind that in 1848 the direction of public affairs had been usurped by those who had not made the revolution. A deplorable logic, that of Assi — but logic nonetheless!

Giuseppe Monti and his men fought on, from house to house, until not a crumb of powder remained to them. And so effective was their resistance that fully one hundred and fifty of the enemy lay upon the cobblestones and ceased their torment of Italy.

Then came the martyrdom. A military tribunal, hastily constituted, hastily condemned to the gallows Giuseppe Monti and fifteen other brave souls. ¹

They faced their punishment with serene courage, teaching the faint-hearted how to die, after having taught them how to rise in insurrection.

Then came the arts of Tartufo. It was needful to diminish the heroism of the worthy in order to lend to one's own defection the character of an enlightened decision befitting supermen. There is no lack of *connoisseurs* who would dismiss the verses of Dante as insipid were they to come before them unsigned. The plans of Fortunato Calvi, attributed to Mazzini — an intellect held to be proof against all military discipline — were declared a foolishness more than puerile. And men were not ashamed, to prove the point, to borrow with a foolish air of triumph the Croatian phrases belched forth during the heroic hours spent at the tavern: "Had the Milanese killed our officers, we would have put the Milanese to the sword — men, women, and children!"

And the people of the *cinque giornate* — the Five Days — to oblige the Croatians and Visconti Venosta, were to have resigned themselves to being skewered on a spit like a dish of limp *gnocchi*!

Then came the infamy!.. To vilify the honest; to reduce, if possible, the stature of the giant, so that the People, deceived and deluded, might resolve to gravitate around faint and false flames. The tactic is an ancient one.

And it was insinuated and written and stated as certain fact that Giuseppe Mazzini was a spy in the pay of Austria. He provoked excesses, while prudently keeping himself well out of reach, in order to furnish the oppressor with the means to terrorise the timid by strangling the bold.

Then came the nauseating cynicism!..

To stupefy a conscience fitfully reawakened by the tormenting thought of one's own guilt; to seek to deceive oneself and others by disowning even one's own martyrs. To this they descended: they caused to circulate a protest in which, with all roles inverted, Giuseppe Monti and the other unfortunates were portrayed as assassins, and the assassins righteously slain in that desperate, glorious attempt to restore a Fatherland to us were portrayed as martyrs. Never had creatures born and raised beneath the fair sky of Italy prostrated themselves so low!

I insist: the failure of the risings of the 6th of February 1853 is due principally to the fact that a handful of wealthy, privileged, and influential young men recoiled in horror before the possibility of a popular Régime, and already preferred that Italy should not be made at all, rather than that she be made other than conservative and monarchical.

Yet those risings were not without fruit. They hardened Austria and, rendering her ever more blind to her own true interests, drove her back towards reckless sequestrations, towards fresh persecutions; towards remedying our errors with the momentum of her own, greater and irreparable, errors.

Giuseppe Monti and his companions in martyrdom thus indirectly rendered impossible any accord between the oppressed and their oppressors. The sacred work of redemption hastened its course and became inevitable!
