THE word to the Count di Rudio: The very year I settled into married life I was drawn into an ill-fated affair that caused a great deal of noise. The exiles who were not well furnished with *baiocchi* — pennies — frequented a certain eating-house in Rupert Street, where for a modest sum they could savour a dish or two cooked in our own fashion. I had found my way there more than once myself, and had there struck up an acquaintance with one Rossi, a young man from Mantua, who quickly earned my sympathy through his open and cheerful character. Rossi was excessively loose in matters of the heart, but I could discover no other vice in him; and when the Romagnol Foschini warned me, in the strictest confidence, to keep well clear of that "ugly customer" because he was a spy in the pay of the French Government, I observed that to lend easy credence to calumnies most probably insinuated, for dark purposes, by enemies of our party, was to play into their hands in the most childish manner imaginable.

Foschini, sullen, turned his back on me, and I — drew closer to Rossi still — partly, also, from that imperious instinct within me to see clearly to the very bottom of things. I came to be persuaded that Foschini was genuinely mistaken. That conviction, truth to tell, is not so firmly rooted in me today. A certain Rossi, as I learned several years later, had been darkly implicated in the conspiracies of Mantua. Yet Rossi is a very common surname in Italy, and I have never since had the means of ascertaining whether my London acquaintance and the spy of the Austrian Government were one and the same person. For anyone who cares to pursue these minutiae, I will add that the Rossi of London had a brother who was a priest — a circumstance which might serve as a guide toward establishing the truth.

One evening, at a late hour, I found myself in the tavern in Rupert Street in the company of the "ugly customer." Foschini was there as well, seated quite alone, apart from the rest; but from the black looks he had cast at us, and from certain syllables muttered through clenched teeth, I had been able to gather that there was some risk of sailing into troubled waters.

The other patrons thinned away, and we were left nearly alone. At a certain point Foschini rose to his feet and, passing stiffly beside Rossi, jostled him at the elbow and said: "Spy!"

Rossi answered with a compliment worse still, and Foschini, stung across the face, wheeled round in a fury. I thought it my duty to intervene, and was urging them to make no scandal, so as not to compromise our emigrant community, when the Romagnol drew a dagger and sprang forward. One second more and Rossi would have been done for. To prevent the crime, I seized a chair and did my best to bring it down upon the attacker. Unfortunately I struck the lamp hanging from the ceiling; it shattered, and we were left in darkness. I remember the sensation of a cold blade entering my breast — then nothing more.

When a glimmer of consciousness returned to me, I understood that I was in a hospital. I could not keep my eyes open, but I heard a voice — that of the physician — pronounce that I should not see tomorrow's sun. They made me swallow some preparation or other, and that brief effort was enough for me to lose my senses once again.

But my youth reasserted itself, and in spite of the grim prophecy, my first impression was precisely that of the sun's rays beating upon my face. They roused in me a firm will to cling to life.

I learned that the doctor, considering the case hopeless, had resorted, in order to arrest the internal haemorrhage, to a heroic remedy of his own devising. He regarded the outcome as a marvel, and for a long succession of days he brought to my bedside now one colleague, now another, engaging in discussions of probably great scientific value — which to me, a layman and a man of irritable nerves, arrived at the most singularly inopportune moments.

Before I received the regulation visit from a Police Inspector, the doctor informed me that Foschini had made two other victims. But Rossi — the one man Foschini had beyond all doubt wished to dispose of — had come away without a scratch.

Upon Foschini, who had gone into hiding, the Authorities placed a reward of two hundred pounds. But he was a good patriot, and although his hiding-place was known to many, no one breathed a word. On the contrary, in the praiseworthy effort to shield him, the political motive that had impelled him to act was greatly exaggerated, with little scruple as to whether, in saving the reputation of the guilty man, the reputations of the innocent were diminished.

Now it reasserted itself, and in spite of the strangeness of a fate which in Europe first — upon my face. They roused in me in America afterwards — a will to face the gravest perils; and although I was more than once a hair's breadth from leaving my bones there, I always came away with my skin intact. It was reserved for my companions in faith to repay my disinterestedness with thirty-six stab wounds, two of which had penetrated vital cavities and were judged mortal.

Foschini closed his days splendidly, fighting at the side of Carlo Pisacane in the ill-fated, glorious expedition of Sapri.

Sapri was the first grave manifestation of the new change in tactics adopted by Giuseppe Mazzini. The Crimean War being ended, the great Cavour, notwithstanding the splendid showing of our soldiers and their victory at the Cernaia, had obtained nothing for his King — not even the hoped-for annexation of Parma and Modena, whose petty princes it was wished to foist upon Moldavia and Wallachia. But Napoleon III had promised that one day he would do something for Piedmont as well, and Mazzini held it as certain, from those very days, that an alliance between the Empire and Vittorio Emanuele II would come. He was, however, firmly convinced that from such an alliance nothing would result save an enlargement of the Sardinian States, or at best, in the most favourable hypothesis, a federation of Italian States governed, in large part, by French princes. To forestall so great a calamity, he abandoned his old field of action and laboured, together with his most trusted followers, to raise up the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies. To set against the monarchical agitation of the North the insurrection of the southernmost extremity of Italy; to impose from Palermo and Naples the unity of the Nation. This inspired conception earned Giuseppe Mazzini and Garibaldi a sentence of "ignominious death," yet it was destined to figure among the greatest factors in our future greatness.

Left entirely to one side — most probably because it was assumed I had grown soft under the influence of new affections — I was already resigning myself to long inaction, when suddenly the call reached me from the quarter I least expected.
