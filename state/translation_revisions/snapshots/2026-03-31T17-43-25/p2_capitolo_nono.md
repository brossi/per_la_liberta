## Chapter Nine

ON the evening of the following day, the 14th of January, Pieri and I dined, as was our custom, at the trattoria in the Galerie Montmartre. We repaired immediately afterwards to the hotel, and the porter, handing Pieri his room key, informed him that half an hour earlier a gentleman had come asking for him. He had not disclosed his name, but had assured the porter that he would return shortly.

There we were in the room, frantically searching for a match to light the gas. We had left the door open so as to make temporary use of the light coming from the corridor. Turning instinctively, I made out, drawn in black against that light, the figure of Felice Orsini.

His words were brief: "Compatriots, the Emperor has given the signal. Come to my lodgings to collect the implements. I shall be there within the hour. Everything is arranged for the best — success cannot fail us. But given that the unforeseen may occur... here is the means to facilitate your escape and your safety."

He drew out a purse and counted two hundred francs for each of us. Then he continued: "I would have wished to provide better for your needs, but it proves impossible for me. To the triumph of the great unacknowledged principles one contributes more by integrity of spirit than by the force of gold. Gold is wanting to the persecuted and is more properly the weapon of despotism. Bear in mind that upon your punctuality, upon your steadfastness, depends the redemption of the Fatherland. I say no more!"

He bade us farewell with a powerful handshake and disappeared. But something remained.

A tormenting spirit that seemed to saturate the air, and for a moment my heart was very nearly seized with dread. The moments that precede action always arrive more painful than those in which the action itself is accomplished. Doing engenders a fever that mounts with the danger and leaves no room for reflection or repentance. However that may be, from the tumult of thoughts that assailed me, the sense of duty emerged triumphant, and the spirit was left, as it were, armoured by it.

I turned my gaze upon Pieri, and at that very instant, to my astonishment, I saw him fling upon the bed the money left him by Orsini, as though he resented it. A little later he drew from a case a revolver — that marvellous American invention which had recently been introduced into Europe — and holding it out to me said: "Take it! It holds six shots... six friends, true friends! They might serve you well!" He tossed the case into the drawer of the commode, locked it, took up his money again, and we went out.

We went out to walk, in silence and for the last time, the road that led to the well-known lodging in the Rue de Monthabor. Having arrived before Felice Orsini's apartment, and as we were about to knock at the door, it opened and two men crossed the threshold. They had the collars of their overcoats turned up, their faces turned toward the darkness of the courtyard. I did not recognise them.

Once more we found ourselves assembled together — I, Pieri, Gomez, and Orsini — as on the day when I had wished to submit my samples of beer to Tomaso Allsop. But Gomez, no sooner had he seen us, begged leave to absent himself in order to make, he said, a small purchase. We awaited him in silence, Orsini leaning, almost seated, against the table. Those long moments of waiting made him nervous. He rose and said: "It passes through my mind that that man has gone to sell the mice to the cat."

But Gomez returned, and Orsini smiled. He then proceeded to distribute the bombs. He said simply: "For you... for you!..." So that they should not slip from our hands, he had wrapped them in a black handkerchief which, held by the knotted corners, would acquire the force of a sling. Gomez and I received one each of large dimensions; Pieri a small one; Orsini cautiously thrust two small ones upon himself, one into the right pocket of his overcoat, the other into the left. We set out, I at Orsini's side; Gomez and Pieri went ahead of us. At this point I could not forbear to note that Pieri had kept his lips constantly pressed together, as though the imminence of the action, or some secret thought, had rendered him suddenly mute. I drew conclusions that were none too favourable. Soon we lost sight of him, and Orsini observed to me, without my having opened my mouth, that "the loquacious Tuscan had every appearance of a man who intended to desert."

I must now relate an incident that was reported to me later by Orsini himself and which Pieri confirmed. At a certain point, Pieri turned back upon his path and passed close by us; but he was not alone. He was accompanied by a stranger, neatly dressed, with a contented, almost cheerful countenance. This man walked on without heeding us; Pieri, however, exchanged a glance with Orsini. Absorbed in my own thoughts, or distracted, I could not rightly say which, I noticed nothing, and Orsini did not think it fitting to mention the matter to me. He imagined that a wholly harmless intruder had attached himself to the heels of "the loquacious Tuscan," who would undoubtedly have known how to rid himself of the fellow, perhaps in some wine-shop, so as to be found afterwards at his station at the appointed hour. That silence exerted a decisive influence upon my conduct immediately after the attempt.

We are now on the point of setting foot in the Rue Le Pelletier. Felice Orsini stops and looks about him as though impatient to see some expected person. A few seconds pass, and a man with a hurried step, with long moustaches projecting beyond his turned-up collar, emerges from the crowd and advances toward him. Out of courtesy I withdrew a few paces; but the ear of the conspirator acquires habits that are not precisely those of the well-bred man. For this reason, perhaps, and not by deliberate intent, I caught the following brief exchange:

— How does the business stand?

— All is well!... Tonight!

And they touched hands. Immediately, the man with the long moustaches moved off, in haste, as he had come. We resumed our way, and Orsini said to me: "Do you know that man?" — "Certainly! It is Francesco Crispi!" Whereupon he, with a slight shade of vexation: "The devil! I believed you did not know him!"
