CHAPTER SEVEN

I do not intend to narrate the detailed history of a siege that dragged on beyond all expectation. With his original plan, General Oudinot sought to seize the Janiculum by direct assault. But the fierce and unexpected resistance offered at Casa Giacometti, and above all that of the Vascello — which was reduced to a heap of ruins yet which French arms never succeeded in taking — had forced Oudinot to abandon that first design. He worked around the difficulty by extending his trenches southward and preparing a flanking attack. This prolonged the agony of the Republic by a month.

During that month I had, between one exchange of rifle-fire and the next, more than one occasion to hear the inspired words of Ugo Bassi and to hold vibrant conversations with Ciceruacchio. Poor martyrs of a cause more than holy, they return at times to enliven my solitude, and in those moments I hear their voices still and dare not stir an eyelid, for fear that reality will reassert itself and the vision vanish — precisely as a son who, after long years, has had the bier enclosing his father's body unsealed and found it intact, dreads that the lightest breath might scatter those dear features and reduce them to a shapeless handful of ash.

Among my memories of that time I also number the sad and ineffable smile of Anita Garibaldi, who, on the very eve of giving life to the hero's child, found the strength to share our toil and to receive into her frail breast the sorrows of a country that was not even her own.

And I had the opportunity to exchange a few words with the Princess Cristina Belgioioso, of whom I shall speak briefly. This cultivated noblewoman, whose very nature was exalted yet morbidly sensitive, and who felt the passions of the age with extraordinary depth, had, after Carlo Alberto's abandonment of Milan, lost for a time all her faith in the Savoyard Monarchy and had flung herself, with an impetus entirely her own, into the republican ranks. She had hastened to the defence of Rome and revealed a resourceful intelligence in the organisation of the hospitals, and an unwavering selflessness in the care of the wounded. And there her hand was sanctified by the touch of a great dying man: Goffredo Mameli.

I shall mention in passing that, when Rome fell, the Belgioioso undertook a journey to the East. But the slaughter of so many heroes was bound to rouse her noble indignation against the man whose treachery had provoked it. She drafted and published, in language at once lofty and scorching, her act of accusation, in which allusion was made — all too plainly — to the dealings that had taken place between the liberals and the prisoner of Ham; dealings which she promised to document.

Before she could, or resolved to do so, she was repeatedly stabbed by a Bergamasque servant in her employ. And there were those who perceived, in that attempted assassination, the influence of Louis Napoleon. True it is that the servant himself harboured a deep grudge against the Princess, who had dismissed him; yet it was observed that the agent of a powerful man would scarcely have exposed himself to carrying out the execrable crime in person. To inflame the servant's hatred and impel him to strike was an undertaking at once less compromising and more reliable.

The Bergamasque, while already imprisoned, contrived to evade punishment by flight, and of him nothing more was heard — a thing exceedingly difficult, not to say impossible, without the assistance of powerful allies. Nor should it pass unnoticed that certain publications by the Princess became mysteriously unobtainable.

I press on towards the end. The French seize, in the dead of night and almost by surprise, the papal walls... our defence is reduced to the inner line of the old Aurelian walls, where stands that Villa Spada which will shortly witness the fall of Luciano Manara, gasping in his last agony. The Genoese Medici, half-buried among the ruins of the Vascello, continues to triumph over the numbers and the arrogance of the foreigner; Giuseppe Garibaldi has moved his headquarters to the elevated Villa Savonarelli, not far from the Porta San Pancrazio. The villa crumbles under the artillery fire ranged from Villa Corsini; yet from it he can still command the field of battle... The night of San Pietro arrived.

Despite the straitened circumstances and the fate that now hung over us with terrible imminence, Michelangelo's dome was illuminated as though it were broad day. The Florentines, besieged by the army of Charles V, had three centuries before illuminated that of Santa Maria del Fiore... History was binding the new heroism to the old. Towards midnight, lightning flashes, thunder rolls, hail lashes down, water rushes in torrents through the streets, and no sooner does the fury of the elements abate than the French fury descends upon the city.

"Courage is the final proof!" We rush to the defence soaked to the bone, firing by the light of the lightning-flashes. Darkness, confusion, oaths, death-rattles!...

In the midst of that chaos, I stumble and fall headlong.

Before I could fully regain my feet, I found myself encircled by a ring bristling with bayonets — and it was well for me that the order to give no quarter was not sufficient to smother the generosity of the French.

Taken prisoner, I am marched back, from trench to trench, as far as San Paolo. I am in a courtyard among a throng of fellow-soldiers... some lie stretched out staring wide-eyed; others pace to and fro like beasts behind bars... here and there bandaged arms, faces streaming with blood.

Thunder still rumbles in the distance; a few paces away, rifle-fire and cannon; the air heavy with electricity and rage... my nerves transmit that impulse to my brain and heart. It was but an instant!... in the wide-open doorway the sentry stood black against the dark. He fell beneath my dagger like an empty sack, without uttering a groan, and I fled across the open country, crouching, creeping, leaping; passing, unobserved, through the trenches one by one. From the last I make out the breach and already flatter myself that I may once more plunge into the thick of the fray, when a lightning-flash betrays my red shirt. I am seized again, brought back to San Paolo, where I find the Judas who denounces me as the killer of the sentry. By the time day broke, I had been arraigned before a court-martial.

I have many times reproached myself, and continue to reproach myself, for having retained in my memory so many incidents of my life that are of no consequence whatever, and for having forgotten the name of the French officer who came to my aid in so desperate a strait.

He spoke for more than half an hour. By what quirk of procedure I was taken from the room, I cannot say. Yet from the adjoining chamber I caught the most forceful passages of his defence.

In substance he maintained the argument that, since I had not yet given my parole, I must be regarded as a lawful combatant, with every right and duty to kill. And pressing his case, he said to my judges:

"Does not the truth that acquits him more than shield you; does not truth itself compel you to silence every resentment and to receive into your breast, as into a temple, the sentiments of humanity and of justice? The prisoner has shed blood. But what thought, other than cowardice, could have stayed his hand? Was not the cannon thundering on every side? Was he not fighting for a cause which even his enemies must acknowledge to be noble and legitimate? Was he not risking his life in defence of his Fatherland and of Liberty?"

And he urged them to imagine the parts reversed: "Suppose Paris besieged by Roman arms; suppose that the beardless youth who awaits your sentence were of French blood and had killed, in the selfsame circumstances, an enemy sentry in order to hasten to the last defence of our beloved France... which of you would not admire his ardour and audacity? Which would not feel proud to call him a fellow-citizen? Which would not have wished to emulate him? Which would refuse to embrace him with fraternal warmth and to honour him as a true hero?"

I was acquitted, and on the 1st of July, together with some three hundred prisoners of every uniform, I was sent to Civitavecchia. Some twenty miles from Rome, I slackened my pace... no one took notice of me, and I took advantage of this to slip behind a thicket, ready and prepared to justify my halt with evidence all too plain. A few minutes later I was joined by another Garibaldino, a Venetian fisherman, who was in the act of furnishing, on his own account, the identical proof... Soldiers and prisoners marched on, they were far off... they disappeared. There we were, free once more, and we congratulated each other heartily, clasping hands with great effusion.

We clambered over hedges, crossed bare fields and pastures; took shelter for the remainder of the day in a shepherd's cottage, where he revived us with black bread and cheese. Late at night we set out again for Rome. But we found it occupied by the French. Garibaldi had departed the previous night from the Porta San Giovanni, with the nucleus of his men, to hasten to the defence of Venice... the last strip of Italy where the People, famished and bleeding, still stood upright, rallied about the banner of emancipation!...

The self-styled procurator of the good Lord, who had quitted the eternal city disguised as a countess's footman, was already preparing to re-enter it from Gaeta with theatrical pomp. With all traces of the titanic struggle effaced, he would find "his" Rome resplendent with a magnificence wrought by the centuries... so that, once his double throne was shored up again, he might splendidly conceal therein his own ignominy.

And there were those who, on the very eve of the catastrophe, suggested leaving of so many marvels not one stone upon another. With motives perhaps less practical, twenty years later, the Paris Commune was seized by a similar fever of desperation. But Giuseppe Mazzini held the manifestations of genius to be sacred, and his authority prevailed.

To the calm eloquence of the Master I had attempted, in my youthful presumption, to oppose the impetuous torrent of altogether different sentiments. "Genius is, in Italy, sown broadcast along every road and asks nothing to flower save a propitious occasion. That occasion would be offered by Liberty! The very hands calloused in pulling down temples would have rebuilt them equally splendid, and dedicated them, I held, no longer to falsehood, but to Truth. The multitude of the bigots, incapable of grasping the arduous abstractions of philosophy, must be educated in the school of deeds. Let the idols which that multitude regards as endowed with supernatural virtue be smashed in the public square. When, contrary to all expectation, they see the Sun continue on its course from East to West; when they are forced to acknowledge that no legion of angels descends from heaven to stay the arm of the alleged sacrilegists, they will also be forced to acknowledge that the demolishing hammer has shattered nothing but wood and marble. Let the Hydra return and find the hideous ruin that befits it. Catholicism, having survived so great a light of science, is by now emptied of every regenerative idea and, to sustain itself, has need of outward magnificence. The gaze of the common people halts at the surface, and its grandiose semblance suffices to make it venerable in their eyes. Strip away the gilding... lay bare the rot... reveal the emptiness! Rome had to belong to the Italians, or vanish from the stage of the world. Her ruins would attest to all peoples the desperation of a people that truly wills; they would be a monument more glorious than any basilica."

I attempted to rejoin Garibaldi on the road to Venice; but the stratagems that baffled the three armies pursuing him led me astray as well, and near Tivoli I very nearly fell into the fraternal arms of the Spanish soldiers. I too ended by taking refuge in San Marino, where, at brief intervals and in uncertain and gloomy outline, there reached me the horrible tales of legionaries disbanded, scattered, shot like wild wolves... Ugo Bassi shot, Ciceruacchio shot, and his twelve-year-old son who clung with both arms to him. Such was the penalty exacted, in my day, for the crime of loving one's Country. And amidst all that horror there passed, tender and sorrowful, the last breath of Anita.

It was at San Marino that I learned of the fall of the Lion of San Marco. With the surrender of Venice, the first part of the great Italian epic came to an end. The bloodied arms were laid away in their hiding-places; wrath was stifled in men's breasts. The picturesque peninsula continued to be the garden of Europe, and the pale races from the North continued to descend and bask in its sunlight. But around our souls there gathered an icy sadness that found a sympathetic echo among the honest, the wise, and the strong, wherever the work of civilisation kindled by the great and generous Latin mother burned bright across the wide earth.
