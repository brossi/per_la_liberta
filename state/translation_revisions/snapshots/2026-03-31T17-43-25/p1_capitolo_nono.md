# Chapter Nine

THANKS to our connections with the Circolo del Paradiso, we were, from the very moment of our arrival in Paris, received as brothers by the Club de Batignolles. We were put to work making cartridges.

The rooms of the Club were permanently occupied by some forty revolutionaries — not a corner in which to lodge ourselves. But we ended by finding lodgings under the roof of a Turinese hatter, dwelling in the Faubourg Saint-Antoine, at the opposite extremity of the metropolis.

To rise before the Sun; to tramp along cheerfully, dwelling on past vicissitudes; to nourish ourselves with Spartan frugality; to submit ourselves to the life of the exploited, to comprehend their sorrows and their hopes, to share in them; to return honest love for love; to fraternally sharpen the capable yet benighted intellect; to lend a hand in preparing its vindication — such was our life until the second of December.

Hastening on the second of December and making our way to the usual meeting-place, we had occasion to be greatly astonished. The Place de la Bastille was occupied by troops: infantry and cannon. The artillerymen with their slow-matches lit, as though a battle were imminent. More soldiers we found drawn up along the boulevard. In the faubourg, beyond the barrier, suddenly a coming and going of bands advancing with gravity..., the silence that betokens a storm.

Quickening our pace and working at conjectures, we soon arrived at the Rue de la Paix. Here we stopped short.

Facing the seat of the Club was a hedge of Guards. We saw the workers employed in the making of cartridges issue from the gateway two by two, four by four, brutally thrust along by the gendarmes.

It was necessary to warn the allied Clubs. In retracing our way, we were obliged to persuade ourselves that the soldiers were not stationed about for mere display of force. Isolated, but with an ill-omened frequency, rifle-shots rang out. We were making for the Club de Saint-Denis. Before reaching it, we came near to stumbling over several corpses — workmen in smocks, unarmed, who had gone out unsuspecting, treacherously launched into eternity.

We found at the Club a number of citizens, nervous with uncertainty as to the situation, pale, incapable of resolve. I recounted what I had seen, and urged them not to let themselves be butchered like sheep, but to act. Forthwith the furniture is piled up in the street; the cobblestones are prised up, and men labour feverishly to raise a barricade. A red handkerchief, fastened to a stick, flutters over that improvised bulwark and is transformed into a banner of Liberty. But before even our work was complete, a battalion of chasseurs de Vincennes, turning the corner at the end of the street, advanced at a run to the assault.

We were sixteen Frenchmen and two Italians. No one moved a finger until the assailants were within sure range — then we fired simultaneously. No sooner had the smoke cleared than we began firing again, with a ragged, scattered fusillade.

The assault was attempted twice and twice repulsed. At the third attempt, the battalion, with ranks closed, flung itself forward with a fury manifestly inflamed and a resolution equal to the tension of their spirit. A third shower of lead was expected — which did not come — and they scaled the barricade with shouts, dealing blindly with the bayonet. They found no living creature before them.

The few had held their ground against the many because they were sustained by the hope that the people of Paris would come rushing to strengthen the resistance, with that admirable *slancio* — surging impetus — so characteristic of them. The shops, the windows, the doors had remained closed; the street desperately deserted. Our last cartridge spent, we took counsel together. To what end would our sacrifice have been? We decided to disperse. The red handkerchief, continuing to flutter, had mocked the assassins. The officer commanding them, believing himself scorned by it, avenged himself by dashing it down with a kick — not the least, perhaps, of the superhuman merits which afterwards earned him the ribbon of the Legion of Honour.

But what precisely had occurred; what was being prepared in the other quarters of the city? By what arts was Louis Napoleon consummating the Coup d'État?

It was in the dead of night that he had begun his dark work. The "suspect" generals — those he had not been able or had not dared to corrupt; those who, enjoying some degree of popularity, might eventually have placed themselves at the head of an insurrection — were arrested in their beds. At the same time, every printing-house in the metropolis was seized and placed under seal; a trusted guard was posted at the foot of every belfry. The people of Paris had not yet wakened, and already they had lost every means of issuing any protest whatsoever, of sounding a rallying-cry, of causing a resonant peal of alarm to ring out from on high. Nor was even this to suffice. Louis Napoleon set the compositors of the National Printing-House to work at pistol-point, and forced Paris, forced France, to speak for him, to lend a semblance of legality to his brigandage. From the earliest hours of the morning, the still half-drowsy population of the city was reading at every street corner three documents which have remained famous: the Proclamation to the People, the Proclamation to the Army, and the Decree by which the President of the Republic dissolved the National Assembly, re-established universal suffrage, convened the electoral assemblies, declared a state of siege, and dissolved the Council of State. This last document bore, coupled with the name of "Louis Napoleon Bonaparte," that of "de Morny," Minister of the Interior. Those two men, strangers in the eyes of the Law, bound by blood through the mysterious embraces of adultery, had strengthened the ties of blood by making themselves brothers in crime as well.

That very day the militia cleared the chamber of the National Assembly at bayonet-point, and the Police undertook to disperse the Supreme Court, which had assembled to protest, in deference to the Constitution, against the new act of parricide. The prisons of Mazas were packed with representatives of the People.

But at liberty remained the Deputies of the Left, who, through a misunderstanding, had attended the Assembly only in the smallest numbers. Upon them fell the arduous task of wiping out the outrage, of saving the Republic. Not a printing-press, not arms, not powder; a people stunned, paralysed by a fatal stupefaction; a forest of bayonets on every side, an entire Army Corps in command of the strategic points, a Police force sold to the transgressor. They were obliged, in order to escape being shot, to go in disguise and to change their meeting-place daily. They acted manfully; they knew how to face martyrdom with serenity — a blaze of heroism! But they could not, but they were not able to surmount the greatest obstacle: their own prejudices.

The nightmare of legality dominated them; the spectre of the Constitution shackled their arms, and to give it no jolt — at such a moment! — they accounted greatness.

They were seen to oppose with speeches the drunken soldiery, athirst for blood; to meet lead with dissertations upon the duties of the good citizen. They set forward, with profound conviction, the inviolability of their own persons — as though they were devotees expecting miracles from a relic. Language well suited to the lips of a despot; ridiculous on those of men who possessed not the force to sustain their own pretension.

In brief: indomitable courage, sublime self-abnegation — and a dotage of childishness.

The arid political questions left the masses cold; instinctively, if not with full awareness, they felt their hollowness. People who toiled from dawn to dusk for a meagre subsistence would have shown themselves sensible only to immediate improvements in their material existence. Certainly, universal suffrage interested, to a greater or lesser degree, even a good number of the unlettered, and the Assembly — or rather its remnants — hastened to cancel the error they had committed and to restore it. But Louis Napoleon had forestalled them; their act appeared a forced concession and was derided.

Victor Hugo, in whose vast mind the truth flashed for a single instant, proposed to his colleagues the immediate abolition of the *dazio murato* — the city toll — and of the tax on spirits. For some time the People had been clamouring against those fiscal measures. The great lyric poet obtained that a decree to that effect be drawn up. But it availed nothing. Once again the practical men showed themselves to have shorter sight than the poets. They chose to defer its publication until the morrow of a victory whose coming was problematical, thereby spitting, virtually, upon the most efficacious means of ensuring it. "If the People do not understand," they said, "that we are risking our heads in defence of their rights; if they do not fight, they are unworthy of any concession" — unworthy of "liberty." A folly of haughty and disdainful bearing, but a folly none the less. Perhaps those men who faced death serenely day by day, hour by hour, feared to give the appearance of conjuring up the spectre of the red republic.

One great intellect, having come out of prison by chance, on parole — for he languished therein for an offence against Louis Napoleon — found the means to cast among those perplexed men a note of cold, hard positiveness. I allude to Proudhon.

"You are the victims of your own illusions. The People have allowed themselves to be gulled. They will not stir, or will stir only to assist Bonaparte. A piece of foolishness, the restoration of universal suffrage, has caught on the hook the fools who are always in the majority. The usurper passes for a socialist. He means to be emperor and he will be. It is a crime of brazen effrontery, but brazenness triumphs when it is backed by bayonets. He has on his side the cannon, the popular prejudices, the errors and the unpopularity of the Assembly. Your attempts are destined to fail. You, Deputies of the Left, are honest men; he is a scoundrel — and that too is a great advantage in his favour. You have scruples; he has none. Desist from the struggle. Given the circumstances, much may perhaps be attempted; but nothing obtained. And you will do nothing but drag a thousand, two thousand, ten thousand generous souls into your ruin."

Prophetic words! Yet it was not in their nature to receive them.

The misfortune is that the most logical, the surest, the most expeditious, the least cruel means of paralysing the parricide occurred to no one, or at the least no one dared to speak of it. Louis Napoleon had contrived the Coup d'État; he was the pivot about which the foul conspiracy revolved. It was necessary to strike the pivot; it was necessary to suppress Louis Napoleon. By what strange aberration was the blood of one man who, by perjury, was enslaving the Fatherland to weigh heavier, in the minds of those representatives of the People, than the lives of thousands upon thousands of innocents?

Victor Hugo wrote, from exile, masterly pages upon the terrible power of the *pieuvre* — the octopus. He knew that the blade of a knife slides off the tentacles of the monster, which releases its prey and collapses like a rag the instant the steel turns about the one truly vulnerable point — the mouth that creates the suction and gives force to the suckers. But had a voice arisen in the Assembly bent on rousing the hero Gilliatt against the baleful *pieuvre* of the Empire — he, Victor Hugo, would have been the first to hurl the thunderbolts of his indignant eloquence to denounce "the shame of a vulgar assassination." A great and ingenuous dreamer, having roused the people of Paris to raise barricades, to fight and to kill, he carried his boldness to the point of facing unarmed the enemy's bullets; he carried his sentimentality to the point of rebuking the friend who offered him arms with which to defend himself and to strike. A puerile contradiction of a sublime intellect!

But the horror of blood was not to restrain "the man without scruples." The regiments, the batteries, stationed in the faubourgs, were, by his order, concentrated on the Boulevard Montmartre. To whet the military ardour, ten thousand francs' worth of brandy had been distributed to each brigade. The hordes of passive obedience do not attain the upper reaches of heroism without the fumes of alcohol. As for those in the higher ranks, broad assurances had been given of more considerable epaulettes and well-stuffed envelopes "to meet any eventuality." And for fear that even this might not suffice, the influence of certain high-class *courtisanes* was skilfully brought into play — the *courtisanes* who afterwards formed one of the most conspicuous features of the Second Empire. The soldiers remained for several hours, arms in hand, silent, forcibly motionless, like hounds straining at the leash. Suddenly, starting from no one knows where, fired by no one knows precisely whom, a rifle-shot rang out. It was a signal.

All simultaneously began to kill. It was not arms levelled against arms; it was the pitiless hunt of the innocent citizen who was quietly attending to his own affairs; it was the premeditated slaughter carried out in order to terrorise, out of a lust for massacre. The intoxication of alcohol being soon joined by that of blood, confusion and death spread from the boulevard across an entire district. Foot-soldiers, lancers, cuirassiers advance in close order, cutting down all who are guilty of being abroad. Nor is the absence from the streets a sufficient defence — they fire into the courtyards; through the gratings, down into the cellars; they send volleys of lead through the windows. By degrees, the random killing becomes a monotonous business. How to enliven it? The human beasts make a sport of it; they wager, laughing, on the accuracy of the shot — women and children the targets of the infernal contest. Soldiers were seen to rekindle the mania for slaughter by torturing the dying, and widening their wounds with cold steel.

After the killing by small arms, the cannon. The carnage lasted three hours — a holocaust of several thousand persons. But Louis Napoleon never dared to publish the revealing figures. A cowardly malefactor, having struck his victims to death, he turns livid at the sight of the enormous quantity of corpses and takes every care to spirit them away from his own eyes no less than from those of others.

Night fell. The heart of Paris became entangled with barricades; but the People are almost annihilated by terror and do not stir. Standing to defend them, unarmed and hopeless of victory, are a handful of virile consciences — an ephemeral obstacle to the tyrant's artillery.

The horrible tragedy is repeated for twelve consecutive nights, at the Champ de Mars, in the courtyard of the Prefecture, in the adjacent Rue de Jérusalem. The slaughter is carried out at the mouth of the sewer so that the human blood, flowing into it, may leave scant traces and not reveal the enormity of the hecatomb.

Then came the persecutions sanctioned by Law. What Law? Louis Napoleon creates the Commissions Militaires and the Commissions Mixtes. It will suffice to indicate the manner of functioning of the former. They sit behind closed doors. They receive from above a list of names, which they run through — the accused being absent — and against each name they affix, without further inquiry, a mark which, according to the case, has the power to acquit, or to send some wretch to Lambèse, or, worse, to the arid guillotine of Cayenne.

Three hundred and thirty-seven are thus taken all at once from the dungeons of the Tuileries, led between a triple file of soldiers to the Champ de Mars, struck down, and buried that same night in the cemetery of Montmartre, with their heads protruding from the earth — a grim expedient of identification.

The prisons overflow with the wretched. How to make room for the throngs that keep pouring in? The answer that came from above is brief and terrible: "Kill them." In the night they are brusquely roused and chosen at random. Neither their name is asked, nor whence they come, nor what position they occupy in civil society — what matters is to reach the fatally established number. Thus the Commissions Militaires and the Commissions Mixtes plunge forty thousand families into mourning! By such deeds does one acquire the sceptre "by divine right!"

And Louis Napoleon announced to Europe that he had — saved "the social order." Blessed, with craven gratitude, by Pius IX, he set forth, from so glorious a beginning, dragging France behind him, toward the dark abyss of Sedan.
