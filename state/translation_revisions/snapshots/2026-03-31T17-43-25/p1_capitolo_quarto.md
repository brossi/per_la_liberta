## Revised Translation — Chapter Four

CHAPTER FOUR

ARRIVED safe and sound in Verona, the old Marshal offered the cadets the option of entering the regular army as ranked soldiers, granting them the privilege of choosing their own Regiment. Not one accepted. We were interned as far as Gratz and incorporated into that Military Academy; until the Minister of War, accepting our petition, permitted us to return to our homes.

September arrived. The valiant Piedmontese army, abandoned to itself through the defection of the Italian princes, its impetus broken by the mental infirmities of its own King, defeated more by the deficiencies of its own organisation than by Austrian valour, had been compelled to abandon Venetia, the Duchies, Lombardy; to retreat behind the line of the Ticino. Milan had fallen once again into the possession of the oppressors... With the royal war virtually ended, there continued, in the expression then in vogue, that of the People.

Among those individuals stripped of every official distinction who distinguished themselves most that year, by valour and by integrity of spirit, was a patriot most dear to my family, a certain Fortunato Calvi...

With a handful of mountain men and arms that were nearly primitive, he had defended the Cadore from the first days of May to the first days of June, inscribing in the annals of our epic the names of Ampezzo, of Termine, of Rivalgo, of Chiusa di Venas, of the pass of Death, of Selva, of Rindimera, and the fateful Mauria.

In this stupendous campaign, which certain historians skip over with a swiftness yet more stupendous, Pietro Fortunato Calvi repelled the attacks — at first separate, then combined — launched by the Austrians against the three natural gateways of the Cadore. When, at the third phase of the war, he laid down arms, it was a necessity created by the complete exhaustion of provisions and ammunition. Nor in that era, so ready to attribute reverses to treachery, did a single voice arise to diminish the glory of the hero. He made his way, by unfrequented routes, to Venice, and wherever he appeared he was received not as a man defeated, but as a conqueror.

In his footsteps, by way of Portogruaro, the only road still open, I entered Venice as well, together with my brother Achille. Calvi at once admitted us to serve in that legion of the Cacciatori delle Alpi, whose name became synonymous with heroism and glory.

Through the final months of 1848 and the early months of the following year, I had thus the opportunity to witness a marvellous spectacle. In my long existence I have known no other more powerful to exalt the human soul... A people which, by sheer force of will, retempers its own fibre, scours away in hardship, in the grip of hunger, in the horrors of pestilence, in the bath of its own blood, the shame of centuries of languor, the cowardly resignation to bondage.

In destruction, the gleams of life renewing itself, of greatness reaffirming itself.

In those months, beyond my share of the common sufferings, I endured a grief intimate and intense. My brother Achille had his saintly resolves and his fervent hopes inexorably cut short by the pestilence. I reached him in time to press my lips to his cold brow, and it seemed to me that at the touch of my lips something like a remnant of feeling stirred in the inert corpse. I believe that only from that moment did the young patriot wholly cease to tremble.

Personally, I took part in but a single action of importance: the sortie towards Mestre which resupplied the city with forage and provisions. On that occasion I saw, for the first time, without speaking with him, a man who was to have a great influence upon my future: Felice Orsini.

Two grave infractions of discipline removed me prematurely from that theatre of sacrifice and grandeur. I had been assigned to the defence of the forts of Marghera, lunette number thirteen. A tedious duty; for Cavedalis, the Minister of War, on the pretext that ammunition was wanting, had ordered us to hold our fire. We looked on in idle inaction at the feverish labours of the Austrians who, concentrated at Mestre, were digging their parallels and preparing for the bombardment. Our inaction had so emboldened them that one day they advanced along the canal to within musket-shot of the forts... and from there they wounded the sentry of my lunette in the arm.

The orders of the Minister, and the Minister himself, vanished from my mind, to say nothing of the face of the globe... I snatched the fuse from the gunner's hands and the shot was fired. Immediately, as though my fury had spread with the swiftness of electricity, all the cannon of the forts thundered... the few enemies who remained standing took to their heels.

Pietro Fortunato Calvi himself pleaded my cause before the Council of War. He urged in my defence my ignorance of the orders, my youth, my love of the Fatherland, the irresistible impulse of the moment, and for that time I was spared. But at the second infraction, political animosities rendered my situation untenable.

The fatal dualism between republicans and monarchists, which in 1848–49 was so great a part of our reverses, had necessarily its repercussions in Venice as well. Republican at first, then annexed to monarchical Piedmont, the city had once again assumed republican government when the royal war seemed irremediably lost. The upper bourgeoisie, which even then arrogated to itself the exclusive possession of practical good sense, did not accommodate itself readily to the popular verdict, and several Ministers lent it their support.

Hence, hybrid measures that irritated the multitude and frequently impelled it to gather in the Piazza San Marco and make its tumult heard.

In March of 1849 (the precise day I do not recall) exasperation, having reached its height, seemed on the verge of degenerating into open revolt. To forestall it, Cavedalis gave the order to Pietro Fortunato Calvi to disperse the crowd at all costs, and Calvi, detaching several patrols of his legion, laid the thankless duty upon the non-commissioned officers. Yet at the moment of our departure he drew us aside and, certain of being understood, spoke these very words: "Remember that in Venice, the Croats no longer give the orders."

I led my patrol through a good stretch of the city without encountering any hindrance; but no sooner had I emerged upon the Piazza San Marco than I found before me a tempestuous sea of humanity. Our green uniform trimmed with white braid and our drooping plumes at once drew general attention, and a vibrant voice cried out: "Look at them! They've come to shoot us down!"

I placed my *kepi* on the point of my sword and, waving it in the manner of a salute, cried at the top of my lungs: "We are here to defend the People, not to tyrannise them!"

Farewell to repression! The crowd parted to give us free passage and, its mood transformed in an instant, roared from a thousand and a thousand throats: "*Evviva*! *Evviva* i Cacciatori delle Alpi!" Lost and as though absorbed into that stormy sea, we ended by becoming an integral part of it.

By good fortune, where our arms fell short, authority and eloquence were supplied by Daniele Manin. I see him still before my eyes, his countenance resembling, as one drop of water resembles another, that of Cavour, though his spirit was so far removed from all diplomatic duplicity. The thoughts he expressed I can repeat today still, almost in his very words. He exhorted the crowd not to exacerbate the miseries of the Fatherland, which in its hour of extremity called for the concord of all its sons. To divide was to diminish its strength; to divide was a crime.

The urgent, the supreme task was to vanquish the foreigner, to shatter the fetters being forged for the Republic. There would be time enough afterwards to compose internal dissensions; when the glorious banner should once again unfurl over free lands and free men. What might not a people then achieve, conscious of its own rights, drunk with enthusiasm, invigorated by victory? What intrigue, what blandishment could avail to wrest from it, even in part, the fruit of so many sacrifices? "Return to your homes, refresh yourselves in the embrace of your mothers, your wives, your anxious children... poor great People, so hardly tried in the school of adversity! Reflect that the cannon may at any moment tear you from those embraces and summon you to where the fighting is, where men die. No Italian worthy of the name should refuse to present himself, unblemished and without remorse, to the ultimate sacrifice!"

He fell silent, and that strong People, and that good People, little by little dispersed. Of how much greater glory would the wise, the humane word be to those Governors, even today, who so lightly prefer it to fratricidal arms!

The following day, Colonel Calvi sent for me. I found him more sorrowful than stern. "The Minister Cavedalis," he said to me, "is in a towering rage. Venice is full of informers, as in the days of the Signoria, and he has learned, chapter and verse, all that... all that has occurred. He demands the names of the non-commissioned officers who commanded the patrols; above all — do you understand me? — the name of a beardless sergeant who uttered seditious phrases and whom he wishes to see punished. The escapade is dealt with in the military regulations..."

I made ready to cobble together a defence; but he silenced me with a gesture. The superior officer would not and could not enter into that discussion; but the patriot extended his right hand to the patriot and gave me a clasp that spoke more than an entire volume.

"It is the second of its kind," he continued, "and there would be no remission. Sergeant de Rudio, you must leave Venice!... You will find enemies to fight in every corner of Italy. Rome has need of defenders, and the Government of the Republic, at the solicitation of those Triumvirs, is sending thither the legionaries of the Unione, who came among us after the volte-face of Pope Pio. Venice has more need of bread than of soldiers, and Colonel Ferrara, who leads the legionaries, will set sail tomorrow from Chioggia. Here is a letter for him... you will be well received. Go — I command it!"

I gave the military salute and departed. The unforeseen awaited me. The Minister Cavedalis had already sent to Chioggia the order to arrest and return to Venice any soldiers who neither belonged to the Unione nor could produce a pass signed by the commandant of the Place. I did not stroll long through the streets of Chioggia...

The barracks in which they confined me flanks a street that leads down to the shore. A single second sufficed to hurl oneself from the gateway and turn the corner into that street; four bounds like a squirrel would carry one along it to the beach. In the courtyard of the barracks there strolled about, in no particular order, legionaries of every region and every uniform... at the wide-open gate stood a sentry on guard — or rather a great lout who, judging by appearances, seemed to be dreaming of the good soups of his homeland. Slowly, slowly, as though my only care were to amuse myself a little by watching the passers-by, I drew up to his side. Then I waved my arms and called out to him in a muffled voice: "Look out! Run... run, there's someone at that window!"

He looks to the left, I spring to the right, round the corner, out of range... Oh, the blue waters of the Adriatic, oh the dark *trabaccoli* [coastal trading vessels], swaying gently on the waves, light as nutshells!... the *trabaccoli* grazed by the wing of the gulls, resounding with the sounds of toil and of song! Before me lay some forty of them, already crowded with legionaries, already manoeuvring for imminent departure.

I flatten myself in a small boat which, driven by two sturdy oarsmen, cuts through the waves swift as an arrow. But as I clamber up the side of a vessel, an officer caught sight, from the shore, of the green uniform trimmed with white braid. Providential for me, in that critical moment, was the manoeuvre of displacement carried out by the mobile flotilla. The officer had undoubtedly fixed his eye on the *trabaccolo* in which I had sought refuge; but in the passage across, he lost his bearings and boarded, in his search, the vessel where I was not. He persisted in inspecting a second ship, then a third, and arrived at last upon mine... but by then I had already found means to put on a uniform of the Unione. I fell into line with the other legionaries and he, passing within a hand's breadth of my face, scarcely deigned to glance at me.

Nevertheless I was not to set foot in Rome without first traversing a wide circuit. Numerous combatants had, like myself, left Venice by stealth — not from cowardice, but because drawn by various considerations towards the heart of Italy. However, upon reaching Ravenna, Colonel Ferrara, invoking I know not what peremptory orders, refused to enlist us and, having formed us into a detachment, had us escorted as far as Bologna under close guard, almost as though we were Austrian prisoners. Our appointed destination: Venice. But I was conscious of having taken up arms to break chains, not to have them imposed upon me... I resolved to face the hazards of a new flight.

As the sole officer among us, I had been entrusted with the task of drawing rations for the entire detachment... one fine morning I took advantage of this to bid farewell to the barracks and to San Petronio. The soldier Angeli, whom I had persuaded to follow me, placed at my disposal a few coins so that I might procure a disguise; but Venetian coinage, unhappily, had no currency in Bologna, and an extreme expedient imposed itself: to strip off my green uniform trimmed with white braid, which I had put on at Ravenna, and exchange it for a suit of *frustano* [coarse fustian cloth]. Deep was the sense of bitterness this caused me; but I found consolation at last in the thought that the working-man's dress is an honoured garb too — the honest and unfortunate *paria* [pariah] of my Country. Besides, the heart that yearns for Liberty, the heart that wins battles, I carried always, unchanged, within my breast.

At one o'clock in the morning, that same night, we arrived without incident at the walls of ancient Ferrara. The gates were shut; in the deep darkness, on the far bank of the Po, in the distance, the fires of the Austrian encampment gleamed. Save for the fortress, the town was in our hands. I knock at the gate.

— Who goes there?

— Italians who have deserted from the Austrian camp. We wish to fight for the cause of independence.

We are admitted, received with open arms, escorted to headquarters, overwhelmed with questions. Our intelligence, gleaned here and there from newspapers along the road, was found plausible... besides, we had passed ourselves off as common soldiers and no one expected revelations of high strategy. It was concluded by advancing us as far as Cento, where Major Mambrini enrolled us in the Legione Garibaldi.

Italy's most glorious sun was shining when, from the heights of Civita Castellana, I saw Michelangelo's dome spring out vigorously against the blue. My weariness vanished as if by enchantment and our arms stretched out towards the plain, towards that place where the great city was stirring — the city which had filled the world with its power and its wisdom. In those days, among the vestiges of her ancient civilisation, among the proud creations of the Middle Ages, the symbol of the new idea blazed forth in the red shirt.

It was the last days of April. On the 30th, the troops of the French invader broke their own onset against our breasts, and the hearts of Italians opened once more to hope!
