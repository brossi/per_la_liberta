CHAPTER XVIII

The following day, at La Roquette. They made us put on the grey uniform of the prisoners; they put us in straitjackets — not from any lust for torment, but to forestall attempts at suicide.

We had been conveyed to the new prison separately and separately confined; yet our cells were situated in such a way that we could see and speak to one another. A rectangular room, rather spacious, bare — four white walls, four windows, four doors. Whoever enters by the door communicating with the courtyard sees, directly opposite, the door and window of Felice Orsini's cell; Pieri's cell lay beyond the left-hand wall; beyond the right-hand wall, mine.

This was, for us, a great advantage over the Conciergerie; death was in prospect, but it saw fit to be preceded by some small relief.

Another circumstance contributed to making the present melancholy hour more bearable. My guards had followed me to La Roquette. They were to sleep in turns so as to keep me under continuous surveillance; but this did not trouble me in the least, for I had no intention whatsoever of anticipating my end by taking my own life.

On the very day of our transfer, our lawyers came to counsel us to lodge an appeal. Nothing concrete was hoped for; nevertheless, both Pieri and I consented without raising any objection. But Orsini would hear nothing of it. He declared that death would by now relieve him of a great burden, and that it would be all the more welcome the sooner it came. Giulio Favre, to obtain his assent, was obliged very nearly to force his hand.

The days of agony began and passed swiftly. In a state of mind only too understandable, the memories of those days flash through my mind in fits and starts, standing out like disconnected images against the sombre background of the hours in which, lost in meditation, I was preparing myself to die.

The chaplain of La Roquette, Monsieur Hughon, paid us a visit at least every twenty-four hours. He had been a former captain of dragoons and retained something of that character — thickset and robust despite his years, liberal despite his position. From his very first appearance I told him plainly that he would always be welcome on one condition: that he should never speak to me on religious subjects. He gave his word and kept it.

Giuseppe Pieri, on the other hand, willingly acknowledged his right to exercise his ministry, but not with the intention of submitting to it passively. From the very first evening he drew the chaplain into argument with a frankly defiant air, and in the days that followed allowed himself to be carried away by a destructive spirit, by a manner of arguing *ad hominem* that was exceedingly sharp and almost offensive. Constrained by the sorrowful circumstances, the former captain was obliged to put a good face on more than one lance-thrust to the ribs.

Pieri's voice was, moreover, the predominant note in that confined and severe atmosphere. And how could he — already so loquacious in even the most colourless circumstances of ordinary life — have brought himself to be silent, now that powerful emotions exalted him?

He talked and argued about everything, obstinately — at times even with a generous vein of comedy, especially when he chanced to refute the ravings of our Judges. But however he turned and twisted, he returned by preference to his political and philosophical ideas, as though a chasm were dragging him inexorably into the neck of a funnel.

A strange contrast with the demeanour of our leader, from whose cell there issued, for hours on end, not the least token of life — so much so that more than once I had asked myself whether it was not the tomb of Felice Orsini, and whether Felice Orsini had not become the tomb of his own soul.

For one hour each day we were permitted to walk in the great courtyard, though not simultaneously. Pieri was taken out for his breath of air alone; upon his return, Orsini and I went out together. We wore our straitjackets even while walking, and were watched not merely at close quarters but at the point of the musket.

In that brief hour, Orsini filled his lungs like a man reborn; yet the gaze he turned towards the blue sky was full of melancholy. He walked at a brisk pace, and I, bred to military discipline, followed him instinctively, keeping a little to one side. It was this way: if I were out of humour and said nothing, he would turn his head and question me with a look as if to say, "A storm?" But if I spoke, he would press on as though absorbed in the vision of other worlds, or if he answered, the words were drawn from deep within him, as with a corkscrew.

Those brief words sufficed to reveal to me a man rapidly undergoing a radical transformation.

Felice Orsini had brought two resentments into prison with him: one against Pieri, and one against Giuseppe Mazzini. It is well known that in his *Memorie* the irascible Romagnol had portrayed Mazzini as a modern Mahomet presuming to mould the destinies of Europe according to the whims of his odalisques — a circumstance of which the monarchist Cavour made admirable use. He had circulated those *Memorie* by the thousand, thus craftily throwing a bridge that offered the means of crossing the Rubicon, of furling the banner with its motto "God and the People," for too many, far too many Mazzinians — whether wavering from weakness of fibre, or eager to lend a semblance of respectability to base and venal calculations under any pretext whatsoever.

But the intense hatred towards the Master dissolved on the day that the guards and Giulio Favre informed Orsini of the noble defence of him made by the "Prophet" on the morrow of the attempt, at a time when other patriots, scrupulous as to the means of redeeming their country, had all but branded him a common assassin.

To this transformation, which I might call regenerating, another and far wider one of quite a different nature made correspondence. It is better to broach the matter at once. Felice Orsini was the only one of the three men condemned to die who listened to the exhortations of the dragoon-chaplain with more than passive, indeed with complaisant attention. And our daily walks had instilled in me a presentiment of what afterward came to pass. The convictions slowly acquired through a combative existence were, in Orsini, slowly disintegrating, and were giving way — indeed, yielding the ascendancy — to the underlying strata saturated with the fables absorbed in an unknowing childhood. In the imminence of his end, he returned to those religious practices to which only the blind multitude should still attribute essential virtue.

He therefore errs and lies — unconsciously — against truth, who, admiring the stoic fortitude with which Orsini mounted the scaffold, sought to hold him up as a model of consistency to be set against the figure of Silvio Pellico and others softened, as it were, by their sufferings. But he would be equally mistaken who should presume to protect Orsini's greatness by passing over his conversion in silence. Silence attenuates nothing and cancels nothing.

It may well be said in his defence that he succumbed to a cause far from ignoble. Capable of leonine boldness under a fleeting impulse, he was powerless — owing to his supremely sensitive nature — against a thought that was tormenting, persistent, obstinate, fixed: not that of the death overhanging him, but the other, deriving from the consciousness of his own responsibility in the ruin of high ideals, from the vision of the tragic deaths of others. His hair had rapidly turned white under it; more than one delicate fibre of his brain must have been left shaken and as it were scored by it.

Felice Orsini never, in any case, presented himself to his contemporaries as a discoverer of philosophical and moral truths. He called himself a patriot, rather, and was one from earliest youth; a patriot he was and proclaimed himself to be until the blade of the guillotine hung over his neck. He had pledged to Italy his every energy and the last drop of his own blood. Italy has had greater sons — none more unconditionally faithful. I say there is no need to glean in other fields to establish the glory of Felice Orsini.

Besides the dragoon-chaplain, there appeared almost daily at La Roquette a very powerful personage, the Prefect of Police, Pietri. He treated us in a most affable manner, and never failed to exert himself to instil some ray of hope into our minds — now asserting that the Court of Appeal would in all probability quash our trial; now assuring us that the Empress and a powerful court faction were disposed towards clemency so openly that the mind of Napoleon must in the end be influenced and won over by them.

But neither his manner nor his intelligent countenance could avail to dispel the instinctive distrust, to bridge the abyss that separates the conspirator from a man who must, by the very force of circumstances, hunt him down and derive his own advancement from the conspirator's ruin.

This distrust was in me sharpened by other circumstances. On more than one occasion I had sent to Elisa letters of the most passionate character. When I wished to write, the guard on duty would free my right hand more or less, and then, having bound it again, would carry the letter to the prison Director for him to affix the *nulla osta*. But my ardent appeals received no answer — absolute silence, as though Elisa had utterly abandoned me, or were dead. It is easy to imagine my torment. Then, all at once, there entered into my heart the suspicion that it was a refined cruelty on the part of the Police, devised for ends I could not clearly comprehend, yet whose existence I divined. From that moment my torment ceased, giving place to a smouldering rancour against those who had inflicted it upon me.

Moreover, Pietri had the fault of bringing to the prison, almost without interruption, one distinguished personage after another. A morbid curiosity seemed to impel the upper classes to verify — what shall I say — whether perhaps those who had dared to provoke such disorder were truly made of flesh and bone. This being put on display, like a wild beast in a cage, weighed upon me terribly.

One day I found myself in my cell with Pietri and a high-ranking military officer whose head was bandaged. I cannot quite recall whether it was General Magnan or General Roguet. Pietri introduced him to me simply as "one of your victims"; which caused my ill humour to break out. I retorted sharply that I had sought to strike no one but the man who had been the ruin of my country — and as for being a victim, I should very willingly have changed places with that wounded man. At this the officer stepped forward and, extending his right hand, affirmed that he bore not the least rancour, neither towards me nor towards any other. And I clasped that hand as best I was able, but made no reply, and, turning to the Prefect, asked him what interest the Police could have in intercepting my wife's letters. He appeared surprised, but contented himself with a shrug of the shoulders. The following day he returned alone, and before entering the cell stood for a few seconds erect on the threshold, filling the doorway with his tall, fine person. It seemed to me that I detected in that countenance something unusual, something inquisitive. He entered and, after repeating in a manner more affable and, I was almost about to say, more paternal than usual, the customary phrases, he rubbed his forehead and, lowering his voice somewhat — the guard had, moreover, withdrawn at his appearance — spoke to me at length of his deep sympathy for my youth. And, commiserating the sad fate reserved for me, in part through force of circumstance, in part also — he said — through the supreme recklessness of my companions, he assured me he had given much thought to finding a means of saving me — and not me alone! I already knew, he said, that at court there was no aversion to adopting measures tempered by clemency. But what I did not know — he added — was that His Majesty the Emperor was animated by a vivid sympathy for Italy, whose greatness he admired and whose misfortunes deeply moved him. Once a man such as this was set upon such a course, who could predict where he would stop? Certainly no sovereign in Europe was better placed to advance the redemption of Italy than Napoleon. So that if I considered matters with the eye of an enlightened patriot, I should not be long in understanding that I could better serve both myself and my country by aiding — allying myself, so to speak — with the man I had attempted to kill.

I believe my face expressed my profound astonishment, for Pietri laid a paternal hand upon my shoulder and, lowering his voice still further, said:

"I know that I am dealing with an intelligent young man and I expect, therefore, not to be considered a boor. Between His Majesty and his great designs stand the internal enemies of the Empire. Too weak to act openly, they have allied themselves in secret with its external enemies and have conspired. I have not come to propose anything dishonourable to you. The safety and well-being of States have their ineluctable demands. Assist the exalted personage upon whom the triumph of the Italian cause may depend; protect him against his internal enemies — and I give you my word of honour that you will not be long in rejoining your young wife. For what strange aberration would you leave her poor, abandoned, and alone to struggle in the world and against the world?"

Now the true reason for Elisa's pretended silence became not merely hinted at but plain to me; now my indignation had double cause to burst forth. "I know nothing," I replied, "and I have nothing to reveal. But even were it otherwise — you yourself, even you, if I were to accept your proposals, would be the first to feel horror for me. Nor from you, a Corsican, would I have expected such proposals — Italians abhor traitors!"

My natural vivacity urged me to gesture, and, being unable to do so, I trembled. But without intending it, I had touched a sensitive chord in a heart not wholly stripped of noble feeling.

I saw that man — hardened to so many emotions — cover his face with his hands. A few seconds, and he was looking me in the face; his eyes were red with tears. He departed with a promise that he would do everything within his power to save our lives. If he failed in that endeavour, he would tender his resignation.

I have no reason whatsoever to doubt the sincerity of that man; but what he may actually have done, or attempted to do, I cannot say. This much is certain: on the eleventh of March he came to La Roquette and communicated to Felice Orsini, if nothing else, his hopes. This was a little before the hour appointed for our walk. The conversation between the two was still in full warmth when that hour struck and the guards led me out into the courtyard alone. On my return, Felice Orsini — always so taciturn and almost sombre — appeared to me all at once transformed, and I was about to say buoyant. The moment he saw me, he said aloud: "Di Rudio, be of good heart. We shall not be executed!"

This took place, I repeat, on the morning of the eleventh of March. That very same evening, the Court of Cassation rejected our appeal. If Orsini's assertion was destined to find confirmation in reality, the events of the twelfth of March would have demonstrated it. That day passed, and our expectations were disappointed. That same night of the twelfth, by the light of torches, men were at work in the square of La Roquette erecting the scaffold.

The explanation of the enigma? I received it later from the lips of Mathieu de la Drôme. Public opinion was exerting powerful pressure in our favour. In spite of the Police, in the popular gathering-places a rousing song was being sung celebrating "the heroes of the fourteenth of January." The Empress and a powerful court faction had declared themselves for clemency. Napoleon III called their demonstrations indecent; but he had not the courage to oppose them openly. To eliminate the enemies who had fallen into his power, to cast terror among those who had escaped him — such was the secret yearning of the enemy of liberty; but it had to be accomplished while appearing to have his hand forced, as though through a scrupulous respect for the Law, and by shifting the responsibility onto others.

The Council of State was convened to decide upon the greater or lesser advisability of a sovereign pardon, and the Emperor made a brief appearance before it. "The Councillors of the Crown," he said, "were free to vote according to their own conscience; but he could not refrain from expressing one conviction of his. Within that Council there lurked traitors."

It was the dark threat. The Councillors had been convened not to yield to the tide of compassion, but to render service to their master. Could the dignitaries of an Empire — whose very essence was profiteering and corruption, and which, in that vortex, would have dragged all France down with it — hesitate between their ample sinecures and the lives of a handful of outcasts, rendered incapable of resistance or of doing harm? And had not the reason of State concealed still other and very different acts of cowardice?

As though this were not enough, the Archbishop of Paris, having scented the situation, dampened the mild influence of Eugenia Montijo by interposing between her and the Emperor the measured, subtle exhortations of the Church. Never had the hatreds and interests of clericalism and of the State shown themselves so closely akin: the conspirators smelt of Freemasonry; they had fought body and soul to put an end to that temporal power which is the ill-concealed supreme aim of every religious intrigue; they, had they been able, would have renewed the struggle. It was well worth suppressing them, even if, in doing so, one repudiated Christ once more.

The Council of State and the clergy — Napoleon III had more than he had hoped for, and his mind, which could plumb characters behind their masks, must have exulted darkly, and perhaps, like the despot of tragedy, exclaimed within himself: "Oh, how many are the consciences for sale!"
