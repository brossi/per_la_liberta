THE SIEGE OF ROME is indissolubly linked to the name of a man fatal to Liberty: that of Luigi Napoleone. The Carbonari, who had received him into their bosom, regarded him with suspicion as early as 1831; for at that time there had spread abroad the rumour that he had done away with his brother (Napoleone Luigi), who, having been born four years before him, stood as a barrier across his path to greatness.

Our revolutionary movements seemed then to open new and adventurous horizons, and the two sons of Hortense, taking part in those movements, sought in the Peninsula a field favourable to their ambitions; a compensation, perhaps, for the loss of the French throne, then warmed for the moment by Luigi Filippo. This train of ideas lent weight to the sinister rumour, which, moreover, could never be thoroughly investigated.

The majority of historians who have addressed the matter state simply that Napoleone Luigi died of measles. Others go so far as to admit that the truth is no longer certain, while vaguely suggesting that, if a crime was indeed perpetrated, it must be attributed to the fanatics of some *Vendita* — a Carbonari lodge.

Be that as it may, the distrust was attenuated by time and by the humanitarian poses of the prince. Moreover, the subversives clearly understood the immense advantage they might derive from a man whose very name was a whole programme of war against the old order, and who could not have emerged from his semi-obscurity save on condition of assuming the full responsibility of that programme.

His hopes of making his fortune in Italy having vanished, Luigi Napoleone turned his eyes toward France. He attempted first the coup de main of Strasbourg, which failed principally through his own want of resolution; then that of Boulogne, which led him straight to the prison cells of the fortress of Ham.

It was then that the Princess Cristina Belgioioso Trivulzio, deeply versed in the affairs of Young Italy, was induced to make use of her own influence at the Court of Luigi Filippo in order to obtain an audience with the prisoner.

The substance of that meeting required no lengthy preamble. The noblewoman offered the support of the liberals of France and Italy to effect an escape and to assist the man in keeping afloat upon the stormy sea of politics. Once he had attained power, he would repay the debt of gratitude by working toward the promulgation of democratic laws and championing our independence.

Luigi Napoleone's hesitation led the illustrious Italian lady to grant him two months for reflection. In the meantime, so as not to be unprepared for events, his friends would set about organising the plan of an escape.

The two months elapsed and a second meeting took place. The prisoner listened without committing himself; then expressed his deep sympathy for our Nation; but concluded by declaring that, for the present, he did not feel disposed to act — whether because he distrusted the accomplished and most cultivated noblewoman, or because he hoped for assistance from parties of ideas more akin to his own instincts and his own secret designs.

The plan seemed to have been abandoned when, like a thunderbolt, the news spread through our circles that Napoleone, suddenly changing his mind, had given his consent; and shortly afterwards, equally swift, came the news of his disguise and his flight — from France to Belgium, from Belgium to England.

At the Brunswick Hotel, he saw the Belgioioso again and sealed, so to speak, the pact: he would aid Italy as soon as affairs in France had been settled.

Events moved swiftly. Luigi Filippo was driven from the throne, and Napoleone requested permission to return home. To overcome the parliamentary opposition, Luigi Blanc arose and, with a single speech, changed the course of France's history as much as ten battles might have done. Napoleone became a candidate for the National Assembly for the Department of the Seine, and triumphantly set foot upon French soil.

A period of melancholy fame! The parties contending for mastery of the country flew at one another's throats and unleashed upon Paris the tempest of blood that prefaces every great political upheaval. Cavaignac emerged grimly from the carnage and aspired to the Presidency of the Republic, which the vanquished contested against him by desperately advancing the candidacy of Ledru Rollin. The resultant of those two forces, foreseen and facilitated by our secret associations, carried to victory a third candidacy: that of Luigi Napoleone. On the 20th of December, 1848, he became the Supreme Magistrate of the glorious Nation.

And his first act was to requite his friends by working, with arms and with treachery, to restore in the very heart of our Peninsula the most feeble, the most pernicious, the most hated of Governments — the government of the Pope!

This diabolical design was so cunningly dissembled as to induce the Italians to welcome with enthusiastic, fraternal demonstrations the landing of the troops of General Oudinot, who had appeared without warning in the waters of Civitavecchia.

In France, suspicions arose more promptly. The indignant protests against the suspected destruction of a sister Republic, to the advantage of the element most hostile to all liberty, spread from the halls of Parliament down among the multitudes, assuming here and there the proportions of full-scale uprisings. And perhaps the energy of the people would at last have imposed honest intentions, when news spread of the reverse suffered, on the 30th of April, by the arms of the invader.

A shudder of stern patriotism ran from one end of France to the other. "The prestige of the flag must be restored; the national honour must be vindicated at every point." As though the shame of a People derived not from the crime itself, but from its failure to be carried through! Luigi Napoleone thus found, unexpectedly, a willing accomplice in his sinister designs — strange to say — the most generous, the most loyal of all the powers of old Europe.

The French, the soldiery of Re Bomba, the Spaniards, the Austrians, and the subtle arts of the priests — how could Rome have escaped utter ruin?

One man alone, Giuseppe Mazzini, foresaw, in that tragic moment, that from death itself — by virtue of heroism and of martyrdom — right might renew itself, life might spring forth. He endeavoured to transfuse his faith into all those around him, and the learned and the humble were left as though transfigured. The "brigands" who defended Rome wrote, in notes of blood, the purest, the most fruitful of the pages of our modern history. The iron determination that one day restored the glorious capital to our Motherland is owed, in great measure, to their defeats.
