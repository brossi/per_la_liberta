## Chapter the Eighteenth

I TOUCHED Coira, where I received from Clementi — who was at that time under prosecution as a participant in the international disturbances — a letter for his younger brother. This brother would, in his turn, see to putting me in contact with the Burrasca brothers.

At San Maurizio, in the Engadina, at Muentzen — the point of contact between Switzerland, Württemberg and the Tyrol — at Salzburg, at Trento, not a single suspicious incident. At this last locality I presented myself to the Austrian Authorities and signed, under the assumed name, my *passaporto giardinetto* — my little pocket passport — and they did not cast upon me so much as a single searching glance.

And from Trento, deviating from the direct route, I ascend again the valley furrowed by the torrent Trevis, meet with young Clementi; and find myself that same evening face to face with the four smugglers.

Of them Clementi had told me that they were the despair of the manhunters and the pride of the valley. Jealous of their own name, they left the easy enterprises to lesser men and made it a point of honour to bring to completion the most hazardous. Spirits intolerant of every restraint, they lent themselves nobly even to the righting of wrongs and to the struggle for others' liberty. They had already rendered no inconsiderable services to the Italian cause.

No masterful phrases were needed to bend them to my purpose, and when the elder of the brothers — the captain of the small and courageous band — set down his glass from which he had been sipping with a vigorous thud, extended his right hand and pressed mine, I understood that I had concluded, with that simple act, a pact for life and for death, far more binding than any notarial document.

Early in the morning, having refused every guide, I set forth on foot along little-trodden paths which traced themselves, little by little, vividly in my memory before they unfolded themselves before my gaze. In the vast horizons, the sudden precipices, the solemn silences, the alpine fragrances, I found again my childhood, and was so filled with it that the phantoms of the future grew pale and almost dissolved.

When, from the heights, I caught sight of my Belluno, the rain was falling in great drops; but there was serenity in my heart. I stood under my broad umbrella, searching among the uncertain lines for my father's house, and having found it, it seemed to me that dear old father and mother and all my beloved ones must feel me near and trembling, as I felt them.

I reached the bank of the Ardo at dusk; but the autumn rains had swollen it so greatly that finding a ford proved impossible. It was a setback! Don Bastiano's house stood on the opposite side of the city. To reach it I now found myself forced to venture upon the main road, to cross the bridge, to traverse Belluno from end to end. I waited patiently for nightfall, hidden in a thicket.

Ten o'clock was striking when, my umbrella lowered over my face, I entered among the houses. The wayfarers were few and hurried. I scrutinised them from afar, in the uncertain light of the lanterns — not one *sbirro*, not one acquaintance, not a living soul that seemed to take any notice of me. I might have said that I was, to my fellow-citizens, an invisible spectre. But when the houses thinned out and I had left every lantern behind me, a sudden thought came to disturb me: "What if someone had laid a trap for me, to lure me into the solitary dwelling of Don Bastiano?"

I slipped into the little garden that surrounds it, with such precautions that not even the mastiffs took note of it. The ground floor — dark; all the shutters closed; those of the upper storey closed likewise — only one window yawned open a gleam of light that went to lose itself among the boughs of a double row of trees. I strained my ear — the voice of the Ardo, a distant rustling of leaves, nothing more! Then I gathered up a small stone and flung it against the window, while with an instinctive movement I gripped the butt of a pistol.

And there, darkening behind the glass, appeared a woman's figure — it was Salvi, Don Bastiano's servant, a handsome Cadorine girl with generous hips and impertinent bosom. She threw open the shutters and whispered into the night: "Is it you, Moretto?"

Don Bastiano received me with an embrace and with sobbing. His first words were: "Praised be Heaven! I had already given up hope of—" he meant to say more, but his voice failed him, and overcome by a surge of tenderness, I too sobbed.

It was my brother Giustiniano, who had remained to one side, almost unnoticed, who managed to utter a coherent syllable. He said: "Sad news. Our friends, the best of them — arrested; Don Bastiano — arrested; poor Father — arrested."

And he recounted to me the particulars of the catastrophe. Don Bastiano had calmly stretched out his wrists for the manacles, "happy to contribute, by his own sacrifice, to completing the series of sufferings demanded by Providence for the redemption of his Country." But Father had wished to break his bamboo cane across the shoulders of the *sgherri* — and can the cruelties of oppressors still be called news! For some days now the unexpected arrests have continued. My own house is under constant surveillance. How have you managed to arrive there without molestation? I have a vague suspicion that you have fallen from the sky!

I reflected, and concluded: "Now the important thing is to bring the family home. We shall think of the rest afterwards."

But poor Salvi, broken by the ordeal, rendered still more helpless by the darkness, stumbled at every step. I placed one of my pistols in her hand and said: "Go ahead and clear the way. Think of who might be lurking about. Sleeping in one's own bed has become an arduous problem in these times!"

At his request I recounted to him what I had seen in my rapid journey. And before Mother had time to notice and react, I had lifted her in my arms and proceeded, proud to bear the one who had borne me. And pressing her to me I printed upon her brow a long kiss. I did not foresee that it would be the last! "Until we meet again, Mother!" And I promised her that, however adverse fortune might show herself, I would always act in such a manner that, when we met again, thinking of her love, my conscience would have no cause to reproach me for having abused it.

"Listen, my son," said Don Bastiano. "You know how I love the Fatherland. For its liberty I would give — everything, save my soul. But it is one thing to sacrifice what belongs to ourselves, and another to imperil the safety of others, the lives of those whom we hold dear. It is a tremendous responsibility for which I should have to render account, sooner or later, to One who counts the drops of blood vainly shed and is not appeased by syllogisms. Heed me: these enterprises succeed — sometimes — if they burst forth suddenly, like lightning! But should the enemy, all-powerful as he is, divine them — Carletto! Carletto! of what avail are generous resolves, the daring of four, of thirty, of a hundred young men, against a colossus that holds all Europe in subjection? Would you offer your neck to the noose, thus light-heartedly, for the sterile glory that might derive from it? And has not our Italy already too many, far too many of her best to mourn and to commiserate? Reserve your energy for more propitious times. How that may come to pass I do not know and could not tell you; but I believe that the Police have some inkling of our plots. Arrests are not made at random. You are not safe here. O times, O times! Friend meets friend and must grieve to see him, and must beseech him to depart. Austria makes us worse than barbarians!"

At that moment, outside and only a few paces off, there resounded a sharp crack; the mastiffs howled — I blew out the candle.

Moments of terrible anxiety on Don Bastiano's behalf. I had gone, on tiptoe, to the door and having opened it a crack, had levelled the barrels of both my pistols through it. Then, thinking that to remain caged precluded every avenue of escape, I drew the door ajar and stepped out — a great darkness, a great silence. As a further precaution I made the circuit of the house — the wind, a shutter banging — that was all! But when I went back in and relit the candle, I was struck by Don Bastiano's mortal pallor.

And I thought again of the fine walks taken in his company, in the autumn, when he, to educate me in manly sentiments, would recount the deeds of our forebears; when his priest's cassock disappeared from before my eyes and I pictured him grave in corselet and helm — so fierce were his words, so intense his enthusiasm!

Don Bastiano, accustomed to reading my thoughts since my earliest years, said: "Do not number me among the cowards. My character as a priest and these grey hairs impose duties upon me. But the die is cast — upright and serene to the very end!"

Early in the morning, while I, wearied by the long journey, was still sleeping soundly, he went to inform Father of my arrival. I found the proud old man still vigorous, though on his noble face I could discern wrinkles that were unknown to me. But our meeting was different from what I had expected. It is probable that Don Bastiano had induced him to throw into the balance all the weight of paternal authority in order to dissuade me from so hazardous an enterprise. No embraces, no kisses; a powerful handshake, but a solitary one, and immediately, sharpened and rendered almost sarcastic, the same exhortations I had heard the evening before from the lips of the good priest. Our discussion grew so heated as to become deeply painful to me. I resolved to cut it short. "When the uniform and the bread of thieves grew hateful to me," I said, "I expected neither celebrations nor honours. Passive submission does not befit the spirit of a Rudio. And since you counsel it — very well then, I shall continue to beat my own path, alone. From this moment I have no longer a father!"

The poor old man remained for a moment with a gesture suspended, as though he had suddenly been turned to stone; then his eyelashes filled with tears. He flung himself forward with a sudden impulse, embraced me and held me fast with a strength of which I should not have thought him capable. When he could speak: "It is you, it is you, Carletto! I recognise my own blood. Go! So long as my strength holds, I shall be with you!"

It was decided that the arms should not be unearthed until the spirits of those bold men who were to handle them had been prepared and well disposed. Father and Don Bastiano undertook to approach them. In the meantime I would, by way of prudence, seek a refuge less carefully watched by the Police. In the environs of Belluno, towards the north, where the plain borders upon the heights, there stood an age-old chestnut tree, known and almost famous throughout the city and the surrounding country for its extraordinary dimensions. It was agreed that I should make my way, under cover of darkness, beneath its boughs. A trustworthy person would come, towards midnight, to indicate to me where I might lay my head. The recognition signal: three whistles, to which I would reply by whistling four times.

At nine o'clock I was already stretched out on the grass, musing as I contemplated the moon through the black foliage; then the moon set and the wind began again its unbridled course. I rose to stretch my limbs and posted myself behind the enormous trunk.

From the tower of Belluno twelve strokes rang out into the void, and my eye, now accustomed to the darkness, searched among thickets and vineyards. Another long hour passed — I was seized by dark forebodings.

Suddenly, softly, timidly, from beyond a group of bushes, there followed the three whistles — then a whisper — "On your guard, Moretto! There are several of them!" I looked intently and made out, through the twigs, something — like the overcoat of a *sbirro*. That I did not fire on the instant was only for fear of missing my mark and finding myself defenceless before the enemy. I answered the signal and waited with both weapons levelled —

Oh, what blood and what remorse were spared me by one moment of hesitation! A shadow advanced with arms outstretched — my mother!
