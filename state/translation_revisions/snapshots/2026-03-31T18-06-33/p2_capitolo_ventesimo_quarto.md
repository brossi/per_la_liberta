DURING my hours of rest, I neglected nothing in my efforts to gather ample information about the men who lived and suffered around me. Antonio Gomez, after having been assigned for some time to the service of the Officers, had recovered, though bronzed in complexion, his old air of a good-natured priest. But an unexpected order removed him from all my influence. He was sent, along with others, to the penitentiary at Corù on the Continent, and I had barely time to press his hand. From that day, although I trust that the political upheavals in France brought him better times, I never saw him again, nor heard any further word of him.

On the island, however, there were other Italians — a dozen, perhaps; Romans for the most part, all condemned for having doubled their political hatred by layering upon it a private one; for having avenged, with our own native tyranny, the outrage inflicted upon them by foreign tyranny. Christ forgave much — so it is said — to those who loved much; He ought to forgive far more to those who have hated much and, in their hatred, found the virtue not to be a sheep. Among these Romans was a Morelli, a tailor, condemned for life. Who lends a more ready ear to hope than he who has lost all hope of his own?

I found, moreover, other Italians condemned, or simply relegated, for political reasons. They resided in part on the Île du Diable, but crossed over frequently at low tide, on the pretext, most commonly, of visiting the pharmacy attached to the Hospital. There was Galli, there was Rossini, there was Tebaldi, and we soon formed a close fraternal familiarity. Their story was known to me, and I could rest assured that, even should they not take part in the escape, they would assist it by every means within their power.

Among the men I had met during my first crossing from Toulon and who had disembarked at the island from the very outset, there was a French archpriest, condemned for life for the murder of a child of whom he had made an abominable use. Throughout the entire voyage, he had jealously clutched beneath his arm a large Bible of his own. In a moment of expansion, he had confided to me, his voice trembling, that he had concealed within the binding of that volume something in the order of twenty thousand francs, and that the tormenting thought of being strangled by hands greedy for his treasure gnawed at him more than any remorse. He intended to use that money to procure the means of escape. I called it to mind and reckoned upon it. To save the indispensable provisions from our limited daily ration would have demanded a sacrifice too severe and too prolonged for our hunger and for my impatience. The supplies would have to be purchased gradually at the penitentiary stores, so as not to arouse suspicion, at usurious prices if need be. Why should the archpriest not furnish us with the *conquibus* — the wherewithal?

But my good fortune willed that there should be on the island yet another individual who might prove far otherwise useful to me. He was a former officer of the French navy, a rabid Orleanist, a man all daring and all nerve; he was serving a life sentence, one more of the circumstances which I counted in my favour and should almost call propitious. That man had already done battle with storms; the Ocean held no terrors for him; it was not for him a boundless plain without paths.

All these thoughts, spokes of a disjointed wheel, remained for a long time motionless and as though crystallised in my mind, until all of a sudden they received an extraordinary impulse through the almost spontaneous formation of the very piece that was missing; which, in my long meditations, I had struggled to devise.

One evening, leaving the workshop, I was making my way towards my hut, which stood a good mile distant, when, upon approaching the quay, I caught sight of Morbioli bent over a gunboat, polishing its brasswork with perfect composure. The gunboats were, in truth, modest skiffs carrying at stem and stern a little pop-gun of a cannon. They were launched upon the water early in the morning and hauled ashore again at five in the evening.

Morbioli, a boatman in Rome, was assigned to their upkeep. How many times had I watched him busying himself about those trifles that were my constant torment! But now only, through some singular disposition of my spirit, those his languid rubbings, which struck me as a kind of caress, roused in me a fierce vexation, and I cried within myself: "Stave them in, you great fool; sink them, if you can!" That simple word — "sink them!" — darted through my mind like lightning illuminating a pitch-dark night. It was a revelation entire, and I shivered at it. I drew near to Morbioli, and I believe my face betrayed the secret tremor, for he looked at me with evident unease and said: "What is the matter?"

"Morbioli, when I was back there at the Montagne d'Argent, a hospital was being built and we had already reached the whitewashing stage. A convict who held me in great regard was one day at the top of his ladder, painting the façade, when from a neighbouring window there came a murmur of voices. It was the Commandant, the Chief Warder, and a condemned man in conference. The whitewasher distinctly heard my name spoken, and stood rigid, listening. I will tell you briefly: the Commandant was guaranteeing the immediate freedom of that convict, provided he succeeded in implicating me in some attempt at escape, in such a manner that the sentinels should have the opportunity to bring me down with rifle fire."

"Why do you tell me this?"

"To persuade you that in the registers, beside my name, a cross has been entered, and that even were I a man so contemptible as to draw you into a plot with the intention of denouncing you — what good would it do me? There would be no pardon for me either way. And now listen..."

I confided to him the secret workings of my mind, the circumstances and the persons upon whom I grounded my hopes; the tactics which would without doubt make us masters of the cutter and of the fishermen; the stratagem which he, all unwittingly, by putting an end to my long torments, had inspired in me. This was precisely the part I should entrust to his loyalty and his intelligence. He scratched his head, as was his habit in moments of anxiety; then he raised his face, and I read his full assent in his eyes, which sparkled. On the spot, I received a compliment of a flavour thoroughly *trasteverino* — the strangest that has ever been addressed to me in all my long existence: "You're a devil! Blessed be the woman that bore you, and may you... *murì amazzato!*" — God grant you a warrior's death!

With Morbioli's assent, the first link of the chain was forged. The others were formed one by one, slowly but wonderfully. On a cloudy night, troubled by the uproar of a fierce storm at sea, I slipped silently from my hut and, armed with a chisel, crept along the rocks to the very foot of the storehouses — a wide timber construction resting in part upon piles. I tried, and tried again, and managed to lift a plank of the boarding. I made my way inside, groping and nosing out my path like a rat, and when I emerged I had upon my back six great hammocks of the sturdiest canvas, which Morelli, the Roman tailor, found means to sew with consummate skill. We had our sail!
