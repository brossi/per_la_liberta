# Chapter Twelve

They led me through three rooms which were occupied, despite the hour, by clerks intent upon their desks. Arriving at a fourth, I sat for a long while before a fireplace in which two brands burned crosswise. And since, in order to better collect my thoughts, I had closed my eyes, I heard a guard say in an undertone: "False alarm! If he were an accomplice, he would not be sleeping so soundly!"

At last they brought me into a great hall, whose brilliant light dazzled me for a moment. The first impression I distinctly perceived was that of the door closing at my back; then I saw take shape before me — I must be forgiven for dwelling on these particulars — a writing-desk strewn with papers, standing perfectly isolated in the middle of the hall. An old man apparently in his sixties, still vigorous in appearance, with smooth skin full of lustrous reflections, sat to one side of it and was frowning over a document. At my appearance he spread it across his knees and raised his face. It was the Prefect of Police.

The desk stood four paces from the entrance, along the right-hand wall. The stirrups were so high as to allow me to discern of the man seated there only the crown of his skull and nothing more — the skull of the Examining Magistrate. Beside the desk, some seated and some standing, were various high-ranking officers of the army, their chests laden with medals, and among those gaudy uniforms there moved, with a sombre air, certain black beetles in civilian dress. Among these last I recognised a personage of the imperial family. The Examining Magistrate rose, and his physiognomy was not unfamiliar to me. He had a history. In the days when Louis Napoleon was in low estate, he had trampled underfoot the very Laws of which he was now the salaried champion. He owed his position to this. Beside the desk, in various attitudes, some seated and some standing, were those high officers; and as my eyes travelled here and there over those showy uniforms with the intensity of one who peers into the abyss by which he might be swallowed, I saw leap out from the red-panelled walls, and as though emerging from them at that very instant, several marble busts which fixed me with their pupil-less eyes. And the men too fixed me without the flutter of an eyelid, as though they partook of the marmoreal nature of those busts!

Then the magistrate asked me my particulars, and I replied in accordance with the passport they had confiscated. But when he asked whether I knew my room-companion and I answered in the negative, his face became animated.

"How can that be?" he asked. "You sleep with a man and do not even know who he is? Where did you first encounter him?"

"During my journey from Calais to Paris. He understands a little of my language. He was the first person I met on arriving in this city, and I asked him the address of a hotel. 'Come to mine,' he answered; 'I have a room with two beds. You will spend less.' I accepted. He told me his name, I told him mine. That is all! Why should I have required an account of his past?"

"But you — what was your purpose in coming to Paris?"

"To earn my bread, selling Burton Ale."

"How did you pass yesterday?"

I had already committed my answer to memory, one which, moreover, differed but little from the truth. Monsieur Pierey, being well acquainted with Paris, had taken me in the morning to the Louvre Gallery. Later Pierey himself had gone to call upon some acquaintance of his, and I had waited for him in a tavern. I then recounted the dinner taken at the restaurant in the rue Montmartre.

"At what hour did you dine?"

"Towards seven. We returned to our hotel. Scarce had we arrived when the porter told Monsieur Pierey that a gentleman had come asking for him — he should wait, the visitor would return shortly. And so it happened. That is all!"

"Who was this gentleman?"

"I do not know. He had come for the other man and not for me. Besides, I noticed that he wished to speak on business, and out of discretion I withdrew."

"Where did you go?"

"To the cabinet at the end of the corridor. When I returned, the gentleman had departed."

"But do you not recall his appearance?"

"The room was dark. I remember, however, that he wore a full beard."

"Would you recognise him if you saw him?"

"Perhaps yes, perhaps no."

At this point the judge turned to a guard and ordered him to bring in "the other prisoner." When the door opened again, Felice Orsini appeared upon the threshold. The bandage bound about his head was steeped in blood. He had the air of a man weak and weary. His first glance was for me. He seemed to address me a mute question, and upon his face I saw pass a fleeting unease.

"Is this the man?" the Magistrate asked me.

"The man who came to the hotel wore a full beard. I believe I said as much."

The judge then left his desk and, approaching Orsini, ran his fingertips beneath his chin. He wished to assure himself that the razor had not passed there recently. Fortunately, the prisoner's skin must have seemed, to the touch, rough with bristles.

It was to Orsini that he directed his next question. He spoke in English.

"From what part of England do you come?"

"From Kent."

"How far is Kent from London?"

"Thirty leagues."

Those two words were enough. In England one speaks of miles, not leagues. Moreover, Orsini had pronounced his "thirty" by converting the sound of the *th* into a *t* and rolling the *r* vigorously — which is not in the habit of English mouths.

The Magistrate said: "You are as English as I am Turkish. Indeed, to tell you the plain truth, you are not even French."

The pale face of Felice Orsini flashed with sudden fire. He drew himself up, disdainful as an ancient Roman. He said — nay, he cried: "Ah no, by God! I am Italian, and I am proud of it. My name is Felice Orsini. Ask me nothing further; I would not answer you."

He had poured his whole soul into his words — the soul of the noble and resolute man who commands the pygmies of character. They were subdued by it and dared not oppose him. He was led away, without more ado. But the Prefect of Police, who throughout the interrogation had remained upright and motionless beside his desk, now set himself to rummage feverishly through the drawers. "Ah, ah!" he said at last, advancing towards the personages in uniform and out of it. "Here is a secret Austrian circular. Felice Orsini — the dangerous revolutionary who escaped from the castle of Mantova. We have made a fine catch!"

Then he returned to his desk and, turning to me: "Monsieur da Sylva, do you know these implements?"

I looked, and it was as though a ray of light illuminated the cells of my brain. Now I understood why Napoleon lived and triumphed! Two of the "implements" which, by their explosion, were to have sounded the signal of awakening to two peoples, lay idle as paperweights upon the Prefect's desk. By what means had they come there? They were there — that was enough for me. Their dimensions were equal; upon both, at the tips of the lower half, the deadly detonating caps still gleamed. Inexplicable imprudence!

That sight sufficed to rouse within my breast the furies which the overriding thought of my own safety had momentarily lulled. And my every fibre swelled again with a turbulent spirit; my every fibre recovered an impulse of rebellion.

I thought: "Perhaps what was not achieved by the direct path will be achieved indirectly, by carrying new confusion into the enemy's camp, already doubtless thrown into disorder — and why not? But in any case — to kill as a human protest, avenging the betrayed and the oppressed!"

I do not say now that my thought was that of the prudent man; I say that such was my thought. And I say that even today I hold supremely human the cry: "Let Samson perish, and with him the Philistines!"

I rose and leaned forward, half-closing my eyes as though short-sighted. Two steps — only two steps without arousing suspicion — and I would have sprung forward — one swift movement, the thunderbolt! — guards, Prefect, Magistrate, generals, myself — ambitions and hopes, servility and the lust for command, crimes and virtues — a few shreds of flesh — nothingness! —

A hand heavy as fate seized me by the shoulder, and I fell back upon my chair. My heart was beating convulsively, but I made an effort and said in a calm, almost bewildered tone: "I do not understand — of what does this concern?"

From that moment my interrogation proceeded swiftly and according to my wishes. In putting the final touches to my account of my movements, I took care to transpose, so to speak, a few minutes, and made my entry into the café coincide precisely with the hour of the attempt. I also alluded to the letter I had written there. The game was dangerous; but the proprietress had not seen me enter, and on that I relied. The Magistrate whispered a few words in a guard's ear, and the guard departed immediately. I gathered that a verification was to be made. It was not long before the same guard returned and fully confirmed my deposition, recounting even the incident of the four two-centime stamps which I had forgotten. The Magistrate then turned to the Prefect and said: "For my part, I have no further question to put."

"Very well!" observed the latter — "But Monsieur da Sylva might be a valuable witness. I would ask him to remain at his hotel at the disposal and at the expense of the State. We will return his passport later. No objection?"

"None whatsoever! Especially if the State undertakes the expenses. My means are extremely limited!"

And for the third time that night I heard the French Police lavishing apologies. The high personages, in uniform and out of it, fell into conversation among themselves, glancing at me from time to time as though exchanging their impressions of me. And the Magistrate, bidding me good night, turned to the guards at my sides. His last words were: "Escort Monsieur da Sylva out of the Prefecture!"

As I passed through the various rooms my thoughts retraced my steps, and I pondered the extraordinary perspicacity of a Magistrate who had let the wolf out of the trap in such a fashion. It would have sufficed for him to interrogate me as to my place of origin; to have asked me which clients I had dealt with in my capacity as seller of Burton Ale; to have addressed to me, or had addressed to me, a single word in Portuguese, and my true nature would have been laid bare. I concluded that the Magistrate and the Police were in a state of over-excitement — perhaps even of vague apprehension — which clouded their vision and halved their dynamic power.

Yet he who dozes tonight, I thought, may be wide awake and watchful tomorrow. Tomorrow? Let me but set foot in the open air once more and clever is he who finds me again! The Orléans station is under close watch; the same must be true of the other stations — but many roads lead out of Paris, and Paris is vast, nor do I lack friends and hiding-places there.

I had reached the last room, and there I found the unexpected. Through the very door which had been opened to grant freedom to the wolf, there entered at that very moment, under close escort, Antonio Gomez. I looked away at once, the only means within my power to make him understand that he should feign ignorance of my presence. But he too had his wits clouded, and passing by my side he murmured in a low voice, in French: "*Siamo perduti!*" — We are lost!

Fate willed that among his escort there should be an intelligent man. "Ah, ah! Do you know one another?" And Gomez, driving home the nail that was piercing me: "Of course we know one another!"

I had hastened to set foot upon the threshold. "*Ferma!*" — Stop! — thundered a voice at my back. I was invited to sit down and the door closed. Gomez continued on his way to the great hall; I remained in the trap in great anxiety, biting my lips. Ten minutes passed. A clock creaked and struck four. Other guards came. They took me by the arm without a word and led me away. We traversed an underground passage.

I passed the rest of the night in a cell of the Conciergerie!
