HOWEVER vast my desires, I could for the moment do nothing other than seek some means of establishing myself decently in London. Before long, Bernard's machinations placed me in such a position that, far from combating the enemies of Italian integrity, I was compelled to attend, as a matter of urgency, to the defence of my own personal integrity.

The slanders of which Rossi had given me the first intimation were swiftly confirmed to me, with a greater abundance of particulars, by various other persons. Dr. Bernard not only persisted, in private conversation, in the assertions he had launched forth when he believed me already gone to ground; but he attempted to lend them weight by maintaining that I had recovered my freedom, not through a premeditated and boldly executed escape, but because the French Authorities, in recompense for my betrayals, had, so to speak, thrown open for me the gates of the penal colony.

Elisa moreover informed me that certain gentlemen of the Times and others — the very same who had initiated the petition for my pardon — had crowned their efforts by collecting, on her behalf, a small capital of one thousand and one hundred pounds. From so much generosity, and from the kind of allowance that had been settled upon her on the eve of my departure for Paris, she had drawn no relief whatsoever. During my long absence, Bernard had delivered to her one single, solitary pound.

Upon whom was I to lay the blame? Was I to regard Bernard's conduct towards me as inspired by the terror of a guilty conscience? Did he slander me because he found himself incapable of defending himself honestly? To what end did his cowardly manoeuvres tend?

I wrote on this matter to several conspirators of the English Committee; but not one deigned to reply to me — whether because Bernard had forestalled me; whether because secret ties induced them to treat the question solely in closed session; whether because human selfishness counselled them to imitate the worthy Pilate, of traditional memory.

I must make a partial exception for Tomaso Allsop, the man who had lent his name to Felice Orsini. And I say partial because he sent me a letter containing very courteous phrases and... a banknote for five pounds; but not the slightest allusion to the pressing questions I had put to him.

Having weighed my situation thoroughly, I resolved that to press the matter of the thousand and one hundred pounds might cause my agitation to appear motivated by venal instincts. I therefore decided not to raise it again, and to confront Bernard openly, upon the other question alone. I would call the English public to judge between his assertions and mine.

Rossi, who never ceased to take an interest in my fortunes, introduced me to a certain Kinaer, a journalist already brought to the extremity of his strength by tuberculosis, but intellectually still full of vigour. He, guided by the information I supplied, wrote a succinct account of the Orsini trial and of my escape. A German publisher undertook to send a printed copy to the most widely read English newspapers. This preamble accomplished, I embarked with Kinaer upon a lecture tour, beginning at Nottingham — my wife's city — and proceeding thence through Derby, Birmingham, Manchester, Lee, and other manufacturing centres. Kinaer attended, with great dexterity, to the financial side of the undertaking. I had reserved for myself the task of setting before the audience the story of my case and of answering the questions put to me once I had finished speaking.

I never failed to make it clear that I was resolved to speak in public not in order to make money, but to provoke a discussion from which I had nothing to fear. And I challenged my accusers to come forward and oppose, to my defence, their alleged documents and their arguments.

Did this ever come to pass? One Sherman, who had attended one of my lectures, spoke of it at a Freethinkers' Club to which he belonged, observing how contrary it was to the English people's sense of justice to witness the spectacle of a man labouring under the weight of a shameful accusation, and not to lend him a helping hand so that he might stand face to face with his detractors.

The *Reasoner*, the organ of that club, then directed by Holyoake, reproduced that sentiment in one of its articles, which came to my attention in Birmingham. I immediately wrote to Holyoake, declaring myself entirely willing to submit to the judgement of whatever Committee he might be pleased to appoint. At the same time, I expressed to him my desire that Giuseppe Mazzini should be among its members, since he, knowing thoroughly the behind-the-scenes of our revolutionary endeavours, and to some small degree my public life as well, would greatly have facilitated the rendering of a dispassionate and impartial verdict.

Three events, in rapid succession, led me to consider the dispute fully resolved, notwithstanding the well-known proverb: "Slander, slander, and something will always stick..."

Among the convicts I had known on the Île Royale du Salut was one Le Roux, a Frenchman who, shortly after my escape, had been transferred to the penitentiary of Coru. From that latter place he had succeeded in escaping and taking refuge in England. Having learned of the rumours circulating on my account, he had, of his own accord, set about contradicting them, affirming himself to be an eye-witness of my "Coup d'État," as he called it. In recounting its particulars, he added to them those of the aftermath it had upon life in the penal colony and upon the severity of its regime.

Now, however absurd it may appear, the faith that had not been accorded to the words of an Italian patriot was freely granted to those of a French convict. It is somewhat humiliating; but I shall not complain of it... the strangeness worked to the advantage of myself and of the truth. So fell the little tale of the "doors thrown open."

The second incident had its origin in Giuseppe Mazzini. The editor of the *Reasoner*, having conferred with him, wrote to me and published that a jury of honour was superfluous. A dispassionate examination of the circumstances, together with the attestations of the Maestro, had placed my innocence beyond all doubt.

The third was brought about, unexpectedly, by Simon Bernard himself. "A chicken born must scratch." The funds entrusted to his care by a certain secret society disappeared in a manner considered not in the least mysterious. He was abandoned by his own supporters, went mad, and — *si vera sunt exposita* — died in a lunatic asylum. Reticence is rendered necessary by the fact that I never troubled to verify personally the death certificate that concerns him...

Let us refresh ourselves at the spectacle of a very different conspirator. During the wanderings to which I have alluded, I made the personal acquaintance of Baron Egassy di Toroefalda, the inventor of the Orsini bombs, the technical mind behind our conspiracy. If the martyr, in his trial, attributed the original idea to himself, the reason is to be sought in his honest circumspection. I have already remarked that he never named any person not already compromised.

Baron Egassy was a native of the Carpathian mountains and had been a lieutenant-colonel of Austrian artillery. A great liberal and a man of action, but rough, military to the last degree, he detested all those who claimed to vindicate their rights by shouting in the public squares, or to stir up revolutions by preaching the postulates of philosophy. His muscular strength exceeded the bounds of the credible.

He maintained that the best way of ridding oneself of a despot is to approach him on some plausible pretext, to place with lightning swiftness one hand upon his shoulder and the other upon his head, and to snap his vertebrae with a single sharp blow. Unhappily it is exceedingly difficult to call Brutus back to life and to endow him, into the bargain, with the muscles of an Egassy di Toroefalda.

Who possessed, among his many gifts, those of a chemist of no small worth. Not only did he teach Felice Orsini the method of preparing mercury fulminate, but he had also invented a mixture which, on exploding, would asphyxiate all those within a circuit of five hundred feet in diameter. I believe it was something akin to modern lyddite. He refused to reveal its secret to Governments, because they would use it — so he said — to perpetuate the oppression of Peoples.

One day, stigmatising with phrases full of passion the pusillanimity of those politicians who labour so mightily to vindicate the right of property, while remaining deaf and dumb and blind to the far more vital question of the freedom of action, he ended by promising me that, sooner or later, he would confide to me the formula of his explosive.

Uttered in a moment of expansive agitation, I attached no great weight to that promise. A few days later we parted, and I thought no more of it. To my surprise, in 1862 I received a letter from him. He begged me to hasten without delay to the hospital at Birmingham. He was there in a grave condition and wished to keep his promise and confide to me "the secret." I departed on the first train; but barely upon entering that sorrowful house, my eagerness was unexpectedly checked by an attendant. "The Baron di Toroefalda? He died this morning."

I wished to see him. The florid vigour, the portentous strength — more than extinguished and destroyed by the onset of a violent malady — seemed to have evaporated... the yellowish skin clung to the bones as though gummed fast. Observing him, I divined the slow work of starvation; although the physicians, taking the final collapse as their pretext, had attributed it to an entirely different cause, distinguished by some name drawn from the Greek which I no longer recall.

And yet that man possessed the secret of a formula which might have restored him to his former wealth. To follow the current... who could have blamed him? For centuries, every great invention has been turned, principally, to profit and to servitude. Progress the exclusive property of the few; science a prostitute, as religion is, as human Justice is. It seems fated!... But not with impunity did the poor Baron enclose within his heart a spark of greatness!

He had preferred to die in penury among the destitute; forgotten, without glory... if glory consists in the applause of men.

To detach myself from that corpse, already almost in dissolution, I was obliged to make a great effort. His eyes were wide open, as though he had died with his thoughts fixed upon my arrival and were still awaiting me. I wished to close them, and felt a shudder pass through me... It seemed to me that, in drawing down the thin veil of his eyelids, I had given the last touches to the work of death; that I had thrust that exceptional man, with my own fingers, into eternity.
