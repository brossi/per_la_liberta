WHAT I am about to relate I gathered from the letters Giustiniano wrote me. They are impressed upon my memory in indelible characters. To hearten our mother, Giustiniano sketched the situation for her with a generous infusion of optimism. Luisa would undergo some interrogation, but would soon be set at liberty. Papa had the reputation of an old patriot, an irreconcilable man, and would not find it so easy to extricate himself. But how to condemn him without proof? The judicial authorities were rigorous beyond all telling; yet they were anxious to appear upright to the last, and would not dare commit so open a villainy. As for me, he himself had accompanied me as far as the mines of Agordo. From that point, I had chosen paths that only I and the chamois knew how to travel. By now I must already have crossed the border, while the Police still believed me in Belluno and were searching for me there. Before long, they would receive letters from me posted in Switzerland, or from some other country where the black and yellow did not fly. Everything, then, depended upon not losing heart, so as to preserve one's health, so necessary in that tempest.

But the confidence he laboured to instil in his mother's soul was far from dwelling in his own. The newspapers, subject to rigorous censorship, seemed agreed in treating the latest conspiracy to a conspiracy of silence. Yet news finds its way a very long distance even without the aid of the press. Now from the lips of trusted friends encountered along the road, now at the café, around a table, between one remark about fine weather and the next about the price of farm produce, there would slip into his ear those words which could not be spoken openly without peril — and from them, as ever in such cases, the hopeful assurances appeared barely sketched in, while the sinister predictions were laid on with a heavy brush.

One evening, it seemed as though these predictions had found, in the facts themselves, a tragic fulfilment. "Among the Alpine gorges, a patrol had barred the path of the fleeing patriot... the order to surrender disregarded... the noble heart shattered by Austrian lead... the long series of generous impulses cut short for ever. That very night, the corpse would arrive in Belluno for the formalities required by Law."

Giustiniano hastened home to prevent the sad news from reaching his mother through an unwary mouth. He found her already abed. Wake her? To what end? She would have more than enough time to weep. He paced the room up and down until very late; then, overcome by weariness, he let himself fall into an armchair and slept.

Through the half-closed shutters, the first light of dawn barely filtered in, when his mother, impatient for news, brought him his customary cup of coffee. She saw him, and divining the sleepless, anguished night, instead of waking him, went to the undisturbed bed, took from it a coverlet and spread it over his knees and chest. At that very moment, the doorbell rang furiously.

Giustiniano started awake, saw, remembered, understood. He threw his arms about the poor woman's neck and said to her:

"Mamma, my poor mamma, be strong and prepare yourself for the worst."

They departed in a carriage, a gendarme up on the box, an inspector at their side. No fits of hysteria, no sobbing, no silent tears. Giustiniano held tight to the right hand of the stricken woman, who paid him no heed, who remained motionless, her gaze fixed upon some distant point visible to her alone.

She was led into a large, cold room. From the opposite wall there jutted a table whose far end rested upon a trestle; upon the table a sheet was spread; beneath the sheet, with sinister uncertainty, the form of a man in the rigidity of death was outlined. A soldier, rifle at the order, stood stiffly erect beside it. Another, in his underjacket, sleeves rolled up, his left hand plunged into a great boot upon which he kept his eyes intent, was working the brush furiously, buffing it with savage strokes. Four officers, arranged in a circle, were conferring in low voices. A burning brazier tainted, with its lurid glow, the clear light of morning.

At the mother's approach, the officers broke off their conversation and turned their faces toward that stern apparition, which advanced to the foot of the corpse with a firm, almost resolute step.

Someone spoke. He alluded to the painful necessity imposed upon him by duty, and lamented the untimely end of those misguided souls who lay hands upon the sacred majesty of the Law... She seemed not to hear; her whole being was gathered into those eyes that blazed beneath the black veil.

Then one stepped forward and with a sudden gesture drew back the sheet. A livid face appeared. At that, a single sob rang out in the sepulchral silence — one alone! — but it sufficed to resolve the tempest of a heart.

Mamma lifted her veil, gazed steadily at the officers, and with a voice in which vibrated the muted menace of an entire People, said: "No, this poor wretch is not my son! Carletto lives!... he lives, and he will avenge the anguish I have suffered, and he will avenge his Country!"

In the general stupefaction, maternal majesty had its triumph. She moved toward the door with as firm a step as that with which she had entered. The soldier in his underjacket blew warm breath upon the boot and laid on more savage strokes than ever... perhaps wishing at that moment that the barracks and the Empire were concentrated in that muddy toecap. The soldier standing stiffly beside the corpse did not stir a lash... an automaton in uniform, he awaited a command that did not come.

The four officers, suddenly grown pale, had their thoughts drawn perforce to a distant land, beyond the Alps, where other mothers suffered.

Into the cold surroundings of the barracks, into hearts hardened by iron discipline, accustomed to the fierce emotions of brutality and bloodshed, the gentle memories of childhood evidently crept, and rekindled within them their deadened pity. For a moment, the despot's minions became once more men of the People. The stern apparition — unmolested, admired — disappeared!
