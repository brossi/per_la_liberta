# Chapter Six

TO INSERT my own person among the great men who laboured in that titanic defence seems to me an act of irreverence. And I will say too that my spirit remains as though overwhelmed by the surging return of those powerful memories.

On the 30th of April, I too saw the backs of the invaders, and certainly, pressing our advantage, we should have driven the French into the sea, had Giuseppe Mazzini not thought it opportune, for his own diplomatic designs, to curb our impetus.

On the 9th of May, I was fighting with Garibaldi at Palestrina against the Bourbon forces of General Lanza. That easy victory sufficed to check the advance of Re Bomba, who evacuated the Alban Hills and hastily re-crossed the frontier a few days later, when Garibaldi, preferring to conquer rather than to appear disciplined, urged our vanguard to press the engagement at Velletri.

In that memorable day I had a part in an episode which I recall with pride. The lancers of Angelo Masina, overwhelmed by an entire enemy column, were falling back at a headlong gallop along the road to Valmontone. Giuseppe Garibaldi, catching sight of them at once, spurred his white horse and, drawing up a short distance from their front, planted himself squarely across the road to bar their way. Behind him, on a horse black as ebony, towered the giant Aguyar, his face as gleaming and dark. Heroism had forgotten that motion has its laws. The lancers made superhuman efforts to pull up short; but to no avail. In less time than it takes to tell, the road was blocked by a tangled mass of men and beasts — Garibaldi and Aguyar were buried beneath them. The enemy cavalry bore down upon the scene.

I was fighting along the road, to the right, among the vineyards. We were but a handful of beardless youths — yet we waited for no orders, and crying "Forward! Forward! Forward!" we charged resolutely to the rescue. The Bourbon troops, assailed from front and flank, took to precipitate flight, leaving a goodly number of prisoners in our hands.

Garibaldi recognized in our charge the instrument of his own salvation. In the evening, when by order of General Rosselli our offensive was brought to its ill-fated close, he came among us. That man, so terrible when the cannon thundered, gave my cheek a good-natured pinch. I smile now at this sort of codicil to the episode; but then — I felt all that I had supposed heroic within me simply dissolve. That gesture of paternal familiarity reduced me, with an ineffable forcefulness, to the humble proportions of a child.

At the end of May I returned to Rome, and we were already on the point of marching against the Austrians, when General Oudinot, adding treachery upon treachery, prematurely broke the armistice concluded after his defeat of the 30th of April, and seized by surprise the Villa Pamfili. There was no longer any need to march to Ancona to water the soil of Italy with our blood!

That day — the 3rd of June — witnessed such proofs of desperate valour as to astonish Europe and the world, long unaccustomed to descrying, among the hills of Rome, a Roman greatness. But the hydra of reaction exacted, on that day, an excessive holocaust of our finest. Would that it had been the last!

The French aimed to subdue Rome by seizing the Janiculum, which commands the entire city. To reach it, they first needed to take our advanced posts. Having seized the Villa Pamfili, they moved, under cover of darkness, against the Villa Corsini which, by virtue of its elevated and strategic position, could be regarded as the key to the Porta San Pancrazio. And by surprise they had it; but in a furious bayonet charge it was retaken by us.

Unfortunately, owing to the enormous disproportion in numbers and to various topographical reasons upon which I consider it needless to dwell, the assault on that villa proved more than arduous to those advancing from the city, whilst it remained easy for the French who had entrenched themselves beyond the Villa Pamfili. Our magnanimous contempt for our own lives could lead us, and repeatedly did lead us, to become masters of it once more; but to hold it was for us a superhuman and impossible undertaking.

None the less we displayed incredible aggressive tenacity from the earliest hours of morning until late evening. That splendid manifestation of the reawakened, virile Italian temper had, so far as I was concerned, an interval. During one of our first reconquests, as I was crossing, weapon in hand, a passage encumbered with the dead, I caught sight, through the crack of a door, of a reddish gleam — the trousers of a Frenchman — an officer who had persisted in the defence and had been unable to retreat with his men. To escape the first fury of the attackers, he had concealed himself in a *gabinetto di decenza* — a privy. Upon my demand that he surrender, he handed me his sword without a word.

I was charged with escorting him to the Chiesa Nuova, where the captured officers were being temporarily confined.

The Romans had treated the prisoners taken on the 30th of April not as enemies but as guests. Those were days when the rosy hope of fraternity between the two Peoples still trembled in the air, and the prospect of inducing the French to protect Rome from the Austrian hordes had been kept alive by the very assurances of the Triumvirs themselves. Carriage rides to the monuments, refreshments, songs, dancing — and then liberty. The pledge not to turn their arms again against the champions of our independence was neither given nor asked.

But by the 3rd of June the spirit of the Romans had been terribly transformed. The bad faith of Oudinot had roused to the pitch of paroxysm the indignation of a people so utterly foreign to all duplicity, so hostile to treachery. I perceived this the moment I passed through the Porta San Pancrazio, and the officer who walked at my side was made to feel it still more keenly, for it was not always in my power to shield him from violent insults.

Reaching the Ponte di San Sisto, I found my way barred by several militiamen of the National Guard who, refusing to hear reason, and acting on the impulse of the moment, demanded that I surrender the prisoner to them. They wished to kill him on the spot and throw his body into the river.

I cried out to them: "Whoever thirsts for French blood, let him run beyond the walls, and happy is he if his hands prove skilled and strong. But not here! Not while I have breath in my body will I permit the Italian name to be dishonoured by cowardly deeds."

The timely intervention of an officer calmed their tempers and averted every tragedy.

We arrived, without further incident, at the Chiesa Nuova, and the Frenchman, grateful, recounted what had passed to the other prisoners. While, not far off, two peoples made for mutual understanding and affection were hurling themselves upon one another, athirst for vengeance, in that comparatively tranquil corner I heard the very invaders extol our courage, and voice the wish that, once rancours were dispelled, French blood and Italian blood might flow mingled together — but in fraternal solidarity, but for a common, sacred vindication.

A prophetic wish! — though at that moment I considered it far more probable that I should be taken up alive into the glory of the heavens!

In climbing back up the narrow and steep road that leads to the Porta San Pancrazio, I was obliged to walk against the current — the current of the wounded being carried, or dragging themselves, to the church of San Pietro in Montorio, which had been converted into a field hospital. Soldiers and civilians applauded them as they passed. From atop the walls a band poured out, amid the shots, the strains of the Marseillaise, flinging in the face of the aggressors their own hymn to Liberty. Amid so much blood, in such an extremity, Rome found the means to make an irony of it all.

I had left the Villa Corsini in the hands of the Legionaries; I found it reoccupied by the French. Our Bersaglieri, led by Luciano Manara, were attempting its recapture. I watched them run up the flanks of the hill, leaving behind them ground strewn with the dead and the dying. The fire cutting them down was so sustained that, a short distance from the building, they were as though brought to a sudden halt by lead. Yet their breasts, swelling with love of country, had no room for weak sentiments. Exposed as they were, they dropped to their knees and continued to discharge their rifles, presenting an undaunted face to the enemy and to fate. Surely they expected to be reinforced by our men gathered at the Vascello and along the walls; surely they were offering themselves, with serene spirit, to death, in order to give those men time to arrive — to renew the attack at their side.

What moments those were for us, who watched their ranks dwindle, who ached with their wounds and were proud of their pride! The awaited order never came. They fell back when the officers commanded it; when discipline forbade the sacrifice to continue. But on issuing from the enclosure, which ended toward Rome in a sharp angle, they became a sure target for a converging and terrible fire, and then — My country, what a slaughter!

Thus the brave Lombard youth blotted out the stain of past civil discord, and saturating with their blood the soil of the Gracchi, traced upon it — invisible to the eye, but blazing in every heart — the fateful words: *"Italia Italia una, Roma capitale d'Italia!"*

It was in that assault that Enrico Dandolo ceased to breathe and to fight for his — for our — Country. His death, exalting the courage of his brother Emilio, drove the latter to attempt the assault on the villa, leading alone twenty of his men. That slender band, thinned along the way by enemy fire, was all but lost among the rough terrain and the orange trees of the slope. Thirteen of them reached the portico, discharged their weapons, and drew back before the inevitable. When they took shelter at the Vascello they were reduced to six — not one of them without blood upon him.

Their deed is one which cold and calculating minds may well call madness — so be it! But it was the madness of natures almost more than human, who were not fighting to win. The defenders of Rome, it is worth making clear, had set themselves the task of disproving the belief that Italians do not fight; of placing between the Temporal Power and the longed-for third Italy the impossibility of any equivocation; of affirming, by a heroic death, our right to live. And we may now proclaim it aloud: they attained their arduous end.

The turn for heroism and sacrifice fell, after Emilio Dandolo, to Angelo Masina of Bologna. By now the upper floors of the villa, cannonaded from the bastions of the Porta San Pancrazio, are half in ruin and in flames; yet for all that, several hundreds of unseen Frenchmen, invulnerable unless dislodged by cold steel, are none the less formidable to the forty lancers of Masina.

They charge up the hill at a gallop, reaching the summit that nothing could hold them from. Here, to the great astonishment of friends and foes alike, Masina, suddenly assuming the dimensions of a figure conjured by the power of enchantment, launches himself on horseback up the steps, dragging after him, all the way to the terrace, that fistful of bold men.

And perhaps all of them would have left their bones there — had we soldiers, concentrated at the Vascello, exalted by that spectacle, not feared that our commanders, persisting in their error, might render even such heroism fruitless. It was as though an electric spark, struck from the heart of Angelo Masina, had instantly kindled a thousand and a thousand hearts with its own fire. We poured out from behind our defences and ran to the assault spontaneously, without waiting for any command from anyone.

A yet stranger circumstance: the populace, thronged upon the bastions as if in a theatre, divined our intent, and, seized by our fever, surges forward, pours like an impetuous flood between the walls that narrow the tight road, and spreads in great confusion, as though borne forward by its own momentum, up the flanks of the hill drenched in so much blood. They have no weapons, or weapons wholly inadequate; yet they seem convinced that their very presence puts strength into our arms — they would have shown, at the very least, that they knew how to face danger, how to die!

Such a breach of discipline, such an absence of all elementary prudence produced an effect contrary to what might logically have been expected. Not only was every Frenchman swept from the Villa Corsini; but, pressing the advantage, our bayonets also retook the Convent of San Pancrazio, where the invader had, since morning, barricaded his advance posts. And a band of daring men, veering to the right, succeeded in seizing the Villa Valentini, from whose windows the French had, a little earlier, been harassing us even within the Vascello.

Never, as in that hour, had the fortunes of the day appeared so propitious to the glory of Rome! When, from the Villa Pamfili, Oudinot drove forward, one after another, his seasoned and rested Regiments to retake the lost positions, numbers, discipline, and superior arms prevailed once more — but not without enormous sacrifice. Neither our men, exhausted by so many ordeals, nor the nearly unarmed People, took flight.

We fell back to the Casa Giacometti, to the Vascello, to the walls — but with our faces ever turned toward the enemy, and fighting still.

Giuseppe Garibaldi had in his heart the certainty that, with the loss of the Villa Corsini, the fate of the young Republic was sealed. The improvident General Rosselli had not troubled himself, during the armistice, to fortify it, to render it, if not invulnerable, at least more defensible. To crown the disaster, Garibaldi received from Rome only in dribs and drabs, and at long intervals, the fresh contingents necessary to sustain the battle. Seeing defeat inevitable, he refused to accept it without pressing his tenacity to the utmost extreme.

Night was drawing near when he, planting himself before the Reggimento Unione and what remnants of other corps the French lead had spared, personally led one last furious assault. The valour was the same — but the outcome was catastrophe.

I had just fired my last cartridges when I saw pass close by me, supported by his comrades in arms, pale but smiling, Goffredo Mameli. They told me he had been wounded in the knee — Italy was on the verge of losing her young Tyrtaeus!

Night fell. Despondency, recriminations? Not a shadow of them. Within us, after defeat, there persisted the same exaltation that had sustained us, that had multiplied our strength a hundredfold throughout that desperate and unequal struggle. And as for Garibaldi, we felt we loved him as though he had led us not to slaughter but to victory. Our spirit felt itself so little vanquished that, barely emerged from that bath of blood, we meditated and attempted to perform a deed which, in an army held together not by the overwhelming desire to act but by discipline, would have earned us the iron sentence of a court martial.

Masina, the heroic Bolognese who, for one moment, had seemed to friend and foe alike like a knight descended among the combatants by the virtue of enchantment, had proved himself all too mortal. Among us were those who swore they had seen him fall during our retreat, a short distance from the Villa Corsini. We looked one another in the face, and it was almost enough to say: "Let us go."

Shortly after, we slipped out of the Casa Giacometti under cover of darkness. We were twelve. We crept along the Via della Nocetta, hugging the wall that encircles the villa. The cannon had breached it in one small place — we entered through that gap into the enclosure, and still crawling, climbed the slope. Not a suspicious sound. We searched — and found. But the stalwart men who lay hands upon those glorious remains forgot their caution, and thinking to escape the danger more quickly, lifted their burden and began to descend toward the breach at a run. In the night, a volley of musket shots rang out with sinister resonance — three of them fell in a heap; the others scattered — I leapt behind a hedge and threw myself flat upon the ground. At my side, touching my elbow, lay the body of an enemy with his skull shattered.

I was the last to return to the Casa Giacometti — four of ours never returned at all. And as I was recounting to my comrades how I had managed to extricate myself, someone cried out: "Lord God — what a stench!" The sleeve of my uniform was smeared all over with French brains.

The 3rd of June had been a torrid day. I stood in silence for a moment, contemplating the sad transformation wrought, in so brief an hour, upon the sublime organ of human thought, beneath the kisses of the splendid Roman sun.
