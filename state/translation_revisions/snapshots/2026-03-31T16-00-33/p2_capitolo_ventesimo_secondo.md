THE *Durr* sailed on, carrying in her wide belly, divided into three decks, some three hundred condemned men. Gomez and I were confined in the lowest of these. But the Commander, who was an Orleanist and could not have harboured excessive antipathy toward us, no sooner learned who we were than he had us transferred to the upper deck. Indeed he pushed his benevolent partiality so far as to permit me, from time to time, to go up on deck to smoke freely — the Regulations prescribed the trifling matter of fifty strokes of the rope for anyone who did so in the compartments below.

We put in at the island of Santa Cruz (*Car marìe*) to take on water, and remained there two days with the portholes sealed, to deprive us of any possibility of escape; then we proceeded directly to the Île Royale du Salut, where some two hundred convicts were disembarked.

I, Gomez, and another hundred wretches boarded a small steamer, and on the eleventh of December 1858, after a day and a half of additional sailing, we were anchored before the Montagne d'Argent, which rises at the confluence of the Saint-Georges and the Oyapock, at a comparatively short distance from the Brazilian border. In that place there had once flourished a free colony where free hands had cultivated coffee and every tropical fruit that might tempt the human palate. The French Government had purchased it. Its officers occupied the planter's house; numerous reed shelters, supported by poles, were erected to house the condemned; the earth was pierced here and there to bury within it, in slow agony, those who resisted discipline; a few more substantial buildings also arose for the warehouses, for defence and for offence: the vanguard of civilisation had been transformed into a penal colony. But the natural beauties of the place marvellously dissembled the hell that men had accumulated there.

The commandant, a Major of the marine infantry whom the Republic had placed on half-pay and whom the Emperor had reinstated and decorated with honorary insignia, hastened to receive us "in custody" and to call our roll. When he pronounced my name, the officer who had accompanied us from the Île du Salut handed him a letter. It evidently concerned me. He read it and, looking me up and down: "Ah, so you are that — —" (down came an expression fit for a pigsty!) "I am glad to see you here, and I swear to you that here you will leave your skin!" ...

That coarseness was so entirely uncalled for that even the guards pulled a grimace of disgust. But I did not lose my composure, and, as though flattered by a compliment: "Thank you for the good wish — I return it with all my heart!" He clenched his jaws; but he possessed the Gallic ferocity, not the French wit, and he kept his mouth shut.

That little incident persuaded me that at the Montagne d'Argent the air was, for me, unwholesome, and from that moment I resolved to restrain every impulse. The relative freedom accorded to the disciplined — I reflected — may offer me the means of winning a greater one: the freedom of civilised men. But it was fated that my wise resolutions should vanish no later than the following day.

In the hut assigned to me there were some fifty convicts who formed my work gang. I passed the first night sleeping, in my hammock, a sleep tormented by a swarm of insects. At sunrise we set off, our bones aching, toward the forest. I wore a wide straw hat, a pair of wooden clogs, a pair of canvas trousers, a brown blouse, on the back of which was marked, in large characters, my number: the baptism of the penal colony!

Crossing a swamp, I had the misfortune to lose one of my clogs, and the stumps of the reeds, cut level with the water to ease the ford, lacerated the sole of my foot. I was obliged to make my way over three miles limping and bloodying every footprint, until I arrived, last of my gang, in the depths of the forest — a splendid forest that, for a little while, almost made me forget that I was a convict and forget the pain.

A pile of logs. The day's task was to carry them on one's shoulder to the penitentiary, making five consecutive journeys. I singled out a log that looked light; but it was *bois de fer* — ironwood — and when I had it at knee height, it fell back noisily onto the pile. Then, rather than make another attempt, I sat down, resolved to rest just long enough to recover my strength. The convict-guard stood watching me, and when he was certain of my intention: "Get up, you filth! If you don't get moving, I'll kick you all the way back. A curse on you and the whole Italian breed!"

Had he been content to goad me with a simple threat, I should probably have taken the matter with the greatest philosophy; but that contempt for my nationality stirred in my breast the anger accumulated against all the tyrants who had trampled upon it. I swung my *sabre d'abattis* and gave a leap of which, considering my condition, I should not have thought myself capable. "Take care! Italians do not suffer insults. The first one — let it pass! At the second, that head of yours will no longer see the sun from the top of your shoulders!"

My companions had set off one by one and were already far away. We were alone, perfectly alone, in a deserted place! He swallowed the pill, turning pale. Nevertheless, by way of a show of bravado, he asked my name. "Be content with the number!" and I turned my back on him.

We were making a bad start! But chance would have it that my guard, though he could spell out letters more or less correctly, did not know how to write. To make his report, he had to resort to the clerk of the superintendent engineer, who, guided by the number, verified my name and identity in the registers. What feelings this clerk harboured toward me I do not know; but I learned that he persuaded my guard not to be cruel. And into his exhortations he skillfully inserted the element of fear: "That man," he said, "fears neither Law, nor punishment, nor God, nor the devil. If he swears vengeance, sooner or later you are done for."

On his return to the forest, the guard called me aside. "We have in the penitentiary certain customs!... But I know how to make my distinctions." And to prove it, he begged my pardon for his ill words and granted me leave never to work, then or thereafter. I was to conceal myself each day in the dense forest... I was to reappear at the last departure of my gang. And he extended his right hand, as if to seal a compact.

Thus, for a good month, I passed the hours assigned to labour stretched out amid a prodigious vegetation, smoking a cigarette, listening to the complex murmur of the forest. In those hours my eyes beheld a fantastic interweaving of embroideries seen no more: enormous leaves cut from velvet, trunks tall and straight as minarets; flowers that seemed proud of their own colours pressed strange insects into service for their not-quite-hidden loves; birds with metallic sheen, darting from the shadows all in a rustle, passed above my head on outstretched wings, swift as lightning. There were caverns of a deep green, full of mystery; there were majestic vaults dripping with dew and perfume, through which the Sun gazed down calmly, regally, as if watching over every blade of grass to see that it had its due. The Sun, which all peoples in all ages have worshipped, consciously or unconsciously, had with its centuries-long energy raised up for itself that temple, worthy of a God. Oh, how beautiful is the Earth when it is not defaced by the cupidity of man! ... A temple worthy of Elisa too! ... Her image, intensely evoked, dominated me as though she were present!

It was in that forest and in those hours that I meditated my second attempt at escape. The plan to which I finally fixed myself was suggested by the primitive craft of the Indians who roam the southern regions of Guiana: to fell a great tree, hollow it out and fashion a serviceable pirogue. With it I would have ascended the Oyapock by night for a stretch of fifty miles, then crossed the Saint-Georges, to land on Brazilian soil. Escaped from the pursuit of the French authorities, I should have had to confront, in all likelihood, the ferocity of the savages, and, indubitably, the almost insurmountable obstacles of unexplored regions and of the climate. But the prize was worth the risk!

None of this could even be begun without the help of several accomplices. I sounded the mind of Antonio Gomez. But the ardent political conspirator, content with his position as assistant canteen-keeper and fearing, in all probability, something worse, feigned not to understand me. I would have to resort to less noble material. By what criteria to choose it? How to acquire over it the ascendancy necessary to bind it to my purpose?

In this instance too, my good star, more than my own wisdom, came to my aid.

The penitentiary received its supplies of fresh meat from Brazil. At intervals, more or less regular, a sailing vessel arrived with a cargo of cattle. The beach, which shelved gradually into the ocean, afforded it no depth sufficient to moor close to the shore, so that the anchor was cast at a distance of some three miles out. The guard would then select one convict from each *decuria* — a squad of ten — and form a special team which made its laborious way, with the water above the waist, out toward the vessel. The wild cattle, maddened, were thrown into the sea, and the men drove them, with great shouts and great blows, all the way into a stockade within the penitentiary.

Once that work had begun, it was carried on without interruption until the unloading was complete, skipping a meal or two when necessary. At the Montagne d'Argent, the men ate by *decurie*. The squad-leader received the provisions — already depleted by the sordid speculation of the officers — and set them out, on a sort of table, in the ten mess-tins. Each convict ran to his own and devoured his portion sitting on some step, or on a log, or on the bare earth. On the days of unloading, it was the duty of the squad-leader to guard the ration of the absent convict and deliver it to him on his return.

Having been chosen to lend a hand at that frantic labour, I made my final return one evening at eight o'clock, when my *decuria* was already snoring in its hammocks. Hunger, sharpened by the incessant exertion, the long wading, and the keen sea-breeze, had grown fierce. I shook the squad-leader and raised something of a commotion, and he showed me, instead of the mess-tin, his fist: "*Tais-toi, Italien de m...*" (the word of Cambronne).

I chewed on nothing and held my tongue. The following evening I took my place in the ranks with the *decuria* and waited at the table. When the squad-leader arrived, bearing the pot brimming with soup, I sprang forward and delivered it a mighty kick. The squad-leader stood stupefied, arms outstretched, hands empty. "It takes nine Frenchmen," I said, "to make one Italian go hungry; but one Italian alone is enough to make nine Frenchmen go hungry!"

Now it was the others who raised the commotion. The guard, a certain Cosson, came running, and having inquired into the matter, demanded of me curtly: "Why, instead of taking your revenge, did you not submit your report?"

— Because Italians do not turn informer!

Cosson, stealing Salomon's trade, sentenced to fifty strokes of the rope both the squad-leader and Count Carlo di Rudio. But my resolution and, above all, my proud reply had raised me suddenly in the estimation of those wretches. They gathered round the guard and pleaded so earnestly that he consented to swallow his sentence. From that day, the convicts condescended, in their generosity, to treat me as a brother, as one worthy of their brotherhood; from that day I found it a comparatively easy undertaking to study the various temperaments, and to select eight individuals sufficiently enamoured of liberty to face cheerfully, in the attempt to recover it, whatever danger might arise.

We left our hut by night, creeping like beasts from their lair, so as to deceive any eyes that might still be open. We proceeded by different routes to an agreed place on the edge of the forest; then, united, we pushed on into its labyrinths. From the very first night we had marked our path with signs practically invisible to other mortals, thus averting the danger of losing our way.

A tree of gigantic proportions was felled at the base with comparatively little effort; but in falling, it struck against the dense growth and lodged there, supported. It was necessary to clamber up and hollow it out while clinging to it — a complication that consumed precious time. We finally decided to resort to fire. The light could not be seen from the penitentiary, and the smoke was rendered invisible by the night; but amid so much resinous vegetation, so many dead shrubs, so many dried canes and so much debris accumulated over centuries, the operation demanded the greatest vigilance, or one would have fed a conflagration so swift and so vast that even the tropical downpours could scarcely have subdued it.

That nocturnal enterprise of ours constituted, for me, one of those incidents whose impression only death can efface. We were not in that land as pioneers of civilisation, and someone might even observe, not without irony, that we were not the stuff of which missionaries are made. But the surroundings — oh, the grandeur of those surroundings, made ineffable by the silent night, how deeply it penetrated, together with its own essence, into ours, and how greatly it transformed us! All around us, there was nothing that had not been born and grown in the most boundless freedom, and freedom became almost tangible in every atom of air that we breathed. It seemed to me that my muscles, as they toiled, exhaled from their every fibre something luminous, as though they were rendering back to things the uncontaminated life that things were lending us. There were moments in which I could have sworn I was as vigorous and inward as the virgin forest. And we became so homogeneous a part of our surroundings that, after the first nights, numberless troops of monkeys, attracted by the glow of the resin, came to observe and almost to encourage, lending it their own gaiety, our work.

After two months or a little more, we succeeded in dragging to a bend in the river, and concealing among the lianas, a canoe seven feet wide and twenty-five feet long. It floated admirably. "And now," I exclaimed, when we parted full of enthusiasm, "to the oars and the sail!"

Then, unexpectedly, an event to which, at its first manifestation, no one paid serious attention, threw the entire penal colony into disorder, and with it every hope of mine.

In broad daylight, beneath a clear sky that is itself a lyric, with a sun capable of hard-boiling an ostrich egg in its direct rays, a sentry was seized by violent trembling. Relieved as quickly as possible, he was seen to make his way with slow steps, shaking, to the antediluvian hut that bore the pompous name of "Cabinet of decency." He emerged from it ashen and distraught. He declared that, struggle as he might, he had not been able to pass a single drop of water. The convicts laughed loudly — the thing seemed to them so comical. By evening, the poor wretch was bringing up clotted blood, as black as charcoal. Two days later he was a corpse.

But before he had even drawn his last breath, five, seven, ten convicts, while showing a high temperature on the thermometer, were shivering like leaves. The penitentiary had been invaded by yellow fever.

The catastrophe was rapid beyond all description. In those days, it was believed that the seat of the disease lay in the intestines; the remedies, extremely few and wholly inadequate to the scale of the mortality, were, to make matters worse, administered on erroneous principles. The Commandant gave orders for the warehouses and the canteen to be thrown open and left at the full disposal of the healthy. From that canteen, wide open and black within, there seemed to me to emanate the sensation of fatality. If yellow fever was not crouching inside it, it had some mysterious ally there. The convicts were drawn to it inexorably by vice and by fear. They turned themselves into beasts and passed from drunkenness to delirium; from delirium to nothingness! — with a death all the swifter, the more robust they were.

The arrangements for the victims were of the simplest: to cast them, without ceremony, into quicklime.

Every evening, the head guard went to make his report at the Commandant's house.

— How many today?

— Twenty, thirty, forty! ...

— And the convict Rudio?

— Healthy as a fish!

One night the Commandant lost, almost at the same moment, his wife and two sons, and he ceased to concern himself with my health. Not much later, it was the head guard who ceased to appear at the usual report. He had yielded too readily to the temptations of the canteen, and when he was found stretched out on the beaten earth, the first suspicion was simply that he was drunk. But his gums were covered in bloody foam. He died without having recovered either speech or a glimmer of consciousness, and was one of the many whom I was obliged to seize by the feet: "One, two, three ..." Before casting him down I should have wished to say a *Requiescat* — he had spared me fifty strokes of the rope. But down there in that pit it would have seemed ...

I lent my hands to burying, one by one in that fashion, my eight accomplices in the escape attempt, and others, and others still. I counted past a hundred; then I lost count and counted no more. Some wretch who already seemed to have crossed the threshold beyond which one suffers no more, barely thrown into that horrible common grave, was seen to revive and writhe with agony!

When the fever, finding no more flesh to gnaw, disappeared, the French Deputies raised in Paris the usual humanitarian clamour. But the Minister replied to them, with an air of triumph, that he had already issued the appropriate orders for the transfer of the entire Colony to the Île du Salut. And indeed one evening we saw outlined against the twilight the black strip of smoke from a vessel proceeding at full steam toward our shore. It was the *Abeille*, bringing us the orders of His Excellency. The following day the entire Colony was on board. Of more than six hundred — convicts, military personnel, civilian employees, and servants — we were reduced to sixty-three! The greater part of the survivors had previously suffered yellow fever in other times and on other shores, which had apparently rendered them, if not absolutely invulnerable, at least resistant; many, shadows of their former selves, showed signs of having been touched by it then and there, if not quite carried off — among these, Antonio Gomez. Oh, how greatly transformed was that good-natured priest's face of his! Three alone had not contracted it, then or before, the infected blood. Among the three, the convict Rudio!

From the deck of the *Abeille*, around whose hull the ocean was already foaming and breaking, I cast a last glance at the Montagne d'Argent and thought, with profound regret, of my canoe floating solitary and motionless beneath the dense lianas. The landscape shrank swiftly. The hell that men had accumulated there was no more; what remained was one of its most baleful consequences, the vast charnel-house from which we had miraculously escaped. Nature, indifferent, spread over so much horror its enchanting hues, and in the scintillating air its mysterious courtships vibrated on!
