CHAPTER TWENTY-FOURTH

THE plan, this time, was to seize the steamers that ply the Lario and to attempt the capture of Como. At the first sign of insurrection in that city, several armed columns were to descend from the Grisons to raise the Valtellina and give force and breadth to the movement.

The proposal to take part was made to me directly by Pompei of Pavia, of the Giovine Italia, Stradella section — yet he spoke to me in the name of Felice Orsini. I note this because Orsini, in his *Memorie*, names Giuseppe Mazzini as the effective and openly acknowledged chief of this attempt, using as pretext the meagre number of persons who answered the call, to demonstrate "the utter want of influence" of that honest man among the ranks of the party that was more properly his own.

In all truthfulness, both Fumagalli and I learned of Mazzini's direct participation and of his presence in the Grisons only some months later, after the enterprise had already failed.

Great is my veneration for the memory of Felice Orsini; yet the motive behind his virulent denunciation of this and other Mazzinian ventures has never been entirely clear to me. Had he not, by taking part in them and at times accepting a directing rôle, implicitly approved of them? And in approving, had he not assumed full responsibility? Every word of blame that Orsini addressed to the Maestro seems to me blame that falls back upon his own head. Never did I have cause to suspect, as when I read his autobiography, that the flashes of that ardent spirit were not always worthy of the hero. And I shall say, in passing, that those very phrases of recognition — recalled and held up to ridicule by the fierce Romagnol — are a variant of those we actually pronounced, in accordance with the instructions we had received, whether he allowed himself to be carried away by the heat of polemic, or whether his memory played him false.

The man posted on the road did not say to us — who wore a flower in our hats — "Halloa, good fellows, where are you bound?", but asked us whether we were Italians. And our answer was not, as Orsini asserts, "We are going to see Signor Francesco, or Signor Giuseppe"; but simply: "Conti." And when the other pressed our hands, I added, still in accordance with the instructions received: "Dov'è Tito Celsi?" (1)

In what condition Fumagalli and I arrived at San Maurizio, Orsini has noted: "weary, famished, in rags, dressed like beggars." He might have added that, for all that, we were no less eager to set our hands to work for our Country.

Be that as it may, the man who questioned us led us to an inn and left us alone. Several days of fruitless waiting followed. Though I had somewhat improved my appearance, I could not assume the air of a prosperous burgher come to those parts to enjoy the fruits of honest industry. This circumstance, which might attract attention, was a source of some unease to me. Nor did the abandonment in which we were left tend to reassure me.

Enterprises such as ours are secretly attempted even by Governments with an interest in fomenting some complication. And it is enough, for those of us who live in California, to recall General Frémont. But Frémont could afford to keep his mind comparatively at ease, for he knew that behind him stood a great force, vigilant and ready to act. And besides, the happy outcome of the attempt matters little when little is at stake. The important thing was to furnish the United States with any pretext for intervention — a trifle suffices the wolf that drinks from the same stream as the lamb.

Matters stood very differently for the Italian patriots, who were compelled to build all their plans upon the fortunate outcome of a first, uncertain surprise. The painful experience of recent years inclined me to suspect, in our abandonment, the cruel tricks of the unforeseeable — a storm that had escaped all calculation, a ford temporarily impassable, a letter gone astray, a misinterpreted order, a conspirator's brief hesitation, an imprudent word, the untimely curiosity of an idler, the barking of a dog — a thousand minute causes, sprung as if from nothing — and nothing behind one to fall back upon!

And the consequences of failure? The reproach of the timid, the arrogant contempt of serious men, the venom of cowards. Any rascal may stone you, any holy man of the Law spit in your face, any miser sell your head, any ruffian put a bullet in your back out of pure lust for slaughter. And the hatred of the modern inquisitors, which multiplies the hardships of your flight, which contrives to have every door slammed in your face, to snatch the bread from your mouth and the pillow from beneath your head; which would seize you in its pincers and wring your neck on the pretext of restoring the age of easy digestions; which, when it cannot rid itself of your body, murders your soul with calumny.

With what intensity must the forerunners of the third Italy have kept their eyes fixed upon their ideal, to prefer, over a life of ease, the wretched *odissea* — the wanderer's odyssey — of the rebel!

One evening Fumagalli appeared before me waving a letter in the air. It was from Tito Celsi, announcing that his arrival was imminent — and he fell upon us just as we were reading his few lines. In our conversation, Felice Orsini named no one; he confined himself, so far as his plans were concerned, to assuring us that he could dispose of two thousand rifles. He handed us a note of introduction for an innkeeper at Maloggia and, assuring us that we should soon receive his instructions, took his leave.

The innkeeper at Maloggia was not privy to the secret affairs, but had some inkling of them and favoured them. He informed us that he was expecting a crate of "ironmongery" from Saint-Étienne — it arrived, and with it the instructions from Tito Celsi. These were the arms that were to have served the redemption of the Fatherland; Orsini indicated to us a locality, not far from the Muretto glacier, where we were to place them in readiness. The innkeeper quietly provided the means of transport.

The chosen location was favourable. A cliff falling sheer on three sides; an abandoned shepherd's hut, hidden among bushes and chestnut trees. From up there we could observe every twist and turn of the path that led to it. No danger of surprise.

It was the middle of August 1854, and we made the most of the long days to press our work with diligence. We rose from our beds of grass at first light and, having contemplated the dawn, set ourselves to the task. Once a rifle was assembled, it was levelled; its action was tested with a silent manoeuver; then it was ranged along the wall overgrown with foliage. We leaned it there with a delicacy, as though it were some precious trinket.

In those days I committed an imprudence which, though it had no bearing whatever on our enterprise, had a very considerable bearing on my little barque and caused it to change its course. I had just finished setting in order my fiftieth rifle, and as I examined it and ran my hands over its various parts, it seemed to me so exceptionally fine that I should have thought myself a fool not to claim it as my own. I used the screwdriver as a bodkin and engraved my initials on the stock. Fumagalli, who was watching me, said: "What are you doing?" And I, paraphrasing an expression of old Domenico's: "A tool like this, and I'll ruin the livers of half Bohemia."

The calm of the mountains reclaimed us. But all too soon came agitation, and collapse.

I had gone out to keep watch, and had barely raised my hand to shade my eyes when, in the distance, through the thickets, I caught sight of a cloud of dust. The breeze, which blew towards the mountain, drove it along in the wake of the wayfarers who raised it; yet it could not hide from me the glinting of arms. They were soldiers, and in good number; nor were they surely climbing the slope in search of hares.

I had great difficulty in persuading Fumagalli that we must seek safety up the heights. He wished to stand his ground and "sell his skin dear." We crawled through the undergrowth and gained the mountain.

They arrived, surrounded the hut and demanded our surrender at rifle-point. Half an hour later they were departing in small detachments, carrying away our rifles and our hopes.

We came down to Maloggia after nightfall and the innkeeper, on seeing us, merely shrugged his shoulders. He counselled us to remain quietly withdrawn in his inn until he had had time to take the wind's measure.

Two days passed. On the third he appeared before us with a downcast air and announced that Tito Celsi had been arrested. He added: "As far as I am concerned, no fear! You are free to make use of my inn as you please — as long as there is polenta and milk for me, there will be for you too. But if I were in your shoes, I should prefer to pitch my tent a mile and a half further down the valley, precisely where the coach horses are changed. News reaches you sooner there, and if matters grow cloudy you will have the means of slipping away close to hand. Besides, a brother of mine lives thereabouts, to whom I shall recommend you — it will be as though you were still among family."

We followed his counsel and on the very evening of our arrival, having with difficulty swallowed a forkful of food, we heard the jingling of the harness bells and the crack of the postilion's whip. Going out with the other onlookers, I noticed a heavily cloaked individual who had just descended from the coach and was scrutinising me from head to foot. I returned his scrutiny in kind, and he stepped forward — the signal! Drawing me aside, he handed me a roll and a note. The roll contained two hundred francs in gold; the note a single word: "Save yourselves!"

With the help of the innkeeper's brother from Maloggia, we hired two saddle horses. We left word to knock at our door at three in the morning; but I was roused abruptly much earlier. At the foot of the bed, drawn up in a semicircle, stood several men in uniform — the barrels of their rifles converging upon my chest.

We were conveyed to Chur and brought before a Federal Commissioner who had come expressly from Bern. The interrogation was brief. We declared ourselves deserters. We had no knowledge of political conspiracies or clandestine arms. The Commissioner allowed us to have our say, then made a sign. They brought in a rifle, and he, smiling, drew my attention to the stock. I had no need to strain my eyes. In characters that, by some strange illusion, seemed suddenly to loom larger, my three initials — *C. C. R.* — stood out plainly. Up there, near the Muretto, without knowing it, I had engraved my own condemnation and another's.

And we were locked away *in domo petri*. My first thought was for my father and for Luisa, who were in the prisons of Mantova; nor did I ever have a clearer vision of them, or feel for them a deeper compassion.

I will say at once that at Chur, Austria's delightful methods were unknown. Imprisonment, but with respect for misfortune; observance of regulations, but humane regulations; some privation, but not Galeazzo's Lenten fare.

The greatest torment for me was the forced inaction — though, to one degree or another, I knew well enough where patience lived. Not so Fumagalli who, as the gaoler told me, paced ceaselessly in his cell like a caged beast.

He was confined on the first floor, I on the third. It was impossible for us to see one another; but taking advantage of the brief moments when the sentry was at a distance, we would exchange a greeting. If the sentry reproved us; if the warder struck the door, I would step back and sink onto the bench with a calm that surprised even me; Fumagalli, on the contrary, would resume his pacing furiously. On his feet he wore heavy mountain boots, reinforced with hobnails no larger and no more lacerating than those displayed by the Christ nailed to the cross in our country churches. The floor, which was of brick, ended by bearing grooves deep enough to resemble cart-ruts, and the warder, pointing them out to him, remarked, half in earnest and half in jest: "You cost the Republic too dear!"

Our fate depended upon the decision that the gentlemen of Bern would take. But before any word could arrive from that capital, whatever it might be, the first signs of winter were already making themselves felt.

Along the prison wall there was a small garden, green and pleasant. A large pear tree spread its broad crown almost level with my little window. When it rustled, the cell filled with a murmur that my mind transformed into indefinite and melancholy melodies. I often asked myself: "Who can tell me whether I shall still be here when the man who tends that garden comes to gather the pears?"

One November morning, pressing my face to the bars, I saw among the branches a man with a thick beard, plucking the fruit and filling a basket that hung by its hook from a bough. We looked at one another — the man swung his arm and tossed me one, two, three, half a dozen of them. There was a mist; the leaves had turned yellow. And I said to myself: "Who can say whether I shall still be here when the tree puts forth its blossoms once more?"

At that very moment the warder threw open the door and, jingling his bunch of keys and smiling, invited me to follow him.

I found Angelo Fumagalli in the corridor, stamping his iron-shod feet like an impatient colt.

We were taken to Bern, and for the second time I heard from the Federal Authorities the sermon upon the sacred duties that rest upon those who receive generous hospitality. As a recidivist, I was banished in perpetuity from the borders of the Republic. And they warned me not to set foot within them again, on pain of severe punishment — extending to me the courtesy of reading aloud the articles of the Code that specified those punishments.

Upon being asked to name the part of Europe or America where I wished to be conveyed, I replied, as on another occasion: "To England."

And so ended, for me, for Fumagalli, and, I believe, for a couple of other participants, that attempt which Felice Orsini calls "more comedy than tragedy." That same Orsini was likewise arrested, as I have already noted; but he slipped from the hands of the gendarmes and, after having sought momentary refuge at Chur and at Zurich and attempting thereafter to foment some new venture at Milan, departed for Vienna, where he entered into negotiations — first to join, for purposes I cannot fathom, the Russian army; then to spread revolutionary ideas within the Austrian one. Both attempts having failed, he passed into Hungary and was arrested at Hermanstadt in Transylvania, whence he was conveyed by painful stages to Vienna, and from Vienna to the castle of Mantova, which swallowed him up, as I have already had occasion to recall.

Giuseppe Mazzini, Maurizio Quadrio, Nicola Ferrari and a few others, who had assembled in the Grisons to prepare the armed bands, had succeeded, after various vicissitudes, in making their escape.

Of the arms gathered here and there at the various passes, I believe only those entrusted to Fumagalli and myself fell into the hands of the Federal Authorities, who, already on the alert since the time of the Clementi trial, had redoubled their vigilance in response to warnings received from the Police of the Lombardo-Veneto kingdom.

And since there were no casualties to lament, we might even accept, without rancour, the definition of "comedy" that issued from the wrathful mind of Felice Orsini.

But to one who reflects that in those very days, through the strangest of alliances, the map of Europe was being pondered and redrawn, that affirmation of the persistent restlessness of the Italians, that renewed protest against the tyranny which forbade us to constitute ourselves a Nation, will seem neither ridiculous, nor inopportune, nor vain!
