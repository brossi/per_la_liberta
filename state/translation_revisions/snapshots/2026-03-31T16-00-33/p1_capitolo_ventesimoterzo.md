## Chapter Twenty-Third

I pushed on as far as Zurich, where I found a nucleus of compatriots who had escaped, through exile, from the reactionary storm that had followed the upheavals of February. Among them was Radonitz, who received me with great courtesy. He made not the slightest allusion to the incident at Locarno. I also went to pay a visit to Caronti. His words could not have been more flattering; yet at the first sight of me, his face had assumed an expression of wonder not entirely free from a tinge of unease. Perhaps, I thought, it is merely my imagination. Giuseppe Mazzini had so thoroughly predisposed me against him!

That dealer in trinkets, who had gone bankrupt at Como, was living in Zurich in great luxury, at the expense of a wealthy lady who manufactured chocolate. He had, however, contrived to set up a modest felt-hat factory, and several Italians found therein the means to earn their keep. I became one of their number, and it seemed to me a ray of good fortune. But scenting the air, it was not long before I discovered something discordant. There were handshakes that resolved themselves into a mere contact of skin; evasive replies; monosyllables, innocuous in themselves, but which acquired a shifty flavour from the grimace of the face that accompanied them. In sum, a coldness, a coldness! — even at the midday break, when lips exchanged somewhat more spontaneous phrases, the mysterious threads that connect souls were wanting.

What was I being reproached for? Perhaps for having come to hover about their bread and to take a small portion of it for myself?

Things suddenly changed, and so radically, as to make me suspect an understanding. But what understanding, and why? The sharp edges vanished; they addressed me with the familiar *tu*; they tapped me confidentially on the shoulder, they offered me tobacco — it seemed they had finally resolved to regard me, in every respect, as one of their own. So much so that one Saturday they proposed a nocturnal pleasure-excursion on the lake. Four strokes of the oar, four tankards, or eight even, a patriotic chorus under the starry sky. It would be as if living again beyond the Alps, in the fair country where our sweethearts languished.

So as not to appear discourteous, I accepted. It so chanced that, in order to remedy some forgotten matter I no longer recall, I returned to the factory that very same evening. To my great surprise I found it lit up. Drawing nearer, I heard distinctly the agitated voice of the exile Grioli saying: "By God, I'll have no part of it! I should never again have the courage to think of my poor old mother!" I entered, and it seemed as though an iron hand clamped a gag upon every mouth. *(1)*

From that day forward no one spoke to me again of the lake, or of the tankards, or of the starlit sky. The reason I never knew, nor did I trouble to investigate it. Nevertheless I grew certain that the sudden cordiality of my companions had been feigned; that I found myself, like an intruder, alone and despised among them. I ended by feeling thoroughly disgusted and resolved to depart for Paris.

Without taking my leave? Not entirely. Caronti, honest or dishonest as he might be, had procured me work; I felt it my duty to pay him a farewell visit. This procured me yet another surprise. Upon my arrival in Zurich, he had received me with cheerful and courteous words, but with a face that betrayed his disillusionment; now, no sooner had he learned the purpose of my visit than he poured forth a deluge of phrases expressing regret; but his inward sensation would not stay suppressed, and stamped itself upon his face with signs that I deemed unmistakable. My departure caused him an intense pleasure.

I arrived in Paris in December 1853 with two francs in my pocket and a letter of recommendation for a certain Zappa, a Turinese hatter, which had been given me by my factory foreman — the only person in Zurich who had shown me any sympathy. The two francs did not carry me far; but the letter served me well. Zappa, a man of liberal opinions and a generous heart, offered me hospitality at once. One had to make do with sleeping on a mattress in the kitchen; but when one is penniless and in that season, the offer was far from contemptible. All the more so as I found means to repay the debt by giving Italian lessons to the lady of the house.

Signora Zappa was French. She held ideas that were more than liberal — they were revolutionary. They were enough to establish between us the most cordial of relations. She was not young, she was not beautiful; but she retained a grace whose charm it was not easy to resist. When she spoke of the 2nd of December, in which she had taken an active part, and of the fierce repressions from which, by good fortune, she managed to escape, she became eloquent and, in the heat of her recollections, forgot even the corsets she manufactured — to which, truth be told, she owed her popularity rather more than to her heroism.

So as not to live at the expense of those good people, I bestirred myself and at last found employment near the Pont de la Bastille. It brought me two francs a day. What employment? For fourteen consecutive hours I turned, by main force of arms, the wheel of a merry-go-round.

Having my hands on the wheel did not prevent me from having my eyes on politics. And at that period, even for those who were not in France, to occupy oneself with politics was to occupy oneself with Luigi Napoleone.

The coup d'état, regarded with execration by upright consciences, blessed by the Pope, was received by the other Sovereigns as a great feat of skill, combined with enviable perspicacity. The blood shed was not of the kind held to be blue, and the stupor that seized liberal France was, in their eyes, the restoration of peace. And since Luigi Napoleone delivered the reactionary Governments from the spectre of the Republic — too often confused, and artfully confused, with that of the Terror — it was entirely natural that the reactionary Governments should hasten to turn their benevolent gaze upon Luigi Napoleone, to recognise his advent, and to expect great things of him.

After the coup d'état, the Dictatorship! Napoleon was too shrewd to abuse it without setting against his odious persecutions a series of measures calculated to beguile the multitude. France was overflowing with hands ready to man the barricades. Unable to put them in chains, he provided, with shrewd haste, to win them over to his side. Prompted by an impulse emanating from Paris, every centre in France, great and small, was seized by the fever of public loans and public improvements. And the wave of well-being that spread through the working classes received a sensible increase from the sanitary laws designed to improve their housing; from the reshaping of taxation, which, lifted from articles of primary necessity, was made to bear upon those of luxury. To this was added the liberal extension of agricultural loans.

Throughout all France, the cannon-shots had thus been succeeded by the hammer-blows, and the moral effect that naturally flowed from the improved economic conditions was reinforced by splendid promises that kept hope fresh and green, that led men to suppose this unexpected period of extraordinary prosperity would endure. Luigi Napoleone, if one took him at his word, abhorred war. He proposed to make many conquests, but they were to be the conquests of Peace — lands to be reclaimed, vast tracts to be opened to cultivation, harbours to be deepened, canals to be dug, rivers to be made navigable. In these useful and glorious undertakings, the sword would yield its primacy to the tools of labour, and every peasant, every workman, animated by good purpose, would become his faithful warrior.

Ideas worthy of praise! But in order to lend them credence, it was necessary to erase from one's mind every bitter memory; to lose sight of the fact that this man of fate spent half his day in disguising, with flattering phrases, the wicked actions he reserved for the other half.

Unfortunately, from the multitudes, constrained almost perpetually to devote every energy to satisfying, with difficulty, the needs of the stomach, it is not reasonable to expect a cult of the ideal that is far-sighted and steadfast. The popular soul does not advance to its sacred claims save under the impulse of exceptional circumstances, and then by lurches, necessarily as tumultuous as they are fleeting. And it is much when it does not attempt to make up for lost time by degrading its magnanimous wrath with ferocious manifestations. Then the ineluctable need drives the herd back to graze the grass — the soul defers to better times its strivings toward the good and falls back to sleep.

The French People did not understand, or would not understand, that every new prince, risen by tasting crime and inclined to perpetuate it, does not venture upon it again until, by astutely practising virtue, he has given the ill seed time to root itself firmly. The laborious digestion dulled thought; the rosy hues of the present prevented men from discerning the menacing phantoms that would have massed on the horizon. And when the servile Senate restored, in the person of Luigi Napoleone and his male heirs, the imperial dignity, the multitude hastened to ratify. Nearly eight million *Sì* — a slender minority of two hundred and fifty-three thousand *No*!

On the 1st of December 1852, a mere year after having stained his hands and his soul with the blood of his own fellow-citizens, Luigi Napoleone placed upon his head, at Saint Cloud, the crown of France; and in assuming it, he engraved upon History the name of Napoleon III.

With the Empire re-established and the Constitution retouched, the various branches of Government assumed, in appearance, their regular functions; in reality, France continued to be governed by Dictatorship — a veiled one, if one wished, but no less absolute.

The Prince Consort, who had come from London in the spring of 1854 to cement the friendship then taking shape between England and the new Empire, had occasion to express his profound astonishment at the matter. Napoleon himself candidly confided to him that he never permitted his Ministers to assemble to discuss public affairs. Each individual Minister dealt directly with him on any matter of importance, and what he had resolved with one was rarely communicated to another.

This wholly personal form of government, deemed pernicious even by the old sovereigns, led us liberals to frame the most sombre prognostications regarding France's future. The fixed idea upon which the policy of Napoleon III was to pivot became manifest at once — to emulate the great intellect that had drawn his own house from nothing; that had stamped deep traces upon so great and so illustrious a part of the world. He set about this arduous enterprise armed with unbridled ambition, with an immoderate lust for intrigue, an utter want of faith in pledged word and in treaties, a capacity for corruption well able to undermine far sturdier constitutions; the absence of every regenerative principle; a mediocre intelligence, barely that; his presumption, and his great name.

How could the immense disproportion between the means and the end have failed to alarm us? The thought and the momentum of a great State, concentrated in that dark and ambiguous nature, were in our eyes the power consecrated to the doing of evil, to driving, with precipitate steps, a noble Nation toward dishonour and ruin. After half a century, I am surely permitted to invoke History in order to establish, with the indisputable wisdom of hindsight, how well-founded our predictions were.

Napoleon III had one flash of practical intelligence in allying himself with England. But in placing the crown upon his head, he had not blown from his soul his natural duplicity. He could not show himself a sincere ally, and his crude manœuvres so deeply irritated the subtle British diplomacy that he found it hostile to him in the moments of supreme need. England would indeed have declared open war upon him, had the Germanic victories not forestalled her.

Napoleon III could not transform the war with Russia into a glorious uprising of peoples yearning to repress the tyranny of the barbarians. It was reduced to a long, bloody siege; and when Sebastopol fell, it was because General Pélissier manfully refused, and not without stinging phrases, to submit to the orders that came from Paris.

The war in Italy? It is insisted upon that he alone championed it, with the forgetfulness of the eagerness with which the French People subscribed to the loan raised to defray its costs. There are two pitched battles in that war: Magenta and Solferino. The first was won almost in despite of the Emperor, through the disobedience of General Mac Mahon. In the second, the Napoleonic fibre had something of a posthumous flash, and it was the only victory of the Second Empire owed to the personal merits of Napoleon III. But immediately afterwards, his vulgarity reasserts itself. He tore up, without warning, the articles of the alliance; he insulted the King who had fought heroically at his side. And the substantial aim of the war — that of substituting his own influence in the Peninsula for that of Austria — was destined, thanks to the virile patriotism of the Italian party of action, to fail miserably. Napoleon III, even as victor, emerged from the bloody struggle irremediably defeated.

The expedition to Mexico, born of financial speculations that could not be confessed, is an adventure worthy of a filibuster. It began with a betrayal analogous to that which cleared the way to Rome for his Oudinot, and ended, as the war in Italy had ended, with the shameful breaking of solemnly undertaken pledges. The fact that Maximilian, and not the Emperor, fell victim to Mexican lead serves to demonstrate how little human, or divine, Justice governs the affairs of the world.

In the secret negotiations that preceded and followed the defeat of Denmark and the resounding victories of Prussia over Austria, Napoleon — playing a double game, simultaneously offering his alliance to both contending parties — aimed at obtaining a gratuity from the victor; at reclaiming for France the line of the Rhine. But he was deceived and mocked and covered with ridicule by the sagacity of Bismarck.

What is one to say of the Franco-Prussian War, willed by him, undertaken by him without the most elementary knowledge either of the enemy's forces or of his own?

I do not measure the greatness of men by their success, for I know of defeats infinitely more glorious than any triumph. But for that to be so, the ruins must appear to us as irradiated and hallowed by the greatness of thought, by the nobility of sacrifice, by the sublimity of purpose. The life of the Third Napoleon seems to me, from this point of view, as arid as a desert.

To be sure! To be sure! No one could outrun his time and discern in the uncertain future the positive catastrophe. Yet already in the spring of 1854, tangible reasons abounded to justify the distrust — nay, the profound hatred — of liberals. Luigi Napoleone, trampling upon the principles he had sworn to defend on pain of death, had, through his crimes, enfeebled France and robbed her of every possibility of those democratic initiatives that stand among the greatest of her glories. He had shown himself most cowardly and ferocious precisely against the factions that had contributed most to drawing him from the near-obscurity in which he had so long lived. Gratitude weighs upon vulgar souls. He would yet have to render a stricter account of all this than can be balanced on a page of History — History, too often venal and mendacious, like the conscience of the men who write it.

To Italian liberals, the power of that man was in a special degree tormenting — the man who had repaid their benefits and their expectations by championing the interests of the age-old enemy of the Fatherland; who had sacrificed the flower of Italian youth to vilify resurgent Rome; to thrust her back into the arms of the papacy; to prostitute her by brute force to that which was most monstrous in what had survived the barbarism of bygone centuries. In the spring of 1854 it was already considered more than probable, indeed imminent, that Napoleon III would form an alliance with the House of Savoy. And since it was well known that the Empire did not desire Italian unity, and that Vittorio Emanuele II and his Ministers did not consider it possible, it was inferred that from such a *connubio* there would issue, at most, a territorial enlargement of Piedmont. Some French prince substituted for the petty tyrants who infested Italy; but the Pope in Rome, sustained by the imperial arms and bound, by force of instinct and circumstance, more to the interests of the Empire than to the cause of Italian independence. History would have renewed its ancient phases; we should have had upon our necks, alongside the old masters, new ones.

This train of ideas drew strength from the marriage of Napoleon III to Eugenia Montijo, Countess of Teba, a Spanish woman of incomparable beauty, restless, intelligent, crafty, tenacious, Catholic not by calculation but by deep-rooted conviction. Thanks to her influence, upon the throne of France there sat, virtually, alongside Napoleon III, the priest.

The cause of Liberty could not have resumed its inexorable course, either in France or in Italy, so long as Napoleon III — the grape-shotter of the People, the usurper — stood between it and the future. Therefore it was necessary to think of suppressing him — to hasten the return of things to their natural order; to spare, at slight cost, a long succession of tumults and hatreds and rivers of blood.

Such ideas take hold like a vice upon natures disposed to action, and it was from that very time that I cast, in my soul, the seeds of an action that was to burst forth some years later. Even then I understood that an event of such political importance would not have produced the fruits I anticipated of it, unless thought were given to preparing a vast organised force ready to profit by it. I meditated at length, took my notes and wrote — and to whom could I write if not to the most steadfast conscience I knew; to the man who had known how to surprise the heartbeat of the Fatherland and to proclaim her living and to foretell her greatness, when all believed her inexorably laid in the tomb?

He was then in London. I communicated to him in full detail a plan of mine (of which I shall have occasion to speak further on) asking him, in order to carry it out, for his aid; prepared to go to the very end, or to draw back, according to his counsel. Felice Orsini, who at that period was still on the most intimate terms with Mazzini, saw my letter, and it is to that letter that I owe my future relations with the patriot of Meldola.

But to that first communication of mine, nor to two others that followed, did Giuseppe Mazzini reply. I bethought myself of Pianciani, and in a fourth letter addressed to him, I poured out all my bitterness at that silence — undoubtedly deliberate — which smacked to me of undeserved contempt.

The reply was not long in coming, and it was brief and terrible. "In London I was accused of having been the deliberate cause of the arrest of Fortunato Calvi and of the failure of the attempts at the Calore. The accusation came from Zurich."

So Austria, powerless to throttle me, had avenged herself for my escape! — to isolate me, to annihilate me through calumny. And it was as though a veil fell suddenly from my eyes. Now I understood the conduct of my companions in Zurich; nor did it require great penetration to interpret the secret motive behind Caronti's strange behaviour. The Austrian poison had been spread by his lips.

I replied to Pianciani without losing a moment and, in my resentment, did not even weigh the phrases that alluded to Mazzini — so quick, I observed, to lend his ear to the slanders of a man, upon whose honesty he himself, only a few months before, had expressed to me the gravest suspicions. I concluded by asking for the means to go and confront my accuser.

Pianciani sent me one hundred and fifty francs; but accompanied by what amounted almost to an injunction to keep quiet and to await from time my vindication. Having pocketed the money, I embraced the Zappa household with great warmth, and turned my back on Paris. It was a splendid morning in May 1854 when, suddenly, from the heights, I saw spread at my feet the blue lake, and gleaming in the first rays of the sun, the damp rooftops of old Zurich.

I had myself announced to Caronti, without revealing my name, as an exile who wished to communicate an important message to him. Long was the wait in the antechamber; but brief was our interview. He was still in his dressing-gown and received me standing, both thumbs hooked into his waistband. The moment he recognised me, he turned pale and muttered through his teeth: "What has brought you here?"

— I shall tell you in a moment! — I closed the door and stepped so close to him that our very breath almost mingled, and I satisfied his curiosity to the full, reinforcing my words with a demand for satisfaction. "And mark this well! I require it, and I shall have it before leaving this room!"

He peered, with a furtive glance, out through the open window, and attempted to gain time by protesting his innocence. Indeed, he had always held me in unconditional esteem, with an affection almost fraternal. Speak ill of anyone? He would sooner his tongue dropped from his mouth in shreds!

— Very well! But words are carried off by the wind, and what is written remains. Attest in black and white your unconditional esteem; furnish me with a formal retraction. That, and nothing else, will satisfy me!

As I spoke, his lips were trembling. I believe he understood, from my countenance, that this was no time for equivocation. He went to the writing-desk, wiped the honest sweat from his brow, and wrote. Then, without turning around, he stretched out his arm and handed me the sheet.

I read it, and contented myself with saying: Very well!

Thereupon, as though we were old friends competing in civilities, he extended himself in offering me his services — in whatever part of the world, at whatever time. I had only to express the wish. If moreover I were bound for Italy and thought it opportune to make a brief stop at Lugano, he would furnish me with a letter of introduction for Count Grillanzoni, who had settled there.

He was evidently trying to reconnoitre the ground in order to prepare some new mischief for me. I cut short his words. What I intended to do I did not know; in any case it concerned me alone. I turned the key and left.

In the corridor I felt a hand placed on my shoulder. And a voice murmured in my ear: "How goes it, Moretto?"

It was Angelo Fumagalli, pale and smiling. He placed a finger across his lips and added: "Come, come to the stables. We shall talk at our ease!" He took me by the right hand and, with gentle force, dragged me along as though I were a child.

"I know everything, I heard everything! I saw you cross the garden and recognised you at once. I said to myself: there is something afoot here. What is afoot? I had dark suspicions and — why hide it from you? — I committed a shabby act, I put my ear to the door. But better so! There will be no more misunderstandings. Do you know that that scoundrel even accused you of having stolen a watch from his coachman? That one, I never believed for a moment. As for the rest — you must forgive us. What would you have? We fell into the trap like a pack of idiots. And you had a narrow escape, I tell you! Do you remember that plan for a nocturnal trip on the lake? They had plotted to stun you with a blow of a cudgel on the head and to throw you, bound hand and foot, as fodder for the fish! — Then Caronti would have denounced you all, and at a single stroke, delivered Austria of an entire wasps' nest. — The Devil take him! Fortunately Grioli had sense enough for all of them, and he saved you. God bless him! We believed we were avenging the Fatherland!"

He embraced me and was sobbing. Then, precisely as the other had done, but with a different aim: "And now, what do you intend to do?"

— My dear friend, I have tried everything in my power to do my duty, and I have found myself suspected by those very men who should have defended me. One road alone remains to me: to persist, to answer the accusations with silence and with action.

I left Zurich with the intention of making for Piedmont, and made my stop at Lugano and paid my visit to Count Grillanzoni. He received me coldly — I understood, and presented him with the Caronti retraction. Thereupon he showed me in his turn a letter received a few days before. In it, that same worthy exhorted him to do everything in his power to prevent my returning to Zurich. My irascible temperament, he wrote, was the cause of continual vexations and of scandals which, for the good name of our emigration and in the interest of the cause "so dear to our hearts," it was imperative to avoid.

I renounced Piedmont and returned at once to Zurich. In the meantime, Angelo Fumagalli had taken his leave of Caronti, with indignant words. From him I learned that Felice Orsini had been making inquiries to obtain my address.

A few days later I was engaged in the attempt at Maloggia.
