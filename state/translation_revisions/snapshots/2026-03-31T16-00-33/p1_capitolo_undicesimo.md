CHAPTER ELEVEN

IN APRIL of 1852 I was once again in Genoa, working for a certain Ferrari, a merchant tailor, when I was informed that Don Bastiano Barozzi, parish priest of San Pietro in Campo, was living as a refugee in Turin.

Barozzi, during the siege of Venice, had served as chaplain to a Legion composed almost exclusively of students from Padua. He was one of those priests whose cassock did not stifle his love of the Fatherland, and who, when his side was defeated, preferred to follow the impulses of his own conscience rather than submit passively. Italy counts many such men among her sons, and the Church of Rome might have drawn great prestige from them, had she not preferred — in order to please the powerful — to hear their confessions, and even lend a hand at their execution.

Deeply versed in ancient history, Don Bastiano judged men through the prism of the ancient world, and would have wished to raise our entire race to the level of Atilius Regulus, the Scipios, the Gracchi. During my school holidays, as we walked along the Ardo, he would, without making a show of it, fill my mind with heroic episodes. I believe I owe him, in no small part, my love of Liberty.

Those priests of murky conscience, who dared not wage open war upon him for his patriotic principles, dragged in the Decretals and turned their attack upon his moustache and goatee, which smacked to them of modernism. He stubbornly refused to have them shaved off, and lost the right to say Mass as a consequence. In his straitened circumstances, he was assisted by my father, and that alone had been enough for Don Bastiano to preserve a gratitude towards him that bordered upon veneration.

It was to this man I resolved to turn, in the hope of re-entering, with his help, the vortex of action. Thinking that two words spoken face to face are worth more than four long letters, I set out for Turin — on foot, so as not to lose the habit.

My first care upon arriving was to make my way to the Caffè Nazionale, the favourite gathering place of political refugees. But before I reached it, I caught sight of a gentleman standing before a theatre bill, his face so close to the printed sheet that he seemed to be reading it with the tip of his nose. Don Bastiano Barozzi's extreme short-sightedness was well known to me, and that single detail was enough to reveal him.

His astonishment at finding me again was great; his joy was boundless. He could not contain it, and there and then, without the slightest regard for the passers-by, he planted two resounding kisses upon my cheeks: "There — that one is from me; and that one is from your father!" He then asked what good wind had brought me to Turin. And I replied:

"Don Bastiano, you know that the di Rudios are no idlers. What is being planned, what is being prepared? I have come so as not to grow rusty, and I am counting on you."

He lowered his eyes and did not answer. But after a brief silence, as though concluding some train of thought: "Good Calvi is here. Four steps and we're at his house. Come!"

Fortunato Calvi — the glorious organiser of the Cacciatori delle Alpi, my Colonel, the intimate friend of my family — received me with his arms folded, without moving a step, with an expression that was almost one of disdain. The flood of feeling that was about to burst forth remained painfully compressed within my breast. I did not even dare provoke an explanation. But the explanation came of its own accord.

Calvi had been informed by a person "worthy of trust" that I, having left Venice on his orders, had instead of proceeding to Chioggia gone over, arms and baggage, to the Austrians. He even cited the name of one who had sworn to have seen me, behind the trenches, firing upon my own countrymen.

Slander! — what man has not been, to some degree, its victim? Little by little, one's breast grows hardened to its blows, so that in time they no longer succeed in raising any tempest within me. I have known bold men who, by their very bearing, almost provoke it deliberately, for the bitter pleasure of showing themselves superior to it. But that was the first time I had come face to face with it, and its bite struck me as cruelly sharp.

Yet it proved easy enough to demonstrate the absurdity of the charge.

Fortunato Calvi, who had no less desire to find me innocent than I had to prove it to him, extended his right hand with a smile, which I grasped with feverish warmth. Meanwhile Don Bastiano, the silent witness of that scene — a man of exceptional sensibility — turned on his heel to conceal a tear.

The consequences of that meeting were, as far as I was concerned, of no small moment. Giuseppe Mazzini, Luigi Kossuth, and Ledru-Rollin had openly founded in London a Revolutionary European Committee, to which innumerable affiliated branches soon attached themselves. Every people that, having overcome the baleful apathy which spreads like a fungus through the spirit, understood that regeneration springs from action and sacrifice, organised its own branches. Italy was a hive of them, and by force of circumstances the Committee of Turin held sway over all the others of ours. To it fell above all the task of inflaming the spirit of rebellion that was spreading through the Peninsula, and of coordinating its movements. Its head in 1852 was Count Grillanzoni; but the command of eventual military operations fell to Fortunato Calvi.

I received from Calvi and from Count Grillanzoni the charge of travelling, as a special envoy, through the principal cities of Lombardy and Venetia — first to distribute subversive manifestos and subscription certificates for the Mazzinian national loan, and then, on a subsequent journey, to inspect the various garrisons and report precise data on their strength, their nationality, and their mood.

The system adopted was a simple one. It was arranged that I should be furnished with a passport in the name of Carlo Moretti, a merchant in silks. I had only to write: In such and such a square I sold two, three, four bales of white silk, or green, or yellow. The number of bales corresponded to that of the Regiments; the colour indicated their uniform and branch of service. A carefully worded allusion to the particular quality of the goods, or to the nature of the client, was sufficient to convey whatever other circumstances I judged it necessary to report.

I remember the admonition given me by Calvi on the very day of my departure. I was concealing in the bowl of my pipe the list of the various Sub-Committees, and I was smiling. Calvi, suspecting that the smile arose from levity, said sternly: "Do not deceive yourself, young man! A slight imprudence, a simple chance incident, and you would be hanged!" I never fell into the hangman's hands, but I passed more than once within close reach of them.

My first stop was at Stradella, where I shook the hand of Nino Bixio, who had survived the wound received at Villa Corsini; and of that Winkler who had been commander, in Venice, of a Hungarian legion, and who was, a short time afterwards, to close his heroic existence fighting on the banks of the Volturno. The company of men of such temper was a source of keen excitement to me. But once I had crossed the blue Ticino with the help of a smuggler and advanced but a little way into Austrian territory, it seemed as though an iron hand had seized me by the throat. The oppressor had, after 1849, sharpened his sluggish wits to transform those lands into a pit of hell.

As a general rule, Austria regarded the provinces subdued by her own arms, and those reconquered by the allied arms of Russia, as a den of brigands. To vent upon them a barbaric hatred, to indulge the most atrocious acts of vengeance — this seemed, in courtly circles, the height of political wisdom. Persuaded that he was fulfilling a mission entrusted to him by God, Francesco Giuseppe did not even consider himself bound by his own word or by the terms of surrender that had been agreed upon. General Klapka, the heroic defender of Komorn, had obtained excellent conditions of capitulation for his garrison. Every one of them was violated. A code of honour exists even among thieves; but under the pretext of *ragion di Stato* — reasons of State — none whatsoever existed for Francesco Giuseppe.

Of the thirty-four Hungarian generals — men of select intelligence and heroic fibre, who had repeatedly compelled the Imperial soldiers to show their backs — three were condemned to life imprisonment, twelve were duly shot or hanged. And the slaughter would have been greater still, had not the others saved themselves by flight and exile. For several months a swarm of executioners, clad, as if in greater mockery, in the colour of hope, busied themselves in Hungary heaping up the corpses of patriots. All this seems to have been erased by now from the memory of those men who cry their hurrahs in the streets for that old and contemptible wretch. And yet there is no end to the imprecations heaped upon the generous souls who, having taken into their own hearts the anguish of the victims, risk their lives to administer to despots a small dose of their own medicine.

In Italy, to tell the truth, Francesco Giuseppe had his claws blunted by Vittorio Emanuele II, who, ascending the throne amid the glacial distrust of his subjects, knew how to bend to the terrible necessities pressing upon him without compromising the future. He refused to sign the peace treaty with Austria unless she undertook the obligation of granting full amnesty to the hundred thousand Italians who, from Lombardy and Venetia, had taken refuge in Piedmont — whether to make war upon her, or to escape her cruelties.

Vittorio Emanuele II, though defeated, held a by no means negligible position of strength, and the Emperor kept his word. Yet his "pardon" was tacitly conditional upon complete submission to the Austrian yoke. Were pretexts lacking? Sequestrations, arbitrary impositions, chains and the gallows were not dispensed sparingly by the paternal solicitude of Francesco Giuseppe, and the rod was applied to the backs even of those — men or women — who, without openly rebelling, gave expression to sentiments of any virile character.

Poor young Italian women, your souls open to all that is beautiful and gentle; and you mothers, aristocratic or of the people, who passed with commanded slowness between two rows of ruffians bringing their rods down with all their force upon your naked flesh — lend me your voice, that it may suffice for a worthy *apoteosi* — a fitting glorification — of the apostolic beast!

And it is of Francesco Giuseppe that I speak deliberately, and not of Austria; for when the sum of power is concentrated in one man, upon his head, as a debt of justice, must be concentrated equally the sum of hatred and of infamy.

Mingled among the Italians, I found in my wanderings a number of Hungarian officers who had been degraded for having loved their country and defended it. Harassed with ferocious persistence, they had nonetheless the courage to rebel against the hateful commands issued to the detriment of a people made brothers by common misfortune. And there were among them those who, despairing of better times, put an end by suicide to a life that had become unbearable.

Fortunately — almost as if to disappoint the prophets of doom — Nature has so arranged her affairs that death itself is the great sower of life, and tyranny itself the great fertiliser of every liberty.

Notwithstanding the defeats and the blood drained from it and the persecutions, life was pulsing through our Fatherland — underground, but intensely. Italians who were organising themselves, who were conspiring, or who gave open sign of their feverish impatience to do so — these I found in abundance in every centre. The logical conclusion to be drawn from the terrible situation was that one must begin again from the beginning, and do better, and conquer at whatever cost.
