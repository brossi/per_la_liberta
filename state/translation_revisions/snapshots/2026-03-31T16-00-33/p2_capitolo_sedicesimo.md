AND so we come to our chief. I shall call up old impressions; I shall express judgements which fifty years of meditation have not availed to alter.

We find ourselves before a character capable, in the highest degree, of generous impulses. Yet, through the mysterious reactions of his soul, the frequent exaltations of feeling were followed, always or almost always, by profound depressions. A nature excessively nervous and sensitive, he oscillated fatally from one extreme to the other, laying bare strange deficiencies which I regarded, even in those days, as maladies — of what precise nature I could not then define, nor can I now.

The conspiracy against Napoleon III reveals in him a mind adroit in conceiving a design in broad outline. But the success of such undertakings, when analysed, proves to consist in the sum of small details patiently worked with a chisel. Felice Orsini, in the practical act, showed so little regard for the most elementary precautions that I do not hesitate to affirm that the essential gifts of the conspirator were conspicuous in him by their complete absence.

Giuseppe Mazzini, censured by Orsini to the point of denigration — to the point of allowing him no genius save that of the spoken word — would certainly never have committed the folly of leaving England, of plunging into an enterprise of such magnitude, while forgetting behind him, intact, recoverable, all but in plain sight, the correspondence that revealed every preliminary of the attempt.

This imprudence will appear all the more egregious when it is known that he himself, in writing to me, frequently urged me, urged us, to destroy his letters and to leave not the slightest trace of them.

It weighs upon me to say it, but I must say it in the name of truth: the trouble with Felice Orsini's mind lay in an overflowing imagination that prevented him from discerning the exact contours of things, despite his pretension to being a practical man.

Having shouldered a difficult enterprise, he would construct in imagination the events which ought, through a long chain of consequence, to lead to triumph; and dwelling upon that triumph, he would exalt himself so thoroughly as to regard it as inevitable, savouring it before it had come to pass.

With an irony that seemed cruel, it was observed how Felice Orsini, on the eve of one of his desperate attempts in the Lunigiana, had carried his faith in success to the point of procuring for himself a general's uniform. One recoils, before the majesty of martyrdom, from being compelled to recall how, setting out for Paris to attempt the life of an Emperor, to initiate a revolution, he — who stood in such need of obscurity and silence — purchased a saddle-horse and rode frequently through the streets of the metropolis, as though anticipating therein the emotions of the epic moments to come.

This explains so many other things. The excessive development of one mental tendency is accomplished at the expense of other faculties, which remain all the more deficient as that tendency manifests itself more turgid and overbearing. An immoderate faith in success, which rests in substance upon an exaggerated estimate of one's own powers, is answered, too often, by a corresponding failure to perceive the true strength and character of others.

What reason led Felice Orsini to break so wrathfully with the Maestro? Let historians seek it in this or that incident, and from their point of view they are right to do so. But the primary cause is entirely psychological. Historical incidents are, in reality, nothing other than the expression assumed, in a given environment, by the deplorable flaw of a soul otherwise noble and great.

In a different field and in different measure, yet from the selfsame source, Felice Orsini was destined to treat us also — his companions in danger and in misfortune — in like fashion. The bitter words uttered during the brief absence of the Gomez, in the house in the rue Monthabor, on the evening of the 14th of January; the no less stinging phrase launched, shortly afterwards, at Pieri's address as we were making our way to the Opéra, demonstrate that a dark reaction was already fermenting in his soul even before the catastrophe.

An ill-starred incident provoked by Gomez served to lend some substance to the phantoms that troubled him. Before resolving upon a full confession, Gomez had attempted to persuade the examining magistrate that Orsini, exasperated by his hesitation, had at the moment of the imperial carriage's approach brusquely snatched the bomb from his hands, and that Orsini himself had hurled it against the Emperor. When confronted, he immediately retracted, and the matter had no legal consequence whatsoever. But it certainly did not help to restore Orsini to a just perception of reality.

He was undoubtedly in such a state when he was ensnared and beguiled by the arts of the police. The insinuation contrived against the integrity of his companions made a wide breach in a soul already so open to suspicion. And whereas before that moment, following his generous impulses, he had shown a wish to assume every responsibility for what had occurred, he now repented of this and dictated a detailed account in which, while assigning to each his share in acts already known to the Police, he let fall towards us words full of bitterness.

He then repented of his repentance and attempted, with a vigorous counter-declaration, to take every responsibility back upon himself; but at bottom the doubt remained, and the bitterness found, from time to time, the means to burst forth impetuously — always overcome thereafter, and with equal impetuosity, by feelings more consonant with his greatness.

A pendulum that bewildered us most grievously!

Even today, I could not interpret with any certainty certain of his denials, certain of his disavowals. These might have been inspired as readily by a transient determination to sever himself from us and stand alone, as by an ardent desire to open for us a path to safety, to offer us the means of softening the rigour of the fate about to strike us.

Here, to avoid misunderstanding, I must insist upon one point: the insinuations of the Police were undoubtedly intended to irritate Orsini, in order to wring from him a full confession. Orsini had his faults, but he was not a coward. He did indeed acknowledge everything that the Police showed they knew with certainty; but he took good care not to disclose particulars that were unknown to them, unless those particulars concerned himself alone.

I will say this further: no human soul could be polished and perfect on every facet of its prism. To constitute greatness, one great side sometimes suffices. Felice Orsini had more than one.

It is easy, for example, to display courage in the light of day, upon a field of battle. So easy, that I have known men transformed into heroes by a single spent bullet, who were incapable of standing without trembling before a snarling cur. The disciplined multitude carries along even the most timid and feeble individual — to keep his legs from giving way beneath him, he has, or imagines he has, the eyes of his fellow citizens fixed upon him in their thousands. But Felice Orsini, alone in his room, spreading mercury fulminate before the fireplace and drying it over the flame; with a watch in one hand and a thermometer in the other, watching for the spark that might reduce him to fragments, without leaving of his work one atom to earn him the applause of men — nothing, not even the memory of generous intentions — Felice Orsini, I say, gave proof of a courage whose temper eclipses any noisy and belligerent heroism.

There is furthermore a trait which has escaped, so far as I am aware, all who have written of him hitherto, and which reveals a courage yet more refined and sublime. The seizure of his correspondence had, among other things, revealed him as the centre from which radiated the entire clandestine operation; the point towards which, in turn, converged the concealed hatreds, the subterranean forces working to undermine the Empire. To suppress Napoleon for the mere pleasure of suppressing him would have been the work of madmen. The French political police had well understood that the blow, by its diligent preparation, must be intended to impart an impulse to broad forces, ready to reap its fruit. To lay hold of secrets which it sensed to be of vital importance was a matter to which it attached careers, wealth, power, glory — everything.

Felice Orsini found himself, therefore, in a position not of submission but of power. A single thread of the vast web, secretly surrendered, would have led him to safety, to opulence perhaps. He would have looked again upon the white crests of the mountains, the green fragrant plains, the incomparable sky of Italy; he would have conspired and fought for her still; he might have cherished the hope of witnessing her third rebirth. Nor was his heart deaf to more modest affections, no less intense — he was a father; on the banks of the Thames, a woman hoped and trembled for him. But between that tragic present hour and that rejoicing lay the blood of the generous-hearted, lay the tears of orphans. He chose instead to set forth, pale and silent, towards the scaffold. An austere greatness, worthy of an ancient Roman!

The defence of Felice Orsini arose spontaneously from the circumstances, and Giulio Favre was not the manner of intellect to ruin, through pusillanimous scruple, the splendid material those circumstances offered him. He laid upon it the powerful hand of the artist who knows the mastery of effects; of the man of heart who makes his own the trembling of a glorious and unhappy People.

His masterstroke was the letter addressed by Felice Orsini to Napoleon III. Without revealing anything, without compromising anyone, it permitted him to flash before the Judges, the jurors, and the civilised world the loftiness of our purpose. It flatters the lion who is exercising his claws in our flesh; but amid the blandishments, it insinuates, unperceived, a muffled threat. A single phrase honestly expressed suffices to pierce a wicked heart. In the silent hours, in the hours of remorse, it will reproach the broken oaths; it will exude the undying wrath of a race righteously vengeful, which was his own and which he has betrayed. The impetus of noble passions raised Giulio Favre above petty legal manoeuvres and mean things. Moving among great matters, he was able to accomplish a great defence. He did not snatch a man from the executioner, but he did something better — he restored his character. He transmitted to posterity a spirit, already so virile in itself, transfigured, as it were, by strokes of captivating beauty.
