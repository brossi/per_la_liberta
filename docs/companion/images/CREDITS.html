Image Credits & Provenance

This is the full provenance record for the images that illustrate the Reader's Companion. It is the picture analogue of the companion's sourcing discipline: every embedded image is either a verified public-domain work or a freely-licensed one (CC-BY / CC-BY-SA) used with attribution, and every entry below records the observed rights statement — read off the source page at the time of access, not assumed.

How these were chosen

  • Public domain by age. A faithful photographic reproduction of a public-domain two-dimensional work (a pre-1900 engraving, painting, or photograph) is itself public domain regardless of which library digitized it. Where an image existed in several places, the copy with the cleanest, least ambiguous rights statement was preferred; the holding institution is credited in every case as a courtesy and for traceability. Anything whose public-domain status could not be cleanly established was rejected — including several modern CC-licensed photographs of monuments and surviving sites.
  • Freely-licensed where it strengthens the framing. A few images are Creative Commons (CC-BY or CC-BY-SA) rather than public domain — used where they engage the narrative more strongly than any public-domain alternative: the surviving original Orsini bomb, the clean 1815 map, the full-coast map of the three Guianas, and the Calvi portrait bust. Each is embedded with its author and exact license named in the caption and below, as those licenses require.
  • Period-preferred. Contemporary 19th-century engravings, lithographs, paintings, and photographs were preferred over modern images. A few are later commemorative or reference works used as the best available public-domain representation where no strictly contemporary image exists — the Belfiore-martyrs engraving (Matania, 1887), the Restoration-Italy map (Shepherd's atlas, 1911), and the Giovine Italia journal cover (a 1918 facsimile of the 1832 original); each is dated honestly in its caption and below. A few period photographs likewise postdate the events they accompany (e.g. the Belluno panorama of 1918, the La Roquette photograph of c. 1876) but remain the best public-domain views of the place.
  • Honest captions. Captions distinguish an actual likeness or site from a contemporary depiction of an event (a representation, not a photograph of the moment). Event scenes — the Five Days, the siege of Rome, the attentat, the coup, Aspromonte — are paintings and drawings, and are labeled as such in-line.
  • Linked, not hosted. Where no cleanly public-domain image of a subject exists but a good, correctly-identified one is held at a stable, linkable source (a museum collection page, a rights-reserved archive), it is not downloaded or embedded; instead the relevant entry links the reader to it, and it is recorded in the Linked, not hosted section below. This is how the companion points to a genuine likeness of Calvi without hosting a non-free image.
  • Stable citations. The "Source page" column gives the canonical Wikimedia Commons File: page (or other repository item page), which persists. The volatile direct-download links used to fetch the files are deliberately not recorded here.

All images accessed 2026-06-08 (first round) and 2026-06-09 (round-two and CC-BY additions). Oversized originals were downscaled to a 1600-px long edge; no other alteration was made.

Deliberately left unillustrated

In keeping with the companion's anti-fabrication discipline, several entries carry no image because no authentic, cleanly public-domain (or linkable) one could be found — and a misrepresented or wrongly-licensed substitute would be worse than none:

  • The execution of Orsini and Pieri (13 March 1858) — no clean-PD contemporary depiction of the scene was located.
  • Cesare Crespi (the author) — no verified photograph of the right Cesare Crespi (the San Francisco journalist–publisher) exists in the open repositories; "Cesare Crespi" hits on Commons are all other men.
  • Signora Elisa, Don Bastiano Barozzi, Baron di Torocfalda, and the named ships (Radetzky, La Durance, Abeille, Amazzone, John Romilly, Virginia) — no authentic likeness or vessel image exists.

(Calvi, formerly unillustrated, now carries a portrait bust (Padua) in the personae, appears in the Belfiore-martyrs engraving in events §5, and links to his period portrait relief at Mantua above.)
