# A Reader's Companion to *Per la libertà!*

***Per la libertà! (Dalle mie conversazioni col conte Carlo di Rudio, complice di Felice Orsini)*** — Cesare Crespi, 1913. Canessa Printing Co., 708 Montgomery St., San Francisco.

This companion gathers reader's aids for the English translation of Crespi's 1913 memoir of Count Carlo di Rudio. It is built around a **spine** — a layered summary of the book — surrounded by **framing companions** that supply the people, events, chronology, and ideas the book assumes its reader already knows.

## How to read this companion

There is no required order. For *what happens*, read the [Summary](summary.md) and stop at whatever depth you need. For *who these people were* and *what was at stake*, dip into the [Personae](personae.md) and [Event Context](events.md). The [Timeline](timeline.md) ties everything to dates and source pages; the [Glossary](glossary.md) is for quick lookups.

## A note on sourcing — how to read the voices

*Per la libertà!* is not a neutral history. It is a **partisan memoir**: di Rudio was a lifelong Mazzinian republican, fiercely anticlerical and hostile to the House of Savoy, and Crespi reproduces his voice deliberately. The book's heroes (Mazzini, Calvi, Orsini) and villains (Napoleon III, Pius IX, Cavour's "practical" monarchy) are *the book's*, and these companions present them as such — as the memoir sees them, citable to the chapters where they appear.

Where these companions add **external historical context** — dates, outcomes, identities, or points where the historical record differs from di Rudio's account — that material is set off in a clearly marked callout:

> **Historical note** — External, independently verifiable context. Everything inside such a callout is checked against outside sources before it is stated; where the record is uncertain, the note says so.

The [Event Context](events.md) companion adds a third, related voice, similarly set off in a callout:

> **Scholarship** — What the standard academic literature on an episode contributes: interpretation, scale, and the points where historians confirm, complicate, or contradict the memoir's partisan telling. Each such callout names the scholarly work it draws on, with full details gathered in that file's *Sources & further reading*.

This keeps the memoir's slanted, first-person account visibly distinct from the historical record and from modern scholarship. It is also where this companion can say what the book itself cannot — most importantly, that di Rudio's story did not end where the book does, in 1864.

> **Historical note** — The book closes with di Rudio sailing for New York in 1864 and never returning to Italy. In fact, "Charles DeRudio" went on to a long career as a U.S. Army officer: commissioned into the 7th Cavalry in 1869, he survived the Battle of the Little Bighorn (25–26 June 1876) under Reno and Benteen, and died at Pasadena, California, on 1 November 1910 — three years before Crespi published this book. The memoir is, in effect, posthumous testimony, exactly as Crespi foresaw: in the preface he writes, "I sensed that this book would see the light when di Rudio would be no more." *(Source: Wikipedia, "Charles DeRudio"; the preface quotation is from the book itself.)*

## A note on provenance

The book-attributed content in these companions was assembled from a chapter-by-chapter reading pass over the English translation, and has since been **independently fact-checked against the source chapters**. External facts in **Historical note** callouts are confirmed against the cited source before being stated; a callout cites only sources actually consulted. Where the record is uncertain, or the book disagrees with itself or with the historical record, the text says so.

The standard applied is **fidelity of substance, not of letter**: a summary may carry a name or date the book establishes in a neighbouring chapter, and may supply a verified historical date for the reader's benefit — but it must not invent what is absent from both the book and the historical record, nor state as fact anything the chapter contradicts. The fact-check was filtered through that test; corrections were made for genuine fabrications, inversions, and misattributions, while faithful compression and legitimate cross-chapter synthesis were left intact.

## A note on the images

The companion is illustrated with **public-domain images** — period portraits, engravings, lithographs, and early photographs, preferred over modern ones to match the book's own era. Each is tied to a specific entry, and the same sourcing discipline applies to pictures as to prose: every embedded image is either a verified public-domain work or a freely-licensed one (CC-BY / CC-BY-SA, used with attribution where a modern map, photograph, or object engages the narrative more strongly), and its captions are honest about what it is — an actual likeness or site versus a *contemporary depiction* of an event (a representation, not a photograph of the moment). Where no public-domain image exists but a genuine one is held at a museum or archive, the entry **links** the reader to it rather than hosting a non-free file (as with the only surviving individual likeness of Calvi). And where no authentic image exists at all — the author Crespi, di Rudio's wife Elisa, the named ships — the entry is left unillustrated rather than given a misrepresented stand-in. Full provenance for every image, including holding institution, source page, and the observed rights statement, is recorded in [images/CREDITS.md](images/CREDITS.md).
