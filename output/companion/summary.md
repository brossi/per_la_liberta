***Per la libertà! (Dalle mie conversazioni col conte Carlo di Rudio, complice di Felice Orsini)*** — Cesare Crespi, 1913. Canessa Printing Co., 708 Montgomery St., San Francisco.

## Logline

An aged Risorgimento conspirator, exiled to America, recounts his life — fighting for [the 1849 Roman Republic](events.md#3-the-roman-republic-and-its-siege-1849), the 1858 Orsini plot to assassinate Napoleon III, a death sentence commuted on the scaffold, escape from a [Guiana penal colony](events.md#7-the-french-penal-colony-of-guiana-18581859), and a homeland never seen again — as testimony gathered by a journalist in his California exile and published in 1913.

## Synopsis

The journalist Cesare Crespi sought out Count Carlo di Rudio, living obscurely near Los Angeles in his final years, and persuaded the old patriot to tell his story; Crespi published it in 1913, after the Count's death. The book is the result: not formal history but, as Crespi insists, a "human document," every chapter approved and contestable passages signed by di Rudio himself.

Di Rudio's life is the arc of the Risorgimento seen from below. Born in Belluno in 1832, he fights for the Roman Republic in 1849 under Garibaldi, is captured and nearly shot, and begins decades of exile and clandestine conspiracy across France, Switzerland, and England under the inspiration of Giuseppe Mazzini and the martyred Pietro Fortunato Calvi. In London he marries an Englishwoman, Elisa. In 1858 Felice Orsini draws him into [the plot to kill Napoleon III](events.md#6-the-orsini-attentat-14-january-1858), whose suppression of the Roman Republic the conspirators hold responsible for Italy's chains. Di Rudio throws the second bomb outside the Paris Opéra on 14 January 1858; the Emperor survives. Condemned to the guillotine alongside Orsini and Pieri, di Rudio is reprieved at the foot of the scaffold and transported to the [penal colonies of French Guiana](events.md#7-the-french-penal-colony-of-guiana-18581859), from which he stages a near-suicidal escape by sea.

Reaching London again, he clears his name of the charge that he bought his freedom by betrayal, watches Italy unified by the monarchy he distrusts, and is denied permission to return home. In a last meeting, a gravely ill Mazzini sends him to fight for the American Union. He sails for New York in 1864. The book closes elegiacally: amid every later fortune, "my Fatherland I have never seen again."

## How it's told

*Per la libertà!* is a memoir at one remove — the life of Count Carlo di Rudio as told to, and vouched for by, the journalist Cesare Crespi. In the **Preface**, Crespi explains both his subject and his method. He has found in the commercial American West a living relic of the Risorgimento, a man he believes history has calumniated or forgotten, and he means to preserve his testimony as a "human document" that captures "the soul of the times" rather than a methodical chronicle. To guarantee authenticity he obtained di Rudio's written approval of every chapter and his signature on every contestable page, and he refuses to soften the old soldier's harsh, virile speech. The narrative voice throughout alternates between Crespi's frame and di Rudio's first-person recollection, supplemented by letters and by his wife Elisa's perspective.

## What it cares about

The recurring concerns of the book are explicit: testimony against forgetting; the moral superiority of self-sacrificing republican idealism (Mazzini, Calvi, Orsini) over the "practical" calculation of the monarchy; the indictment of Napoleon III as the betrayer of Italian liberty; and the human cost — to families, to the obscure, to the women who waited — of the cause of unification.

## The book, part by part

Open a part for its summary, then open any chapter for its outline.

### Prefazione (pp. 7–8)

Cesare Crespi explained why he wrote the book, framing it not as conventional history but as a "human document" that captures "the soul of the times." He revered Count di Rudio as a living relic of the Risorgimento, encountered in the commercial Far West, and feared the man was either calumniated or forgotten — for, as Crespi observes, the triumph of political men is almost always a reward for their worst qualities. To guarantee authenticity, Crespi obtained di Rudio's written approval of every chapter and his signature on every contestable page. He refused to soften the Count's harsh, virile language, declaring that to alter words from lips "silenced for ever" would be cowardice. He closed with "Salute!"

### Part One — The Making of a Patriot and the Defense of Rome (Chapters 1–24, pp. 9–127)

Part One opens with the frame: Crespi's visit to di Rudio's modest home near Los Angeles, the household (Elisa and the children named Italia, Roma, and America), and the study with its portraits of Mazzini and Calvi. Pressed that telling his story is a duty owed to the martyrs and to his children, di Rudio begins.

The early chapters establish his formation — Belluno birth, an anti-papal father, a political education in the oppressions of post-Vienna Italy — and plunge him into 1848: [the Five Days of Milan](events.md#2-the-five-days-of-milan-march-1848), the People's War in Venetia under Pietro Fortunato Calvi, and his refusal of Austrian service. He reaches Rome in time for [the 1849 defense of the Roman Republic](events.md#3-the-roman-republic-and-its-siege-1849), fighting under Garibaldi (Palestrina, Velletri, the slaughter at the Villa Corsini), where he rescues a French prisoner from a mob, is wounded and captured, escapes, and is court-martialed and improbably acquitted.

The remainder of Part One is the machinery of exile and conspiracy: survival as a refugee laborer; the barricades of Napoleon III's [December 1851 Paris coup](events.md#4-the-coup-détat-of-louis-napoleon-december-1851); sworn membership in Giovane Italia; secret intelligence missions into Austrian Lombardy-Venetia disguised as a silk merchant; the abortive 1853 risings (Milan, the Lake Maggiore and Alpine schemes) and Calvi's capture and agonized execution; the betrayal that brings his own father, sister, and Don Bastiano to prison; a celebrated episode in which his mother, shown a corpse, declares with defiance that the dead man is not her son. Hunted, slandered by rival émigrés, and finally banished from Switzerland for life after the failed Maloggia attempt — undone by his own initials carved in a rifle stock — di Rudio chooses exile in England. Throughout, Crespi defends Mazzini and the conspirators against the charge of futility, arguing that even failed sacrifice made Italy's redemption inevitable.

#### Part 1, Chapter 1 (pp. 9–14)
The narrator arrived one summer morning in Los Angeles, drawn to a small wooden house where Major Carlo di Rudio had taken refuge. He was welcomed by di Rudio's wife, Signora Elisa, and offered coffee, toast, and figs. He met the pale, dark-eyed daughter Italia Adair, learning of the other daughters Roma Scott and America Vickery and a son exploring Arizona. Italia rhapsodized about life with the Seventh Cavalry under "Uncle Sam," praising the American temperament. The narrator admired the study's books and portraits of Giuseppe Mazzini and Fortunato Calvi. The chapter closed with the appearance of the bronze-featured old Count, exchanging a morning kiss with Elisa beneath California's giant sequoias.

#### Part 1, Chapter 2 (pp. 15–23)
Pressed by the narrator that telling his story was a historical duty owed to martyrs and his children, di Rudio began his account. Born in Belluno on 26 August 1832 to Count Ercole and Countess Elisabetta de Domini, he recalled his anti-papal father smashing a bust of Pius IX. He then surveyed the Italian question: Napoleon I's paradoxical legacy of liberty, Metternich's tyranny, the 1814–15 carving up of Italy after the Congress of Vienna, and Austrian oppression, informers, and the Spielberg prison. The chapter culminated in the Milanese insurrection of 18 March 1848, the Five Days of barricades, and Radetzky's forced retreat, with di Rudio a cadet at the San Luca Academy.

#### Part 1, Chapter 3 (pp. 23–26)
On the day of his flight from Milan, di Rudio watched the retreating Austrians from the bastions and witnessed a Croatian soldier bayonet a small child, then kill the pregnant mother who ran to him. His brother Achille restrained him from firing. He concluded that the oppressed find salvation only in rebellious action. During the retreat to Verona he saw the sacking of Melegnano and confronted his pro-Austrian uncle De Domini at Desenzano. At Castelnuovo he cradled a young girl shot fleeing a burning house, her martyrdom searing his resolve. That night, he revealed, his and Achille's hands "came to know their first blood" in vengeance.

#### Part 1, Chapter 4 (pp. 26–31)
Refusing Austrian commissions, the cadets were interned at Gratz, then sent home. As the People's War continued after Piedmont's defeat, di Rudio and Achille joined Pietro Fortunato Calvi's Cacciatori delle Alpi in Venice, where he first glimpsed Felice Orsini and lost Achille to pestilence. Disciplinary clashes followed: he fired guns at Marghera against orders, and in March 1849 he sided with the crowd in Piazza San Marco, where Daniele Manin's eloquence dispersed it. Calvi quietly ordered him to leave for Rome. Through repeated escapes, disguises, and bluffs across Chioggia, Ravenna, Bologna, and Ferrara, he reached Rome with the Legione Garibaldi as French troops attacked on 30 April.

#### Part 1, Chapter 5 (pp. 31–34)
Di Rudio traced the career of Luigi Napoleone (Napoleon III), "fatal to Liberty," whom the Carbonari distrusted since 1831 over the suspicious death of his brother Napoleone Luigi. He recounted the failed Strasbourg and Boulogne coups, imprisonment at Ham, and Princess Cristina Belgioioso Triulzio's interviews offering liberal support in exchange for championing Italian independence. After his rise to the French presidency on 20 December 1848, his first notable act was sending Oudinot's troops to restore papal rule in Rome. As France protested and his forces met defeat on 30 April, the chapter emphasized Mazzini's faith that martyrdom would regenerate Italy.

#### Part 1, Chapter 6 (pp. 34–39)
Di Rudio recounted his combat in the Roman Republic's defense: the 30 April victory, fighting alongside Garibaldi at Palestrina (9 May) and Velletri, where his band of youths rescued Garibaldi and Aguyar, earning a fatherly cheek-pinch. After Oudinot treacherously seized Villa Pamfili, the savage 3 June battle for Villa Corsini followed. He escorted a captured French officer to safety, shielding him from a vengeful mob at the Ponte di San Sisto. He described the carnage of Manara's Bersaglieri, the deaths of Enrico Dandolo and the heroism of Masina, and Mameli's wounding. That night he joined a perilous sortie to recover bodies, returning with French brains on his sleeve.

#### Part 1, Chapter 7 (pp. 39–43)
Di Rudio described the siege's final month after Oudinot shifted to flanking trenches, recalling Ugo Bassi, Ciceruacchio, Anita Garibaldi, and Princess Belgioioso, whose later accusation against Louis Napoleon preceded an attempt on her life. On the stormy night of San Pietro the French broke through; di Rudio fell, was captured, escaped by killing a sentry with his dagger, was recaptured and court-martialed. A French officer's impassioned defense—arguing he was a lawful combatant who had not given parole—won his acquittal. Marched toward Civitavecchia on 1 July, he escaped again, learned of Garibaldi's departure for Venice and the executions of Bassi and Ciceruacchio, and took refuge in San Marino.

#### Part 1, Chapter 8 (pp. 43–48)
After Rome's fall, di Rudio drifted to Genoa among thousands of refugees, refusing a Bersaglieri commission arranged by his father, declaring himself a Mazzinian who would sooner serve Satan than swear to a Monarchy. After a deadly cemetery riot over a "God and the People" banner, he fled by ship toward America but was forced ashore at Cartagena. With the ex-seminarian republican Trolli, he crossed Spain, surviving by Trolli's mock sermons in Latin, then reached Perpignan, where his former Villa Corsini prisoner recognized and feted him. In Marseilles he swore Mazzini's oath, joining Giovane Italia. Reaching Paris by June 1851, he watched Louis Napoleon prepare his Coup d'État.

#### Part 1, Chapter 9 (pp. 48–52)
Di Rudio recounted arriving in Paris and being welcomed by the Club of Batignolles, where he and his companions made cartridges. On 2 December 1851, walking to their meeting-place, they found the city under military occupation: Louis Napoleon's coup d'état was underway. With sixteen Frenchmen and two Italians, di Rudio helped raise a barricade by the Club of Saint-Denis; the Vincennes chasseurs were twice repulsed, and on the third assault overran it — the defenders having already slipped away. He then surveyed the wider coup: arrested generals, seized printing presses, the Boulevard Montmartre massacre fueled by brandy, mass nocturnal executions, and the Military and Mixed Commissions that ruined forty thousand families. The chapter indicts Napoleon III's seizure of power.

#### Part 1, Chapter 10 (pp. 53–58)
After abandoning the barricade, di Rudio and Trolli escaped Paris disguised as workmen, slipping past the Barrière du Trône. A tipsy peasant gave them a lift; di Rudio drove while the man slept. Through the secret society's network they reached Troy, then Langres, where a jealous postmaster's wife sent gendarmes after a mysterious letter di Rudio burned. Crossing into penniless Switzerland, Trolli was jailed at Berne and escorted to the frontier; di Rudio, starving, sold his pipe and survived. At Lucerne he turned the tables on a papal recruiting agent, accepting enlistment only to have the man arrested, financing his own journey home over the Saint Gotthard.

#### Part 1, Chapter 11 (pp. 59–62)
In April 1852, working for a merchant tailor in Genoa, di Rudio learned that his old mentor Don Bastiano Barozzi, a patriot priest, was a refugee in Turin. Walking there, he reunited joyfully with Barozzi, who led him to Fortunato Calvi. Calvi, having heard a calumny that di Rudio deserted to the Austrians, received him coldly until di Rudio disproved it. Mazzini, Kossuth, and Ledru Rollin had founded the European Revolutionary Committee in London; its Turin branch, under Count Grillanzoni with Calvi commanding military operations, commissioned di Rudio as a secret envoy to Lombardy-Venetia. The chapter also denounces Francesco Giuseppe's reprisals against Hungarian and Italian patriots.

#### Part 1, Chapter 12 (pp. 63–66)
Di Rudio narrated episodes from his two intelligence missions to Lombardy-Venetia, traveling under a false passport as silk merchant Carlo Moretti, coding troop strengths as bales of colored silk. At Mantova, amid the arrests preceding the Belfiore martyrdoms, a patriot guard chief and an old Academy classmate sheltered him. At Verona's Scudo di Francia he escaped gendarmes by dropping three floors into a dung-heap and bluffing through a hotel kitchen. At Milan, betrayed by a bill-poster's shouted warning, he leapt from the city ramparts and fled to Pavia. Reaching Stradella and then Calvi, he proved a report of his death false, moving Don Bastiano to tears.

#### Part 1, Chapter 13 (pp. 67–74)
Di Rudio defended Mazzini against blame for the 1853 Milan rising, stressing it was begun by the workers' secret *Fratellanza*, organized by the dyer Assi, after the Belfiore hangings. He detailed Calvi's plan: a 6 February strike to seize officers and the Castello's twelve thousand muskets, supported by Hungarian troops, Pavia students, and barricades. Di Rudio's own mission was to carry four thousand lire in gold to Sander and Radonitz at Locarno for Calvi's bold scheme to capture the steamship *Radetzky* and seize Lake Maggiore. He recounted scouting the *Radetzky*, the two officers' hesitation, and his perilous flight from carabinieri at Intra by commandeering a boat at gunpoint.

#### Part 1, Chapter 14 (pp. 74–76)
Fleeing toward Austrian waters for safety, di Rudio battled a violent Alpine storm on the lake, rowing alongside the two boatmen through freezing sleet until they landed, half-frozen, at republican Brissago. The next morning, still believing the 4 February timing left hope, he trudged through deep snow back to the Olivieris' house at Locarno. There he found Sander and Radonitz determined to delay, waiting for the police to relax, and reaching for the gold as funds for their own escape. Outraged, di Rudio locked the door, drew his double-barreled pistol, forced them to surrender the money meant for arms, and departed with an ironic salute to the two officers.

#### Part 1, Chapter 15 (pp. 76–79)
On 6 February 1853, instead of fighting in Milan, di Rudio climbed to a white villa above Lugano to meet Giuseppe Mazzini. He described his first impression of the slight, black-clad agitator, whose serene gaze and written-like speech moved him deeply. Mazzini accepted the recovered gold without comment, declared nothing yet lost, and resolved to kindle a parallel rising at Varese, needing one daring man. Di Rudio pleaded his record at Venice, Rome, and Paris and claimed the right to serve; Mazzini granted him the Varese enterprise. Setting out with smugglers, he reached the frontier inn, only for a courier to deliver Mazzini's note: "Return immediately... In Milan all is lost."

#### Part 1, Chapter 16 (pp. 80–83)
Di Rudio analyzed why the 6 February 1853 Milan rising failed despite perfect secrecy and surprise. At the moment of action Assi and other chiefs vanished, leaving the few to disperse. The assault on the Gran Guardia succeeded under a charcoal-burner, but the impetuous insurgents abandoned the plan, killed officers, and fell back to Porta Romana, where innkeeper Giuseppe Monti fought to the last cartridge, killing 150 of the enemy. Di Rudio blamed the bourgeois and aristocratic patriots—Emilio Visconti Venosta and his kind—who withheld men and arms and even disowned the executed martyrs, amid the era's anonymous insinuation that Mazzini was an Austrian spy. Yet the bloodshed, he argued, made redemption inevitable.

#### Part 1, Chapter 17 (pp. 83–88)
Returning to Locarno to deliver a message from Mazzini to Calvi, Carlo di Rudio was arrested, then expelled from Switzerland and forced to choose exile in England. In London he joined fellow refugees, supported himself gardening at Pianciani's villa, and grew close to Mazzini. Sent to deliver a letter to the Hungarian exile Kossuth, he received funds for the cause. Mazzini then unveiled, on Fortunato Calvi's counsel, a plan for Alpine guerrilla insurrection, justifying audacity over caution. Di Rudio traveled to Zurich, where Calvi detailed an elaborate scheme to seize Belluno's Finance Office, Gendarmerie, and Barracks, then capture the Osoppo fortress as an insurgent base.

#### Part 1, Chapter 18 (pp. 88–92)
Traveling under a false name, di Rudio crossed into the Tyrol unhindered, recruited the four Burrasca smuggler brothers to move munitions, and slipped into his native Belluno by night. He reached the priest Don Bastiano Barozzi, who, like his father, urged him to abandon the doomed enterprise and spare his loved ones. After an emotional confrontation, his father embraced the cause. The plan was to delay unearthing hidden arms until the local fighters were ready. Sent to hide beneath an ancient chestnut tree to await a guide signaling with three whistles, di Rudio leveled his pistols at an approaching figure in the dark, only to recognize his own mother.

#### Part 1, Chapter 19 (pp. 92–98)
Di Rudio carried his weeping mother to safety, learning from his brother Giustiniano that the conspiracy had collapsed: Don Bastiano and the best patriots were arrested, and the police hunted him alive or dead. He took refuge with the peasant Domenico in the mountains. His sister Luisa was arrested after a compromising letter from Calvi (mentioning Calvi's beloved Teresa Duodo) was hidden in their father's guitar. After a chance encounter with an Austrian officer, gendarmes raided Domenico's house; di Rudio watched from a hidden cavern as a sergeant tortured the peasant by hanging, yet Domenico and Caterina never betrayed him. He resolved to flee at dawn.

#### Part 1, Chapter 20 (pp. 99–100)
Drawn from Giustiniano's letters, the chapter recounts how he shielded their mother with optimism while dreading the worst. Rumor spread that a fleeing patriot had been shot dead in the Alpine gorges, his body brought to Belluno. Summoned by the authorities, the mother was led to a cold chamber to identify a sheeted corpse. When the officers drew back the sheet, revealing a livid face, she gave a single sob, then declared with maternal defiance that the dead man was not her son: Carletto lived and would avenge his country. Her dignity stunned the Austrian officers, who let her depart unmolested.

#### Part 1, Chapter 21 (pp. 101–108)
Di Rudio's flight across the high Alps nearly killed him: exhausted, he collapsed in the snow and was saved only by a nightmare jolting him awake, glimpsing soldiers bayonet a fallen smuggler mistaken for him. At Bolzano's fair he found a posted notice offering six thousand florins for his head. The Burrasca brothers brawled with gendarmes demanding his passport, letting him escape into the crowd. Sheltered by a sympathetic cobbler who took him for a deserter, he eluded patrols in town, then at Salzburg fooled gendarmes by playing cards. He finally crossed into free Swiss territory at Muntzen, taunting a frustrated guard, his elation soon darkened by news of disaster.

#### Part 1, Chapter 22 (pp. 108–112)
The chapter recounts the conspiracy's collapse and Calvi's martyrdom. The spy Felicita Buanvecchiato had betrayed the plot; arrests followed when conspirators carried incriminating itineraries. Pietro Fortunato Calvi, caught on 17 September with his commission as general-in-chief signed by Mazzini, Kossuth, and Ledru Rollin, was imprisoned at Mantua's Castello di San Giorgio alongside di Rudio's father, Luisa, and Don Bastiano. Felice Orsini, jailed there in March 1855, later escaped. Calvi protected his fellow conspirators, refused to beg Austria for clemency, and was hanged on a too-short gallows, dying in prolonged agony. Crespi laments how official slander and partisan cliques diminished Calvi's heroic memory.

#### Part 1, Chapter 23 (pp. 112–120)
In Zurich, di Rudio found work at Caronti's hat factory but sensed his fellow exiles' hostility, catching a menacing remark (the actual plot to murder him on the lake he would learn only later, from Fumagalli). He fled to Paris in December 1853, lodging with the hatter Zappa and laboring fourteen-hour days turning a merry-go-round wheel. The narrative digresses into a sustained indictment of Napoleon III's coup, empire, and faithless foreign policy, arguing his removal was necessary for liberty. Di Rudio's letters to Mazzini went unanswered; he learned that Austrian calumny from Zurich had falsely blamed him for Calvi's arrest. Returning to Zurich, he forced a written retraction from Caronti, and Fumagalli revealed the exiles had nearly murdered him on the lake.

#### Part 1, Chapter 24 (pp. 120–127)
Di Rudio joined the Maloggia attempt to seize Lake Como's steamboats and raise the Valtellina, recruited by Pompei in Orsini's name. He disputes Orsini's later memoir, which blamed Mazzini and belittled the effort. With Fumagalli he assembled rifles near the Muretto glacier, fatally carving his initials "C. C. R." into the fiftieth stock. Militia seized the arms; both men fled but were captured and jailed at Chur, where the engraved rifle confirmed his guilt. Banished as a recidivist, di Rudio was expelled from Switzerland in perpetuity and again chose England. di Rudio closes Part One defending the attempt as a genuine protest against tyranny, not mere comedy.

### Part Two — The Orsini Conspiracy and American Exile (Chapters 1–33, pp. 127–268)

Part Two begins as a love story: in London's poverty-stricken émigré community di Rudio teaches Italian, is anonymously fed by the young Englishwoman Elisa, and — overcoming his principled objection to marriage in the midst of the cause — weds her in December 1855. In September 1857 Felice Orsini seeks him out and revives [the plan to assassinate Napoleon III](events.md#6-the-orsini-attentat-14-january-1858). Di Rudio joins; the conspiracy, brokered partly by Simon Bernard in London, equips itself with new fulminate bombs. The central chapters narrate the plot with the intimacy of an eyewitness: the crossing to Paris under a forged passport, the lodgings, the reconnaissance of the Opéra, and the attack of 14 January 1858, in which di Rudio throws the second bomb. The Emperor survives behind his armored carriage.

The aftermath dominates the rest of the book. Arrested, di Rudio maintains a false identity until betrayed, endures the famous trial, and is condemned to death with Orsini and Pieri — then reprieved at the scaffold itself, his sentence commuted through appeals to the Empress. Transported to [French Guiana](events.md#7-the-french-penal-colony-of-guiana-18581859), he survives a yellow-fever epidemic that kills his fellow conspirators and mounts a harrowing escape by sea, reaching British asylum. Returning to London, he labors to clear his name of the charge of betrayal and is vindicated by Mazzini's own testimony. The closing chapters turn to history and elegy: poverty and the death of a child; sustained polemic against the House of Savoy and the "practical" unification that di Rudio sees as a betrayal of republican ideals; Garibaldi's wounding at [Aspromonte (1862)](events.md#9-aspromonte-1862); and a final meeting in which the gravely ill Mazzini redirects him from Poland toward the American Civil War. Di Rudio sails for New York in February 1864, and the book ends on the lasting sorrow of a patriot who never saw his homeland again.

#### Part 2, Chapter 1 (pp. 127–134)
Crespi opened Part 2 with the love story underlying di Rudio's London exile, drawn from the Count, his wife, and old letters. Arriving in London, di Rudio sang in the Drury Lane chorus, then labored at the Wapping Docks making bottle-coverings, amid the destitution of the émigré community, against whose misery the pensive figure of Mazzini stood out. He lodged with the figurine-maker Mancherini, befriended Fumagalli and Leoni, and routed the spy-provocateur Bonacina in a brawl, later disarming him with words. He began teaching Italian to Sara Mancherini and her cousin Elisa. Losing his job, he sank into hunger until, one night, a mysterious parcel of food appeared on his bed.

#### Part 2, Chapter 2 (pp. 134–136)
The anonymous nightly food parcels continued, and di Rudio resolved to catch his benefactor by hiding behind his window curtain. Instead, Elisa entered, parcel in hand, claiming an unnamed well-dressed Italian had delivered it that morning. Distracted by the puzzle, di Rudio failed to respond to her evident feeling. Sara then arrived bearing a rose bush, explaining that Elisa had chosen it because roses were the di Rudio family emblem and would recall his mother and Italy. After both withdrew, di Rudio sat transfixed, and for the first time felt love stir as a redeeming force above the misery of exile.

#### Part 2, Chapter 3 (pp. 136–141)
Di Rudio wrestled with whether he loved Sara or Elisa while resisting marriage on principle. His poverty ended when Luigi Pianciani, the Roman exile whose *Storia dei Papi* manuscript had vanished (rumored seized by Jesuits), hired him as amanuensis in London, having just returned from Victor Hugo's exile circle on Jersey. He learned the rent had secretly been paid all along by Elisa from her savings. When Leoni left to enlist for the Crimea, di Rudio refused, denouncing mercenary service. Crespi situates this against Mazzini's strategy and the Crimean War. Confronted with Elisa's love and her insistence on sharing his cause, di Rudio yielded; they wed on 9 December 1855.

#### Part 2, Chapter 4 (pp. 142–143)
Narrated by di Rudio: the year he married he was drawn into a notorious affair. He befriended a Mantuan named Rossi at a Rupert Street eating-house, dismissing the Romagnol Foschini's warning that Rossi was a French spy. One night Foschini called Rossi a spy and lunged with a dagger; intervening, di Rudio smashed the lamp and was stabbed in the chest, near-fatally, surviving by a doctor's heroic remedy. Foschini, sheltered by sympathizers, later died fighting beside Carlo Pisacane at Sapri. Crespi frames Sapri as the first sign of Mazzini's new southern strategy after Cavour gained little from the Crimean War.

#### Part 2, Chapter 5 (pp. 144–148)
In September 1857, while teaching languages in Nottingham, di Rudio was brought into the plot when an exile he had once aided mentioned him to Felice Orsini at Birmingham; Orsini—"the very man I am looking for!"—summoned him to London. Orsini revived di Rudio's old proposal to Mazzini to kill Napoleon, deliberately excluding Mazzini and his suspect lieutenant Massarenti. He laid out his grand aim: regicide in Paris would spark French revolution, free Italy, and let Orsini himself take command of the French army at Rome and proclaim the Republic. Di Rudio committed. After bringing wife Elisa and their child to London, he met Orsini's emissary Simon Bernard. The Secret Committee first granted Elisa fourteen shillings weekly, then abruptly relieved di Rudio of any part for being a family man.

#### Part 2, Chapter 6 (pp. 149–152)
After Orsini wrote that he needed di Rudio specifically or would abandon the plot, di Rudio renewed his pledge. Simon Bernard revealed the chosen weapon was bombs of a new hedgehog design devised by Baron di Torocfalda, cast in Birmingham and charged with mercury fulminate. Di Rudio failed to secure Mazzini's aid, gaining only a brush-off referral to Massarenti. Crespi recounts the era's regicidal climate, including the Tebaldi and Pianori attempts. Di Rudio then collected the remaining fulminate from Miss Elisa Cheney at Camden Town in a tiger-embroidered carpet-bag. On 8 January 1858 Bernard ordered his departure; di Rudio bade a wrenching farewell to Elisa and their child and fled.

#### Part 2, Chapter 7 (pp. 154–156)
Di Rudio crossed to France carrying the fulminate and a blade hidden in his carpet-bag, traveling on a forged Portuguese passport as Giovanni da Sylva. A violent storm delayed the crossing — the steamer made Calais twelve hours late, on 9 January — and on reaching Paris he found he had missed Orsini at the station. Forced to visit Orsini's rue Monthabor lodgings, he found the conspirator with Antonio Gomez (whom di Rudio at first takes for an Englishman, "the false Allsop"). di Rudio spread the damp fulminate on a newspaper while Orsini ran his fingertips over it, lamenting the "twelve apostles" to be cast. Giuseppe Pieri then arrived; di Rudio noted that Orsini's secrecy plan had collapsed, the conspirators now all known to one another. Orsini evoked a united Italy awakening.

#### Part 2, Chapter 8 (pp. 157–160)
Di Rudio lodged with Pieri at the Hôtel France et Champagne. He offers a sympathetic portrait of the candid, atheist, philosophizing Lucchese, including Pieri's unconventional views on marriage and his amicable separation from his wife. On Saturday 12 January, Orsini led the group to the piazza of the Opéra and detailed his plan: Gomez, Pieri, and di Rudio with Orsini stationed around the Rue Le Peletier to bomb Napoleon's carriage as he arrived for a performance with La Ristori. Di Rudio, alarmed by Pieri's indiscretion in Brussels, urged Orsini to let him act alone with fewer participants, even disguised as a city guard. Orsini refused: "It is too late."

#### Part 2, Chapter 9 (pp. 161–162)
On the evening of 14 January 1858, di Rudio and Pieri dined as usual, then learned an unnamed visitor had sought Pieri. Orsini appeared, announced the Emperor had given the signal, distributed two hundred francs apiece, and urged steadfastness for the Fatherland. Pieri, vexed, flung down the money but handed di Rudio a six-shot American revolver. At Orsini's lodgings the bombs were distributed in knotted black handkerchiefs to serve as slings. Setting out, di Rudio suspected the now-silent Pieri of meaning to desert, and missed the sign Pieri gave when shadowed by a stranger. On the way, di Rudio recognized Francesco Crispi exchanging words with Orsini, who was vexed to be seen.

#### Part 2, Chapter 10 (pp. 163–165)
Stationed opposite the Opéra by the Broggi trattoria, di Rudio and Felice Orsini awaited the imperial cortège on the night of 14 January 1858. As Napoleon III's iron-plated carriage arrived, Antonio Gomez—unable to wait—threw the first bomb prematurely. Di Rudio flung himself down, then, urged on by the bleeding Orsini ("Your turn!"), hurled the second, shattering a carriage wheel and felling the horses. A third bomb followed amid darkness, screams, and the Lancers of the Imperial Guard firing on the fleeing crowd. Di Rudio crawled clear, convinced the tyrant was dead and Italy's road to liberty now open.

#### Part 2, Chapter 11 (pp. 166–168)
Believing Napoleon dead, di Rudio first thought of his wife Elisa, writing her a note ("all is well") from a café before discarding his revolver in the Seine. He soon learned the truth: shielded by his carriage's iron plates, the Emperor had reappeared unhurt in his theater box beside the pale Eugénie. The attempt had failed; the Empire stood stronger. Returning to the Hôtel France et Champagne, di Rudio was twice visited by plain-clothes police, escaped briefly, missed the Bordeaux train, then was arrested in his room. At the Prefecture he glimpsed the bandaged Felice Orsini being brought in.

#### Part 2, Chapter 12 (pp. 169–172)
Interrogated before the Prefect of Police and an examining magistrate (with Plon-Plon among the dignitaries), di Rudio sustained his false identity as the Portuguese "da Sylva," a Burton Ale seller who barely knew his roommate. Confronted with Orsini, he avoided identifying him; the magistrate exposed Orsini's feigned English by his accent and "leagues," prompting Orsini's proud declaration of his name. Seeing two unexploded bombs lying on the desk, di Rudio nearly lunged to detonate them but was restrained. His alibi about the café and stamps was verified, and he was released as a "witness"—only to be undone when Antonio Gomez, brought in, betrayed that they knew each other.

#### Part 2, Chapter 13 (pp. 173–177)
Di Rudio enumerated the blunders that doomed the conspiracy: Pieri's trust in Hartmann, a storm preventing his meeting with Orsini, the executors' improvised alliance, Pieri's loose talk, Gomez's nerves, Orsini's unsuitability for conspiracy, the unwitting good faith of the love-blinded Elisa Cheney, and Simon Bernard's defection in safe London. Brought to the Palais de Justice, he found witnesses assembled to identify him, including the Morand concierges. The magistrate greeted him by his true name—Carlo di Rudio, count—revealing Gomez had let it slip. Abandoning the "da Sylva" pose, di Rudio admitted visiting Orsini but denied conspiratorial intent, before being committed to Mazas for trial.

#### Part 2, Chapter 14 (pp. 178–183)
Resigned to his fate, di Rudio endured Mazas alongside planted police spies. A one-eyed street-sweeper gave perjured testimony placing him at the Opéra an hour before the attack. Transferred to the Conciergerie (cells 1–4 holding di Rudio, Pieri, Gomez, Orsini), he learned public opinion was shifting in their favor. A letter he had written Orsini from London—about the suspected spy Carlotti—was produced as damning evidence. Then his wife Elisa was brought to Paris: detective Sanders had used Miss Cheney's love for Orsini to extract testimony. After di Rudio insulted Cheney to discredit the incriminating traveling-bag, Elisa revealed herself as his wife; overwrought, di Rudio publicly confessed to throwing the second bomb.

#### Part 2, Chapter 15 (pp. 184–186)
Back in his cell, di Rudio wagered champagne with his fellow prisoners over whether he would be sentenced to death. His lawyer, Mathieu de la Drôme, urged him—for the sake of his aged parents, wife, and child—to permit a defense portraying him as a weak youth swayed by his leaders, "cheating the executioner"; di Rudio consented, explaining why trial accounts depict him as colorless. He then surveyed his co-defendants' defenses: Gomez, undone by his own letters, confessed to the first bomb while feigning ignorance of its lethality; Pieri, caught with a loaded bomb and betrayed by Cheney and Hartmann, spun fabrications before refusing further answers, reserving his case for trial.

#### Part 2, Chapter 16 (pp. 187–190)
Di Rudio offered a psychological portrait of Felice Orsini: a man of generous impulses but oscillating between exaltation and depression, gifted at conceiving grand designs yet hopelessly negligent of the small precautions a conspirator needs—he had preserved incriminating letters despite urging others to burn theirs. His overflowing imagination bred overconfidence and contempt for companions, explaining his bitter break with Giuseppe Mazzini and his stinging words toward Pieri and Gomez. Yet di Rudio insisted on Orsini's true greatness: his lonely courage drying fulminate by the fire, and above all his refusal to betray the conspiracy's secrets to save himself. He praised Jules Favre's defense, especially Orsini's celebrated letter to Napoleon III.

#### Part 2, Chapter 17 (pp. 191–195)
The trial opened 25 February before a fashionable, secretly sympathetic crowd. Di Rudio argued the proceedings aimed not merely to convict but to defame the accused, since the Empire's own legitimacy stood implicitly on trial. Prosecutor Chaix d'Est-Ange's indictment and President Delangle's hostile conduct branded the conspirators as base assassins—Pieri as a thief, Orsini as a liar over the third bomb, di Rudio as a mercenary who killed for 200 francs. He denounced the perjured street-sweeper "Kim" and denied meeting Crispi (Orsini having shielded the third bomb-thrower). On the night of 26 February, sentences fell: life for Gomez; the parricide's penalty—decapitation—for Pieri, Orsini, and di Rudio. He passed the night drinking champagne with his guards.

#### Part 2, Chapter 18 (pp. 196–201)
At La Roquette the three condemned men, in straitjackets, were housed within sight of one another. Orsini refused to appeal until Favre forced his hand. The chaplain Hughon visited daily; the loquacious Pieri baited him in argument, while Orsini, formerly bitter toward Mazzini and Pieri, softened on learning of Mazzini's noble public defense—and, more strikingly, drifted back toward religion. Prefect of Police Pietri visited often, dangling hopes of clemency, and finally proposed that di Rudio betray the Empire's internal enemies in exchange for his life and reunion with Elisa, whose letters had been suppressed. Di Rudio indignantly refused. After the Cassation appeal was rejected on 11 March, the scaffold was built on the night of the 12th.

#### Part 2, Chapter 19 (pp. 202–206)
Sensing the truth from the chaplain Hughon's trembling hand, di Rudio learned he would be executed at dawn and asked Saturno to wake him at five to write his last letters. Orsini refused to inform ("Felice Orsini knows how to die"); Pieri vowed to die singing. Led barefoot through rain and snow toward the scaffold—Pieri intoning "Mourir pour la Patrie"—di Rudio was halted at the last moment by de Collet, the Empress's major-domo: his sentence was commuted. Passing Orsini ("Better two than three!"), he returned to find Saturno collapsed. The narrative closes in California with Elisa displaying two newly hatched chicks, as di Rudio, watching the setting sun, envisions Orsini's severed head crying "Viva l'Italia!"

#### Part 2, Chapter 20 (pp. 207–210)
Monsieur de Collet revealed to di Rudio that English personages had petitioned the Empress Eugénie, who, on the crown prince's second birthday, commuted his death sentence to perpetual imprisonment; he was pressed to thank the Emperor and complied, treating any subterfuge as legitimate self-preservation. Before President Delangle the commutation (dated 12 March) was ratified. Transferred to La Roquette, he reunited with Antonio Gomez and mourned Orsini and Pieri. He received a manipulative letter from "Sister Teresa," a Carmelite serving Church-and-Empire espionage. Most movingly, his wife Elisa and their child visited; he urged her caution and vowed to be free within a year.

#### Part 2, Chapter 21 (pp. 212–215)
Di Rudio recounted his first escape plot: a cultured English debtor at La Roquette, who hated Napoleon, offered to fund a breakout from Toulon. Transferred to Toulon in April 1858, he wore convict garb, gnawed at his chain-ring, and endured curious visitors, including Russian officers. The spy-nun "Sister Teresa" sent a missionary bishop bound for China bearing gifts (rosary, scapulars, missal), all refused. The chapter recounts Elisa's parallel ordeal at Nottingham, where a bishop falsely reported di Rudio's death and Catholic conversion; she defiantly insisted he lived. Just as the Englishman's scheme matured, di Rudio was abruptly shipped toward Cayenne, having refused to betray accomplices.

#### Part 2, Chapter 22 (pp. 216–222)
Aboard the convict transport, di Rudio and Gomez reached the penal colony at the Montagne d'Argent (11 December 1858), near the Brazilian frontier. After the commandant threatened him and an overseer insulted Italians, di Rudio's fierce defiance—including kicking over a soup pot to avenge a withheld ration—won the convicts' respect. He planned a second escape: a hollowed-out pirogue built by night with eight accomplices. The canoe was completed, but yellow fever then devastated the colony — a population of more than six hundred reduced to sixty-three, some 540 dead, his eight escape-plot accomplices among them; di Rudio was one of only three never infected. The survivors were evacuated aboard the *Abeille*.

#### Part 2, Chapter 23 (pp. 222–225)
Landed on the Île du Salut, di Rudio's hope revived at the sight of a fishing boat. He befriended Morbioli, a proud Trasteveran serving ten years for killing his sister's seducer. Morbioli explained why escape was futile: informers were rewarded with pardons, and past attempts ended in horror—shipwrecked convicts who turned cannibal, the overland fugitives reduced to one flesh-eating survivor (beheaded), and the son of Giacomo Pianori, whose raft party perished in the mud-banks (*vase*) and crab-infested shores of Dutch Guiana. Assigned to the anchor workshop, di Rudio nonetheless began devising a plan built upon his predecessors' failures: seize the fishing boat, not a raft.

#### Part 2, Chapter 24 (pp. 225–228)
Di Rudio gathered intelligence on potential allies. Gomez was transferred away to Corù and vanished from his life. He recruited fellow Italians (Morelli the tailor; Galli, Rossini, Tebaldi) and identified two key assets: a French archpriest hiding twenty thousand francs in his Bible binding to fund provisions, and a daring former Orleanist naval officer fit to captain the voyage. Watching Morbioli polish the gunboats, di Rudio was struck by the revelation "sink it!"—a plan to scuttle the pursuit boats. Morbioli enthusiastically joined. By night di Rudio broke into the warehouses and stole six canvas hammocks, which Morelli sewed into a sail.

#### Part 2, Chapter 25 (pp. 228–232)
The frigate *Amazzone* brought news of Magenta, prompting di Rudio's bitter reflection that Vittorio Emanuele II's reliance on Napoleon would yield only paltry Piedmontese gains, not true Italian liberty—he wished Napoleon had been destroyed there instead. An amnesty decree freed only French subjects; Galli, Rossini, and Tebaldi withdrew to petition the Emperor, abandoning the escape. The Cousin recruited replacements. On 9 December, after the *Abeille* departed, di Rudio lured the fishing boat ashore with a five-franc piece; armed accomplices seized it. Morbioli had secretly scuttled the gunboats, which sank during pursuit as the cheering convicts watched. Eleven men, the Cousin captaining, sailed north into a roaring storm toward freedom.

#### Part 2, Chapter 26 (pp. 232–235)
Di Rudio described the harrowing voyage: ferocious storms forced them to bail ceaselessly for two days and three nights, using palmetto hats and wooden clogs. On the third night a great Dutch sailing vessel nearly ran them down, the Cousin barely swerving clear. Salt water then ruined their provisions, raising the specter of cannibalism. After evading what they took to be the pursuing *Abeille*, they reached the coast of Dutch Guiana—the very land that had doomed Pianori's men—and fled its fish-drying slaves and their armed white overseer. Sailing west toward British Guiana, the canoe became trapped in a *vase*; for hours they stood in mud, tormented by crabs, until the rising tide freed them.

#### Part 2, Chapter 27 (pp. 235–240)
Adrift after escaping the penal colony, di Rudio and his fellow fugitives were hailed by a Black English cutter captain, who fed them and pointed them toward the Berbice river. On 15 December 1859 they reached New Amsterdam in British Guiana, where the white-whiskered Governor offered asylum conditional on honest labor. A mulatto physician, Brussels-educated and a Times correspondent, took di Rudio in. When the French warship Abeille arrived demanding extradition, the Governor refused, citing England's protection of political prisoners. Di Rudio was free; his companions, fearing recapture, fled by sea and vanished forever.

#### Part 2, Chapter 28 (pp. 240–245)
The Governor (whom di Rudio recalls as Melville) gave him a Bible and offered passage on a March man-of-war, but impatience prevailed. Feigning illness and a faked death-by-savages story to deceive a French spy, di Rudio sailed Christmas Eve aboard the brig John Romelly. After a storm-battered crossing he reached London on 29 February 1860, penniless. He learned Simon Bernard, acquitted after trial, was slandering him as a police informer; a confrontation followed. Seeking Mazzini in vain, he was reunited with his wife Elisa, who fainted into his arms.

#### Part 2, Chapter 29 (pp. 245–249)
In London di Rudio fought to clear his name against Bernard's calumny that he had bought his freedom by betraying accomplices. Aided by Rossi and the dying journalist Kinaer, he toured manufacturing cities lecturing on the Orsini trial and his escape. Vindication came threefold: a French ex-convict, Le Houx, corroborated his escape; Mazzini's attestation, published in the Reasoner, settled the matter; and Bernard himself, ruined and demented, died. Di Rudio also recalled Baron Egassy di Torocfalda, inventor of the Orsini bombs, who died of want in a Birmingham hospital before sharing his explosive's formula.

#### Part 2, Chapter 30 (pp. 249–252)
In 1860 the French Police made a last bid to corrupt di Rudio, an inspector offering money as Elisa, again pregnant, lived in poverty; he refused, sustained by his wife's devotion and supported by work in a marble mill arranged by Le Houx. A Catholic priest then pressed him to baptize his child, dangling a lucrative position; di Rudio, denouncing religion as solar myth and Church history as obscurantism, threw him out. That same Christmas Eve his infant second child died, too poor even to bury it, yet he counseled Elisa that an upright conscience expects no reward.

#### Part 2, Chapter 31 (pp. 253–258)
Di Rudio reviewed Italian history from his London exile: Napoleon III's betrayal at Villafranca, the central duchies' uprisings, and Garibaldi's landing at Marsala, Calatafimi, and the conquest of the Two Sicilies — the expedition organized by Mazzini, Pilo, Bertani, and Bixio. He condemned the cession of Nice and Savoy and Cavour's opportunism. The chapter culminates in the betrayal at Aspromonte (28 August 1862), where Garibaldi, falling with the cry "Long live Italy!", was wounded by royal troops. Throughout, di Rudio exalted the self-sacrificing republican spirit over the Savoyard Monarchy's self-serving calculation.

#### Part 2, Chapter 32 (pp. 259–263)
A sweeping polemic against the House of Savoy across half a century: the humiliating Italian defeats of Custoza and Lissa in 1866 (Venetia won in spite of them), Rome gained only reluctantly after Mentana, and the nation corrupted by a "practical" pragmatism that abandoned ideals. Di Rudio likened King and Pope as twin dogmas oppressing Italy, lamented the alliance with Austria, and praised Carlo Alberto, "the Hamlet of Italy," over Vittorio Emanuele II. He affirmed Mazzini's vision of a free Italy and, addressing Vittorio Emanuele III, warned that the Monarchy must honor its compact or forfeit its throne. The editor (C. C.) notes di Rudio died before the Libyan war.

#### Part 2, Chapter 33 (pp. 264–268)
Roused by Aspromonte, di Rudio joined London protests against the Pope's temporal power, defending the "O Roma o Morte" banner against an Irish mob. Denied passage home by consul Corti, who warned he would be handed to the French, he resolved to serve liberty everywhere. A final meeting with the gravely aged Mazzini followed: the Maestro dissuaded him from fighting for Poland, a lost cause, and urged him instead to sail for the American Union's war against slavery, furnishing letters and a commendation di Rudio treasured for life. On 8 February 1864 he embarked at Liverpool aboard the *Virginia* for New York. The book closes elegiacally: half a century on, amid triumph and fortune, a secret sorrow endured, for he never saw his Fatherland again.
